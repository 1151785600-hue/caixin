What will Hong Kong’s proposed reforms to sexual offences laws change?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 888 words | 2026-07-08

Hong Kong authorities have put forward a sweeping overhaul of the city’s sexual offences laws, such as broadening the definition of rape, introducing new offences covering non-consensual acts involving children and persons with mental impairment, setting a uniform age of consent at 16, and removing gender-specific provisions.

The proposals, which follow the Law Reform Commission’s reviews of sexual offences over the past decades, are undergoing a one-month public consultation. The government aims to introduce the new legislation by July next year.

Here is what you need to know about the proposed reforms.

1. Why is the government reforming the laws?

The commission formed a subcommittee in 2006 to conduct a comprehensive review of sexual offences. Between 2012 and 2020, it launched four consultation papers covering non-consensual sexual offences and rape, offences involving children and persons with mental impairment, as well as sentencing and related matters.

The current proposals are based on 72 final recommendations set out in the commission’s reports published in 2019 and 2022.

2. What is the scope of the overhaul?

The government has proposed 37 new offences across four major areas, including 18 relating to non-consensual sexual acts involving persons with mental impairment and eight aimed at protecting minors under 16. The rest cover other non-consensual and miscellaneous offences.

The new framework will replace 23 outdated offences.

Penalties for some offences will be significantly increased.

For example, the maximum jail term for sexual penetration with persons with severe mental impairment will be increased from 10 years to life imprisonment.

Sexual assault against minors under 16 and persons with mental impairment will carry a maximum penalty of 14 years’ imprisonment, up from the current two years.

The reforms also step up protection for victims of sexual offences, such as empowering the court to remove individuals convicted of sexual offences involving children and persons with mental impairments from the victim’s guardianship.

3. Why introduce gender neutrality?

The proposed reforms will render offences such as rape gender-neutral, ensuring equal protection regardless of gender.

Under existing law, only men can be convicted as principal offenders in rape cases, and only women can be victims.

The new laws also standardise the age of consent at 16, eliminating inconsistencies. For example, while the current age of consent for buggery by males is 16, a man is not allowed to commit buggery with a girl under 21. The change will eliminate legal challenges and distinctions based on sexual orientation.

4. What new offences protect vulnerable groups?

A range of new crimes is proposed to strengthen protection for children and persons with severe mental impairment. These include causing them to witness sexual acts, watch sexual imagery, or send sexual images, with proposed penalties of five to 10 years in jail.

Sexual grooming of a child – in which an offender communicates, often through digital means, with the intent to sexually abuse them – will carry a maximum sentence of 10 years.

Other offences cover arranging or facilitating the commission of a child sexual offence, and inciting minors to engage in sexual acts.

5. How will the offence of rape change?

The definition of rape will be extended to cover non-consensual penile or non-penile penetration of the vagina, anus or urethra, as well as non-consensual penile penetration of the mouth, to broaden protection and adhere to gender neutrality.

The government has also decided to retain the term “rape” despite the commission’s suggestion to replace it with “sexual penetration without consent”. It cited the term’s established public understanding and its connotation of severity, warning that abandoning it could create a misleading impression that the offence was less serious.

6. What is the new statutory definition for consent for sexual acts?

The proposed law will specify a list of circumstances in which consent is considered absent for sexual acts. These include situations where there is no verbal or physical consent; submission due to abuse of trust or authority, extortion, coercion, blackmail, or violence; the victim being asleep, unconscious, or unlawfully detained; and where sexually protective measures are intentionally not used or are removed.

The reforms also recognise the withdrawal of consent, protecting victims in scenarios where consent is withdrawn before or during the sexual act. It will also spell out that consent to a particular sexual act does not imply consent to other acts.

7. What are the new sexual assault offences?

The overhaul introduces two new offences to replace the existing offence of indecent assault, which lacks clearly defined elements.

“Sexual assault involving touching” targets non-consensual touching of the victim, emission of bodily fluids such as semen, urine and saliva, or spilling liquid onto the victim.

“Sexual assault without touching” covers acts that cause the victim to fear violence or experience humiliation, alarm or distress.

Both offences carry a maximum jail term of 10 years, and penalties can be increased to 14 years’ imprisonment if the victim is under 16 or has a mental impairment.

8. What other new offences are proposed?

The reforms also include criminalisation of sexual acts with a corpse, with offenders facing up to 10 years in prison. The government describes such conduct as an affront to the dignity of the deceased.

The definition of incest will be expanded to cover a wider range of familial relationships to better protect children, including full-blood or half blood uncles, aunts, nephews and nieces; lineal ancestors such as great-grandparents and great-grandchildren; and adoptive parents and step-parents.
