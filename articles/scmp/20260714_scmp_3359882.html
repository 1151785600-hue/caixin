Why China is seriously cultivating AI entertainment

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 850 words | 2026-07-14

China’s artificial intelligence ambitions tend to be depicted in the news as predominantly related to defence, large language models and semiconductors. But one emerging use case for AI revolves around a market projected to grow to US$441 billion by 2035: China’s entertainment market.

Just last week, Chinese regulators announced policies encouraging cinemas to reinvent themselves with AI-powered experiences. Simultaneously, Chinese content creators have embraced AI to hasten content ideation, creation and development. Together, these developments point to a strong projection of growth for AI in Chinese entertainment. Chinese policymakers appear to be positioning AI and its entertainment nexus as the foundation for a new cultural economy.

China Film Administration (CFA) and the State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR) have jointly issued guidelines encouraging cinemas to diversify beyond film screenings, specifically supporting the introduction of AI agents to create hubs for “trendy cultural consumption”. This policy aims to move the industry from a traditional ticket-sales model to an all-inclusive, AI-driven consumer experience encompassing retail, gaming, karaoke and more.

The move reflects a desire to bring Chinese consumers back to the box office, particularly as cinema revenues continue to slump amid a rise in the consumption of online, smartphone-based content.

In a nod to these consumer preferences, the proposed AI-enabled multipurpose venues suggest that both the CFA and SAMR are seeking to enable more personalised recommendations, facilitate multifaceted commerce and generally create immersive entertainment experiences that tie to the cinematic ecosystem.

Beyond this, the guidance highlights China’s broader economic strategy of promoting growth through domestic consumption. From this point of view, the entertainment industry is set to be another sector where AI can increase engagement, spending and efficiency all at once.

The Chinese market for AI short dramas and AI animation grew explosively in the first three months of this year, driven by AI tools that resulted in nearly 130 billion views on short-video platform Douyin; dramas featuring AI actors alone generated close to 75 billion views.

This rapid growth is accelerating the transformation of China’s short-form entertainment sector by shifting production towards AI-generated content, attracting game companies, broadcasters and traditional media, while enabling smaller creators and one-person studios to compete alongside established entertainment companies.

AI is lowering the production barriers, leading to increased content production at significantly lower costs. This democratisation of content production in China mirrors earlier waves of digital disruption, with some referring to this as the “no-collar” economy.

With China displaying an ability to quickly scale up digital consumer products, AI-generated entertainment could become another area where domestic companies establish both commercial and technological leadership.

Rapid growth has also facilitated rapid regulatory attention, with Chinese regulators jumping on to facilitate legal frameworks designed to support AI and entertainment synergies.

On April 3, a draft regulation by China’s Cyberspace Administration proposed that entities must obtain a person’s consent before using that person’s personal information to create an avatar, including for modelling or image generation. If the person later withdraws consent, providers would be required to delete the relevant personal information and, unless otherwise agreed, deactivate the digital avatar. This follows a requirement last year that AI-generated content be clearly labelled.

In a recent controversy, AI-generated drama Peach Blossom Hairpin (桃花簪) was taken down from Hongguo, a microdrama app owned by TikTok’s parent company ByteDance, after complaints from users that the AI actors had been generated from their likeness without permission. Incidents such as this highlight how AI content generation can outpace legal protection, especially as increasingly realistic digital humans become increasingly valuable on a commercial scale.

Meanwhile, the government has taken its own steps into the AI-generated entertainment space, with state-backed CGTN releasing in 2024 its “A Fractured America” series detailing the socio-economic conflicts of Americans.

Rather than treating AI entertainment as just another emerging industry, Beijing is positioning itself as both the architect of the regulatory framework that will govern its AI development while promoting and developing its own AI-generated content.

All in all, these developments suggest that China is pursuing a strategy that aims to integrate AI into all facets of the country’s entertainment value chain while still introducing regulatory guardrails to address issues of consent, transparency and digital identity. As such, this new synergy will depend on consumer adoption and how effectively regulators can balance innovation with oversight.

With this in mind, it becomes clear how AI is increasingly being woven into the everyday experience for China’s population. As countries compete to define the future of AI, China’s entertainment industry may prove to be one of the most visible demonstrations of how AI can reshape not only economies, but also the way people consume culture itself.
