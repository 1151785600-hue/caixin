Indonesia restarts South China Sea gas project Beijing opposed

SCMP | FREE | 1308 words | 2026-07-22

Indonesia’s long-stalled gas project in the North Natuna Sea is stirring back to life, with its new operator confirming that activities have resumed at a strategically sensitive field after years of commercial uncertainty and pressure from China.

Prime Group, the Indonesian company that took over operatorship from Britain’s Harbour Energy, told This Week in Asia that “activities associated with the Tuna PSC have resumed in accordance with applicable regulatory requirements”. The PSC – production-sharing contract – is the agreement governing development of the gas field, named Tuna after the licensed offshore area in which the project sits.

The Tuna field lies within Indonesia’s exclusive economic zone in waters where Beijing has previously objected to Jakarta’s drilling activity. Its revival also depends on a Russian state-owned partner, at a time when sanctions and compliance concerns have complicated energy investment.

Analysts said the revived project would test whether President Prabowo Subianto’s government could advance Indonesia’s resource rights without triggering a public sovereignty contest with China.

Jakarta is expected to try keeping the project low profile, but Russia’s involvement, commercial doubts and uncertainty over Prabowo’s China policy could prove as consequential as any response from Beijing.

Job notices posted by Prime Group via its subsidiaries on oil and gas recruitment sites offer a clue to the project’s next steps. They show the company has been recruiting for several Tuna-related roles linked to early engineering work for offshore production facilities, with that work planned for September. The notices do not indicate whether any work has resumed at sea.

Indonesia’s energy ministry said in June that Russian state-owned oil company Zarubezhneft, which holds the remaining 50 per cent through ZN Asia, was ready to continue the delayed project with government support.

This Week in Asia has contacted the energy ministry for comment.

Indonesia approved a US$3.07 billion development plan for the Tuna field in late 2022, with regulators then projecting peak output of 115 million standard cubic feet (3.26 million cubic metres) of gas per day in 2027.

That timetable has since been overtaken by delays and the change of operator, but the Tuna field retains its potential as Jakarta pushes for greater energy self-sufficiency and tries to reverse a long decline in domestic oil and gas production.

Aristyo Rizka Darmawan, an international law lecturer at the University of Indonesia specialising in the law of the sea and Indonesia’s maritime disputes, said the project mattered first for practical reasons. “Energy self-reliance is very important,” he said.

But the Tuna field also carries political weight far beyond its projected output. The field lies close to Indonesia’s maritime boundary with Vietnam, in an area where Jakarta says it has sovereign rights over seabed resources under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (Unclos).

China’s expansive South China Sea claim, represented on its maps by the nine-dash line, cuts into Indonesia’s exclusive economic zone around the Natuna Islands.

A 2016 international arbitral tribunal found no legal basis for China’s claim to historic rights within the nine-dash line beyond what Unclos permits. Beijing rejects the ruling and says its sovereignty and maritime rights in the South China Sea are grounded in history and law.

Indonesia has already felt the backlash from pursuing energy exploration in contested waters. During a months-long stand-off in 2021, Chinese diplomats demanded Jakarta halt drilling of two appraisal wells at Tuna, claiming the activity took place in Chinese territory. Indonesia refused, maintaining that exploration there was its sovereign right, while Chinese and Indonesian ships shadowed one another around the drilling rig.

In October 2024, days after Prabowo took office, Indonesia said it had repeatedly driven away a Chinese coastguard vessel that disrupted a seismic survey for state-owned energy company Pertamina elsewhere in the North Natuna Sea. China said the vessel had been operating in waters under its jurisdiction.

‘Malaysia’s blueprint’

Aristyo said the long-standing friction over Natuna limited how far Jakarta could go in trusting Beijing on security matters, despite their strong economic ties.

“We can’t really have a full-trust security relationship with Beijing when we still have the North Natuna issue,” he said.

Still, he expected Indonesia to press ahead with the gas project even if China objected, while trying to avoid elevating the issue publicly. “They want to keep it silent,” he said.

Such a low profile approach will be familiar to other Southeast Asian nations.

Malaysia and Vietnam had continued offshore energy activity in waters also claimed by China without letting the dispute badly strain ties, said Collin Koh, a senior fellow at Singapore’s S. Rajaratnam School of International Studies.

“We can expect Beijing to register its protest, as it routinely does, usually through the diplomatic backchannels,” Koh said.

Chinese maritime forces might also maintain a presence to monitor activities and signal Beijing’s position without necessarily attempting to halt the work, he added.

Indonesia appears to be following “Malaysia’s blueprint” of avoiding a politically explosive fight over sovereignty while concentrating on areas where cooperation with China remained possible, according to Yohanes Sulaiman, an associate professor of international relations at General Achmad Yani University who studies Indonesian foreign policy and security issues.

But Prabowo’s own diplomacy has complicated that balancing act.

A joint statement issued during his visit to Beijing in November 2024 referenced cooperation and joint development in “areas of overlapping claims” – wording China treated as tacit recognition of its position.

Jakarta’s foreign ministry later insisted otherwise, maintaining that it did not recognise China’s nine-dash line and that the agreement did not affect Indonesia’s sovereignty, sovereign rights or jurisdiction in the North Natuna Sea.

“They know that the ‘overlapping claims’ [wording] was a blunder and thus they pretended it didn’t happen,” Yohanes said.

Resumed activity at the Tuna field gives Jakarta a chance to show that it still regards North Natuna energy development as a matter of Indonesian resource rights, despite the 2024 joint statement.

Beijing could invoke the “overlapping claims” language if it wished to press its position, but Koh said the question was “whether it’s politically wise to do that”.

China would have to weigh whether opposing the project more aggressively was “worth imperilling the generally cordial and lucrative relations with Indonesia”, he said.

The Russia factor

Zarubezhneft’s continued involvement adds another layer of uncertainty.

The Russian company acquired its Tuna interest in 2020, but became an increasingly awkward partner for Harbour Energy after the 2022 invasion of Ukraine triggered an expansion of Western sanctions.

Indonesia’s regulator said in 2023 that Zarubezhneft was preparing to sell its stake, but ultimately it was Harbour that left, completing a US$215 million sale of its stake and operatorship to Indonesia’s Prime Group on May 26.

Koh said Russia’s presence might actually work in Indonesia’s favour, as the deepening alignment between Moscow and Beijing over what both have called US-led “global hegemony” likely outweighed any incentive China could have to disrupt “a single offshore energy project”.

Yohanes was more sceptical of any grand strategic reading, however, describing Indonesia’s approach as more opportunistic.

“It just looks for countries willing to invest,” he said, adding that Prabowo on the whole appeared to be making “ad hoc decisions on specific issues” rather than deliberately playing Washington, Moscow and Beijing off against each other.

Aristyo agreed, contrasting the current “incoherence” with the clearer line adopted by Prabowo’s predecessor Joko Widodo, who pursued economic cooperation with Beijing while refusing to negotiate on sovereignty.

The real test will come if and when the early engineering and paperwork surrounding the Tuna field turns into visible activity at sea, according to Koh, who questioned whether Beijing “wants to open up new ‘trouble fronts’ in the South China Sea beyond its existing preoccupation” with the Philippines.
