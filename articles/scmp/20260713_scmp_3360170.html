What does China’s J-16 fighter jet in ‘beast mode’ mean for PLA Air Force patrols?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 692 words | 2026-07-13

A newly emerged image of a PLA J-16 multirole fighter jet in a rare, heavy air‑superiority “extreme loadout” configuration points to an extension of long-range tactical capabilities, according to a Chinese military magazine.

In an analysis published this month, Ordnance Science and Technology said the photo of a J‑16 carrying multiple long‑range missiles circulating in May showed that China’s air force had developed more offensive tactical capabilities.

“This J-16 sortie used 10 external-weapon hardpoints, already approaching the extreme-load ‘beast mode’, forming a sharp contrast with the restrained loadout methods shown previously,” the article said.

“Beast mode” refers to a fighter jet’s maximum-payload configuration, aimed at maximising its single-aircraft ordnance load and sustained combat capability.

Chinese state media have used the term to describe other fighter jets operating with a full weapons load.

The photo was originally issued by a Chinese aviation oversight body and then quickly spread through international open-source intelligence networks, according to Malaysia-based digital media platform Defence Security Asia.

The Ordnance article said the photo showed a J-16 carrying eight PL-15 medium-range beyond-visual-range missiles and two PL-10 short-range dogfighting missiles.

It added that previous publicly displayed J-16 loadouts had been relatively restrained, usually consisting of two medium-range missiles and two dogfighting missiles, with a few instances involving four medium-range missiles and two dogfighting missiles.

The J-16 is a 4.5-generation multirole fighter derived from and improving upon Russian Sukhoi designs. It entered operational service with the People’s Liberation Army in 2015, has 12 weapon hardpoints, and can carry up to 12 tonnes of munitions.

“The J-16 in this photo did not carry any ground-attack or anti-ship missiles, nor was it fitted with external fuel tanks,” the article said. “It was entirely for air combat.”

The magazine is popular among military enthusiasts and is supervised by the Shaanxi Provincial Association for Science and Technology.

It mainly reports on popular science content and the latest Chinese military developments and defence dynamics, and offers analyses of military developments in other countries and regions.

The heavy combat configuration mirrors US and Russian fighter jets, which have a history of frequently using beast-mode operations.

“Some loadout configurations of US-made fighter jets need to be adapted to their operational requirements for offensive air superiority,” the article said.

It noted that the US military’s standard combat air patrol tactics required fighter formations to remain in mission airspace for extended periods, actively patrolling and suppressing threats.

This demands fighter jets capable of multiple interception cycles and repeated air combat engagements, relying on large weapons payloads to sustain long‑duration operations.

The article said the beast-mode appearance on Chinese fighter jets indicated that the PLA Air Force had begun using combat air patrol tactics.

“Relying on the J-16’s long range and large weapons payload, fighter formations could remain stationed for long periods over far-sea airspace to maintain air superiority,” it added.

Fu Qianshao, a military analyst and former PLA Air Force officer, said: “The fighter itself has the ability to carry large weapons, but the new images of their loadouts indicate that China is conducting exercises with even more munitions under specific mission requirements, improving its tactical capabilities.”

“But maximum loadout has a significant impact on aircraft deployment, increasing drag considerably,” Fu said. “Maximum loadout is generally used to gain air superiority and intercept multiple targets within the airspace.”

According to the Asian Defence Journal, this configuration “forms a specific mode of operation, almost entirely optimised to maximise missile density and long-range air-to-air kill capability”.

China’s other fighter jets have also made a modest number of beast-mode appearances in recent years.

In 2024, the J-10CE – the export version of China’s 4.5-generation medium fighter J-10C – appeared in beast mode at the China International Aviation and Aerospace Exhibition, also known as the Zhuhai Air Show, according to state broadcaster CCTV.

China’s primary stealth fighter, the J-20, appeared in 2025 in a configuration carrying eight PL-15 missiles externally out of a possible total of 14 missiles, according to US-based military publication The War Zone.
