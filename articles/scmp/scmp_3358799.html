Hong Kong proposes sweeping overhaul of sex crime laws

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 731 words | 2026-07-08

Hong Kong authorities have proposed a major overhaul of outdated sexual offences laws, including criminalising same-sex rape, introducing new offences and establishing a uniform age of consent at 16 while removing gender-specific provisions and increasing penalties.

In a 59-page consultation paper submitted to the Legislative Council on Monday, the Security Bureau proposed a series of measures to strengthen protection for minors and mentally incapacitated people from sexual exploitation and abuse.

The government said the review was necessary to modernise sexual offence laws, two decades after the Law Reform Commission (LRC) set up a subcommittee in 2006 to examine the issue.

Eight proposed new crimes involving minors under 16 would target non-consensual sexual offences, abuse of vulnerable groups such as children, and offences of a preparatory nature aimed at early prevention of sexual exploitation.

In the document, the bureau proposed creating a new offence of having sex with a child under 16, regardless of gender, with a maximum sentence of life imprisonment.

Under existing law, sexual intercourse with a girl under 13 carries a maximum sentence of life imprisonment but if the victim is under 16, the maximum jail term is five years.

The bureau said some existing offences, including intercourse or buggery with girls under certain ages and gross indecency by a man towards children, were narrow because they specified the sex of the offender or victim.

Authorities said the uniform age of consent at 16 was well-established in society and should remain applicable irrespective of gender and sexual orientation to reflect the principles of “gender neutrality” and avoid “distinctions based on sexual orientation”.

“Sexual penetration of a child under 16 is socially unacceptable and warrants heavier punishment,” the bureau said.

“To reflect the severity of the offences, we propose that the maximum penalty for the above proposed offences be life imprisonment to enhance the deterrent effect.”

The bureau proposed two new offences covering sexual assault of a child under 16, with or without touching, punishable by a jail term of up to 14 years.

Sexual grooming of a child – in which an offender communicates with a minor, often through digital means, with the intention to meet and sexually abuse them – would be punishable with a maximum sentence of 10 years under a proposed new law.

Causing a minor to witness sexual acts, watch sexual imagery, or make them send sexual images could be punishable by five to 10 years in jail. Those facilitating such acts could also be punished similarly to the principal offender to reflect the severity of the crime.

The bureau also proposed a set of laws to protect mentally incapacitated people, with laws targeting abuse by carers or those in a position of authority.

Offences, such as incest and rape, will also be made gender neutral under the proposed reforms.

“Based on our proposal, the scope of rape will be expanded to non-consensual penile or non-penile penetration of the vagina, anus or urethra, as well as non-consensual penile penetration of the mouth,” the bureau said.

“Apart from women, men can also be victims, and a woman can be found guilty as a principal offender of rape.”

The offence of incest, currently separated by gender, will be combined into one, without specifying the age of the offender.

The scope will also be extended to cover a wider range of family relationships as well as types of sexual acts.

The bureau also proposed devising a statutory definition of consent where the person consenting must have the capacity to consent and do so freely and voluntarily. The consent to one act should not imply consent to another, and it can be withdrawn at any time.

Other proposed new laws also criminalise indecent exposure and sexual activity involving animals and dead bodies.

Indecent assault, an offence currently used for “a wide spectrum of criminal conduct of different degrees of gravity”, ranging from molestation to non-penile penetration, will be replaced by two new laws, involving sexual assault with or without touching.

This includes touching the victim, emitting bodily fluids or spilling liquid onto the victim, and causing the victim humiliation, alarm or distress, with or without touching them.

Lawmaker Elizabeth Quat welcomed the proposed amendments, saying existing laws lagged severely behind societal development and had numerous flaws, including gender discrimination, sexual orientation distinctions, narrow coverage and excessively lenient penalties.

Quat said the changes would better protect vulnerable groups, close loopholes and strengthen privacy protection for victims.
