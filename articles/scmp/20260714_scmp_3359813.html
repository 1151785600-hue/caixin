How the New Left won the battle of ideas for 21st-century China

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 788 words | 2026-07-14

We are all used to Western assessments of Deng Xiaoping. You may even be convinced by them, as I was for a long time.

Ezra Vogel, for example, wrote in Deng Xiaoping and the Transformation of China: “It was [Deng] who would finally realise the mission that others had tried for almost two centuries to achieve, of finding a path that would make China rich and powerful.”

And, according to Orville Schell and John Delury in Wealth and Power: China’s Long March to the Twenty-first Century, “Once in power, he did not hesitate to foment a new kind of frenzy – for making money. By the mid-1980s, he had named his counterrevolution gaige kaifang, ‘reform and opening-up’.

“Deng’s strange hybrid reform combined Vladimir Lenin’s recipe for a disciplined and well-organized state and Milton Friedman’s celebration of free market economics.”

The last sentence is especially absurd. Mao Zedong was a great party disciplinarian in the mould of China’s first emperor Qin Shi Huang and his totalitarian school of Legalism. Mao and Deng hardly needed lessons from Lenin.

Once the Gang of Four was eliminated, Deng took over a highly disciplined party. He also inherited much else on the economic and social fronts from Mao, without which gaige kaifang would not have succeeded. That was the story Western neoliberals tried to bury but which China’s New Left, now more like the middle-aged Left, has successfully recovered.

While Friedman has had an outsize influence in the West, he had no comparable impact in China.

Friedman did visit and counsel cadres in Beijing during the 1980s, but it was Eastern European reformist economists, such as Janos Kornai from Hungary, Wlodzimierz Brus from Poland and Ota Šik from the former Czechoslovakia, who had the most practical impact on the ground.

After the frozen prices of the Cultural Revolution, price reform was the most urgent task. For that, economic reformists from Soviet satellite states had far more practical and realistic advice than the dumbed-down free-market ideology of the Friedmanites.

It was fortunate that China never went for the “big bang” shock therapy that collapsed the post-Soviet Russian economy and created the parasitic oligarchs.

Wu Jinglian, one of the most important theoreticians of gaige kaifang, was an early advocate of those reformist ideas. Kornai was especially famous for his work on introducing price discovery processes to reform a command economy.

But Deng has been lionised in the West, while Mao has been demonised. That fits the neoliberal and Friedmanite narratives, as well as the anti-communist discourse of the West.

No one should minimise the horrors Mao inflicted on the nation, but the foundations he laid made reform and opening up possible.

Gaige kaifang also caused the most extreme labour exploitation, economic inequality and official corruption worthy of 19th-century Western industrial capitalism.

It would take the New Left, starting from the 1990s, and then the ascent of President Xi Jinping, to correct course, much to the chagrin of the West, but especially America.

In the process, New Left social scientists and economists recovered a truer continuity from Mao through Deng to Xi. Their revisionism correctly challenges the neoliberal perspective which treats Maoist command economics and Deng-era capitalist reforms as diametrically opposed.

For example, agricultural economist and social historian Wen Tiejun has argued that the Mao-era state-building, establishment of rural collectives and early localised industrialisation provided the socio-economic cushion necessary for China to pursue market reforms without collapsing during the early transitions.

Political scientist Wang Shaoguang and economist Hu Angang have argued that the Mao era built up high state capacities to direct subsequent massive infrastructure building and reform of state-owned enterprises. Rural health progress and mass education, especially for women, have also played a key role.

Wang Hui, a former editor of the influential magazine Dushu, argued that Maoist discipline ensured the party retained strategic and ideological coherence during the opening of the economy, preventing wholesale privatisation and protecting national autonomy. That’s even more obvious in light of the catastrophic economic experience of the Soviet collapse. Mass education and healthcare created a literate workforce for the post-1978 economic boom.

Political scientist Pan Wei has argued that only a strong, centralised state and social cohesion under communist one-party rule could have contained, directed and managed China’s market-oriented reforms.

Ultimately, the New Left was the victim of its own success. By the 2010s, they were proved to have been right all along and Xi became the practical embodiment of their theory as he moved to clean house, reverse capitalist excesses and restore party legitimacy.

After all, what is Marxism but a philosophy of praxis?
