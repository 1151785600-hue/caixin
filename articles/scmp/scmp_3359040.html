How bitter Persian Gulf rivals can make peace the Asean way

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 931 words | 2026-07-08

“The Emiratis – by far the most hawkish, by far the most pro-Israel country in the [Gulf Cooperation Council] – they’re having conversations with the Iranians that have never happened before,” US Vice-President J.D. Vance told British media outlet UnHerd following the Iran-US negotiations in Switzerland.

The Iranians “are certainly talking differently than they have in the past”, he added in a note of cautious optimism, highlighting how both Washington and its allies in the Persian Gulf are pursuing a new modus vivendi with post-war Iran. Aside from Pakistan, Qatar played a pivotal role in brokering the official end of the Iran-US war. Oman and Qatar are also pushing for a pan-regional agreement on the Strait of Hormuz.

Even Saudi Arabia is reportedly organising a reconciliation summit between Tehran and its neighbours. Given the depth of hostilities and the sheer scale of devastation wrought by the war in Iran, any long-term peace plan will clearly face major hurdles, including stiff opposition by hardliners in multiple governments, especially Israel. Fortunately, however, Southeast Asia provides an encouraging model for the Middle East.

Forged in the thick of the Cold War, the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (Asean) shows that even the most conflict-ridden regions can gradually transform into havens of prosperity and relative stability. The combination of trauma from war, visionary leadership and shared prosperity inspired Asean to engage and even integrate its bitter enemies, most notably post-war Vietnam.

Over time, the regional body even managed to develop balanced relations with rival superpowers, including the US and China, protect its strategic autonomy and create a relatively stable security architecture in Asia. Of course, the situation in the Middle East is fragile, as seen from the recent tit-for-tat military strikes between the Iranians and the Americans. But this is precisely where Asean is instructive for fellow postcolonial regions.

Asean is often dismissed as a toothless “talk shop”. But as British prime minister Winston Churchill once said: “To jaw-jaw is always better than to war-war.” Institutionalised dialogue has been Southeast Asia’s most successful approach to preventing the recurrence of past horrors. With the notable exception of recent low-intensity Thai-Cambodian border clashes, Southeast Asia has been free from major international wars for at least a generation.

Asean was established under extremely tortuous conditions, which echo the current situation in the Middle East. Indonesia, Malaysia, Singapore, the Philippines and Thailand founded the grouping to prevent the spread of communism amid the Indochina wars. Putting a check on Indonesian expansionism in the Konfrontasi era and preventing a full-blown Philippine-Malaysia war over oil-rich Sabah were also among the grouping’s early priorities.

In the 1990s, Asean even expanded its ranks to include communist Vietnam along with Cambodia and Laos. This underscored the pull of regional integration in one of the most war-torn corners of the world. Soon after, Asean also engaged major regional powers, including China. Once a communist threat supporting insurgencies in US-aligned Asean countries and later a direct enemy to Vietnam in the third Indochina war, Beijing rapidly transformed into a top trading partner and a major source of investment for Southeast Asian nations.

Asean’s successful march was undergirded by three related factors. First, Southeast Asian states realised that reliance on superpowers was detrimental to their national interests. While the Soviet Union reduced support for Hanoi in the 1980s, US allies such as Thailand were heavily affected by the Indochina wars.

Moreover, the decades-long conflicts killed millions and traumatised a generation of Southeast Asian leaders, who were determined to prevent renewed conflicts. Fortuitous economic forces were also at play. With the region straddling major global trading routes and bordering economic powerhouses of Asia, leaders from Hanoi to Jakarta took a page from the success of Singapore, which went from an impoverished nation to a global economic dynamo.

Crucially, providing economic prosperity became a major source of legitimacy for Southeast Asian countries, especially post-war Vietnam, which embraced diplomatic caution and trade-oriented foreign policy after the Cold War.

The elements that made Asean possible and successful exist in the Persian Gulf. For example, Iran’s current leadership seems unified in pursuing an “economy first” agenda to reconstruct the heavily devastated nation and also prevent protests based on material grievances.

Iran’s late security chief Ali Larijani emphasised the importance of pursuing “the path of development” and ensuring “prosperity and calm” for Iranian people before the Israeli government assassinated him. The current powerful parliamentary speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf has underscored the need to “take over the trench from our young people manning the launchers” and focus on the economy.

This is precisely why pursuing peace matters to Tehran, as well as securing sanctions relief and collecting Strait of Hormuz transit fees, non-negotiable elements in any long-term settlement.

Meanwhile, Arab states in the Persian Gulf realise the dangers of reliance on external superpowers. They ended up victims of a war unilaterally initiated by the US despite their opposition. Saudi Arabia to Qatar are apparently reassessing the wisdom of relying on expensive American weapons and hosting American bases, which only attracted Iranian retaliation.

Intent on pursuing their vision of economic transformation, wealthy Persian Gulf states have the incentive and resources to integrate post-war Iran into a more inclusive and stable order no longer hostage to American whims, one open to constructive engagement with all major powers, including China. This is precisely where the Asean provides an encouraging lesson in regional integration against all odds.
