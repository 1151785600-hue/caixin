How Beijing proposes to solve South China Sea disputes 10 years after Hague ruling

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 728 words | 2026-07-15

A decade after the Hague’s South China Sea ruling, Beijing should continue engaging in good-faith diplomacy and explore international mediation, analysts say, arguing that the unenforced decision had failed to curb regional maritime tensions.

The assessments came after a weekend of fresh accusations over the 2016 decision by an international tribunal that most of China’s claims to historical and economic rights in the South China Sea were invalid, a ruling that Beijing firmly rejected.

On Sunday, the ruling’s 10th anniversary, a coalition of 14 countries led by the United States and the Philippines called the decision “legally binding and definitive”, and criticised Beijing for refusing to recognise or implement it.

Beijing responded on the same day by describing US-led military deployments as the “primary threat” to regional peace in the South China Sea.

It dismissed the Hague ruling as “null and void”, calling it a politically motivated process that lacked state consent. It has also refused to recognise the tribunal’s authority to hear the case and said it would not implement its findings.

Beijing claims most of the islands and rocks in the disputed waters and rights over their adjacent waters. This has fuelled protracted territorial disputes with rival claimants, with friction escalating most intensely with Manila in recent years.

The risks from those disputes were the centre of attention at a South China Sea security forum on Monday co-hosted by the Asian Academy of International Law, the National Institute for South China Sea Studies (NISCSS) and the Huayang Centre for Maritime Cooperation and Ocean Governance.

Chen Xiangmiao, a research fellow at the Chinese government-backed NISCSS, told a panel that a commitment to the principle of good faith was essential for China’s future approach to the disputed waters.

“The Philippines’ continuous attacks on China are steadily escalating bilateral tensions and fuelling nationalist sentiments on both sides,” Chen said.

“This friction will constrain and complicate decision-making for both governments. Therefore, the principle of good faith is essential. Neither side should weaponise international law.”

Chen said the 2016 ruling had undermined the practical application of international law in the South China Sea and countries should repair the damage done to the maritime order.

Rather than remaining fixated on the arbitral ruling, both sides should redirect their efforts towards practical avenues of cooperation, such as marine conservation, fishery management and renewable energy, he added.

The maritime tensions between Beijing and Manila have continued unabated this year.

In January, the US and the Philippines conducted joint naval and air patrols near the disputed Scarborough Shoal. Days later, Beijing responded with combat air and naval patrols.

Meanwhile, as the 2026 chair of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (Asean), Manila has pushed for finalising a code of conduct in the disputed waters within this year.

At the Shangri-La Dialogue in May, Philippine defence chief Gilberto Teodoro Jnr singled out China as the biggest obstacle to the code, triggering sanctions from Beijing that barred him and his family from entering the country.

Bao Yinan, an associate research fellow at the Huayang Centre in China’s southern Hainan province, proposed the International Organisation for Mediation (IOMed) as a viable alternative to break the diplomatic deadlock.

Established last year and headquartered in Hong Kong, the China-led IOMed is the world’s first intergovernmental body focused on mediation-based dispute solutions.

The IOMed has 13 contracting states and 44 signatories, including five Asean countries such as Indonesia and Myanmar.

Beijing has criticised some existing international mechanisms that have exerted pressure on China over disputes such as competing territorial claims in the South China Sea.

In May, the mediation body settled a maritime dispute involving parties from China and Singapore.

Mediation and conciliation offered a viable middle ground between China’s preference for negotiation and the Philippines’ push for arbitration and adjudication, Bao said at Monday’s gathering.

The mechanism itself was technically viable for China and rival claimants to implement in territorial and sovereignty disputes, given that Beijing had yet to file any reservations excluding its use, he added.

But the willingness to do so largely “depends on political considerations rather than legal aspects”, Bao said.

“Should the parties to the South China Sea disputes engage in good-faith interactions … making proactive use of the IOMed by taking this ‘important first step’ would offer significant benefits with virtually no corresponding drawbacks.”
