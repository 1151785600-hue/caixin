How a remark about Najib’s pardon just blew Malaysia’s Johor state election wide open

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 839 words | 2026-07-10

Malaysia’s Barisan Nasional (BN), the dominant political coalition in Johor, might have expected to cruise to an easy victory come Saturday when the southern state goes to the polls – until a single remark from the son of jailed former prime minister Najib Razak suddenly gave its rivals political ammunition.

On July 3, Nazifuddin Najib, who heads BN in Langkawi, suggested that a strong showing by the coalition would validate calls for his father’s pardon.

“If we win big, it will send a signal that [Johor voters] still cherish Bossku and hope that Bossku can be with the people,” he told reporters on July 2, using the nickname Najib’s supporters gave him, meaning “my boss”.

Najib was convicted of seven charges of criminal breach of trust, corruption and money laundering linked to SRC International, a former 1MDB subsidiary, in 2020. He was sentenced to 12 years in prison and a 210 million ringgit (US$51.5 million) fine, and began serving it from August 23, 2022, after exhausting all appeals.

In 2024, the Pardons Board cut that sentence to six years and the fine to 50 million ringgit, sparking public backlash. This means that Najib could be released by August 2028.

The Malaysian Bar is challenging the legality of the partial pardon, arguing the board’s decision-making process was flawed. The case will be heard by the Court of Appeal on July 31.

Najib also faces a 15-year sentence for abuse of power and money laundering tied to the broader 1MDB scandal, which will take effect only after his current term ends. He is appealing against the ruling.

Stirring emotions

“Nazifuddin’s remarks seem to have energised the PH [Pakatan Harapan] campaign at a time when they were struggling to find a message to drive voter turnout,” said Ibrahim Suffian, co-founder of the Merdeka Centre for Opinion Research.

PH’s fortunes in Johor hinge on voter turnout, especially those working out of the state, whom Ibrahim says have shown low enthusiasm to make the journey home to vote.

Nazifuddin’s remark, however, could now galvanise segments of the electorate to vote, not necessarily in support of PH but as a protest against the idea of rewarding the convicted former prime minister.

“PH’s messaging was based on selling their manifesto, which was a typical bland set of promises,” Ibrahim said.

A Najib pardon could be the new emotive angle it needed. After years of enduring criticism over stalled reforms, this issue was a “red line” PH could defend, he said.

BN’s leadership moved fast to contain the fallout, with coalition chairman Ahmad Zahid Hamidi, deputy Mohamad Hasan and other senior political leaders publicly distancing the party from Nazifuddin’s statement.

Caretaker chief minister Onn Hafiz Ghazi also waded in, saying the election had nothing to do with the former prime minister but was about seeking the people’s mandate for a new term.

Oh Ei Sun, Najib’s former political secretary and now a senior fellow at the Singapore Institute of International Affairs, sees less calculation and more genuine conviction behind Nazifuddin’s statement.

“The main thrust of his comment was due more to his – and perhaps a significant cohort of elites and voters – [desire] to see Najib pardoned and even return to politics soonest,” Oh said.

The reaction of senior party leaders was expected, he said, as it was no secret that there were power struggles and differing opinions on the issue of Najib’s pardon within the United Malays National Organisation, BN’s leading party.

Both analysts differed, however, on whether the 1MDB-era anger has faded or simply gone quiet.

Oh said non-Malay animosity towards Najib “has largely vaporised” with a growing number of voters turning nostalgic about the “good old days” under his leadership. Ibrahim, however, noted that the anger “remains strong among urban voters”.

A 2025 survey by civil society organisation Projek Stability and Accountability for Malaysia showed that 57.8 per cent of the 1,210 polled opposed a royal pardon for Najib over the 1MDB scandal.

The survey was split along ethnic and political lines. Those in favour of letting Najib out were mainly ethnic Indians (62.2 per cent), Muslim bumiputras (50.5 per cent) and Malays (43 per cent). An overwhelming 95 per cent of Chinese surveyed opposed the bid, followed by 51 per cent of non-Muslim bumiputras.

Both analysts agree, though, that this will not be the last time the spectre of Najib’s pardon is raised.

“Animosity towards Najib’s case remains strong among urban voters, [but] rural voters continue to harbour some nostalgic sentiment towards Najib,” Ibrahim said.

Oh noted that the issue was “likely to pop up from time to time” right through to the next general election.

Even politicians outside BN have staked positions on the issue pre-emptively. Housing and Local Government Minister Nga Kor Ming, from the rival Democratic Action Party (DAP), previously vowed to resign if Najib were pardoned – a sign the topic already contributes to the growing rift between BN and DAP, nominal partners in Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s unity government, but rivals in Saturday’s state poll.
