Chinese researchers find a peephole to any smartphone in its leaked radio signal

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 530 words | 2026-07-08

Even if a smartphone is offline, in flight mode, encrypted or locked, Chinese authorities could still be able to determine which apps are being used by analysing faint electromagnetic signals emitted as the device operates.

Researchers at the People’s Public Security University of China have developed a method that identifies smartphone applications and user actions through low-frequency electromagnetic radiation.

In their findings published in the peer-reviewed journal Radioengineering on May 22, the researchers described it as a non-contact forensic technique that worked without accessing a phone’s operating system or stored data.

“This technical approach can provide objective technical corroboration for evidence reinforcement in digital forensics and non-contact investigations,” the authors wrote.

According to the paper, the researchers tested the system on three smartphones: an iPhone 15 Pro, Xiaomi 15 Pro and Oppo Reno 13.

The model achieved up to 99.07 per cent accuracy in identifying mobile applications, including Douyin, WeChat video calls, Baidu Maps, SMS messaging, browsers, cameras and cloud storage.

It tallied 95.61 per cent accuracy in distinguishing user actions such as pause, normal playback and playing video or audio at twice its normal speed on YouTube, Bilibili and Tencent Video.

“The method effectively circumvents challenges posed by device-locking mechanisms and anti-forensics techniques”, allowing investigators to obtain behavioural evidence, they added.

The South China Morning Post has contacted Apple, which makes the iPhone, as well as Xiaomi and Oppo, for comment.

Unlike conventional digital forensic techniques that typically require investigators to unlock a device, extract stored data, analyse application code or intercept network traffic, the new approach relies entirely on physical side-channel signals leaking from smartphone hardware.

The researchers said different applications activated different combinations of hardware components, including processors, graphics chips, GPS receivers, Wi-fi modules, storage controllers and wireless communication circuits.

Those varying hardware workloads alter power consumption patterns, generating characteristic low-frequency electromagnetic emissions that can be distinguished by artificial intelligence.

Video streaming, for example, places heavier demands on decoding hardware and graphics processors, while navigation apps periodically activate GPS and storage components, producing distinct electromagnetic signatures.

To capture those signals, the team placed a custom-built induction coil connected to a digital monitoring receiver against the back of a smartphone, collecting electromagnetic emissions between 150kHz and 650kHz.

The recorded signals were converted into time-frequency spectrograms before being analysed using a spectrogram transformer deep-learning model.

“[This] provides solid technical support for unobtrusive screening in specialised scenarios and for characterising the behaviour of individuals involved in investigations,” the authors said.

While the system claims high accuracy, the results were achieved under controlled laboratory conditions using a high-precision digital monitoring receiver placed close to the smartphone.

The paper did not show how the system would perform in crowded electromagnetic environments or over longer distances.

The researchers outlined several future research directions, including improving recognition of previously unseen devices and developing device-specific electromagnetic fingerprints.

“Future research will explore investigative and forensic methods that offer stronger concealment while requiring less specialised equipment,” they wrote.

“For example, signal collection could be performed using a smartphone’s built-in magnetometer or by directly using electrodes on wearable devices to sniff leakage currents from electronic equipment.”
