Hong Kong graduates at crossroads as AI takes over entry-level jobs, experts warn

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1095 words | 2026-07-12

AI’s rapid takeover of entry-level tasks is disrupting Hong Kong’s career ladder, leaving fresh graduates with fewer opportunities to gain practical experience, according to experts.

Lam Wai-kong, an employee representative on the Labour Advisory Board, said a growing emphasis on immediate productivity, coupled with a reluctance to invest in graduate training, had also led some employers to bypass local young people in favour of imported workers to fill technical roles.

He warned that this trend could result in a persistent shortage of mid-level local talent.

“One question is whether the ladder for upward mobility will be cut off midway,” Lam said. “The second is the concern about [the breakdown in] the transmission of experience.”

Lam, who is also a lawmaker for the labour sector, added that as the Northern Metropolis developed, incoming mainland Chinese and international technology firms might similarly sideline local graduates unless authorities imposed stricter hiring requirements tied to land allocation and tax incentives.

A third-quarter outlook survey by ManpowerGroup noted that the city’s net employment outlook – defined as the difference between the percentage of employers planning to increase staff and those planning to reduce staff – had plunged to minus 9 per cent, reflecting a sharp decrease of 20 percentage points compared with the previous quarter.

The survey added that Hong Kong’s hiring outlook was the second-worst among the 42 countries and regions surveyed.

Health and social services recorded the weakest outlook at minus 47 per cent, followed by professional scientific and technical services at minus 29 per cent, while the finance and insurance industry was positive at 33 per cent.

A report by the Legislative Council Secretariat in mid-May said that vacancies on the Joint Institution Job Information System – an online platform run by the city’s eight publicly funded universities – had plummeted by 51.5 per cent since 2021, driven by a 76.7 per cent decline in information and communications openings.

Jackie Wong, 24, spent the past year looking for a full-time job in photography or advertising, but had to settle for freelance work to make a living.

The 2025 mass media graduate said employers were reluctant to offer entry-level opportunities.

“How am I supposed to get experience if nobody will give me a chance? It is a vicious cycle,” Wong said, noting that most companies preferred to hire experienced people to save time and money on training.

While Wong believed artificial intelligence (AI) would not fully replace roles in the media industry, as many still required hands-on work, she said some jobs were already disappearing.

“Certain positions such as copywriters and transcribers are getting replaced by AI. This has reduced the number of available openings, forcing more people to compete for those remaining roles,” she said.

Lawmaker Elvin Lee Ka-kui said the structural shift was eroding entry points for young people.

“The deeper concern behind this trend is that if graduates find it harder to gain suitable first-job experience, it will eventually leave enterprises lacking mid-level talent with practical experience,” said Lee, who is also chairman of the Hong Kong Publishing Federation.

Lee added that the government should consider offering long-term wage subsidies or tax concessions to employers willing to convert standard junior roles into training positions, effectively co-funding entry-level positions.

Government data showed that private sector vacancies dropped by 12 per cent year on year to 48,610 in March. Vacancies for clerical support workers plummeted by 21 per cent to 3,910, while openings for professionals plunged by 16 per cent.

Wong Man-shun, unit-in-charge at the Hong Kong Federation of Youth Groups’ youth employment network, said that fresh graduates were squeezed into a hyper-competitive bottleneck, with entry-level postings attracting far more applicants than in previous years.

Data from the Legco Secretariat showed that first-time jobseekers accounted for 55.2 per cent of all unemployed young people in 2025, compared with 10.9 per cent of the overall unemployed population, underscoring how inexperienced graduates have borne the brunt of labour market displacement.

Wong added that as commercial firms and major local banks dismantled up to a third of their junior back-office roles, graduates had grown increasingly anxious after prolonged job searches.

“If they remain unemployed for 18 months, they can fall into what we call ‘learned helplessness’,” said Wong, who is also a social worker.

He said that graduates from social sciences disciplines – including anthropology, sociology and social policy – alongside those with business administration degrees, faced the greatest confusion because their disciplines lacked a clear vocational path or were too broad compared with structured professions such as nursing.

Roy Ying, a senior lecturer in marketing at Hang Seng University and co-chairman of advocacy and policy at the Hong Kong Institute of Human Resource Management, pointed out that the Joint University Programmes Admissions System required students as young as 17, often without any workplace exposure, to make life-shaping decisions under parental and peer pressure.

This, he said, could lead to disengagement, with students focusing on obtaining a degree rather than actively building technical skills.

Ying added employers wanted fresh graduates to perform “like 15-year veterans” with maturity, professional intuition and the ability to navigate ambiguity – traits that used to be developed across a decade of on-the-job learning.

Newly demanded skills included those that had historically appeared later in a career, such as strategic decision-making, stakeholder management, leadership and judgment, Ying added.

Ricky Chan Wai-chung, executive deputy chairman of the Federation of Hong Kong Industries, pointed out that hiring tech-savvy young people was a forward-looking cost-saving strategy, as their digital fluency eliminated corporate reliance on expensive external consultants.

Chan, who is also an employer representative on the Labour Advisory Board, said industry-academic integration should begin earlier at the secondary school level, citing private sector pipelines such as the federation’s “Innopreneur Experience Journey” and internship partnerships under City University’s “Global Research Enrichment and Technopreneurship” programme.

The Labour Department said that it had expanded its youth employment and training programme by raising the trainee age limit to 29 while offering employers monthly training subsidies of up to HK$5,000 (US$637), among other measures.

A Legco Secretariat paper noted that the policy change had resulted in a 46.4 per cent surge in applications last year.

But Lam said young graduates might have to shift to blue-collar roles as the fourth industrial revolution or Industry 4.0 – the integration of digital technologies with industries – gathered pace.

These would include jobs as drone pilots for the low-altitude economy or remote crane operators controlling heavy construction machinery from computer rooms using gaming joysticks.
