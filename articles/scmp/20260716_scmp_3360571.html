2 men arrested over 123 ‘crash-for-cash’ traffic accidents in Hong Kong

SCMP | FREE | 369 words | 2026-07-16

Hong Kong police have arrested a 21-year-old student and a jobless 19-year-old on suspicion of deliberately causing more than 120 traffic accidents across the city in “crash-for-cash” scams, racking up more than HK$1 million (US$127,570) in fraudulent compensation.

Chief Inspector Tam Tsz-wai of the New Territories North regional crime unit said officers suspected the pair were linked to 123 staged traffic accidents between May last year and this July.

“We believe the 21-year-old was the mastermind and recruited the 19-year-old. They were both smooth operators of the crime, creating many accidents across different locations in the city,” Tam said.

Officers seized vehicles used in the accidents, Tam added, without disclosing the exact number.

Investigations suggested the pair caused collisions at roundabouts, bends on narrow roads and lane-cutting blind spots across the city, before demanding money from drivers.

“There were 34 days when the two suspects were involved in two or more traffic accidents on the same day,” Tam said.

The chief inspector added that the two men had frequently changed the vehicles in a bid to evade detection.

Surveillance footage from the force’s SmartView programme showed the two men wandering around sites where a crash later occurred, suggesting they were searching for targets, Tam said.

“The two suspects would wander around the crime scene in search of prey, accelerating to collide with cars that were switching lanes,” Tam explained.

Victims included taxi and minibus drivers, the chief inspector said.

Even though some victims suspected the crashes were deliberate, they tended to pay due to a sense of guilt or an unwillingness to complicate matters, he said.

The amount of compensation demanded ranged from HK$4,000 to HK$16,000 per case, Tam added.

Police estimated the two men had collected more than HK$1 million in total.

The men were arrested on Monday morning on suspicion of conspiracy to defraud and dangerous driving.

Acting Chief Inspector Yiu Cheuk-lun of the New Territories North traffic investigation and support division urged drivers to remain vigilant and ensure their dashcams worked properly.

“If you encounter any suspicious activity, please immediately preserve relevant footage and evidence and report it to police as soon as possible for follow-up,” Yiu said.
