Why a Chinese academic claim over Batanes has unsettled the Philippines

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1152 words | 2026-07-14

A university symposium in Guangzhou has placed the Philippines’ northernmost province of Batanes at the centre of a new argument over history, geography and sovereignty, after scholars at the event claimed the islands belonged to Taiwan and, on that basis, fell under overall Chinese sovereignty.

The claim, made at a June 30 symposium hosted by Jinan University in Guangzhou, has been rejected by Philippine historians and officials, who dispute the scholars’ reading of geography, dynastic administration, cultural links and post-war treaties.

Beijing sees Taiwan as part of China, to be reunited by force if necessary. Most countries, including the United States, do not recognise Taiwan as an independent state, but Washington opposes any attempt to take the self-governed island by force and is committed to supplying it with weapons.

Beijing has not formally adopted the scholars’ position on Batanes, but the discussion has fuelled concern in Manila that an apparently academic argument could be used to test or shape a wider narrative around the islands, which sit near one of the most important waterways between the South China Sea and the Pacific.

The Guangzhou symposium was convened in reaction to a May 28 joint statement by the Philippines and Japan that the two would negotiate to delimit their exclusive economic zones and continental shelves, including areas east of Taiwan also claimed by mainland China.

Symposium participants said the Philippines-Japan agreement infringed on China’s sovereignty, and that the Batanes islands belonged to China.

“The islands are not merely closer to Taiwan in distance, but constitute a natural geographical extension of Taiwan,” said Ju Hailong, dean of the School of International Studies at Jinan University.

In a statement issued on Friday rejecting the symposium’s claims, the National Historical Commission of the Philippines challenged that geographical argument, saying “current satellite and oceanographic data by the Philippines and other nations show a continuous shelf extending from northern Luzon through the Babuyan and Batanes Islands, and into parts of the Taiwan archipelago”.

It said “the Philippines claims a greater right over the subject territories from this perspective”.

Ju had claimed that during the Ming and Qing dynasties, the islands were under the jurisdiction of the Taiwan prefecture.

But the commission said “the earliest known extensive documentation of the Batanes Islands by British explorer William Dampier in 1687 noted no trace of Chinese governance over the islands”.

It noted that “since 1783, the Philippine government and its legal predecessors have held continuous and undisputed sovereignty over Batanes”.

The symposium also claimed the Ivatan residents of Batanes could be linguistically and culturally linked with Taiwan’s Tao people, with heritage originating from mainland China.

The commission noted instead that the Ivatan people “lived in protected communities and traded with other maritime trading nations”.

According to the Chinese scholars, post-World War II treaties required Japan to return all territories it had seized from China and, because they regarded Batanes as part of Taiwan, the islands should have been included.

The commission rejected that premise, saying the islands could not be returned because “Japan cannot give to China what clearly belongs to the Philippines”.

In its statement, the commission rejected the Chinese scholars’ assertions, saying they had “no rational basis in substantive research and operate from evident bad faith. They would never stand under scrutiny in academic discourse outside of the People’s Republic of China.”

Philippine foreign affairs department acting spokeswoman Analyn Ratonel said on Friday: “Flights of fancy should not be dignified with a response. Nevertheless, the Philippines’ sovereignty over Batanes is settled and not up for debate.”

She pointed out that when Manila approved a consular district of the Chinese consulate general in the northern city of Laoag, the Chinese request explicitly included Batanes.

Beyond academia?

Some Philippine officials and analysts said the symposium should not be viewed only as an academic event, warning that it could help test or shape public perceptions around Batanes.

Renato de Castro, a distinguished professor in the international studies department of De La Salle University Manila who once taught at Jinan University in 2007, said the symposium was unlikely to have taken place without political space from Beijing, arguing that “this is probably a prelude to a possible offensive in terms of psychological warfare”.

Speaking in a news forum on Saturday, retired rear admiral Roy Vincent Trinidad, spokesman for Philippine operations in the South China Sea, described what was happening as “salami-slicing in the information domain”.

He said it could begin with a simple statement before moving towards a point where “it’s officially adopted by the government”.

Former congressman Robert Ace Barbers, who once offered his province of Surigao del Norte to host US troops, told This Week in Asia that claiming Batanes “is another part of bullying, this is what we call creeping invasion”.

Raymond Powell, director of SeaLight, a maritime transparency project of Stanford University’s Gordian Knot Centre, cautioned during the news forum on Saturday that dismissing such claims would be “playing directly into Beijing’s hand”, as they served a broader purpose.

“The Chinese government needs the ‘possibility’ that it owns Batanes to justify” its actions in waters claimed by the Philippines, Powell said in a social media post on Monday, adding that Beijing “isn’t ready to say it directly” when it came to Batanes, therefore it “left it to academics”.

Philippine Defence Secretary Gilberto Teodoro called the Chinese scholars’ assertions a “quasi-state statement”.

The concern is sharpened by Batanes’ location near Taiwan and the Luzon Strait, a strategic corridor between the South China Sea and the Pacific.

Batanes comprises 10 islands, with the northernmost one, Mavulis, a mere 142km southeast of Taiwan. It is being built up by the Philippine military as part of its Comprehensive Archipelagic Defence Concept, which refocuses the country’s security strategies to include defence of its exclusive economic zone.

Teodoro has described the islands as “the spearhead of the Philippines as far as the northern baseline is concerned”.

In 2024, a Naval Forward Operating Base was constructed on the main island of Batan, while structures were built in Mavulis to house members of the Naval Forces Northern Luzon, a unit dedicated to defending the area.

Military drills have also taken place there in the two recent Balikatan, or shoulder-to-shoulder, exercises with the US and other nations, including Philippine and US marines rappelling from Black Hawk helicopters to the remote Batanes town of Itbayat.

Dr Ricardo Jose, an emeritus professor of history specialising in World War II, told This Week in Asia in 2024 that Batanes was strategically important in any conflict involving Taiwan.

Japanese forces had seized the airfield in Basco, Batanes, “because from Taiwan, Basco is almost halfway between Taiwan and the northern Luzon tip”.

De Castro said in a forum by think tank Stratbase on Friday that “the northern Philippines is key terrain – the control of which will give you a marked advantage over your adversary.”
