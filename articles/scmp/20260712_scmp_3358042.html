Why Hong Kong’s bilingualism is uniquely indispensable in the AI era

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 870 words | 2026-07-12

Last week, while preparing a lecture on the visual culture of the Global South, I caught Google’s Gemini in a double hallucination. Cross-referencing a historical event between English and Chinese data sets, I found the English AI to be authoritative but inventing citations. In Chinese, the fabrications vanished but so did global context, replaced by an insular perspective.

Disturbingly, the system cloaked Chinese content in English citations, creating a deceptive authenticity that made the hallucinations difficult to verify. AI claims to bridge all languages, but gaps remain. Knowing both sides is the only way to see them.

This systemic vulnerability is inherent in how large language models handle cross-lingual alignment. When bridging asymmetric data pools – the vast English web and the structurally distinct Chinese digital ecosystem – these models suffer from epistemic asymmetry. Instead of true synthesis, the system often takes localised, unverified Chinese content and cross-contaminates it with invented English academic sources.

For instance, if a Chinese art blog mistakenly claims a specific local painting inspired Vincent van Gogh’s The Starry Night, the AI doesn’t just translate the claim – it might fabricate a non-existent Oxford University Press citation to back it up in English. This synthetic authority, delivered in flawless academic prose, hides real gaps between the two digital ecosystems, making the fabrication almost impossible to detect.

This separates monolingual and bilingual users. When an AI fabricates citations, a monolingual user is trapped within a single semantic loop, unable to test the boundaries of the model’s bias. In my case, bilingualism broke this loop through cross-examination, manually tracing the AI’s citations back to their linguistic origins and verifying them across both language webs until the illusion collapsed.

In a world where algorithms blend and flatten multiple information streams, independently checking both sides of the digital curtain is a critical step: synthesised digital authority must not be taken at face value.

The world has more than two languages, and no bilingual person can catch everything. Yet, Hong Kong is fortunate to have a command of English and Chinese – the world’s two most spoken languages. It provides a vital vantage point and makes it less easy to be locked in a single linguistic cage.

Other regional hubs approach language differently. Singapore’s bilingualism is outward-facing: English for global markets, Mandarin as a cultural anchor. Hong Kong’s bilingualism operates around a unique friction point. Here, English and Chinese run deep across law, education and media, woven into daily life.

For years, Hongkongers have switched between courtrooms where English precedent meets Cantonese testimony and classrooms where textbooks are in one language but discussions in another. This lived, structural bilingualism has cultivated a reflex to cross-check information. It also reveals a counter-intuitive truth: as AI creates the illusion of a seamless global information system, Hong Kong’s bilingualism becomes a critical regional asset. Rival cities pitch themselves as “seamless” tech hubs with friction-free communication, but when information flows without resistance, bias slips through undetected.

Hong Kong’s experience shows the opposite: semantic friction is a feature, not a bug. This daily clash between two information spheres breeds a cross-referencing mechanism no streamlined algorithm can replicate, cultivating independent thinking at its core. This level of proficiency is where other East Asian societies have struggled. Across the region, English education has been a persistent priority, yet results remain superficial.

The region’s current rush towards bilingualism is telling. Taiwan has set a Bilingual 2030 target, while Hanoi pursues a mandate to establish English as Vietnam’s second language in schools by 2035. This exposes the gap between legislative ambition and linguistic depth. The temptation for anyone engineering a bilingual society is to take a short cut, mistaking a legislative mandate for a lived reality, or a classroom curriculum for cultural sediments.

Hong Kong’s bilingual infrastructure is a unique product of colonial history that cannot be easily replicated. It was forged over two centuries to detect discrepancies and triangulate truth across two knowledge systems. Deep bilingualism requires decades to become a truth-sorting mechanism.

Bilingualism must be treated as public intellectual infrastructure, not market-driven privilege. Today, as Hong Kong drifts towards treating bilingualism as a corporate checkbox skill, it cannot afford to let this core competency atrophy. Without institutional grounding, short cuts leave a digital society vulnerable to algorithmic manipulation.

The Hong Kong model is living proof that a society can be globally integrated yet culturally autonomous. True bilingualism is about reclaiming the ability to think, question and create using two information systems.

Bilingualism is nothing new to East Asia. My lecture on the Global South was eventually delivered, but I spent more time than expected double-checking the AI’s data. That experience reshaped my entire view.

Tech can do the heavy lifting of searching across languages now, but bilingualism trains a much deeper skill: the gut instinct to cross-check when something doesn’t fit between two different systems. That is what makes Hong Kong indispensable in the AI era. In an age of algorithmic authority, the critical research skill is not access to data but the ability to read across two languages and catch what falls between them.
