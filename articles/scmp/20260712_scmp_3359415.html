Cabo Verde’s Sal makes a comeback: the tide is turning as its diaspora returns to the island

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 2411 words | 2026-07-12

“Just remember, the sharks can touch you, but you can’t touch them,” warns João, raising his hand with the tip of his middle finger curled away, a mock amputation delivered with such casual theatre that, for a moment, I believe the island really did claim it. He produces a pair of Crocs bleached thin by salt and sun and adds, “Oh, and no open blisters on your feet, either. That tends to excite them, too.”

João is part of a quiet reversal happening across Cabo Verde, an archipelagic country off the coast of West Africa that was, until 1975, a Portuguese colony. After decades defined by departure, Cabo Verde is experiencing return.

The guide had planned to stay in Lisbon, Portugal, for five years. He stayed for 12. Hotel maintenance in Bairro Alto paid well, the work was steady and like hundreds of thousands of Cabo Verdeans before him, he’d followed his grandparents away from his island – in this case, Sal – towards somewhere with more possibilities than salt, sand and sea could offer.

Then, three years ago, he came back to guide tourists into these shallow Atlantic waters where lemon sharks learn to hunt.

“They always come back,” he says, nodding towards the pale blue of Shark Bay. “The young ones. This is where they learn.” He smiles. “A bit like us.”

Although the Cabo Verdean diaspora still outnumbers the domestic population of just over 500,000, net migration has narrowed significantly in recent years.

The economy expanded by around 7.3 per cent in 2024, with tourism creating the kinds of opportunities that once required a plane ticket to find.

We step into the water at low tide, when the surf breaks farther out and the shallows warm into a pale nursery blue. Within seconds of João slapping the surface with a fish spine, the bay answers: metre-long shadows slide past my calves, blunt snouts nosing forward with soft urgency. I am standing in a cradle, a shallow bowl of warmth and safety where juvenile lemon sharks learn to be apex predators before facing the open Atlantic.

“The mama and papa sharks stay out there,” João says, gesturing towards deeper water. “They can’t easily come in here when the tide is low. This part is just for the young ones.”

It’s an apt metaphor for what’s happening across the island. Sal – the name means “salt” in Portuguese – once had a singular economic purpose: extract, dry, export. But as tourism has grown and the economy has diversified, Sal, which attracts more than half of all international visitors to Cabo Verde, is becoming a place worth staying for. Along with neighbouring Boa Vista, it acts as the hub for the country’s tourism industry, largely due to its white-sand beaches, reliable sunshine and abundance of all-inclusive resorts.

Shark Bay lies on the eastern shoulder of Sal. A rusting mini-tanker crouches on the rocks, its hull oxidising patiently into the same cinnamon colour as the desert that spills across the plain to meet it, as if land and wreck were slowly learning to resemble each other. Nothing here looks hurried. Even decay seems to have found a comfortable rhythm. “No Work, No Stress” is the island’s T-shirt motto.

From the air, Sal appeared less an island than a thoughtful doodle in rock and sand: pale, economical, faintly aloof, as if someone began sketching a tropical paradise and then stopped early, deciding that too much greenery might distract from the more serious business of light and space. On the ground, the palette remains disciplined: dunes the colour of shortbread, skies in extravagant blues and an ocean that practises every shade of turquoise like a master stained-glass painter.

There are no jungles here to wrestle, no waterfalls throwing tantrums. Instead, Sal traffics in trade winds, sea breezes, long horizons and the peculiar theatre of time slowing down. Even the ever-present street dogs nap with intent. Everything, from the rare palm tree to your own ambition, eventually agrees to lie flatter. A planned hike becomes a long stare at the sea; a resolved itinerary dissolves in the face of a slow conversation.

The towns are dispersed like points on a clock around this 216 sq km island and there are two main ways to travel between them. Taxis can be flagged down easily, but it’s advisable to set a price before getting in, and shared minibuses, called aluguers – typically white Toyota Hiace vehicles, occasionally modified pickup trucks – run on set routes and leave only when full.

The distance between the two main population centres, Santa Maria and Espargos, is 20km. The former is where most of the tourist resorts stand but the latter, Sal’s modest capital, is where the island gathers itself, schoolchildren threading their way past motorbikes; laundry fluttering from balconies; and brightly painted breeze-block houses offering gentle defiance against the dun landscape. From open windows comes the melancholic acoustic music known as morna: guitar and cavaquinho lute braided with violin, dripping with saudade, or longing, and moving at the pace of a slow strum, the way music sounds when it belongs to a place rather than performs for it.

The ever-present influence of Portugal lingers not only in the cuisine – the bread rolls that arrive with every meal, the bica espresso drunk standing at a counter, the Sagres and Super Bock beer sweating in the afternoon heat – but beneath your feet in the calçada portuguesa: mosaic pavements of limestone and basalt that stitch Sal into the wider Lusophone world, all the way to Macau.

The newly built houses here are too large for single families. Many belong to returnees from Lisbon, Boston, Rotterdam … built to function as guest houses, small hotels, futures. Some of these returnees speak little or no Crioulo, the national language, and some have never lived on Sal before. But they’re investing in an ancestral geography known through family stories and remittance transfers.

I stay for a few nights at the Académico do Sal hostel, above the city’s public 25-metre swimming pool. The building carries the cheerful pragmatism of that arrangement: part-school dormitory, part-old-fashioned American roadside motel, the rooms offer exactly what they promise and nothing they don’t.

One morning, I descend to the pool before the heat has fully committed to the day. I am midway through my third length when I notice a man watching from the edge, his attention fixed not on me but on a girl of maybe 12 years old cutting clean lines up and down the lane beside mine. Adilson took an aluguer up from Santa Maria with his daughter for her morning training session.

“The resorts down there would rather you were a guest paying for the sunbed,” he says, without particular bitterness. “Here, the pool is open to everyone. She gets proper lanes, proper conditions. She learns what a race actually feels like.”

By mid-afternoon, the pool has transformed entirely. A local football team has taken over the terrace for their annual gathering, members arriving in ones and twos with dishes wrapped in foil and plastic containers smelling of cumin and slow cooking. The pool bar does steady business, music appears from somewhere. The Académico do Sal, it turns out, is less hostel, more civic living room.

I meet João again between tours, at Espargos’ Girasol e Museu restaurant, which stands on a backstreet and doubles, as its name suggests, as a small museum, its walls hung with local art and artefacts that give the place the feeling of a family home that quietly decided to become important. The menu is short and unambiguous and depends on what’s available: cachupa, the archipelago’s signature stew, made here from recipes guarded by avós, grandmothers, and spoken of with the reverence due to any secret kept long enough to become identity; fresh tuna grilled over charcoal; roast chicken; crisps fried from real potatoes rather than extruded from a factory; whatever came in on the last ship from the Canary Islands or was grown in the thin, salt-tolerant soil of the island.

The food arrives on clay dishes glazed in strong colours – deep blue, ochre, a particular shade of terracotta red – and the effect, against the spare whitewashed walls, is similar to that of those brightly painted breeze-block houses out on the street: an insistence on colour in a landscape that otherwise declines to offer much of it.

“In Lisbon, I had a job. Here, I have a life,” says João. The difference, he says, wasn’t just about money; it was about what kind of future felt possible. His daughter speaks Crioulo. She learns morna music at school. “She knows where she’s from. I didn’t, not really, until I came back.”

There are no roads to Shark Bay, only wandering rutted tracks in the dust left by the daily pilgrimages of tour-group Toyota Hiluxes with bench seats bolted into their beds. Leaving the bay, you bounce up-island as the land rises slightly, then hollows itself into a vast volcanic bowl, towards Pedra de Lume and its historic salinas.

Inside the crater, salt pans shimmer rose white with a light so fierce it seems to erase edges. Tourists of varying hues of pink bob like penguins in water that is below sea level and mineral-rich, with more than 20 times the salinity of the open sea. João’s warning about blisters suddenly feels less whimsical: even the smallest abrasion blooms instantly into pain. A second warning follows: don’t, whatever you do, touch your eyes.

West towards Palmeira, the ground reddens and the vicious erosion cuts deeper furrows as the land eases down to the island’s harbour and ferry terminal, which links Sal to Boa Vista, São Nicolau and Santiago (Cabo Verde’s most populous island and home to the Chinese-built national football stadium). Fishing boats and private catamarans lean into the quay like animals at a trough. Men mend nets under tin roofs and the Atlantic breathes steadily against the kaleidoscope-coloured homes. Some of the landed tuna, another of the island’s main exports, ends up on the charcoal grills that overlook the marina.

Under a tin awning at the harbour, where lengths of sail cloth – vivid blues, sun-bleached whites, panels of red and orange – are draped over the port railings and the balustrades of the ferry terminal for mending, I meet Steven. He is from southern Nigeria, from a Christian farming town where work thinned and wages never quite arrived.

“I came for a job on the boats,” he says, referring to the tuna-fishing vessels. “I stayed because it felt calm. Because people greet you. Because you can breathe.”

As tourism proliferates, Steven says, finding somewhere to live has become his hardest battle.

Rents rise with every new resort opening. Building cranes and scaffolding abound on the island, but entire buildings disappear into short-term holiday listings as soon as the paint dries.

North of Palmeira lies the bay of Buracona, where the Atlantic has spent millennia arguing with volcanic rock, finally punching through and leaving a cathedral-sized window into darkness. At midday, when the sun reaches its apex, the “blue eye” opens: a circle beamed down into the cenote below of a colour so impossible, it seems like a flaw in reality. Had anyone lived on these islands before the Portuguese turned them into slaving ports, this blue eye might have felt like the genesis of belief itself.

I stand at the edge and watch it glow. For perhaps 10 minutes, the light holds, then shifts and the magic closes again like a pupil contracting. It feels important that the wonder is brief, that you can’t schedule it or guarantee it – you can only arrive and hope and sometimes witness.

Down the ambitious four-lane motorway that is being built to bisect the island, Murdeira Bay opens on the left, a protected crescent yawning beneath Monte Leão. The “Lion Mountain” rises 165 metres from the surrounding basalt flatness and watches over the calm water below, its cliffs hosting the few birds found on Sal: kestrels and ospreys that look outward to the ocean for sustenance rather than inward to the sparse land. The small resort and scatter of holiday rentals almost dissolve into a landscape purpose-built for watching sunsets.

By the time you reach Santa Maria, the island has changed its posture entirely. Here, Sal stands upright, opens its shoulders and smiles.

I sit one evening near the crumbling pier as the light turns amber, watching a German family photograph the sunset while a Cabo Verdean guitarist plays morna for a cluster of swaying French tourists. Behind them, bougainvillea spills over pastel walls. The beach curves in long pale ribbons. More music drifts from somewhere unseen.

The hotels are here, the flights arrive daily from a dozen European cities – Sal’s Amílcar Cabral is the busiest of Cabo Verde’s four international airports – but something of the island’s original temperament persists beneath the welcome. You see it in the local staff running the kitesurf schools, in the Crioulo spoken in hotel hallways, in the menus that list cachupa alongside burgers and club sandwiches.

The same winds that once carried salt ships now beckon kitesurfers. The same desert that meant death is now flown across on zip lines and traversed by quad bikes. And the same beaches that once cradled only turtle eggs now embrace sunbathers.

From June to October, when the moon lifts silver over the dunes and the beaches empty of people, loggerhead sea turtles haul themselves ashore. They return not to just any coast, but to the exact sands that once saw them take their first steps, their ancient bodies guided by a geography written somewhere deeper than memory.

Decades ago, their numbers here were collapsing. Eggs were taken, beaches were unguarded, the future uncertain. Today, local patrols walk the shoreline at night, nests are protected, hatchlings are shepherded to the sea and Sal has become one of the most important loggerhead nesting sites in the entire Atlantic.

This island once did little more than dry and trade salt to keep passing sailors alive, but now, it quietly protects the fragile arithmetic of tomorrow itself. Like the young sharks in Shark Bay, Sal is learning how to belong.
