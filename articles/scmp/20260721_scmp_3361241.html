Why Singapore PM Wong moved swiftly on Faishal Ibrahim’s resignation

SCMP | FREE | 1157 words | 2026-07-21

Singapore’s acting minister-in-charge of Muslim affairs Muhammad Faishal Ibrahim resigned about a month after the prime minister learned of his improper interactions with a woman, a move observers say shows Lawrence Wong’s swift handling of the first scandal under his watch to contain any political fallout.

They told This Week in Asia that while there was no affair in Faishal’s case, it made sense for the party to deal with the consequences quickly in case of leaks as the woman was a member of the public, not a party member whom the ruling People’s Action Party (PAP) could counsel or discipline.

Furthermore, Faishal’s position as acting minister-in-charge of Muslim affairs meant Wong had to be sensitive to the community’s reaction – and potential embarrassment – if the matter was not swiftly extinguished and a replacement sought for the post.

On Monday, Faishal resigned from the cabinet and parliament after inappropriate interactions he had with a woman that resulted in harassment accusations by both parties became known to the prime minister.

No criminal action was found but Faishal, 58, who was also senior minister of state for home affairs, reflected and accepted that his conduct fell short of the standards “expected of a political office holder and MP”, Wong said in a statement on Monday.

Wong revealed that most of their interactions took place through online messages and they also met on the sidelines of public events. Both parties later made allegations of harassment against each other, and the matter was referred to police.

Faishal, who is married with two children, wrote in his resignation letter from government and his party: “While there was no physical relationship between us, and I did not intend for the interactions to develop into one, there was a lapse of judgment on my part in the way I handled the interactions, and in failing to establish clearer boundaries at an earlier stage.”

Faishal’s case inevitably drew comparisons with another explosive revelation in July 2023, when former speaker of parliament Tan Chuan-Jin and MP Cheng Li Hui, both from the PAP, resigned from their positions after being in an extramarital affair.

Political observers noted the much shorter timeline in Wong’s handling of Faishal’s resignation compared with the affair between Tan and Cheng, which then prime minister Lee Hsien Loong found out about after the 2020 general election, at least two years before they stepped down in 2023.

Lee said in a press conference in July 2023 that as recently as February that year, he had counselled the lawmakers involved to end it. Tan offered his resignation then, but Lee said he told him to ensure his constituents were taken care of first and that the affair had to end.

Lee added then that he had “very recently” come across information strongly suggesting the pair’s relationship had continued.

Teo Kay Key, senior research fellow at the Institute of Policy Studies, noted that the full details of Faishal’s interactions were unknown, making comparisons difficult. However, the difference in timelines was “quite clear”, perhaps reflecting lessons learned from the public reaction to Tan’s affair, she said.

“Of course, given that the woman had sent details to the PMO [Prime Minister’s Office] and also that both parties had actually made harassment allegations, there is a possibility that the news would have gotten out from other channels and that it would have been more expedient to deal with the whole matter swiftly,” Teo said.

While the details of the relationship were scant and what actually happened was unclear, Teo added that this incident, taken together with the previous resignations, revealed the PAP’s expectations for how its MPs and political office holders should behave in their personal relationships and how this would affect their political careers.

Independent political analyst Felix Tan raised concern that the lack of details could lead to greater public speculation online.

He said Wong was not being stricter than his predecessor but wanted to stem the damage early instead of letting it “evolve into something greater that he has to deal with in the future”.

“One possible question that may arise is whether Faishal’s decision to step down was intended to bring the matter to a close, particularly if there was concern that it could become public or be pursued further. While there is no evidence to support such a conclusion, it is the kind of question that may emerge in the absence of greater clarity,” Felix Tan said.

He also noted that Leader of the House Indranee Rajah had earlier this year stressed integrity and honesty regarding Workers’ Party chief Pritam Singh’s conviction for lying to a parliamentary committee, and this was a reflection of how the PAP believed parliamentarians should conduct themselves.

Former nominated MP (NMP) Calvin Cheng wrote on social media that Faishal should not resign since he did not have an affair. “One has to balance losing a good minister/MP with small failings that all human beings have,” Cheng said.

Walid Jumblatt Abdullah, an associate professor of public policy at Nanyang Technological University, said on social media that given what was known about the interactions, he did not believe Faishal had to resign and that impossible standards were being set that would deter young people from politics.

“The criteria I would use is: is Singapore better off today than yesterday? I think the answer is no. He was a good minister for the country,” Walid said.

Yet Zulkifli Baharudin, a former NMP, told This Week in Asia that the office of Minister-in-charge of Muslim Affairs had to be held to a higher standard.

“The institution of the Muslim minister is a very peculiar one,” he said. “It’s one that’s based on preserving the dignity and the position of Malay Muslims in Singapore and Lawrence Wong must treat the institution of Muslim minister very sensitively so even a minor indiscretion will not be tolerated.”

While some might not consider Faishal’s actions a big or serious deal, Zulkifli noted that the prime minister was taking an uncompromising and high standard expected of the minister-in-charge of Muslim affairs, which would strengthen the institution in the long run.

“It is a tragedy and the community is embarrassed and upset. But one, this is human failure and two, at the end of the day, this will strengthen the institution. This is not just another ministry, it reflects the standing and position of Malay Muslims in Singapore,” Zulkifli said.

Faishal, who has a PhD in management science, entered politics in 2006 in the then Marine Parade group representation constituency following an uncontested walkover.

He was appointed minister of state for national development and home affairs in 2020 and replaced Masagos Zulkifli as acting minister-in-charge of Muslim affairs following the 2025 election, when he was also co-opted into the central executive committee of the ruling PAP.
