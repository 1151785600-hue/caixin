Latest US political divide emerges: the age gap over whether China is cause for concern

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 555 words | 2026-07-08

A generational gap over China is emerging in the United States, with younger Americans significantly less likely to see Beijing as a cause for concern, according to a new survey.

“Across almost every potential concern tested regarding Beijing, American unease climbs steeply with age,” the 2026 Reagan Institute Summer Survey report said.

It also found that despite the deep political divisions within the US over foreign and domestic policy, there was less sign of a bipartisan split over China compared with any other specific foreign policy issue.

“There is bipartisan consensus across most concerns regarding China, from its role in the fentanyl crisis to its human rights abuses. But domestic-facing worries top the list of concerns about China,” the report said.

Both countries have agreed to try to stabilise their relationship following US President Donald Trump’s visit to Beijing in May, but the world’s two largest economies remain locked in competition across a range of issues, from security and ideology to AI and technology.

The survey questioned 1,555 respondents about 11 China-specific issues, and concluded that “younger Americans are markedly less worried than their older counterparts”.

The widest generational divide emerged on issues such as espionage, technology theft and unfair trade, as well as Taiwan.

The single largest gap was over the Chinese government’s alleged ability to spy on American citizens – something Beijing denies.

While 93 per cent of those aged 65 and older expressed concern about this issue, only 62 per cent of those aged 18 to 29 shared that anxiety, a 31-point split.

While 86 per cent of over-65s expressed concern about Beijing attempting to take over Taiwan by force, that share dropped to just 56 per cent among the younger demographic.

Beijing sees Taiwan as part of China and has not ruled out the use of force to reunite it with the mainland.

Most countries, including the US, do not recognise Taiwan as an independent state, but Washington is opposed to any move to take the island by force and is legally bound to supply weapons to the island.

Accusations of censorship and surveillance within China were the biggest issue for the 18-29 age group, with 73 per cent expressing concern, just two points less than those aged 65 or older.

In recent years, Beijing has introduced a raft of new national security policies, fuelling growing anxieties abroad regarding its domestic policy trajectory.

In contrast, only 55 per cent of the younger age group were concerned about the prospect of China surpassing the US as the world’s biggest economy, compared with 77 per cent of over-65s.

The two biggest concerns for the older age group were Beijing’s ability to spy on Americans and the government buying land in the US, both of which stood at 93 per cent.

The survey also found that “Americans’ single greatest concern is China’s role in the flow of fentanyl into the United States”, which attracted an “intensely bipartisan” consensus at 82 per cent among all respondents.

But this unity was shattered over whether the Trump administration is taking the right approach towards China.

In the aftermath of the May summit in Beijing, 44 per cent of Americans said this approach was “about right”. But 59 per cent of Republicans shared this view, while the Democrats were split with 32 per cent agreeing compared with 37 per cent who said the White House had not been tough enough.
