Hong Kong consults on sex law reform over cult coercion, campus and online abuse

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 725 words | 2026-07-11

Cults luring victims overseas for sex in a bid to “change their fate”, as well as suggestive games hosted at orientation camps in Hong Kong, could amount to sexual offences, authorities have warned, as they launched a public consultation on the city’s sexual law reforms.

Secretary for Security Chris Tang Ping-keung said on Tuesday that sexual offences involving misleading claims would fall within the city’s legal remit if the deception took place in Hong Kong, even if the incident occurred overseas.

He was speaking at a Legislative Council panel meeting in response to a question from Chan Hok-fung regarding “sexual favours”, often used by religious cults to defraud believers into “changing their fate”.

As part of the sweeping overhaul, Tang said a new statutory definition of consent for sexual acts would be introduced.

“In such situations, it would be assumed that the act is non-consensual and the victim is mistaken as to the nature or purpose of the sexual act,” he said.

Earlier in the day, the Security Bureau announced the launch of a month-long public consultation on the reforms and invited members of the public to submit their views by August 5.

More than a dozen lawmakers spoke on the issue during the two-hour security panel meeting, with most seeking clarification on details of proposed new offences, including sexual assault without touching, as well as new laws on underage sex and incest.

Legislators also urged authorities to minimise the re-traumatisation of sexual assault victims during legal proceedings through measures such as live-streamed testimony, and to strengthen education and publicity so people would better understand the scope of the new laws.

Lawmaker Nixie Lam noted that summer was the peak season for university orientation camps for freshmen, where female students had reportedly been made to wear white tops and then soaked, revealing their undergarments.

A second-year student at the University of Hong Kong was arrested and charged with groping a first-year student at an orientation event in 2023.

At a Lingnan University orientation event, students reportedly played kissing games, while allegations of rape and voyeurism arose at an event organised by the Education University of Hong Kong.

“Sexual assault with touching includes the spraying of liquid or bodily fluids with sexual intentions. This targets these games at orientation camps, which makes female students feel embarrassed or expose their bodies,” Tang said.

Lam also questioned whether there would be a loophole in regulating student-teacher relationships, given that the age of consent was set at 16.

“Although they have reached the age of consent, they are still a pupil. When faced with authoritative figures such as teachers or coaches, they might think they are truly in love,” she said.

In response, Tang said many jurisdictions also set the age of consent at 16, adding that teenagers above that age would still be protected.

“In our reforms on consent, the consent does not hold if the victim submits to the sexual act because of the abuse of a position of trust or authority,” he said.

Legislator Elizabeth Quat questioned whether AI-generated deepfake pornographic images would be regulated under the reforms.

“If I used Secretary Tang’s face to create pornographic images, is that acceptable?” she asked.

Tang said digital sexual images included real footage, composite videos and deepfakes, as well as cartoons and depictions of animals having sex.

“If the victim felt that the person’s non-consensual sexual act caused them humiliation, alarm or distress, this could count as sexual assault without touching, as it is done online,” he said.

“If they are not distributed to others, they might not necessarily constitute the crime of sexual assault without touching, but possessing such images is already illegal.”

He added that the reforms would widen the scope of familial relationships covered to better protect children, including full-blood or half-blood uncles, aunts, nephews and nieces, lineal ancestors such as great-grandparents and great-grandchildren, and adoptive and step-parents.

The definition of familial relationships would be broadened to align with the Marriage Ordinance, which bans unions between parties within specified degrees of kindred or consanguinity.

“Sex between persons prohibited from marriage under the ordinance would be considered incest,” Tang said.

“If the intercourse is not consensual or with a minor, there would also be an added charge of rape.”
