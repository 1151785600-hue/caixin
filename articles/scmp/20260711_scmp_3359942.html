China’s central bank vows flexible approach as it acknowledges uneven economic picture

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 680 words | 2026-07-11

China’s central bank pledged to maintain a “moderately loose” monetary policy while calling for a more forward-looking, flexible and targeted approach, flagging “structural divergence” as a key domestic challenge for the first time, in a statement published on Wednesday.

The remarks – in a readout from a meeting of the monetary policy committee of the People’s Bank of China (PBOC) on Saturday – came as China’s economy grows increasingly uneven, with artificial intelligence-related sectors, hi-tech manufacturing and exports outperforming the broader economy while domestic consumption and private investment remain weak.

The committee reiterated its accommodative policy stance, saying it would promote stable growth and “a reasonable recovery in prices”.

In outlining its approach for the coming months, the readout dropped last quarter’s pledge to “strengthen monetary policy regulation”, placing greater emphasis on policy flexibility in response to domestic and global conditions.

The readout described the external environment as “more complex and volatile”, citing more frequent geopolitical tensions and trade frictions. Domestically, it added “structural divergence” to its assessment of challenges, alongside weak demand and external shocks.

Compared with the meeting readout in the first quarter, the PBOC appeared to “have acknowledged softer economic momentum” this time, according to a Goldman Sachs research note on Thursday.

“The newly added reference to ‘structural divergence’ among domestic challenges suggested clearer recognition of more bifurcated growth with weakening domestic demand,” the note said.

Former officials have been voicing concerns about the widening imbalance in China’s economy.

The economy was “at an important turning point”, said Liu Shijin, a former member of the PBOC’s monetary policy committee, at the China Macroeconomic Forum’s midyear conference on June 27. He warned that the combination of falling investment and contracting consumer spending was “unseen since the start of reform and opening up”.

Despite China posting a record goods trade surplus of nearly US$1.2 trillion last year, fixed-asset investment in the first five months of 2026 fell 4.1 per cent year on year, while retail sales dropped 0.6 per cent in May from a year earlier, the first decline since pandemic restrictions were lifted at the end of 2022.

Similar concerns were raised by Sheng Laiyun, former deputy head of the National Bureau of Statistics, who on Sunday urged policymakers to pay closer attention to the gap between official data and households’ lived experience. He urged additional policy measures early in the second half of the year, according to a post by the Academic Centre for Chinese Economic Practice and Thinking at Tsinghua University.

The PBOC readout also called for “strengthening the internal drivers of economic growth” and reinforcing both domestic and international economic circulation.

It made no explicit reference to cutting banks’ reserve requirement ratios or policy interest rates, despite Governor Pan Gongsheng saying in March that there was still room to reduce both this year.

“The statement did not hint at an imminent easing push,” Goldman Sachs said in its note.

Instead, the near-term policy response was more likely to come through faster fiscal policy implementation, ample interbank liquidity and targeted credit easing, it said. The investment bank maintained its baseline forecast of no cuts in policy rates or reserve requirement ratios in 2026.

The PBOC readout also mentioned that uncertainty remained over inflation trends and monetary policy adjustments in major economies – a recurring assessment in the central bank’s policy readouts.

While Kevin Warsh’s first policy meeting as US Federal Reserve chair in June delivered a widely expected decision to hold rates steady, minutes of the meeting released on Wednesday showed that inflation concerns grew at the meeting.

According to the minutes, “a few” participants saw a case for raising interest rates, but ultimately supported no change.

At the meeting, Fed officials generally assessed “that upside risks to price stability remained elevated while downside risks to achieving maximum employment had moderated a bit”.

Meanwhile, AI-related demand was raised as a factor that could drive up inflation, alongside the conflict in the Middle East and the effects of tariffs, according to the minutes.
