Why scrapping Nepal’s ‘dollar fare’ could leave locals grounded

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 936 words | 2026-07-15

A plan to make Nepal’s domestic flights cheaper for foreign visitors could end up making them more expensive for citizens, according to airline operators who warn against scrapping one of the country’s most contentious tourism pricing policies.

Under Nepal’s two-tier airfare system, foreign passengers must pay in US dollars for domestic flights, with prices usually two to three times higher than for Nepalis and sometimes more, depending on the route and season.

In April, Minister for Culture, Tourism and Civil Aviation Khadak Raj Paudel pledged to end the sky-high airfares to boost tourism and provide respite to non-resident Nepalis holding foreign citizenship who are also required to pay higher fares.

He told the Kantipur newspaper that plans to scrap the “dollar fare” would initially start with flights to the far-western Karnali and Sudurpaschim provinces – home to lesser-visited attractions such as Rara and Shey Phoksundo lakes and Khaptad National Park – before being rolled out nationally.

The plan stalled due to the fuel crisis linked to the Iran-US conflict and the tourism ministry said there were no ongoing discussions on the issue.

The proposal has since reignited a debate over its feasibility, with tourism stakeholders saying the repercussions could extend far beyond the aviation sector.

“If you’re only looking from a tourism perspective, then lower airfares would be a good thing,” said Murali Dhar Joshi, general secretary for the Airlines Operators Association of Nepal. “But from an airline perspective, it’s not feasible. It won’t be economically sustainable.”

Nepal introduced the dollar fare in the 1970s when the state-owned carrier, then known as Royal Nepal Airlines, started charging separately for its mountain flight offering a close-up of the Himalayas, including Mount Everest. After Nepal liberalised its aviation sector in the early 1990s, private airlines adopted the same two-tier pricing on domestic routes.

Foreign travellers, however, have long voiced frustration over the exorbitant pricing, with some calling the two-tier system “unfair and discriminatory”.

A round-trip flight from Kathmandu to Lukla – the gateway to Everest – costs over US$250 for foreigners, which is more than the US$200 for some international flights to New Delhi, and the roughly 12,000 rupees (US$80) for locals.

Similarly, foreigners pay about US$225 for a two-way ticket from Kathmandu to Pokhara, versus 11,500 rupees for Nepalis.

The price difference might appear steep, but Joshi said the rationale behind the dollar fare was best understood in a broader context.

According to Joshi, dollar fares are crucial for flights serving short take-off and landing (STOL) airports in remote mountainous regions, such as Lukla, as well as major trunk routes, including Pokhara. He said the perceived higher fares for tourists were actual operational costs for the flights, which helped make flying affordable for locals.

“The revenue we receive in dollars helps us subsidise the costs for Nepalis, but there is a misconception that locals are getting special privileges, which is not accurate,” Joshi said.

Mani Raj Lamichhane, a senior director at the semi-government Nepal Tourism Board, said abolishing the dollar fare would be “impractical” and noted that many tourism-dependent countries, including the Maldives, had similar pricing models.

He said he was part of the committee that also advised against the move when then tourism minister Yogesh Bhattarai pushed for it in 2019.

In a low-income country like Nepal, where the monthly minimum wage for workers is 19,550 rupees (US$125), observers say lower domestic airfares make flying possible for many, particularly those in remote mountain districts where road access is limited or unreliable. Such cheaper flights can provide vital access to healthcare facilities in cities, along with education, employment and other essential services.

Madushika Lansakara, who has been working in Nepal for four years, says she has “mixed feelings” about the dollar fare, but that visitors paying more is not unfamiliar to her. In her home country of Sri Lanka, foreign tourists pay significantly more than locals at many attractions.

Lansakara said she did not mind paying more if the tourist dollars helped locals.

“The purchasing power of foreigners is higher than that of locals, and we’re being paid more,” she said. “So, if the higher fares are allowing more Nepalis to travel efficiently, I don’t mind paying more.”

However, some tourism entrepreneurs argue that dollar fares discourage foreigners from exploring some of Nepal’s hidden gems; one even filed a petition at the Supreme Court earlier this year, demanding its removal.

For instance, flights to Dhangadhi, the starting point for Api Base Camp in Sudurpaschim, remain steep at around US$450 for foreigners versus 20,000 rupees for locals.

Some critics have also suggested scrapping the dollar fare on non-STOL routes while maintaining it for flights to mountainous regions, but airline operators do not see that as a viable solution.

Rupesh Joshi, director of marketing and sales at domestic carrier Buddha Air, said the dollar fare was crucial in generating foreign exchange for Nepal, used to finance essential imports. At a time when the domestic aviation market has already contracted by nearly 40 per cent as rising fuel costs push up ticket prices, scrapping the dollar fare could send the sector into a tailspin.

“Airlines need to pay their loans, maintenance costs, and insurance all in dollars, and this won’t be sustainable. Once we introduce a uniform fare policy, it will make flying very expensive for Nepalis,” he said.

“Even if we scrap the dollar fare, what will the government do for hotels and Unesco-listed tourist sites that also have a dual pricing policy for locals and foreigners? It’s not feasible.”
