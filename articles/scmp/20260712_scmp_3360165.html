Shein receives approval for Hong Kong IPO from Chinese regulators

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 811 words | 2026-07-12

Online retail giant Shein Global Holdings has secured approval from the China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) to seek an initial public offering in Hong Kong, a long-awaited step for the firm after its attempts to list in New York or London stalled.

The firm plans to issue up to 341.6 million shares and list on the Hong Kong stock exchange, according to a Friday statement by the CSRC.

Shein previously sought to go public in New York or London, according to earlier media reports, but those attempts have halted amid US and European regulatory scrutiny of the firm’s operations on issues ranging from its supply chain to tax.

Shein, which was founded in China in 2008 but later moved its headquarters to Singapore, previously attempted to distance itself from China as its global profile rose, despite most of its suppliers being based on the mainland. But the firm has been trying to build closer ties with Beijing amid difficulties in securing a listing outside China.

Analysts said the approval was positive news for Hong Kong’s equities market as it would expand the pool of high-quality listed companies, though it was too early to say whether the listing itself would materially lift the broader market.

“While one listing alone will not transform the IPO landscape, [it] reinforces the city’s ability to attract large, internationally relevant listings,” said Matteo Giovannini, senior finance manager at the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China.

“It should help improve market sentiment and could encourage other Chinese companies considering overseas listings to view Hong Kong as the preferred venue.”

Beijing has rolled out a series of supportive measures recently to strengthen Hong Kong’s position as an offshore yuan hub and international financial centre. Earlier this week, Pan Gongsheng, governor of the People’s Bank of China, vowed to provide further momentum to Hong Kong’s capital market development.

“Shein is special for the Hong Kong stock exchange because there has rarely been a retail company with such a big size,” said Luo Zhiyu, a partner at DeHeng Law Offices in Beijing who specialises in cross-border initial public offerings, mergers and acquisitions.

Hong Kong’s IPO market hit a five-year high in terms of both total funds raised and the number of listings in the first half of this year, according to data from PricewaterhouseCoopers (PwC) Hong Kong.

The city’s IPO market raised HK$210 billion (US$26.8 billion), up 92 per cent year on year, ranking second globally. The number of new listings soared to 87, up 98 per cent from a year earlier.

But the approval does not mean that a Shein listing is confirmed. “The CSRC will conduct a comprehensive review of all risks associated with Shein’s overseas listing, including national security, data and cybersecurity,” said Hong Jiayang, managing partner of US law firm Joint-Win Partners LLP, which focuses on trade compliance.

According to Shein’s corporate records, its main Chinese mainland entity is owned by a Singaporean company – a common arrangement known as a “red-chip” structure, where a Chinese mainland company moves its ownership offshore to raise capital or list overseas.

“As the Chinese securities regulator has increasingly enhanced its scrutiny of the red-chip structure, an approval signals and points out a clear direction for companies under such a structure,” Luo added.

Shein reportedly filed for an IPO in the United States confidentially as early as 2023, later switching its attention to London as tensions rose between Beijing and Washington.

But a London IPO stalled as regulators received a number of complaints, including from an advocacy group for China’s Uygur population highlighting supply chain issues.

The firm has also faced wider regulatory pressures across Europe as its sales on the continent have soared. In June, French authorities fined the firm €22 million (US$25.5 million) for breaching consumer protection laws.

Institutional investors have reportedly been enraged by the company’s postponed IPO plans. Some major shareholders lost patience and privately asked Shein to consider buying back their shares, the South China Morning Post reported in 2024.

“If Shein successfully lists, it would definitely be a positive development for Chinese-backed companies facing relatively high trade compliance risks seeking to raise capital in Hong Kong,” Hong said.

Shein has been trying to build closer ties with Beijing amid difficulties in securing a listing outside China.

Earlier this year, company founder Xu Yangtian made his first-ever public appearance at an event held by the government of southern China’s Guangdong province, where he pledged to “continue to take root in Guangdong” and called Guangzhou, the provincial capital, the company’s “supply chain headquarters”.

The IPO approval “serves as a reminder” to Chinese companies operating overseas that “distancing themselves from their Chinese roots is unlikely to be the right strategy in the current environment”, said Xu Tianchen, senior economist at the Economist Intelligence Unit.
