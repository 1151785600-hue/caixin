China rejects US, Panamanian claims of ‘weaponising’ ship inspections

SCMP | FREE | 438 words | 2026-07-13

China has defended its inspections of Panama-flagged ships as routine safety checks, rejecting US and Panamanian accusations that it is weaponising shipping lines for geopolitical leverage.

In Beijing on Monday, Chinese foreign ministry spokesman Lin Jian said the measures were lawful and aimed at protecting navigation and the marine environment.

“At present, port-state control inspections are an important measure for countries to manage the safety of foreign vessels calling at their ports and to keep waters clean,” Lin said.

“China attaches great importance to navigation safety and personal safety, and conducts port-state control inspections of vessels calling at Chinese ports in accordance with law and regulations, fully in line with international conventions.”

Lin said Chinese data showed that Panama-flagged vessels were involved in multiple serious accidents in Chinese waters this year, making them the flag state with the highest share of incidents.

From January to July, they accounted for less than 20 per cent of foreign arrivals but about half of all accidents and casualties, he said.

The dispute flared after Warren Stephens, the US ambassador to Britain, raised concerns at the International Maritime Organisation’s 137th Council session in London on Tuesday.

Stephens accused Beijing of using port inspections and vessel detentions as instruments of pressure.

China was seeking to turn ports, ships and supply chains into geopolitical leverage, the envoy said, warning of Beijing’s growing maritime footprint, including its dominant shipbuilding capacity and role in port equipment supply.

The row traces back to late June, when Panamanian President Jose Raul Mulino told an Organisation of American States meeting that Panama had recorded a sharp rise in abnormal inspections and detentions of Panama-flagged vessels at Chinese ports with no technical or safety justification.

Xie Feng, China’s ambassador to the US, responded at the same forum that Beijing conducted inspections under Chinese law and international conventions, and did not target particular flags.

The disagreement is one of the biggest strains on bilateral relations since Panama officially established ties with Beijing in 2017.

Panama, which was the first Latin American country to join China’s Belt and Road Initiative, exited the trade and infrastructure network last year amid rising US pressure.

Tensions intensified earlier this year after Panama’s Supreme Court revoked the concession held by Panama Ports Company, a unit of Hong Kong’s CK Hutchison, for the Balboa and Cristobal terminals.

Earlier, US President Donald Trump had repeatedly questioned China’s influence around the strategic waterway, and threatened to restore direct US control.

The ruling handed interim operations to two European shipping firms, while a fresh international public tender is being organised.
