Energy storage sector tested as China sets 60-day payment cap in anti-involution campaign

SCMP | FREE | 426 words | 2026-07-08

Beijing’s campaign to curb disorderly low-price market competition – neijuan in Chinese or involution – has reached the energy storage sector, with new rules shortening payment cycles and the financial pressure expected to ripple through leading players.

A payment cap, released by the China Automotive Battery Innovation Alliance (Cabia) and the China Energy Storage Alliance on Monday, covers both electric vehicle (EV) and energy storage batteries and aims to build a mutually beneficial industrial ecosystem.

Eleven key industry players including Contemporary Amperex Technology Limited (CATL) and BYD’s Fudi Battery have signalled commitment to the initiative.

Under the guidelines, buyers of materials and components must complete inspection and acceptance within seven working days of receipt, while EV and energy storage battery makers must limit payment terms to small and medium-sized enterprise suppliers to a maximum of 60 calendar days from delivery or formal acceptance.

The requirements were consistent with China’s anti-involution regulatory framework, which had been elevated to a national strategy to shift key industrial chains from quantity-driven growth to quality and restore pricing power, said Jing Yang, director of Asia-Pacific corporate ratings at Fitch Ratings.

“More stringent payment requirements may modestly pressure downstream players’ flexibility to extend payable days,” she said, adding that Fitch did not expect immediate financial impacts.

Shares of listed battery makers were mixed on Tuesday, with CATL and Ganfeng Lithium edging up over 1 per cent, while BYD and CALB Group fell more than 0.5 and 2 per cent respectively.

The Ministry of Industry and Information Technology said upstream suppliers faced a dual squeeze of research and development investment and prolonged payment cycles, leading to unstable business expectations and weak overall supply chain resilience, a department under the ministry said on Monday.

The framework mirrors a precedent set by China’s automotive sector. In June 2025, 17 major domestic carmakers publicly committed to a 60-day supplier payment cap to stabilise a sector fractured by intense price wars.

Energy storage has emerged as the second growth engine for Chinese battery makers amid weakening domestic demand for EVs.

In the first five months of the year, China’s sales of energy storage batteries surged nearly 88 per cent year on year to 255.5 gigawatt-hours (GWh), outpacing EV batteries’ 35 per cent rise to 528GWh, data from Cabia showed.

Amid worldwide energy security fears and the spread of data centres, global shipments of batteries used in energy storage systems were expected to grow 55 per cent to 850GWh this year and by 25 per cent to 1,060GWh next year, according to a report by BofA Global Research in April.
