Fifa World Cup: Hong Kong pubs, restaurants enjoy net gains from big matches

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 990 words | 2026-07-13

Many bars, restaurants and shopping centres in Hong Kong have enjoyed brisk business thanks to the World Cup, with top nightlife district Lan Kwai Fong recording up to a 70 per cent jump in patronage during big matches.

Lan Kwai Fong Group chairman Allan Zeman told the South China Morning Post that he had joined football fans in the entertainment district in Central for the England-Norway game in the early hours of Sunday morning.

“It’s [been] a crazy night. It’ll only get crazier once we get into the final,” Zeman said. “There are not many nights that get that full at 4am with both locals and tourists.

“Business is up 60 to 70 per cent,” he said, comparing important World Cup games involving big-name teams such as England, Brazil and Argentina with normal days.

But Zeman stopped short of sharing his expectations for the final, only expressing confidence that Lan Kwai Fong would be packed for the occasion.

The Fifa World Cup has injected much-needed buoyancy into business at local pubs, bars and restaurants, which have grappled with a years-long consumer trend of Hongkongers heading to mainland China for cheaper options.

Patrons flocked to their venues of choice to watch the showdown between Norway and England, with the latter winning 2-1 after extra time to set up a semi-final clash with Argentina.

A highlight in the run-up to the game was Norwegian player Erling Haaland, whose online persona and promotion of Chinese herbal tea brand Wanglaoji, also known as Wong Lo Kat in Cantonese, have made him popular across China.

The high-profile match even prompted budget airline Norwegian Air Shuttle to temporarily replace its Instagram icon with the British Airways logo in a sporting nod to England’s win.

But at Lin Heung Lau, a 100-year-old dim sum restaurant in Central showing the match, some fans expressed disappointment at being unable to see the match on the second floor’s gigantic screen because of the crowds.

Others also complained about being unable to secure tables for the event.

“Why did people on the second floor tell me I had to book, but the restaurant staff said there was no booking service?” football fan Amy Ng said.

Viral online footage shows a queue of at least 100 people outside the restaurant at around 4am.

Zeman said many bars and restaurants were offering various World Cup experiences for patrons, including breakfast services for early-morning matches.

“We’re just in the final stretch. In this time in the world, with the Iran war and everything else, you could not have a better attraction to bring the world together,” he said.

In Tseung Kwan O, Chi Lin Buddhist Primary School held a “fun day” for parents and students to watch the match in the morning, after ditching an earlier plan to hold an overnight screening.

The Norway-England match was far from the only crowd-pleaser of the day, with hundreds visiting the Citywalk 2 shopping centre in Tsuen Wan at 9am to watch Argentina take on Switzerland.

Among the crowd of standing and sitting football fans was Mr Yip, who said he was excited to see his idol, Argentinian superstar Lionel Messi, take his team into the semi-finals.

At MCP New Town Plaza in Tseung Kwan O, more than 1,000 football fans gathered to watch Argentina beat Switzerland 3-1 after extra time. They were joined by local footballers Jason Kam Chi-kin, Jordan Lam Lok-kan and others for the game, performances and activities.

Large crowds, many wearing Messi jerseys, were also spotted at Tuen Mun Town Plaza for the Sunday morning match, bringing picnic mats, stools and snacks to an area decorated as a football pitch.

Olympian City, a shopping centre in Tai Kok Tsui, also attracted crowds watching the match. Fans were seated in a designated area with a big screen, with passers-by occasionally stopping to take a look.

Chin Chun-wing, chairman of the Hong Kong Bar and Club Association, said about half of the city’s 1,100 bars had made arrangements to show World Cup matches, with those screening games generally reporting 30 per cent more business than those that did not.

He expressed optimism about business as the competition edged towards the final, adding that many bars were already 80 per cent full with bookings despite matches starting at 3am on a weekday, which was “more than what they would have had”.

“Most people in Hong Kong live in small flats and do not want to wake their families when they watch matches, and tourists can’t watch in their hotels, so many will go to bars. So the World Cup has been beneficial for business across Hong Kong,” Chin said.

The Dorsett hotel in Kai Tak also reported an uptick in business thanks to the World Cup, with a spokesman saying the 373-room property had enjoyed a significant increase in bookings for the semi-finals and the final.

“We expect a full house for the final night as fans want to watch the 3am match in comfort and crash right after,” he said.

The spokesman said the hotel expected the momentum generated by the World Cup to carry through July and August with events such as the Hong Kong Football Festival and the Audi Football Summit featuring FC Bayern Munich vs Aston Villa FC.

However, Simon Wong Ka-wo, president of the Hong Kong Federation of Restaurants and Related Trades, said the World Cup was benefiting only certain areas of Hong Kong, thinning out extra gains generated by the tournament.

He added that some residents were travelling across the border to watch matches in Shenzhen, with massage parlour promotions offering rest and relaxation before and after early morning games attracting people.

The semi-final match between France and Spain will take place at 3am Hong Kong time on Wednesday, while England and Argentina will go head to head at the same time the next day.
