Tech stocks capture China’s elderly retail investors amid faith in nation’s tech sector

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 764 words | 2026-07-14

Technology stocks have become the new darlings of China’s retail investors who are betting their savings on chipmakers and artificial intelligence (AI) developers amid belief in the country’s technology ascendancy.

“Foam is the best bit of a beer,” Fang Yan’an, a veteran investor in his late 70s, told the SCMP, referring to his tactic of chasing the rally of some technology companies.

“Our government has ramped up support for technological development over the past few years, and so have many other nations,” Fang said. “Since technology is now crucial for global competition, I tend to believe in the AI industry, and I’m prepared to hold the shares through a long-haul journey.”

Higher earnings could support the high price of technology stocks as more commercial products are developed from research, he added.

Fang, who has more than 30 years of experience in trading A-shares on the Shanghai and Shenzhen stock exchanges, is among millions of Chinese retail investors who have faith in technology stocks, hoping to strike it rich during volatile rides.

The sector has staged a blistering rally over the past two years, with prices of several leading firms skyrocketing more than tenfold, and it remains hotly debated whether technology stocks on the A-share market are in a bubble.

The tech-heavy Star Market Composite Index has kept hitting record highs in recent months, surging around 43.5 per cent so far this year. The ChiNext Index has also delivered a stand-out performance, notching a 20 per cent gain so far this year.

Fang said that every day he arrives at a brokerage outlet in Shanghai’s suburban Minhang district 30 minutes before trading starts. He said he had already benefited from the bull run.

Early last year, he started building positions in technology equities, and now AI-related companies account for nearly half of his investment portfolio. His trading account is now worth more than 3 million yuan (US$441,790), with the best performer surging by over 1,000 per cent.

According to the annual report from China Securities Depository and Clearing Corporation (CSDC), the institution responsible for securities registration, clearing and settlement on the Chinese mainland stock market, the number of A-share investors in China exceeded 250 million by the end of 2025, with retail investors accounting for around 99.7 per cent of the total investor base.

A survey conducted by China Data Pay, a government-backed big-data research and development organisation, showed that investors aged 66 and above made up nearly 14 per cent of all individual market participants as of 2020.

A Shanghai-based broker, who asked not to be identified, told the SCMP that the proportion of the elderly retail investors at his firm still stood above 10 per cent by the end of last year.

Many elderly retail investors say they shunned blue chips such as banks, frustrated by their slowing share price movements after having traded stocks for decades.

One 67-year-old investor, who would only identify himself as Liu, said he first dabbled in A-shares three decades ago, when he quit his job after pouring savings into the stock market. Liu said he was comfortable with punting on swings in share prices, describing himself as a “professional speculator.”

“Tech stocks have already run up sharply, but I think there’s still room for further upside,” he argued.

Huang Cendong, a senior analyst with Sinolink Securities, acknowledged that technology stocks have developed price bubbles, yet noted that “we maintain a bullish stance on AI-linked equities over the medium- to long-term. In the long run, China has pressed ahead with domestic substitution in these critical semiconductor segments.”

In the short-run, “as markets kick off the earnings season, the volatility is set to persist in the near term. Market sentiment has turned fragile in the past two weeks, with prices perched at elevated levels and even minor headlines triggering sharp swings,” Huang said. “Both the technology-laden STAR Market Composite Index and ChiNext Index have exhibited far greater volatility than they did at the beginning of this year.”

Experienced investors are becoming cautious, he noted.

“The tech stock rally mirrors the frenzy seen in new energy companies five years ago, when capital flooded into the sector. It doesn’t matter when you buy in. When to sell is what really matters,” said Liu, who has cut his exposure to tech stocks.

“Now I execute trades in smaller position sizes each order. Earlier this year, I’d scoop up a minimum of 30,000 shares whenever a tech stock caught my eye. Now my entry size has been trimmed to [10,000] or 20,000 shares,” Liu added.
