Rolex Oyster celebrates 100 years of revolutionising watchmaking

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1062 words | 2026-07-15

Few timepieces can genuinely claim to have changed the course of watchmaking. As it celebrates its centenary this year, the Oyster stands not merely as Rolex’s longest-lasting creation, but also as the conceptual blueprint for the modern mechanical wristwatch. Its influence is embedded so deeply into contemporary horology that its once-radical ideas – water resistance, everyday durability – are now taken for granted.

Until the late 19th century, wristwatches had been regarded as feminine accessories while men relied on pocket watches that were protected from dust and moisture. Warfare transformed that convention. Soldiers, needing easy access to the time in combat, began strapping pocket watches to their wrists, giving rise to the “wristlet”. That practicality caught on beyond the battlefield, and, by the first world war, the wristwatch had firmly established itself among men, too, even though functional drawbacks still existed.

In 1926, Rolex was still far from the global force it would become. Founded in London in 1905 as Wilsdorf & Davis, by German businessman Hans Wilsdorf and his brother-in-law, Alfred Davis, the company had spent its early decades building credibility in precision timekeeping while harbouring ambitions to compete as an equal with long-established, prestigious Swiss manufacturers such as Vacheron Constantin, Blancpain and Patek Philippe.

In 1910, a Rolex became the first wristwatch to receive a chronometric certificate from the Official Watch Rating Centre in Switzerland. Four years later, another achieved the Class A certificate from Britain’s Kew Observatory, an honour previously reserved for marine chronometers. Precision was no longer the question. Durability was.

Wilsdorf understood that accuracy meant little if a watch could not survive real-life challenges. His answer arrived in 1926, by which time Rolex had relocated its base to Switzerland. The innovation that went into producing the Oyster was not simply water resistance – waterproof watches had existed in experimental forms before – but the creation of a hermetically sealed case designed for daily, long-term wear.

Rolex achieved this by engineering a system in which the bezel, case back and winding crown were screwed tightly against the middle case, forming an airtight architecture that protected the movement from water, dust and debris. The name captured the idea; like an oyster protecting the pearl within, the case became a shell safeguarding the movement. The watch could accompany its wearer through rain, travel, sport and work.

The new watch case’s first serious test came in 1927, when British swimmer Mercedes Gleitze attempted to cross the English Channel wearing a Rolex Oyster. After more than 10 hours in freezing waters, she and the watch emerged, the latter still running.

From there, the Oyster became a companion to other adventurers. On Malcolm Campbell’s land-speed record attempts in the 1920s and 30s, it endured extreme vibration, further proving that a wristwatch could maintain accuracy far beyond the controlled conditions of the watchmaker’s bench.

It accompanied pioneering aviators such as Chuck Yeager, surviving the thin air, freezing temperatures and physical strain of early high-altitude flight. It reached the highest peaks with mountaineers, proving that mechanical precision could endure where human performance itself was pushed to its limits.

Still, one challenge remained. Like all watches of its era, a Rolex required daily winding. That changed in 1931, with the Perpetual rotor. Using a freely oscillating weight powered by the motion of the wrist, Rolex created a self-winding mechanism that continuously replenished the mainspring. It eliminated the need for manual winding, reducing wear on the crown and preserving the integrity of the waterproof case.

The Oyster case and Perpetual rotor transformed the wristwatch into something new: an object that not only endured, but maintained itself, and in the process formed the foundation for an entire ecosystem of timepieces.

The Rolex Explorer, launched in 1953, was born from it. Shaped by high-altitude expeditions, it prioritised reliability and legibility – essential in low-oxygen conditions, freezing temperatures and reduced visibility.

The Submariner (also 1953) extended these principles beneath the waves, combining water resistance – initially rated to 100 metres and later 200 metres – with a rotating bezel that enabled divers to track elapsed immersion time. Its unidirectional 60-minute bezel became a safety feature, preventing accidental adjustment during a dive. High-contrast luminous markers and hands ensured legibility in low-light conditions.

The 1950s also saw the launch of the Rolex GMT-Master. Designed for Pan American World Airways pilots, it displays two time zones simultaneously and has a 24-hour rotating bezel and dedicated GMT hand that transform the watch into a navigation tool.

The Rolex Day-Date was launched in 1956 exclusively in precious metals – gold or platinum – setting it apart from the brand’s tool watches. It would become associated with political and cultural figures, most notably a United States president, Lyndon B. Johnson.

Later came the Rolex Cosmograph Daytona (1963), which was designed for racing drivers, combining a tachymeter bezel for calculating average speed, a precision timing mechanism for lap measurement and high-legibility sub-dials for use under pressure. Named after the Daytona International Speedway in Florida, its identity was amplified by celebrities such as actor and racing-car driver Paul Newman.

Other brands noticed how the watches of the Oyster family had become companions built to travel, endure and participate in life, and rose to the challenge. Omega refined water-resistant sports models and professional timing tools; Blancpain defined early modern diving capability with the Fifty Fathoms collection; IWC developed robust, engineer-led pilot’s watches; and Jaeger-LeCoultre explored durable, utility-driven complications such as those of the Memovox series.

But it had been the ingenuity of the Oyster case, 100 years ago, that opened those doors for others to follow.

To mark the centenary, Rolex has introduced commemorative Oyster Perpetual models that remain faithful to the design’s restrained identity. A 41mm version pairs a slate dial with yellow-gold detailing while a 36mm model references classic dial textures with subtle anniversary markings. Discreet engravings, including a “100” crown detail, acknowledge the milestone without disrupting the Oyster’s defining understatement.

The celebration has extended beyond product launches. In Shanghai, Rolex presented the “Oyster Story”, an immersive exhibition at the West Bund Dome that ran from June 10 to 28. The exhibition traced the Oyster’s evolution through archival pieces, technical components and interactive installations that revealed the engineering behind the design.

One hundred years on, the Oyster’s innovations have become industry standards, absorbed into the very language of watchmaking.
