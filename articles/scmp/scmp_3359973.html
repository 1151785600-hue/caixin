Indonesia blocks 4.8 million underage social media accounts but are kids any safer?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1197 words | 2026-07-09

Indonesia’s attempt to keep children aged under 16 off social media platforms deemed high risk has quickly become one of the world’s biggest tests of online safety regulation, with more than 4.8 million suspected underage accounts removed or deactivated in the first three months of enforcement.

For President Prabowo Subianto’s government, the figures are an early sign that major platforms are falling in line with its policy aimed at making the digital world safer for children. Communications and Digital Affairs Minister Meutya Hafid called TikTok’s first reported removals in April “an early victory for the public, parents, children in Indonesia and us”.

But analysts and digital rights advocates say the real question is not how many accounts have disappeared, but whether those removals can be verified and if they have actually made children safer online.

By late June, the figures reported by platforms to Indonesia’s Ministry of Communication and Digital Affairs (Komdigi) included about 4.1 million suspected underage accounts on TikTok, 600,000 on YouTube and 185,000 on Meta’s Facebook, Instagram and Threads.

Yet the same numbers that have given the government early compliance headlines also expose the central weakness of the policy, critics say: enforcement depends heavily on platforms to identify underage users, report their own compliance and decide how age checks should work.

“Honestly, the 4.8 million figure looks like enforcement, but what it really shows is dependence,” said Ika Idris, a Monash University Indonesia public policy scholar who researches social media analytics and digital platform policy. “Right now, ‘enforcement’ means platforms grading their own homework and the regulator accepting the grade.”

Indonesia’s child online safety rules require digital services to assess the risks their products pose to children, verify users’ ages and apply safeguards, from parental supervision tools to protections against harmful content, risky contact, profiling and misuse of children’s data.

Non-compliant platforms can face written warnings, fines and, ultimately, access blocking. But the rules give companies significant discretion over how they assess risk and what technology they use for age verification.

Komdigi has said it is reviewing the self-assessments but critics say it is difficult to set in place an auditable system when much of the evidence comes from the companies being regulated.

More data needed

Nenden Sekar Arum, executive director of the Southeast Asia Freedom of Expression Network, said that while the figures showed platforms had started responding to their obligations, they should not be treated as proof that the policy had succeeded.

“Child protection should be measured by the reduction of risks children face, not simply by the number of accounts successfully closed,” she said.

Without more information, she added, the public could not know whether the accounts removed really belonged to underage users.

The risk is not hypothetical. In April, after TikTok reported deactivating 1.7 million underage accounts, Komdigi said some adult users had also been affected. Meutya said that those users could file an appeal with TikTok to restore their access.

In response to questions about users who may have been wrongly affected and the appeals process, TikTok explained the company respected Komdigi’s guidance and said that it was coordinating with the ministry on digital safety and literacy.

This Week in Asia has also approached Meta and YouTube for their response regarding their compliance with Indonesia’s child safety regulations.

Nenden said such issues were not surprising. “No age-verification technology is 100 per cent accurate.”

Age checks, she said, involved trade-offs between accuracy, privacy and inclusivity. Stricter verification may require more personal data – especially if platforms use identity checks, behavioural analysis or a combination of methods to estimate users’ ages.

The government has also acknowledged the challenge.

Deputy Communications and Digital Affairs Minister Nezar Patria on Sunday cited a survey saying that three in five children had falsified their ages to access social media. He said the practice posed a major obstacle because age verification was still carried out by individual digital platforms.

Danica Radovanovic, a senior researcher at the GESIS – Leibniz Institute for the Social Sciences whose recent work has examined child online protection in Indonesia, said the early account-removal figures were an “unstable indicator” of real-world safety because they primarily reduced visibility rather than harm.

“Young people do not disappear from digital life when mainstream platforms are restricted; connections relocate,” she said. Children could move to gaming ecosystems, private messaging and encrypted networks where moderation was weaker and risks are harder to identify.

Ika said Indonesia had moved into one of the most complex areas of digital regulation before building the institutions to supervise it. Though Indonesia passed a Personal Data Protection Law in 2022, the independent data protection authority required under the law has still not been established.

In comparison, Australia’s under-16 social media restrictions are overseen by the eSafety Commissioner, France’s approach to minors’ online consent and age verification has been shaped by data protection authority CNIL, and Britain’s Online Safety Act gives enforcement powers to Ofcom, the country’s communications regulator.

Privacy risks

In the absence of a regulator, the privacy risks were real, Ika said. Age verification was “essentially a data-processing exercise involving children’s data”.

TikTok’s Indonesian help pages, for example, say age appeals can require users to submit a government-issued ID and a selfie.

“Letting platforms choose methods is defensible in principle,” she said. “Doing it without minimum standards, audit rights or a data protection regulator means a public standard is effectively being outsourced to private discretion.”

The challenge is set to grow as enforcement expands. Meutya said in late April that the government would not stop with the eight platforms initially targeted under the regulations: X, Bigo Live, Facebook, Threads, Instagram, TikTok, YouTube and Roblox.

“In the digital realm, if we pay attention to or intervene in one or two, they will move to others,” she said.

Komdigi has since said about 200 digital platforms – spanning sectors such as e-commerce, gaming, entertainment, streaming and artificial intelligence – have submitted child safety self-assessments.

That wider scope could make Indonesia a regional test case for child online safety regulation, but also complicates enforcement.

Radovanovic said the lesson from Indonesia’s experience was that governments had to “regulate digital ecosystems, not only individual platforms”, because children’s digital lives include not just social media but also gaming, live streaming, messaging, payments and AI services.

That would require risk-based rules for different kinds of services, independent audits, trusted reporting systems, safer alternatives for young people and research into where children go when they are pushed off mainstream platforms, she said.

As the rules expand, takedown numbers alone will not be sufficient to measure compliance, according to Ika. Regulators will also need to assess how platforms verify age, handle appeals, protect children’s data and prove their safeguards work.

“Regulatory ambition scales much faster than enforcement capacity,” she said. “A ministry that can’t independently audit three social media platforms today is not going to audit dozens of services across five sectors tomorrow.”

For Radovanovic, the success of Indonesia’s policy should ultimately be measured “not by how many accounts disappear, but by whether children experience healthier, safer and more empowering digital environments”.
