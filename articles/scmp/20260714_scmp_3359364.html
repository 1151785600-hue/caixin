Are the US’ AI models better than China’s? That may be beside the point

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 909 words | 2026-07-14

As another heatwave rolled across Europe, the warehouses emptied before the politics could catch up. Air conditioners and fans sold out across Spain, Italy and Germany, most of them Chinese. Fan sales in Spain alone on the retail platform of Alibaba Group Holding (which owns the South China Morning Post) nearly doubled last month while Midea Group’s air-conditioner sales in western Europe surged by over 70 per cent in the first six months of the year.

A decade from now, such heatwaves may become ordinary. Air-conditioning units in the European Union are expected to more than double from 2019 levels to 275 million by 2050. The question will not be whether Europeans approve of air conditioning or whether their governments are comfortable depending on Chinese supply chains. It will be who can deliver affordable units fast enough when demand becomes desperate.

Increasingly, the answer is China – because its air conditioners are available, affordable and good enough exactly when it matters. The same logic may be reshaping something far less visible: artificial intelligence.

At first glance, Chinese AI looks like an extension of China’s manufacturing playbook: take a complex technology, compress the cost, iterate quickly, accept thinner margins and make it cheap enough for mass adoption. China did this with solar panels, batteries and electric vehicles. Now, through open-weight models, it appears to be doing something similar with AI.

The analogy works until it reaches the strategic bottleneck. In manufacturing, China’s advantage became durable because the supply chain stayed in China. Once built, it was brutally hard to relocate. Open-weight AI moves differently.

Once released, a model can live on someone else’s graphics processing units (GPUs), be fine-tuned with someone else’s data and create value inside someone else’s systems. The feedback loop shifts too: in manufacturing, the learning compounds inside the Chinese system. In open-weight AI, much of it accrues to whoever adapts the model.

The vulnerability has inverted. In manufacturing, China often owns the expensive node – polysilicon, wafers, batteries, components, factories, industrial scale. In AI, the expensive node is advanced compute: chips, data centres, electricity, training infrastructure. The United States is actively restricting this layer.

Those restrictions are not meaningless. Nvidia reported US$75.2 billion in data centre revenue for its last quarter, up 92 per cent year on year. Washington’s controls are aimed at a chokepoint, and appear to be doing their narrow job – making it harder for China to stay at the frontier. The logic is also spreading from chips to model access itself.

But the strategy may be too narrow. If China cannot fully contest the gated compute layer, the rational response is to attack the layer above it. By releasing capable, cheap, locally runnable models, Chinese companies can make model access less scarce. They may not capture the same economics as OpenAI or Anthropic, but they can make those economics harder for anyone else to capture.

This is commoditisation: turning the model from a gated product into a common input. The gap is narrowing – Stanford University found that US and Chinese models have traded the lead several times since early 2025, with Anthropic’s best ahead of the strongest Chinese model by just 2.7 per cent as of March. A study of 851,000 models and 2.2 billion downloads on Hugging Face found US open-weight dominance declining through 2025 as DeepSeek and Qwen rose.

The question is not whether Chinese models are the best. It is whether they are good enough for companies. Most companies are not building artificial general intelligence. They are answering email, reviewing contracts, writing code, automating workflows. The frontier gap still matters, but is increasingly weighed against cost, latency, privacy, customisation and control.

If frontier capability keeps compounding faster than ordinary tasks commoditise, American closed-model firms may preserve premium margins. But if capability is already saturated for many enterprise use cases, the buyer’s question changes – into which model is good enough, cheap enough, customisable enough and controllable enough at scale.

This is where export controls can work and backfire at once. They can keep China a step behind on frontier chips while pushing it towards a strategy that devalues the model layer American firms are trying to monetise – even as falling prices make intelligence harder to sell as a scarce, premium product.

For investors, that means a repricing. OpenAI and Anthropic are valued on the assumption that frontier access remains scarce, premium and defensible. The risk is not only that a Chinese laboratory captures their margin; it is that Chinese open weights make that margin disappear.

Yet giving away the model layer only creates lasting leverage if it builds lock-in somewhere else: developer tools, deployment pipelines, cloud ecosystems. If open models remain interchangeable, China may simply train the world to treat all models as commodities.

But even that would change the scoreboard. China does not need to win at the frontier if it can help redefine intelligence as cheap, open, local and abundant.

That is the lesson from Europe’s air conditioners. When a technology becomes necessary infrastructure, prestige gives way to cost, availability and control – the more essential it becomes, the more governments want sovereignty over it.

The difference is air conditioners are physical. They can be tariffed, inspected and blocked at a port. Model weights are harder to contain. Once they become a global default, the border arrives too late.

America controls the gate. China is trying to decide whether the gate still matters.
