Trump to restore Iran blockade, charge fees for Strait of Hormuz shipping

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1115 words | 2026-07-14

US President Donald Trump said on Monday the United States would begin charging commercial vessels for safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz as Washington reinstates its blockade of Iran following a renewed escalation in the conflict.

Trump said Iranian vessels and ships trading with Iran would be barred from using the strategic waterway, while other countries would retain access under a system that would impose a 20 per cent fee on eligible cargoes.

“We are reinstating the THE IRANIAN BLOCKADE, so named because it is only stopping Iran’s ships or customers from entering or leaving,” Trump said online. “All other countries will have fair and open use of the Strait.”

The US president said the charge would help pay for “any and all costs necessary to do the job of providing safety and security to this very volatile section of the World”.

As US forces struck Iran for a third consecutive night, Trump argued that the naval blockade had proven effective, particularly when paired with attacks on Iranian military infrastructure.

He also suggested Washington should be compensated for protecting Gulf allies and ensuring the flow of commerce through the region.

“We expect to be reimbursed because we are protecting a very rich part of the world ... Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, they do very well,” Trump said on Monday afternoon.

“We are doing it to protect allies. We are doing it to protect them.”

Trump's proposal prompted questions about whether Washington could legally impose fees on vessels using one of the world’s most important international waterways.

The International Maritime Organization said it was seeking further details on Trump’s announcement and reiterated its long-standing opposition to mandatory charges for passage through international straits.

“IMO stands firmly against charging fees for passage through straits used for international navigation,” a spokesperson for the UN shipping agency said.

“There is no legal basis through which to introduce mandatory tolls simply to transit through a strait.”

International maritime law experts said, however, that Trump’s proposal could instead be interpreted as an optional charge for naval protection services rather than a fee for transit itself.

“This is a fee for services rendered (protection), not a fee for transit,” said James Kraska, chair of international maritime law at the US Naval War College and a visiting professor at Harvard Law School.

“The regime of transit passage would remain unaffected,” he said, adding that vessels would not be required to pay for passage through the strait but could choose to pay for American naval protection while transiting the waterway.

Kraska compared the proposal to anti-piracy arrangements used in waters off Somalia, where commercial vessels have at times hired private security companies or government protection detachments to safeguard voyages through high-risk areas.

Elisabeth Braw, a senior fellow at the Atlantic Council, said shipping companies would likely be reluctant to accept any arrangement that could be interpreted as a charge for access to international waters.

“If a country, in this case the United States, starts charging tolls for access to a strait, then other countries can say, ‘Well, in our strait, we will start charging tolls too,’” she said, warning that the move could undermine the principle of freedom of navigation that has governed global shipping for decades.

Braw argued that comparisons with anti-piracy measures off Somalia were misplaced because shipping companies pay private security providers or armed guards rather than governments for access to international waters.

Karen Young, a senior research scholar at Columbia University’s Centre on Global Energy Policy, said she viewed Trump’s proposal less as a concrete policy than as a negotiating tactic or warning directed at Tehran.

But she said the announcement risked further eroding confidence in the US security role in the Gulf.

“It lowers the already limited sentiment of trust between Iran and the US and will make regional states feel even more insecure about the US role and commitment in the region,” Young said.

Oil markets reacted sharply to the latest escalation, with Brent crude jumping 9.6 per cent and WTI rising 9.4 per cent on Monday as traders assessed the implications for energy supplies.

Young cautioned that markets may still be underestimating the risks to global energy supplies because disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz are coinciding with continued security concerns in the Red Sea shipping corridor.

“We are moving into another period of low traffic and threatened security in the Strait of Hormuz and this time the Red Sea corridor is under threat at the same time,” she said.

The latest escalation followed an Iranian attack on a container vessel in the Strait of Hormuz on Sunday, an incident that heightened concerns over shipping security and the stability of global energy markets.

The US military will begin enforcing the blockade from 2000 GMT on Tuesday, according to an advisory issued by the US Navy-led Joint Maritime Information Center.

The notice said the blockade would cover Iranian ports, oil terminals and coastal areas and apply to all vessel traffic regardless of flag.

“Any vessel suspected of entering or departing the blockaded area without authorisation is subject to interception, diversion, and capture,” the advisory said, adding that non-compliant vessels could be compelled “with force”.

The centre said, however, that neutral transit through the Strait of Hormuz to and from non-Iranian destinations would continue uninterrupted.

US Central Command (CENTCOM) said on Monday that American forces had struck a submarine and ship maintenance facility at Iran’s Bandar Abbas naval base using unmanned surface vessels, describing it as the first combat use of sea drones by US forces.

CENTCOM said the attack was intended to degrade Iran’s ability to continue targeting commercial shipping in the region.

Before the conflict began, about one-fifth of global oil and gas supplies passed through the narrow waterway linking the Gulf to international markets.

Iran has exercised effective control over the strait since the outbreak of hostilities, turning it into one of Tehran’s most important sources of leverage in its confrontation with Washington.

The prospect of new costs for transiting Hormuz could carry particular significance for Asian economies, including China, Japan and South Korea, which rely heavily on energy imports shipped through the waterway.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi mocked Trump’s proposal on social media, arguing that Tehran rather than Washington had long ensured the security of shipping through the strait.

“Whoever provides secure and safe passage of commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz should be compensated for this service,” Araghchi wrote.

“Iran has always been the GUARDIAN of the Strait and will remain so FOREVER. Twenty per cent is of course too much. We will be fair.”
