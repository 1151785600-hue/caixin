‘China shock 3.0’ is coming. And it’ll be AI-powered robots

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 853 words | 2026-07-08

The world’s attention is fixed on frontier artificial intelligence (AI) models, but China’s robot-making factories deserve just as much attention. Chinese e-commerce company JD.com has predicted that robots would ultimately replace its 700,000 delivery workers, while workers at South Korean carmaker Hyundai are threatening strike action over issues including the roll-out of robots.

These are early signs of what could be China’s next export shock. The first “China shock” was from its low-cost manufactured goods. “China shock 2.0” centres on high-end manufacturing, from electric vehicles (EV) to batteries. The next shock could come from AI-powered industrial, humanoid and service robots.

In China, they are intended to offset a shrinking workforce, since the country’s working-age population is projected to fall from 1 billion at its peak to just 300 million by the end of the century. But abroad, robots are poised to become China’s next export machine.

Beijing wants 10,000 AI-powered robots operating in commercial settings this year alone. Deploying them is one thing; turning them into sustained productivity gains is another. Beijing won’t leave that transition to chance: its latest five-year plan stresses human-machine collaboration and the roll-out of robots across the economy.

Many people see AI models as the next battleground for economic leadership. But they may be looking at only half the picture. AI models matter but they do not create sufficient economic value on their own. That happens only when they are turned into products, deployed at scale and woven into the fabric of the economy.

So the front line of the AI race is shifting from the model to the ecosystem around it: the power, infrastructure, applications and adoption needed to put AI to work. The model is only one layer of a much larger stack, whose value comes from how the various parts are stitched together. Embodied AI – melding models with industrial and humanoid robots – will become the next frontier in the coming decade.

China has assembled three advantages that position it well for that shift. First, its manufacturing lead: it installs more industrial robots than the rest of the world, and produces some 90 per cent of the world’s humanoid robots, according to Morgan Stanley. Humanoid robots remain a small part of the market, however; industrial and service robots account for most deployments.

Still, China is the world’s biggest training ground for embodied AI – the second advantage – because it is the largest manufacturing economy on the planet. Unlike large language models, robots learn through repeated interaction with the physical world. And no country offers more factories and warehouses in which to train them. China has around 2 million industrial robots in operation, roughly 4.5 times the stock of second-placed Japan.

Third, many of China’s leading AI models are open-source, making them easier to modify and adapt to specific industrial tasks. Together, they give China an edge that will take years for other economies to replicate.

For these reasons, AI-powered robots could become China’s next export success, much as EVs and batteries have over the past decade. The world’s second-biggest economy has become its largest producer and exporter of EVs, accounting for nearly three-quarters of global production and about 40 per cent of exports, according to the International Energy Agency’s Global EV Outlook 2025.

But many Western and Japanese carmakers were slow to recognise the true source of China’s advantage: its manufacturing ecosystem. Subsidies played a role, but so did the factories, engineers, software and supply chains China was quietly building.

Only after losing market share did many global companies begin turning to China for technology, software and engineering expertise. By then, they had already fallen behind. The risk is that they may make the same mistake with embodied AI.

But it is not too late to respond. Much like in the EV industry, many companies will end up turning to Chinese companies for capabilities they didn’t build in time. The companies that remain competitive will be those that learn from China, not pretend it can be ignored.

That also changes how we should think about the AI race itself. It is widely portrayed as a battle between China and the United States. But that captures only part of the picture: the bigger divide will not be between countries, but between the companies that prepare their workforces for AI and robotics, and those that do not.

Yet embodied AI will not arrive overnight. Most robots in use today are industrial and service machines carrying out repetitive tasks, not the humanoids that capture the public imagination. Unlike large language models, robots have to deal with the messiness of the real world. Every movement depends on advances in sensors and motors, and every task has to be learned through repeated interaction with the physical environment.

But governments and businesses that continue to think of AI as software risk preparing for yesterday’s technology. AI’s greatest impact will not come from the text or code it writes on a screen, but from what it does in the physical world.
