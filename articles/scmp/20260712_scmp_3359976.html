In Malaysia’s Johor, Chinese voters prize affordability over politics

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1106 words | 2026-07-12

Koo can see Johor changing around him, even if the promised wealth has not reached his bank account.

The 30-year-old service worker has lived for 25 years in Skudai, a busy Johor Bahru suburb anchored by universities, older housing estates and the kind of Chinese shoplot economy that helped make the Democratic Action Party (DAP) a force in Malaysia’s southern state.

He sees cranes, rail works and projects built by Chinese companies. But before Saturday’s state election, and as the China-linked boom loses its political shine, the question nagging at him is more prosaic than geopolitics: whether Johor can still offer a decent living to Malaysians who do not want to chase the Singapore dollar just next door.

“My primary concern is skyrocketing housing prices and living costs,” Koo, who asked to be identified only by his surname, told This Week in Asia. “Parties need to prove that the economy in Johor can support those who work in Johor, instead of forcing them to go into Singapore to make a living.”

That unease cuts into one of the most closely watched fights in Johor’s election: the Chinese vote.

DAP, the Chinese-majority party in Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s Pakatan Harapan (PH) coalition, is trying to defend its urban base against Barisan Nasional’s (BN) Malaysian Chinese Association (MCA), BN’s long-time Chinese partner, which is leaning on its Johor business and state links to regain ground.

Mainland Chinese buyers and China-linked investment still carry political meaning in Johor, analysts say, especially in places shaped by the Iskandar development corridor. But among local Chinese voters, the argument has moved from questioning whether China money should come in, to whether locals can afford the boom it helps create.

Recent China-linked investments in Johor include TikTok parent ByteDance’s 1.5 billion ringgit (US$368 million) data centre expansion in Kulai and Tianjin-based Rianlon Corporation’s 1.27 billion ringgit research and development and manufacturing base.

Last year, Johor drew 110 billion ringgit in total approved investments.

Johor, home to just over 4 million people, is Malaysia’s southern gateway to Singapore. Bumiputera, Malaysia’s official category for Malays and indigenous groups, make up about 60 per cent of the population, with Chinese nearly 33 per cent and Indians 6.6 per cent. Its 56-seat election has drawn 172 candidates.

DAP Secretary General Anthony Loke told This Week in Asia on Monday that his party’s minimum target was to defend the 10 seats it won in 2022, even as it contests 17 this time.

No longer a China scare

Forest City, the Country Garden-backed mega development on reclaimed islands off Johor’s coast near Singapore, was once the perfect political villain: vast, China-funded and widely cast by critics as a luxury enclave built for outsiders rather than Malaysians.

In the 2018 general election, it fed the narrative that BN, under jailed former prime minister Najib Razak, had allowed too much foreign money to shape Malaysia’s future.

That anger has not disappeared, but it has lost its old potency. Forest City has fewer sharp edges now, while the Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ), data centres, manufacturing and logistics have made Chinese and Singapore-linked capital part of the growth pitch for both BN and PH.

Francis E. Hutchinson, principal fellow at the ISEAS – Yusof Ishak Institute, said mainland Chinese presence was “not politically salient” in this campaign because cost of living was dominating the race, while both the Johor government and Anwar’s federal administration supported new investment into sectors with some China participation.

Samuel Tan, founder of property consultancy Olive Tree, said Forest City “does not appear to be the electoral flashpoint it was in 2018”, when it became a lightning rod for attacks on Umno and BN over claims of selling out to foreigners.

Tan said the backlash then was sharper among Malay voters than their Chinese counterparts.

Among Chinese voters now, he said, the mood was more pragmatic, with cost of living, jobs and business opportunities weighing more heavily than party loyalty or old arguments over Forest City.

“Forest City is now more of a governance or economic-delivery symbol, tied to JS-SEZ credibility, than an ethnic wedge issue,” Tan said.

Forest City’s early buyer base, along with newer arrivals, tenants and investors from China, does not form an electoral bloc because most are not Malaysian voters. But their presence still colours how voters read Johor’s rapid transformation.

Peter T.C. Chang, former deputy director of Universiti Malaya’s Institute of China Studies, said recent mainland Chinese migrants mattered politically “more as a symbolic presence than as a direct electoral constituency”.

“They have become a proxy for wider debates about China-linked investment, housing affordability, local employment and Johor’s rapid transformation,” Chang said.

The Singapore-dollar trap

Voters are now weighing grand projects against their own household maths.

In its Johor election manifesto, PH has promised 250,000 high-paying jobs, a 30 per cent rise in median wages, 80,000 affordable homes and a Balik Johor programme to bring home professionals working in Singapore or abroad by 2031. BN countered with a plan to create 200,000 quality jobs and expand Johor’s economy to 260 billion ringgit by 2030.

Malaysia and Singapore last year signed the JS-SEZ agreement, targeting 50 projects and 20,000 skilled jobs in the first five years, along with smoother movement across one of the world’s busiest borders.

Loke, who is also transport minister, said the SEZ, RTS Link and border improvements depended heavily on federal ministries, making PH’s presence in Johor crucial.

“We have been losing a lot to Singapore,” he said, naming manufacturing, services, shipping and bunkering as sectors that Malaysia could have held more strongly.

“Instead of our people crossing the border every day, they can still work in Johor, but at the same time with better-paying jobs.”

MCA could still lean on its old image as the business-minded Chinese party in Johor. But Chang said mainland Chinese investors were a “double-edged sword” for the party, especially when projects such as Forest City were seen as helping foreign investors more than local communities.

Chin Yee Mun, director of Universiti Tunku Abdul Rahman’s Tun Tan Cheng Lock Institute of Social Studies, said Chinese support for DAP had become “more conditional”, shaped by high expectations after years of reform slogans and disappointment when delivery fell short.

“Main issues remain bread-and-butter concerns, governance and equal treatment,” Chin said.

Voters like Koo are still unconvinced. “Salary in Johor is definitely not as high as Kuala Lumpur,” he said, “but the living cost in Johor Bahru is as high as, if not higher than, KL.”
