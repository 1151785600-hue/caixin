EU isn’t just selling aircraft to China. It’s helping strengthen a competitor

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 785 words | 2026-07-14

Last month, China Eastern ordered 25 A330neo jets from Airbus, which come at a catalogue price of US$9.35 billion. The airline, which operates the inaugural commercial routes of the C919, China’s home-grown passenger jet, had placed another Airbus order just three months earlier.

Why is this major Chinese airline transferring billions to a European company China’s government is trying to supplant?

As a widebody jet, the A330neo competes in a segment where the Commercial Aircraft Corporation of China (Comac) currently has no product, so this is not a simple hedge against delays. These orders represent something more strategic: capital paid now for operational knowledge, trained personnel and supply-chain integration that China’s aviation sector will absorb over time.

This fits a clear pattern. Earlier this year, China’s Civil Aviation Administration (CAAC) reportedly withheld approvals for nearly 20 completed Airbus aircraft, an episode Airbus CEO Guillaume Faury described as an “administrative delay”. The hold-up contributed to the lowest first-quarter deliveries for Airbus since 2009 and left roughly €5 billion (US$5.7 billion) in completed aircraft undelivered. Analysts noted the obvious: the Airbus relationship has become leverage for Beijing’s pressure on the European Union.

Generosity one month, coercion the next. That is not inconsistency. It is leverage, applied through different instruments towards the same long-term goal.

The relationship that makes this possible was built over two decades. It deepened last October when Airbus opened its second A320 family assembly line in Tianjin, its 10th final assembly line worldwide. This came just nine days after a similar ceremony in Mobile, Alabama, a sequence seen as intended to balance US-China trade tensions. Since 2008, the Airbus operation in Tianjin has assembled more than 780 A320 aircraft. Last year, Airbus held 55 per cent of China’s commercial aircraft market, with most of its Tianjin production going to Chinese airlines.

Boeing sells to China. Airbus builds with China. Completing a commercial aircraft involves more than manufacturing. In May, Sichuan Service Aeroengine Maintenance, Air China’s joint venture with Franco-American engine-maker CFM International, became the first facility in China authorised to maintain the C919’s LEAP-1C engines, extending technology transfer into the aircraft’s operational life.

Last year, Washington briefly suspended licences for exports related to the LEAP-1C engine, which is co-produced by Safran and GE Aerospace through CFM. The exports were restored after a few weeks under pressure, including widely assumed French objections to the hit on Safran’s revenue. What Washington intended as coercion became a demonstration of its limits. The joint venture’s commercial structure did the rest.

China’s 15th five-year plan designated aircraft as a strategic priority for supply-chain self-sufficiency. Globalised supply chains were “no longer suitable” for China’s aviation industry, declared aviation expert Zhang Yanzhong. It’s clear that technology transfer is a key objective.

None of this means Airbus acted irrationally. China is expected to need about 9,500 new aircraft over the next two decades, around one-fifth of global demand. Lower production costs and insulation from Washington’s trade pressure made deeper investment commercially sensible.

But commercially sensible decisions don’t amount to a European strategy. No institution stepped back to ask what those choices would collectively produce. When Beijing apparently tested that gap this spring by withholding approval for deliveries seemingly to pressure Europe’s aviation regulator over C919 certification, no coordinated response followed.

The European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) faces a structural dilemma. Certifying the C919 helps legitimise a direct competitor to an aircraft whose production supports tens of thousands of European jobs. Refusing to certify it, however, risks accusations of protectionism and could weaken EASA’s credibility as a world-leading aviation regulator. EASA executive director Florian Guillermet has said that C919 certification is unlikely before 2028 and may take till 2031.

Meanwhile, Brunei and Vietnam have adopted CAAC’s airworthiness standards. A regulator’s influence depends on how many countries choose to follow its rules. If more markets accept CAAC standards, EASA’s eventual decision will matter less. China will no longer need European approval for markets it serves on its own terms. Europe risks losing not the certification decision itself, but the influence that once made that decision important.

In February, at the World Government Summit in Dubai, Faury acknowledged that Comac was no longer a distant rival. China Eastern’s latest multibillion Airbus order is not a contradiction of China’s earlier coercion. It is the other side of the same strategy. Each Airbus decision, made without a broader European strategy, has produced an outcome no government in Brussels intended. Europe is no longer just selling aircraft. It is helping to strengthen a long-term competitor.
