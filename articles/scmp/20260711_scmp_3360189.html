The loudest voices in US-China trade talks? Christmas tree growers

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 903 words | 2026-07-11

It’s not yet the winter holiday season, but Christmas trees are back in the spotlight, caught in the geopolitical tug of war between Washington and Beijing.

As the United States and China work to stabilise ties through a newly proposed bilateral trade mechanism, one of the most vocal groups weighing in is not the technology industry or manufacturers, but American Christmas tree growers determined to keep tariffs on Chinese-made artificial trees.

Dozens of growers from across the country flooded the Office of the US Trade Representative’s public comment portal, urging officials not to classify artificial Christmas trees as “non-sensitive” products eligible for lower tariffs.

“Lowering the additional tariffs that currently apply to Chinese artificial trees would re-expose tens of thousands of family farms to the below-cost import pricing that has eroded their market for two decades,” said Scott Powell, president of the National Christmas Tree Association and a second-generation farmer in Michigan.

The dispute stems from the proposed “Board of Trade”, a key outcome of the May summit between US President Donald Trump and Chinese President Xi Jinping in Beijing.

Under the proposal, the two countries would establish a joint mechanism to oversee bilateral trade with US$30 billion worth of “non-sensitive” goods that could receive reduced or even zero tariffs.

To begin that process, the USTR invited public comment from businesses, trade groups and consumers.

By Friday morning, the USTR had received roughly 280 submissions from businesses across a wide range of industries seeking lower tariffs on products including clothing, kitchenware, furniture, toys, snacks and other consumer goods.

But Christmas tree growers stood out. About a quarter of the comments came from them seeking to keep duties on artificial Christmas trees imported from China.

“For most people, selecting and decorating a fresh, farm-grown Christmas tree each year makes it look, smell and feel like Christmas,” Powell told the South China Morning Post.

“Certainly more so than pulling a green plastic tree-shaped decoration out of a box from storage does.”

Powell said the association alerted its roughly 300 members nationwide, along with others in the Christmas tree industry, encouraging them to submit comments to the USTR.

A niche market where Chinese imports dominate

In the association’s filing, Powell argued that the US industry has steadily declined over the past two decades, with both Christmas tree businesses and farm acreage shrinking.

He also warned that about 87 to 90 per cent of artificial Christmas trees imported into the United States come from China, creating “a near-total dependence on a single adversary supplier for a culturally significant consumer good”.

Other comments opposing lower tariffs came from growers across the country, including Florida, Connecticut, North Carolina and Idaho.

The growers’ campaign was partly prompted by testimony at a USTR hearing in May from an importer and retailer of artificial Christmas trees.

Nathaniel Roland, an executive and general counsel at Balsam Hill, a major importer of artificial Christmas trees, argued that the products should qualify for lower tariffs because they are not sensitive or related to national security.

“Let me be clear on who bears the cost when tariff policy gets this wrong,” Roland told the USTR. “Christmas trees are not strategic products like microchips or critical pharmaceuticals.”

Roland said about 85 million American households display a Christmas tree each year, and roughly four out of five choose an artificial one.

He added that his suppliers are mostly family-owned manufacturers located in China, Indonesia and Cambodia.

Artificial Christmas trees were historically not subject to tariffs, but were swept up in last year’s blanket tariff increases, which importers say have hurt their business.

Other public comments supporting tariff reductions argued that artificial and natural Christmas trees serve different markets and consumer preferences.

But some tree growers countered that the products compete directly and that, because a real Christmas tree takes four to nine years to grow, farmers cannot quickly recover if there’s a price war.

The market prices for both types of trees vary widely. A fresh Christmas tree can cost as little as about US$60 for a smaller tree, while premium trees over 10 feet tall – requiring up to a decade to grow – can sell for several hundred dollars. Artificial trees also range widely in price, from around US$20 for basic models to over US$1,000 for larger, pre-lit versions.

Siva Yam, president of the Chicago-based US-China Chamber of Commerce, said the Christmas tree market is relatively small compared with major agricultural exports such as soybeans and meat.

“It is not surprising that they are making a lot of noise,” Yam said. “I just don’t know what the real impact will be.”

He argued that the proposed Board of Trade is more likely to help American farmers gain greater access to the Chinese market with lower tariffs, making it somewhat ironic that Christmas tree growers are pushing for higher tariffs on Chinese imports.

The public comment period will close after Friday, at which time the USTR will accept rebuttal comments before deciding how the proposed Board of Trade should be implemented.

For Powell, however, the debate is about more than politics. He argues that real Christmas trees are a more environmentally friendly choice than “fake, plastic trees” and offer something less tangible that endures beyond the holiday season.

“They create joy in the moment, traditions over the years, and memories that last a lifetime,” Powell said.
