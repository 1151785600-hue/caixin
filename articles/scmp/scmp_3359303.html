3 tracks are shaping the Middle East’s post-Iran war order

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 925 words | 2026-07-08

The Iran battlefield might be quieter and diplomacy might have resumed, but the main contest has shifted to the architecture of the Middle East. The question is no longer whether Iran can be contained, the Strait of Hormuz can remain open or another round of escalation can be avoided. The larger issue is who will write the rules after the shock.

Three tracks are taking shape. The first is led by the United States and built around the expansion of the Abraham Accords, the integration of Israel into the regional order and the external restraint of Iran. The second is a regional autonomy track centred on the quartet of Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Turkey and Pakistan. The third is a China-supported de-escalation track based in Saudi-Iranian rapprochement.

They overlap more than they might appear. All three want to prevent a wider war, keep the strait open and protect energy markets, maritime insurance and supply chains. All three see value in a US-Iran understanding, even if they disagree over who should interpret and enforce it. Nevertheless, their divergence lies deeper: where Iran should sit in the regional order, how far Israel can be normalised, whether Palestine remains central and who provides security when the crisis returns.

The US track remains the strongest in terms of hard security. Washington has military presence, sanctions architecture, intelligence networks and defence relationships that no other external actor can match. Its preferred regional design links a managed Iran file to renewed Gulf security commitments and a wider Abraham Accords framework.

Israel would become a more accepted regional security and economic actor. Iran would be constrained through inspections, reversible sanctions relief, pressure on missiles, drones and proxies, and freedom of navigation in the Hormuz Strait.

This track has advantages. Gulf states still need US air defence, intelligence and military reassurance. Israel and several Arab governments share concerns about Iran’s nuclear infrastructure and regional networks. Economic normalisation, technology cooperation and infrastructure corridors remain attractive.

However, the US track faces the hardest legitimacy problem. Saudi Arabia will not join without security guarantees, advanced weapons, nuclear cooperation and a credible Palestinian component. Israel’s military behaviour also complicates Washington’s strategy. The more Israel expands its military action, the harder it becomes for Arab and Muslim states to embrace it publicly.

The second track reflects a different impulse. Having had four foreign ministers’ meetings, Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Turkey and Pakistan want a regional caucus that can prevent the Middle East’s future from being settled only by Washington, Tehran, Tel Aviv or Beijing.

Saudi Arabia brings Gulf weight, finance and Islamic legitimacy. Egypt brings Arab political depth, the Suez Canal and Gaza diplomacy. Turkey brings military industry, regional reach and a Nato connection. Pakistan brings military depth, nuclear status, Saudi security ties and channels to Iran, China and the US.

This track is still fragile. It has no secretariat, joint command, treaty or enforcement mechanism. Its value lies in controlled ambiguity. The quartet can support US-Iran talks without becoming a US instrument. It can warn Iran without forming an anti-Iranian bloc. It can keep Palestine central without closing the door to wider security coordination.

The third track flows from the Saudi-Iranian rapprochement brokered by Beijing. Its logic is inclusion under restraint. China does not offer the Gulf air cover, missile defence, naval dominance or formal security guarantees. Its contribution is diplomatic. It gives Saudi Arabia and Iran a platform to reduce hostility, preserve channels and extend dialogue to Yemen, energy infrastructure and crisis communication.

This track serves several interests. Saudi Arabia gains a way to reduce risks to its energy facilities and Vision 2030. Iran gains a route away from isolation. China protects energy security and maritime stability. Pakistan, Oman and Qatar can fit into this layer as mediators and functional nodes.

That said, its weakness is clear. China cannot force Iran to restrain its proxies, accept intrusive inspections or abandon coercive behaviour in the Strait of Hormuz. It cannot restrain Israel or replace the US security umbrella.

The three tracks discipline the region in different ways. The US can punish Iran materially through sanctions, force and financial pressure. The quartet can deny legitimacy by refusing to endorse arrangements that ignore regional concerns or Palestine. China can narrow Iran’s path back to diplomatic respectability if Tehran undermines the de-escalation that gives it room to manoeuvre.

Saudi Arabia is the central swing actor. The US track offers protection but also seeks Saudi-Israeli normalisation and closer Saudi participation in a US-led regional security framework. The quartet offers leadership but requires regional credibility. The China track offers autonomy and a channel to Iran but cannot protect Saudi airspace. Riyadh is likely to try to use all three.

For the US, power no longer converts automatically into order. Washington remains indispensable, but it must work through regional ownership and cannot ignore the alternatives opened by China. For Beijing, influence through mediation creates expectations. It can gain credit for lowering tensions, but its credibility will suffer if Iran takes the benefits of reintegration while continuing destabilising behaviour.

The most likely outcome is an uneasy hybrid. The question is whether the three tracks can discipline one another: keeping Iran constrained but not cornered, Israel recognised but not unconstrained, Gulf states reassured but not subordinated and regional powers engaged rather than bypassed.

If they can, the Middle East can move towards a grey but manageable order. If they cannot, the moment will harden into another cycle of blocs, chokepoints and proxy war.
