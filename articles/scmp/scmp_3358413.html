Talking when you eat is bad for you, and other Chinese beliefs

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 910 words | 2026-07-08

“What did your family talk about at the dinner table?”

Snug under the Tuscan sun, at a writing retreat on a permaculture farm outside Florence, I was ready to mine my fondest food memories. For a moment, scenes from films and television flashed across my mind: a montage of vivid dinner conversations and emotional check-ins, stitched together from various coming-of-age stories.

The only problem was that I could not claim any of those vignettes as my own. I squinted and dug deeper, into countless meals with my family. It seemed our focus never shifted far from the meal itself. We cared too much about the fundamental function of the meal – nutrition – to ask one another about our day.

“When eating, he did not converse. When in bed, he did not speak.” The Analects of Confucius, advising silence in aid of digestion and sleep, may well have anticipated our table manners. Although the Chinese philosopher was never directly quoted at home, the logic feels familiar.

In my family, drinks were also absent from the dinner table, even something as benign as warm water. As a child, I assumed my parents set out to discourage sugary beverages but despotically banned all other drinks too. They insisted that too much liquid interfered with digestion.

Only later would I discover that traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) shares the same suspicion. In TCM, digestion depends on the yang vital energy of the spleen and stomach, a kind of metabolic warmth that can be dampened by excess liquid. The body is understood to be a delicate system whose balance should not be disturbed unnecessarily.

But beyond Confucian restraint or traditional medicine, my family’s most urgent rule was far more practical: “Don’t talk when you are eating fish.”

Freshwater fish was a staple of my childhood, but most of the species we ate seemed to have evolved specifically to resist human consumption, by arming themselves with a vicious number of tiny bones.

Eating them was an acquired skill. My father gave up eating most whole fish dishes, preferring deep-fried versions with the bones rendered crunchy and harmless. Unlike my father, I took pride in my ability to deftly separate flesh from bone. Perhaps as a side effect, I was never especially vocal at the dinner table. I was too busy eating fish. The same caution applied to most poultry dishes prepared at home. Beware of the bones. Stay alert. Eat carefully.

For all our obsession with health and safety, we cared just as much about taste. One expression my family often invoked was yi re san xian: roughly, eating food hot unlocks all three dimensions of xian, an elusive but wildly pleasurable combination of tenderness, crunch and sweet freshness.

The British food writer Fuchsia Dunlop, trained in Sichuan cuisine, compares xian to umami while arguing that the Chinese concept boasts its own cultural lineage. Long before the Japanese chemist Kikunae Ikeda named umami in 1908, Chinese cooks had already spent centuries obsessing over the very sensory experience.

My mother promulgated a punchier philosophy: “Eat while it’s hot!” Who would want to miss that narrow peak window of enjoying food at its best? This may have been the origin of my own fixation with the temperature of food. (Yes, I microwave plates before serving pastries, so they stay warm and flaky for eternity.)

That is not to say our dinner table was shrouded in silence. When we did speak, it was rarely about ourselves and more often about the dishes in front of us. We discussed the provenance of the produce: vegetables gifted by relatives who grew their own, chickens raised by family friends, fish caught in lakes nearby, or my mother’s latest order from a farmer in Yunnan.

The cook narrated the meal practically: where something came from, when it was in season, why this batch tasted sweeter than the last. As I write this now, lotus shoots, harvested before they mature into lotus root, are briefly available. It is through these comments that knowledge travels in my family.

The cooks in my family constantly refreshed their recipes after comparing notes with friends and trading health advice and cooking techniques during post-dinner walks. But they depended on the diners for feedback: was it too salty, oily or bland?

We didn’t ask about our feelings, only about the food. Having our doubts assuaged on either matter amounted to the same. And the ensuing effort to cater to our individual tastes offered as much solace as a string of thoughtful questions would.

Still, perhaps influenced by Western expectations of emotional openness, I have tried to initiate more small talk at the dinner table. My family seems more willing to indulge me as the meal ends and we clear the table for the fruit platter du jour.

My father asks about my precarious work arrangements and offers his views on the global economy. I often suppress the urge to dismiss his observations as platitudes and reinterpret them as concern translated into the language he knows best.

Part of this shift – this slow assertion of my preferences at dinner – comes with age, I suspect. But that is another story.

For now, I am more than happy with the low hum of chewing that still dominates our meals, with steam rising from dishes meant to be eaten immediately. Our silence is shaped less by emotional withholding than by vigilance about balance, bones and temperature.
