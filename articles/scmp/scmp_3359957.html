South Korea set to power US fight for naval dominance amid rising geopolitical tensions

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 989 words | 2026-07-09

The United States is turning to South Korea’s world-leading shipbuilding industry as it seeks to refresh its ageing naval fleet, a move set to expand the bilateral alliance beyond security into defence manufacturing, technology and industrial production.

According to analysts, the bid reflects Washington’s efforts to address long-standing problems in its own industry – such as chronic construction delays, cost overruns, shortages of skilled workers and supply chain constraints. It also marks a strategy to tap trusted allies to narrow naval capability gaps amid rising maritime geopolitical tensions worldwide.

“South Korea is widely regarded as one of the most practical partners for such cooperation,” said Doo Jin-ho, director of the Eurasia Research Centre at the Korea Research Institute for National Strategy.

At the G7 summit last month, US President Donald Trump reportedly asked his South Korean counterpart, Lee Jae Myung, if Seoul could rapidly build 10 warships for the US. Lee’s office said both leaders held follow-up discussions on the sidelines of the Nato summit this week.

Washington’s interest in South Korean shipyards went beyond cost reduction bids or a one-time procurement contract, Doo said. “It reflects Washington’s effort to address persistent problems in the US shipbuilding industry.”

He said Seoul was using this opportunity to position itself not merely as a defence supplier but as a player capable of helping the US expand its naval power. “As the second Trump administration reassesses traditional transatlantic alliances, South Korea’s defence industry could emerge as a strategically attractive partner.”

Indo-Pacific slant

More broadly, the initiative could signal Washington’s intention to build a new maritime security ecosystem with Indo-Pacific allies rather than relying primarily on traditional Atlantic partners, Doo said.

“If so, the project could mark an important turning point in the US-South Korea alliance, transforming it from a conventional security partnership into a comprehensive strategic alliance encompassing defence, industrial cooperation, advanced technology and military production.”

Participation in US naval programmes would also give South Korean shipbuilders first-hand experience with American standards, technologies and operational requirements, strengthening their competitiveness in the global naval market and future multinational programmes, he said.

However, he warned that significant hurdles remained, including approval by the US Congress, possible resistance from the American shipbuilding industry and labour unions, and China’s likely strategic response.

“Even so, the discussions are expected to become an important test of whether the US and its allies can reshape the Indo-Pacific maritime order and build a more resilient, alliance-based defence industrial supply chain,” he said.

In a related development, Washington has begun assessing Seoul’s shipbuilding capabilities – a potential breakthrough for South Korea’s defence industry, which recently suffered a setback after losing Canada’s patrol submarine programme to Germany.

The US Department of Defence recently sent Requests for Information (RFIs) on combat ships to HD Hyundai Heavy Industries and Hanwha Ocean.

Separately, the US Navy issued RFIs for fleet replenishment oilers to HD Hyundai Heavy Industries, Hanwha Ocean and Samsung Heavy Industries. The three companies said they submitted responses last month detailing their track records, engineering expertise, workforce and production capacity.

RFIs are used to gather information on pricing, production capacity and delivery schedules before a formal acquisition programme begins.

Riding the wave

The requests mark the first time the US government has formally sought detailed information from South Korean shipbuilders since Seoul and Washington agreed last year to pursue the Make American Shipbuilding Great Again (MASGA) initiative.

Construction of combat ships and replenishment oilers is widely believed to be included in the broader US$350 billion investment package agreed between Seoul and Washington, under which US$150 billion is earmarked for shipbuilding cooperation.

The Pentagon is reportedly particularly interested in the South Korean navy’s newest frontline surface combatant, the Chungnam-class, or Ulsan-class Batch III, frigate.

“This is more than a trial balloon from the US side because Washington has few realistic alternatives capable of rapidly expanding its naval shipbuilding capacity,” said Cho Han-bum, a senior researcher at the Korea Institute for National Unification.

South Korean companies are already expanding into the US naval maintenance, repair and overhaul market. Hanwha Ocean, which acquired Philly Shipyard in Pennsylvania, is seeking licences to build combat vessels in the US. HD Hyundai has partnered Huntington Ingalls Industries, while Samsung Heavy Industries is cooperating with General Dynamics NASSCO.

Although the US retained a technological edge, it was increasingly falling behind China in fleet size, Cho said, because Beijing benefited from the world’s largest commercial shipbuilding industry. Meanwhile, the US commercial shipbuilding sector has shrunk dramatically, raising concerns about national security and supply chain resilience.

According to a 2024 report by the Korea Trade-Investment Promotion Agency, the US plans to expand its naval fleet from 296 vessels in 2024 to 381 by 2054, requiring an estimated US$1.075 trillion in investment.

Current American law generally prohibits the construction of US naval vessels in foreign shipyards, although the Trump administration is reportedly considering changes through executive action.

Retired navy admiral Yun Gong-yong said the US was rebuilding its shipbuilding industry as part of a broader strategy to counter China’s growing maritime influence.

“With its world-class shipbuilding technology and strong cost competitiveness, South Korea is well positioned to become a key partner in meeting this demand,” he said. “South Korea stands to gain significant economic benefits by securing a long-term presence in the US shipbuilding market.”

South Korea has assembled a wide range of advanced naval platforms, including patrol vessels, frigates, destroyers, amphibious assault ships, Aegis destroyers and diesel-electric submarines.

“The cost and time required to build a US Arleigh Burke-class Aegis destroyer are currently about two to three times higher than in South Korea,” Yun said.

In comparison, South Korea built its King Jeongjo-class Aegis destroyers for about 1.3 trillion won (US$865 million), completing the first ship in three years and the second in just two years.
