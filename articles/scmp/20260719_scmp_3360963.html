Malaysia’s Network School probe raises questions over openness to tech talent

SCMP | FREE | 1015 words | 2026-07-19

A controversy in Malaysia over a foreign tech community in Johor state has raised questions on whether immigration scrutiny and political sensitivities could slow the country’s push to attract digital nomads and other global talent.

Johor authorities are investigating Network School, a private co-living and co-working community based in Forest City and founded by American tech investor and former Coinbase chief technology officer Balaji Srinivasan, following online allegations that Israelis were involved in the project and had entered Malaysia using secondary passports.

For 38-year-old Malaysian digital nomad Harinder Singh, however, the episode is unlikely to deter global remote workers from considering living and working in Malaysia. What matters is whether the country is affordable, connected and transparent about its rules, according to Harinder.

“Digital nomads don’t really make decisions based on news cycles like this,” said Harinder, who has worked remotely for more than a decade between cities in Malaysia and elsewhere in Southeast Asia.

“Incidents like this get attention, but the real decision factors are visa clarity, cost of living, internet reliability and community.”

Malaysia does not have diplomatic relations with Israel and generally prohibits Israeli passport holders from entering the country without special approval.

The country has spent the last three years attracting remote workers through its DE Rantau Nomad Pass, competing with destinations including Thailand, Indonesia and Vietnam.

As of December 2024, the Malaysia Digital Economy Corporation (MDEC) had approved 2,344 DE Rantau applications, allowing visitors to stay between three and 12 months, with an option to renew for an additional year.

The investigation has raised questions about whether the investigation could prompt tighter scrutiny of foreign tech communities based in Malaysia and undermine confidence in the country as a viable destination for global workers and investors.

Srinivasan said the controversy had disrupted Network School’s plans in Malaysia and that further investment was suspended pending assurances from the government.

“At this point, all further investment we were planning to make in Malaysia is on hold until we get sufficient assurance that such issues won’t recur,” he said in a video posted on social media on Thursday.

Srinivasan said false claims of harbouring illegal aliens by an anonymous social media account had prompted a visit by authorities days later, during which hundreds of passports were checked, including those belonging to dual passport holders.

He added that immigration officials were polite and professional, but added that “the process is the punishment”.

The Immigration Department inspected 266 foreigners from 40 countries at the site and found that all held valid travel documents. It said further checks were ongoing into any misuse of identities, passports, immigration facilities or permit conditions.

Network School claimed it had invested more than US$25 million in its Forest City campus and was preparing further expansion worth more than US$123 million.

Srinivasan said Forest City was chosen for its proximity to Singapore, inexpensive space and Malaysia’s policies aimed at attracting international technology workers and investors.

He called for a meeting with Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s office and proposed a memorandum of understanding to commit Network School to Malaysian law and sovereignty.

“If Malaysia still wants continued global tech investment, if it wants to be a top 20 tech hub, if it wants us to revitalise Forest City, then we request an audience with the Prime Minister’s office,” he said, warning that Network School could redirect its capital elsewhere if confidence were not restored.

Johor has benefited from a data centre investment boom, as the state aims to draw more cross-border capital and technology activity.

Farlina Said, director of cyber and technology policy at the Institute of Strategic and International Studies Malaysia, said the controversy could lead to stricter scrutiny of dual nationals and immigration documents. Such enforcement would require clearer policies, greater resources and stronger cross-border co-ordination, she said.

Restrictions on technology communities could undermine exchanges of knowledge and talent, Farlina warned. “An overcorrection that does not acknowledge the overall role of community in innovation could be short-sighted.”

Johor’s Chief Minister Onn Hafiz Ghazi said in a statement on Tuesday that the state would not allow any party to be utilised as a base for an ideology or movement in contravention of Malaysian law.

“The investigation must cover the nationality of those involved, travel documents, as well as possible usage of second passports and passes used to enter the country, while ensuring the purpose of the visit aligns with the activities conducted,” Onn said.

Anwar said at a news conference on Wednesday that Malaysia would not allow any Israeli nationals found to have entered and stayed in the country.

“If there are Israeli nationals, since we do not recognise Israel, they will be deported immediately.”

The Home Ministry has said it takes seriously any matter involving national security, compliance with immigration laws and abuse of Malaysia’s immigration facilities, and is conducting a comprehensive review and investigation.

Country Garden Pacificview, the developer of Forest City, said it had zero tolerance for any breach of law and would cooperate fully with authorities while enforcing its legal and contractual rights in the event of any breaches.

For Harinder, the controversy has not affected Malaysia’s attraction as a base for global tech professionals.

“I don’t think this moves the needle for someone weighing their options next quarter,” he said.

A greater challenge for local authorities was ensuring that remote workers understood how long they were permitted to stay in Malaysia and the activities they could undertake legally, he added.

“The biggest deterrent isn’t one headline,” Harinder said. “It’s whether the rules are clear and whether the place still works for the life you’re trying to build.”
