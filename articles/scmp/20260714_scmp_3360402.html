Myanmar edges out of isolation as Asean steps up engagement

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 964 words | 2026-07-14

Myanmar’s gradual return from the diplomatic deep freeze continued over the weekend as Southeast Asian foreign ministers met their counterpart from the junta-run nation in Bangkok, a re-engagement that analysts warn lends legitimacy to a government still at war with its own people, without any commitment to end the violence.

Thousands have been killed in the nationwide civil conflict resulting from the military’s 2021 coup, a power grab that prompted Asean to take the unprecedented step of barring the junta’s representatives from regional summits after it ignored a five-point peace plan.

Senior General Min Aung Hlaing has since traded his fatigues for civilian dress, becoming president following an election widely decried as a sham as it offered no genuine challenge to the army’s proxy parties.

Even so, years after the Association of Southeast Asian Nations froze Myanmar out, the election appears to have opened a pathway back into a bloc that, by convention, is loath to interfere in members’ domestic affairs.

On Sunday, Myanmar’s foreign minister Tin Maung Swe met several of Southeast Asia’s senior diplomats in Bangkok to discuss a renewed peace initiative and the welfare of former leader Aung San Suu Kyi.

The meeting had the backing of host nation Thailand, which alongside current Asean chair the Philippines has led efforts to bring Myanmar back into the fold.

Thailand’s top diplomat, Sihasak Phuangketkeow, described the invitation as “calibrated engagement” designed to revive the stalled five-point consensus and put Myanmar on a path towards peace.

He said Asean held “concrete expectations” around humanitarian access and a broader release of prisoners.

“Let’s hope we will see some response and some progress in the next few months,” said Singapore’s Vivian Balakrishnan, adding that the bloc “wants to engage across the board, widely and in an inclusive manner” – a reference to pro-democracy rebels and the patchwork of ethnic armed organisations also fighting the military.

Member states also called for the release of political detainees, including 81-year-old Aung San Suu Kyi.

Activists said any move towards ending the bloodshed should be welcomed, but warned against embracing the military before it halted its campaign against civilians.

“Asean must find and enforce solutions to stop the junta’s bombing and deliver aid to conflict-affected populations,” said Khin Ohmar, a Myanmar human rights expert and founder of the organisation Progressive Voice.

“Even informal meetings such as the one held in Bangkok pose further risk of Asean lending political legitimacy to the criminal junta” and being “complicit in its atrocity crimes against Myanmar’s peoples”, she said.

Naypyidaw has ramped diplomatic efforts since the election. Min Aung Hlaing, who became president in April, made back-to-back state visits to China and India in recent weeks, signalling that his government retains the backing of both giant neighbours.

Asean’s softer stance towards Myanmar would “at most move the needle at the margins,” said Joanne Lin, a senior fellow at the ISEAS – Yusof Ishak Institute.

While engagement could help secure humanitarian access and information on political detainees, she said it was no indication of a political breakthrough.

Myanmar’s military, which has ruled the country with an iron fist for most of its postcolonial history, has little reason to comply with the peace plan given its lack of firm timelines, according to analysts.

“It is clear that the peace plan functions more as a reason to stay at the table than as something anyone expects to be implemented,” said Asean observer and policy analyst Vu Lam.

Growing support for Min Aung Hlaing made advancing the peace process “difficult and problematic” as Asean faced increasing pressure, said Thitinan Pongsudhirak, a senior fellow at Chulalongkorn University’s Institute of Security and International Studies.

He noted that several member states were advancing ties with Naypyidaw, citing Min Aung Hlaing’s visit to Laos earlier this month – his first to an Asean state since becoming president. “There are a couple of Asean countries that are holding out, but they are under pressure because Min Aung Hlaing has all the momentum now.”

Deepening engagement while the military was carrying out deadly air strikes on civilians risked legitimising the junta, Vu warned, adding that re-engagement without clear conditions attached also risked a perception of “betrayal” among Myanmar’s people.

It also reflected how the bloc’s institutional priorities of consensus and non-interference overrode humanitarian protection in times of conflict, Vu added. “That’s not unique to Myanmar, but rather how Asean handles crises generally.”

A UN report published last month found the junta responsible for more than 700 civilian deaths, including 224 women and 153 children, during the election period between August and January.

Lin concurred that high-level engagement with Naypyidaw could come across as “a step towards legitimising the authorities”.

“Asean needs to be very clear that engagement is not recognition and that the purpose of engagement is to widen humanitarian access and push for an inclusive political process,” she said, adding: “If Asean stays completely disengaged, others will shape the political and security environment around Myanmar with little Asean input.”

The bloc was likely to continue on its “middle path”, Lin said – increasing practical engagement while stopping short of full political normalisation. “The challenge is to make sure this does not become normalised by default.”

Thitinan warned against hasty re-engagement and said the bloc should use the peace plan as leverage to demand concessions such as Aung San Suu Kyi’s whereabouts and compliance with provisions on dialogue and inclusiveness.

“Asean has to be very careful about being taken for a ride by Min Aung Hlaing, otherwise, he gets what he wants and Asean won’t get anything, which is a big risk for Asean down the road,” he said.
