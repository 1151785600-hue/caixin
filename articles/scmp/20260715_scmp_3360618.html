Chinese in the driver’s seat? India-China reset hits a bump on Delhi’s streets

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1259 words | 2026-07-15

It was supposed to be just another hectic day on New Delhi’s roads. Instead, early this month, several e-rickshaws on the Indian capital’s crowded streets reportedly came to a halt mid-ride in separate incidents that left drivers stranded and passengers searching for answers.

Videos of the disruption quickly went viral on social media, turning a local transport breakdown into a national debate. The alleged culprit was not a mechanical failure – it was software. And the trail led to China.

Chinese battery management apps – including BAT-BMS, Epoch Li-ion and Lossigy – were said to have been used to remotely disable the vehicles, raising concerns over the security of connected electric vehicles and the possibility of Bluetooth-enabled tampering.

The apps did not respond to requests for comment from the South China Morning Post.

India’s IT secretary S. Krishnan later confirmed that a probe had been launched and that the government had ordered the removal of at least three Chinese battery management applications from app stores amid worries that connected EV systems could be exploited. Some reports mentioned up to seven apps being targeted.

The incident comes at a delicate moment in India-China relations. Years of tensions triggered by the deadly 2020 border clashes and India’s subsequent ban on Chinese apps such as WeChat and TikTok have given way to a cautious diplomatic thaw, with Delhi and Beijing seeking to restore economic engagement despite enduring strategic mistrust.

For a developing India, the challenge is increasingly one of balance. The country wants Chinese investment, manufacturing expertise and supply chains to support sectors such as electric vehicles, but it also wants to reduce vulnerabilities in areas where technology, data and connectivity could have security implications.

Analysts say the e-rickshaw episode has brought that contradiction into sharper focus. It is not only a question of whether India should engage with Chinese technology or move away from it. The larger challenge is how India manages dependence on China in critical sectors while protecting against potential strategic risks.

According to Amit Kumar of the Takshashila Institution, a think tank based in Bengaluru, “given the nature of India-China relations, the atmosphere is always charged, and episodes like these only make things worse”.

“The recent e-rickshaw incident shows how technology and apps can be weaponised, posing real security threats. The incident is reflective of the broader concern that India has. And it is exactly these kind of incidents that derail progress,” he added.

Kumar noted that the Chinese presence in India’s green energy transition was inevitable given China’s dominance of global supply chains.

Aadil Brar, a geopolitical analyst, said the case was a “reminder that dependence on Chinese technology is not always visible at the surface level” as it shows how a seemingly small mobility product can raise larger questions about surveillance, dependency and control.

“Even when a product is made in India, the more sensitive layers can still be Chinese, especially when it comes to batteries, software, connectivity and remote monitoring,” he added.

Kalpit Mankikar of the Observer Research Foundation, a think tank in Delhi, argued that the incident in the capital “feeds into the narrative of Chinese wielding a ‘kill switch’ in electronics systems”.

He added that it “will definitely impact the pace and scope of India’s economic normalisation with China, especially in sensitive sectors”.

EV battery experts, however, caution against viewing the issue only through the lens of geography. They argue that the underlying vulnerability lies in product security rather than the country where the technology is manufactured.

“As EV adoption grows, cybersecurity should be treated with the same importance as battery safety and vehicle reliability,” said Manju Verma, founder of Hyderabad-based EV diagnostics and battery intelligence company EVXpertz.

While such incidents “are not widespread”, Verma said they highlighted the need for manufacturers to strengthen security measures in connected battery systems.

Describing the Delhi disruption as “a product security issue rather than a Bluetooth issue”, Verma said the main concern was authentication.

Battery management systems are designed to monitor a battery’s voltage, temperature and current in real time. However, if these systems rely on weak or default passwords, they may also allow unauthorised users to send control commands.

“If a BMS uses default or commonly known passwords that are never changed, an unauthorised person with the appropriate BMS management app and within Bluetooth range could potentially access certain functions, including disabling the battery where that capability exists,” Verma said.

At EVXpertz workshops, Verma said the company regularly saw Bluetooth-enabled battery packs that could be discovered by nearby devices.

“Bluetooth visibility itself is not the issue,” she said, noting that the typical range was around 20 to 50 metres (65 to 164 feet).

The bigger concern, she added, was whether manufacturers had implemented proper authentication and access controls.

The debate in India echoes a broader discussion under way in the West, especially Washington, where policymakers have increasingly viewed connected vehicles as strategic technologies rather than simply consumer products.

A report from the American Enterprise Institute, a Washington-based think tank, argued that modern vehicles are no longer just machines but “connected computers” that collect data, communicate externally and rely on complex software systems.

The report, co-authored by Chris Miller, author of Chip War: The Fight for the World’s Most Critical Technology, warned that “packed with sensors, connected to the internet, and controlled by complex and opaque layers of software, modern cars represent a significant cybersecurity risk”.

In a separate email to the SCMP, Miller called requiring security reviews or “even prohibiting Chinese battery management systems a reasonable approach to mitigating these risks”.

He stressed that “connectivity software and hardware is another sphere where governments should carefully scrutinise reliance on Chinese components that could enable remote access”.

As some members of the European Union, and Canada, court Chinese investment and technology as part of their “reverse tech” strategy, the US is heading the other way, hitting Chinese EVs with 120 per cent tariffs and banning certain Chinese firms from selling new vehicles under the Commerce Department’s Connected Vehicle Rule.

In May, John Moolenaar, chairman of the House Select Committee on China, co-introduced the Connected Vehicle Security Act to ban Chinese vehicles and components from US roads. He has repeatedly slammed connected vehicles as “rolling data hubs”.

This week, Norwegian public transport operator Ruter announced plans to tighten anti-hacking measures after a test on new Chinese-made electric buses showed the manufacturer could remotely turn them off.

China, meanwhile, is moving in the opposite direction, making connected features such as remote monitoring, data recording and management increasingly central to its automotive framework.

Even as India finds itself at a crossroads after the e-rickshaw disruption, cybersecurity experts caution against reducing the debate to the question of Chinese origin alone.

“I believe this issue should be viewed from the perspective of cybersecurity practices rather than the country of origin,” Verma said.

The key question, she added, was whether manufacturers followed secure design principles “regardless of where the hardware is made”.

“Weak authentication and poor access control can create vulnerabilities in any connected device,” she said.

Kumar argued that India’s strategy with China must be to “manage dependencies, de-risk rather than reduce dependence, and create counter-dependencies”.

He said the problem was “not dependence or investments per se”, but creating a framework to de-risk trade and screen investments for strategic vulnerabilities.

“Once we get that right, imports and investments shouldn’t be a problem. And we must be open to it.”
