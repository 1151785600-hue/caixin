Nasa knows how to deflect an asteroid. Can Japan’s Hayabusa2 pull it off?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 768 words | 2026-07-13

Japan’s Hayabusa2 fly-by of Torifune marks a leap in asteroid defence, showcasing the country’s growing space prowess and “goodwill science” role vital for protecting Earth from cosmic threats.

The space probe, named after the Peregrine falcon in Japanese, successfully flew within 800 metres (0.5 miles) of the asteroid, the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) said on July 5.

JAXA said Hayabusa2 performed as expected and responded to all instructions from land-based mission control, about 100 million km away.

According to Yuya Mimasa, head of the mission team, the fly-by had been “as difficult as hitting a 1-yen coin placed in Hokkaido from Okinawa”.

Masaki Fujimoto, director of the JAXA Institute of Space and Astronomical Science, hailed the expedition.

“We were able to demonstrate to the world that we possess the technology to change the orbit of an asteroid heading towards the Earth,” he told reporters on July 6.

Launched in 2014, Hayabusa2 reached the near-Earth asteroid 162173 Ryugu in 2018, collecting surface samples that were returned to our planet aboard a capsule two years later.

Kazuto Suzuki, a professor at the University of Tokyo, called Japan “one of the most capable countries” at asteroid defence.

“[It] is taking the lead in missions designed to prepare a response if an asteroid that is on a collision course with the Earth is detected,” said Suzuki, a member of the Japanese government’s committee on National Space Policy.

He, however, acknowledged that Japan was yet to catch up with Nasa, the European Space Agency (ESA) and China’s space programme. These agencies are working separately to counter the threat posed by an extinction-level asteroid strike, similar to the Chicxulub event, which hit the Yucatan Peninsula in Mexico 66 million years ago, wiping out about 75 per cent of all species.

Suzuki said Beijing was less open to collaboration with other space agencies due to concerns over potential leaks of sensitive data.

He added that Tokyo played an “active” role within COSPAR (Committee on Space Research) that brought together the international community to cooperate, build shared capacity and work on detection – a “very difficult” task.

Scientists at the France-based agency then “exchange ideas on what could be done when an asteroid that posed a threat was identified”, he said.

According to Suzuki, using robots to excavate the surface of an asteroid, deploying explosives to destroy it or alter its course, and deliberately crashing a spacecraft into a celestial body are some of the strategies for planetary defence.

Another option was to position a team of astronauts on an asteroid’s surface and detonate it, a tactic he called the “Bruce Willis scenario”, replicating the storyline from the 1998 film Armageddon.

“Everything is on the table to be discussed,” Suzuki said, adding that the Hayabusa mission was a major step forward since such asteroid-deflecting technology had yet to take flight.

Space hazards

The threat is real. Of the 1.2 million identified asteroids in our solar system, more than 36,000 are classified as near-Earth objects, with 1,700 on the ESA’s risk list, requiring regular monitoring.

According to Nasa, the 74-million-tonne asteroid 101955 Bennu poses the greatest risk to Earth. There is a one-in-2,700 chance that the 500-metre-wide object will collide with Earth when its trajectory brings it close in September 2182.

“Hayabusa was primarily designed to be an asteroid rendezvous, recovery and return mission, with this additional fly-by an extension,” said Lance Gatling, president of Tokyo-based Nexial Research. “But it is important because there is real concern that the Earth will sooner or later be impacted by a gigantic asteroid.”

The aviation and aerospace analyst said the asteroids must be detected by coordinating with other nations.

“The data has to be analysed because if an asteroid on a collision course was identified tomorrow, then there would be a huge scramble to intercept it.”

Nasa pioneered the controlled collision with an asteroid in 2022, when its DART spacecraft crashed into Dimorphos, reducing the object’s orbital period by 32 minutes.

China is also aiming to alter the orbit of 2015 XF261, a 30-metre asteroid, by 2030.

While JAXA’s research was focused on space science, Japan was “keen to take the lead in space situational awareness, which is a bit like air traffic control for space”, Gatling said.

“If JAXA can repeat the Hayabusa mission multiple times and demonstrate that it can rendezvous with asteroids at great distances, then this will be a big step towards reducing the threat they pose,” he said.

He urged JAXA to work closely with other Asian nations to put their satellites into orbit to raise the profile of Japan’s “goodwill science” policy.
