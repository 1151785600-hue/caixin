Negeri Sembilan snap poll threatens to chip away at Malaysian state’s tech hub goals

SCMP | FREE | 1014 words | 2026-07-19

Negeri Sembilan’s bid to turn itself into Malaysia’s next hi-tech industrial hub is heading into an unusual political stress test, as a royal dispute spills into a snap election on August 1.

The vote matters not because existing semiconductor and aerospace projects are expected to flee but because it could slow future commitments, reshape the state’s policy environment and sharpen wider uncertainty over the prospect of a snap national poll, analysts say.

Long regarded as an affordable base for Klang Valley commuters, Negeri Sembilan has recently sought to capitalise on its proximity to Kuala Lumpur, Selangor and major transport links to attract higher-value semiconductor and aerospace industries.

Last year, it recorded 19.1 billion ringgit (US$4.7 billion) in approved investments across 295 projects, almost three times the US$1.77 billion logged in 2024. The investments were expected to create more than 3,800 jobs, according to Chief Minister Aminuddin Harun.

While capital-intensive projects were unlikely to be derailed, given the cost and complexity of moving them, analysts warned political uncertainty could still test the momentum behind the state’s industrial push.

The turmoil began after four territorial chiefs, known as undang, claimed they had removed Tuanku Muhriz Tuanku Munawir as the state’s ruler over alleged misconduct and named his cousin and son of the late ruler, Tunku Nadzaruddin Tuanku Ja’afar, as the 12th monarch. Negeri Sembilan is the only royal state in Malaysia where the ruler is chosen by undangs through a customary process rather than by simple hereditary succession.

Both the federal and state government continue to recognise Tuanku Muhriz, but the dispute spilled into politics when all 14 state legislators from Barisan Nasional (BN) withdrew their support for Aminuddin, resulting in the snap election.

Unusual and confusing

Constitutional disputes could affect economies by causing businesses to postpone investments and other major decisions, said Geoffrey Williams, an economist and business consultant based in Malaysia.

“In Negeri Sembilan, the constitutional issue is quite unusual and confusing to investors,” he said.

While a state election would normally provide clarity, Williams said BN’s recent landslide victory in the southern state of Johor had added another layer of risk.

Pakatan Harapan (PH) – Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s multiethnic coalition – won 17 of Negeri Sembilan’s 36 seats in 2023 and governed with BN’s support, but that pact has unravelled after PH received a drubbing in Johor, winning only eight of 56 seats while federal partner BN swept 48.

BN’s announcement that it would contest only 25 seats in the poll has also fuelled expectations of cooperation with Islamist party PAS and its ally, Parti Wawasan Negara, in the remaining constituencies. Such a partnership could concentrate the anti-PH vote, making it much harder for the coalition to hold on to the state, Williams said.

Businesses did not like change – something an election might bring about, he said. “A snap GE16 [general election] is the main risk to the Malaysian economy in the second half of 2026. For now, the Negeri Sembilan election has become the biggest challenge in economic terms.”

However, semiconductor projects were likely to proceed as planned, unless the political crisis caused a major change, said Ooi Tze Howe, a supply-chain executive.

“Semiconductor facilities require heavy capital expenditure, moving an expansion to another state would mean restarting land and utility approvals, and also [finding] proximity to existing plants and supply networks,” Ooi said. “As long as the situation is under control, it is very rare that any investment will be cancelled or put on hold.”

In the existing climate, companies were more likely to accelerate efforts to create manufacturing capacity, he said. “Nevertheless, land and water supply are still critical … for semiconductor plants, so the state governments’ responsiveness on land, water and infrastructure can still influence where companies place subsequent investments.”

When asked what investors would want to hear after the election, Ooi’s answer was simple: “The incoming state leader should say it is ‘business as usual’.”

Bigger issues

On the aerospace side, Shamsul Kamar Abu Samah, the chief executive of the National Aerospace Industry Corporation Malaysia, said his organisation had not received any reports of delays or hesitation as a result of Negeri Sembilan’s political situation.

Negeri Sembilan has been described as an emerging aerospace location given its proximity to more mature clusters in Selangor. Shamsul estimated there were about nine aerospace companies operating in the state – including Safran Landing Systems and the National Aerospace Fasteners Corporation, both of which are expanding.

Most investment, trade and industrial policies were administered at a federal level, allowing operations and approved projects to continue despite changes in political leadership, he said. “Current state policies are enough to support ongoing development activities.”

The industry was more worried about larger issues. “Industry concerns are focused on the conflicts in the Middle East and Ukraine, such as their effects on raw materials, logistics and fuel prices,” Shamsul said.

The Johor election had “woken up” Negeri Sembilan’s business community, said a local businessman.

“There was a lot of discontent because of federal policies like the fuel subsidy rationalisation, and e-invoicing, which meant a lot of people cannot manipulate their tax returns,” said Philip Liu, who is based in the state capital of Seremban. E-invoicing is a federal digital tax-reporting mandate for businesses above a certain revenue threshold.

But after BN’s landslide victory in Johor and the possibility of a state government formed with PAS in the mix, many local businesses were now moving towards supporting PH, Liu said.

“Even e-invoicing is something we know we would have to adopt eventually, and it comes from federal policy. But locally, the state government has actually been very business-friendly even for local small and medium-sized enterprises, such as involving local businesses in global giants’ service chains and providing financing and digitalisation support.”

If PAS got involved, businesses were worried they could become bogged down by increased state bureaucracy, he said. Paperwork has been streamlined since 2018, to improve business efficiency.

“Other friends in the hospitality industry are also worried about the introduction of more restrictive social policies,” Liu added.
