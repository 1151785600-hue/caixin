Dashcam row allegedly involving singer David Lui could be referred to police

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 540 words | 2026-07-14

Hong Kong’s privacy watchdog has received a complaint over a viral dashcam video of a heated dispute in a ride-hailing vehicle involving a passenger identified as singer David Lui Fong, warning that the case could be referred to police.

Assistant Privacy Commissioner Fiona Lai Ho-yan said on a radio programme on Monday that passengers had a higher expectation of privacy inside for-hire vehicles, which are considered semi-private spaces.

“The driver involved, by uploading an identifiable video to social media without the passenger’s consent, has already violated data protection principles,” Lai said.

The footage showed a dispute over an apparent GPS error that quickly escalated into a profanity-laced shouting match.

It drew widespread attention on social media platform Threads last Friday. The driver identified the passenger as Lui in the video, which also clearly showed his face.

The watchdog subsequently ordered the removal of the clip, noting that any video that clearly identified an individual constituted “personal data” under the Personal Data (Privacy) Ordinance. The clip had been removed as of Saturday.

Lai said the office had received one complaint related to the incident, adding that it would not exclude the possibility of referring the case to law enforcement agencies.

“This is possible to be referred to relevant authorities for further follow-up, but it depends on whether a need arises in the case,” she said.

She added that regardless of whether a complaint was filed, the watchdog would consider “a range of factors” in deciding whether to request the removal of content suspected of breaching personal data protection laws or issue a cessation notice.

Lai also warned that resharing content disclosing personal data without consent could incur liability under the city’s anti-doxxing law.

Under the ordinance, anyone who discloses personal data without consent, with intent to cause harm or recklessness as to whether harm would be caused, is liable to a maximum penalty of two years’ imprisonment and a HK$100,000 (US$12,755) fine upon summary conviction.

“If professional drivers wish to install dashcams inside their vehicles, they must follow the office’s guidelines, including putting up clear signage to safeguard passengers’ right to be informed and their privacy,” she said.

In 2019, a racy dashcam video showing singer Andy Hui Chi-on and actress Jacqueline Wong Sum-wing in an intimate moment, published by the now-defunct tabloid Apple Daily, garnered widespread public attention.

It also prompted the disgraced singer, who is married to Cantopop star Sammi Cheng Sau-man, to apologise at a press conference hours after the video was released.

Then privacy commissioner Stephen Wong said a taxi was considered a semi-private space and that a driver should comply with privacy laws if a camera was installed, but he did not call for the video’s removal.

Hong Kong authorities introduced a criminal offence for doxxing in October 2021, following the online publication of personal details, such as addresses and mobile phone numbers, of protesters and police officers during the 2019 anti-government unrest.

Since then, the privacy watchdog has been able to issue cessation notices requiring platforms to remove doxxing content, with non-compliance constituting an offence.

Before the legal amendment, compliance with the watchdog’s removal requests was not mandatory.
