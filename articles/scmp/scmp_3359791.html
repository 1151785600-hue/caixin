China weighs open-weight AI’s security risks against national tech innovation strategy: researchers

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 821 words | 2026-07-08

China is facing a delicate regulatory balancing act as it weighs the security risks of open-weight AI models against an innovation strategy that has been crucial to its race for technological supremacy with the United States, researchers said.

Traditionally, open-weight models – which allow anyone to download code for free and run it on local hardware – have lagged months behind proprietary frontier models. But recent releases from Chinese labs have significantly narrowed that gap.

Zhipu AI’s GLM-5.2 recently became the first Chinese large language model (LLM) to rank among the top three globally on leading benchmarks, earning praise from users as the country’s first open-weight model reliable enough for daily coding workflows.

But as these AI models advance rapidly, analysts warned that a regulatory response could be on the horizon.

“As open-weight models approach the kind of cyber and biosecurity risks of Mythos and other leading-edge models, China may make the same calculation as the US and find them to be too dangerous to be released, especially in open form,” said Mark Witzke, a non-resident scholar at the University of California San Diego who researches US-China tech policy.

The emerging concerns stem from Anthropic’s Claude Mythos. Announced in April, the powerful LLM rattled global industries with its ability to autonomously identify and exploit cybersecurity vulnerabilities. Amid warnings that its advanced reasoning capabilities could lower the technical barriers to developing bioweapons, the US lab restricted Mythos to a select group of American organisations.

Washington’s caution was on full display last month when it imposed export controls on Fable 5 – a public version of Mythos that redirects sensitive queries to a less advanced model – and ordered Anthropic to block foreign access due to reported vulnerabilities.

While those controls were lifted last week after Anthropic pledged to cooperate with the US government on frontier safety, the episode underscored the high stakes of advanced AI.

Estimates vary on when China would successfully build an open-weight model on par with Mythos, but analysts and industry insiders agreed the timeline was shrinking. While xAI founder Elon Musk posted on X last month that it could happen by the first quarter of next year, Zhipu founder Tang Jie publicly asserted that it “won’t take that long”.

Taking a slightly more conservative view, Hamish Low, a researcher at the US think tank Institute for AI Policy and Strategy, projected in a research report last week that Chinese firms would produce a Mythos-equivalent model by February 2027, based on comparative compute budgets.

Michael Zeng Jinghan, professor and head of the Department of Public and International Affairs at City University of Hong Kong, said the imminent arrival of these powerful open-source models had greatly intensified the urgency for governance.

Unlike closed models, open-weight models can be copied, fine-tuned and redistributed globally upon release. This made traditional access controls more difficult, Zeng said, noting that advanced open-weight systems could easily lower the barrier for malicious actors because their safeguards can be stripped away after download.

The uncertainty presents a major dilemma for Beijing. “Open-weight models are central to China’s global diffusion strategy,” said Witzke of the University of California San Diego. “Walking it back would mean surrendering their biggest AI soft power win.”

Kristy Loke, a fellow at MATS Research focusing on China’s AI governance, said Beijing was unlikely to impose an outright ban any time soon, though “more active and formalised regulatory responses to growing open-weight risks are very much on the table”.

“Beijing is increasingly worried about open-weight AI-related security risks, especially in cyber and critical‑infrastructure domains,” Loke said. The country had also made strengthening AI risk‑management systems one of its goals in the 2026 five-year‑plan, she noted.

In a State Council meeting on June 29, Premier Li Qiang urged the government to “firmly hold the bottom line of AI safety”, improve institutional rules in areas such as tech ethics and testing certification and build a “dynamic, adaptive and tiered” safety regulatory framework.

While Beijing’s current stance remained “ambivalent”, Zeng expected the country to eventually deploy targeted restrictions or graded access controls, which would make its approach similar to those introduced by Washington in recent months.

This could include tighter rules on the public release of highly advanced model weights, or restrictions on using certain open-weight systems within government agencies, state-owned enterprises, critical infrastructure or politically sensitive sectors, he said.

Regulators could also mandate stricter registration, evaluation, watermarking or security testing before models hit Chinese platforms, Zeng added.

Chinese authorities, including the Ministry of Commerce, have summoned companies such as Alibaba Group Holding, ByteDance and Zhipu AI to discuss potentially restricting overseas access to China’s most advanced AI models, according to a Reuters report on Tuesday. Alibaba, the owner of the SCMP, ByteDance and Zhipu did not immediately respond to requests for comment.
