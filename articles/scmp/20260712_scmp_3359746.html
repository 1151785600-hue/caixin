US-China AI war boils down to a contest over electricity

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 865 words | 2026-07-12

The proliferation of artificial intelligence models and the increasing parity in their performance suggest they are becoming a commodity. Soon, AI services will be priced by cost rather than the uniqueness of their model. With electricity the main cost of the services, the AI war between China and the United States is turning into a straightforward contest over electricity output and price.

Open-source AI models are sprouting up like bamboo shoots in China. The American AI community has argued that they are not as good as the closed-source models in the US. Yet recent releases by DeepSeek and Zhipu AI have eliminated the perceived gap. The American goal of making the world dependent on its AI and paying dearly for it now seems like a pipe dream.

This rapid proliferation reveals the true nature of AI, whether a large language model or specifically a generative pre-trained transformer. It turns out it is not that hard to create an AI model. Hence, the value of owning a model, whether frontier or not, isn’t so high any more. As more models join the market, they will become a commodity, like steel or aluminium. And the price of services, or tokens, will be driven by the dominant cost component: electricity.

As the AI contest turns into an electricity competition, it is becoming a race between China’s renewable energy path and America’s emphasis on hydrocarbons. The outcome will depend on which model can deliver cheaper and more reliable electricity.

China’s energy strategy is defined by self-reliance and carbon neutrality. Renewable energy, such as solar, wind and hydropower, has naturally become the preferred choice. The rapid drop in the price of solar and wind power has made them competitive against hydrocarbons.

Last year, the levelised cost of solar power dropped to about two US cents per kWh. Given that transmission and delivery prices from the deserts in the west to the industrial belt in the east can easily double the electricity cost, the rational choice is to locate the data centres in the west, next to the solar farms.

Meanwhile, better storage choices are becoming available to address solar power’s reliability and quality challenges. Sodium-ion batteries good for over 10,000 charge cycles are being mass-produced and sold for less than US$20 per kWh. Energy storage costs are on their way to becoming negligible.

Further, solar power investment costs have room to fall. Solar panels only account for a fraction of the total. The cost of other components as well as construction has not declined massively as yet – automation, cheaper materials and AI management could halve this. By my estimation, electricity costs could fall to one US cent per kWh.

Similarly with wind power, investment costs are falling as output rises. And it could complement solar power, easing electricity storage requirements.

Last year, China’s solar power output increased by roughly 334 billion kWh, and wind by 130 billion kWh. This accounted for almost all of China’s electricity output increase for the year. The International Energy Agency estimates that data centres globally consumed 415 billion kWh of electricity in 2024, with consumption having grown at 12 per cent annually over several years.

As China’s solar and wind power capacity continues to increase strongly, soon it may well be able to handle the electricity requirements for AI for the whole world.

In comparison, US electricity output last year was about 4.43 trillion kWh, an increase of around 7 per cent since 2010 or less than 0.5 per cent per year. The US doesn’t seem positioned to increase output massively. China’s total in 2025 was 10.4 trillion kWh. Further, US electricity prices are about 2.5 times those in China, and US prices are expected to increase while China’s are poised to decline. The US is in no position to compete with China on electricity output or cost.

American AI executives know this and have major plans to build for data centres in the Middle East to take advantage of the region’s abundant natural gas. But China is already producing electricity at 2 US cents per kWh and could halve this in five to 10 years. Even if Gulf countries supply gas for free, the capital expenditure of the projects alone would drive up costs.

The Iran war has also revealed the vulnerabilities of such a pivot – data centres have been bombed during the conflict. They will only be secure, now and in the future, if the United States reaches an agreement with Iran for a lasting peace deal. Given that the US has been trying to collapse the Iranian regime for more than four decades, there seems little chance Washington will give up that goal. US data centres in the region can probably never be secure enough for businesses.

In conclusion, the commoditisation of AI models is forcing America towards open-source models. Its closed-source AI models will simply not be able to survive the market competition. This in turn renders the huge valuations of US AI entities as hot air. The bubble may not even see out the year. Trillions of dollars in paper wealth could be destroyed soon. That will pull the global economy into a deep recession.
