When and how will China ease capital controls?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 914 words | 2026-07-08

The China Securities Regulatory Commission recently fined three Hong Kong brokerages – Tiger Brokers, Futu Securities International and Longbridge Securities – over US$330 million for offering mainland investors access to overseas stocks without authorisation. This should not be misconstrued as a move to discourage overseas investment.

It is merely an attempt to discourage mainland investors from illegal channels that violate China’s capital controls. This is evidenced by the fact that the investors were not penalised and instead given two years to unwind their positions.

In principle, legal channels to buy overseas securities consist of the Hong Kong Stock Connect scheme, Qualified Domestic Institutional Investor (QDII) funds and the Cross-boundary Wealth Management Connect scheme for Greater Bay Area residents. Long-term capital inflows and outflows, including direct investment, long-term loans and portfolio investments, have also been encouraged and, subject to prior approval, permitted in recent years.

While the offshore yuan is essentially fully convertible into all other major currencies in Hong Kong, control remains on certain capital flow items.

In the 1980s and 1990s, foreign exchange was a scarce resource, and capital flight was a real concern. After generous annual trade surpluses for the past decade or so, however, foreign exchange is no longer scarce. Today, China has the world’s largest official foreign exchange reserves, at US$3.4 trillion, and the yuan’s share in international clearing and settlement has risen steadily.

Moreover, over time, the yuan has been holding steady against the US dollar, and is widely expected to appreciate in the long run. What then is the purpose of this form of capital control?

The primary purpose is to prevent disruptive short-term flows of hot money, which can lead to a very volatile yuan and hence affect Chinese exports and imports, as well as direct and portfolio investments. These disturbances can in turn affect the real economy negatively without providing any durable compensating benefits.

Exporters, importers, cross-border direct investors and long-term portfolio investors everywhere prefer stable exchange rates to daily fluctuating ones. But yuan stability does not mean a fixed parity with respect to the US dollar, only the stability over time of the average international purchasing power of the yuan, as reflected in a trade-weighted index of the exchange rates of China’s trading partner countries.

This relative stability is also essential for the yuan to become an international store of value in addition to being an international medium of exchange.

A secondary purpose of capital controls is to prevent sudden surges of capital from affecting the financial and capital markets, which are substantively and psychologically important.

The intent is to protect Chinese investors from asset price bubbles or Ponzi schemes, in or outside China, which almost always end up with small individual investors holding the bag. This is also one of the reasons China has decided to ban cryptocurrencies altogether on the mainland.

Finally, capital controls can also prevent predatory speculation. What happened to the British sterling in 1992 and East Asian currencies (including the Hong Kong dollar) during the 1997 Asian financial crisis can wreak economic havoc and cause prolonged crises.

Since short-term hot money should be discouraged and long-term capital flows should be welcomed, they should be treated differently. But how do we know whether a given capital flow is really long-term?

Some countries require that foreign funds be deposited in a domestic bank for a period, say a year, before they can be used. Enforcement may be cumbersome, but with digital currencies and distributed ledger technology, it should be quite feasible.

A Tobin tax, first proposed in the 1970s by the late Nobel laureate in Economics James Tobin, can also be helpful. The basic idea of the Tobin tax is that any time capital is converted from one currency into another, it is subject to a tax, say 1 per cent. So, if the intended duration is short-term, say a month, the cost is very high, amounting to 24 per cent per year. But if the intended duration is five years, the tax amounts to 0.4 per cent per year, which is quite affordable.

There are many legitimate reasons for long-term capital to flow in and out of the mainland. The leading one is the desire for diversification by both Chinese and foreign investors to spread risk. Another is the saturation of the domestic markets, which forces entrepreneurs to look for opportunities abroad.

The best time to relax capital controls is when inflows and outflows are close to equilibrium, so the relaxation has no significant economic impact. One obvious indicator is the spread between the onshore and offshore yuan exchange rates. Where there is a significant divergence, they are not close to equilibrium.

Another possibility is to compare the relative prices of securities listed both in China and elsewhere. Whether China Depositary Receipts (CDRs), which represent shares of foreign companies, trade at a premium or discount can be a useful indicator. A premium means unsatisfied demand for foreign assets; a discount means more than enough supply of foreign assets. The best time to relax capital controls is when there is little premium or discount.

In time, Chinese capital controls will be relaxed. The differential treatment between short-term and long-term capital flows is most likely to remain. China should have no problem with long-term capital flows. But short-term capital flows are likely to be more strictly scrutinised and regulated, either through a Tobin-like tax, or through distributed ledger technology.
