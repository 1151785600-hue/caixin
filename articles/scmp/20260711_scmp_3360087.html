Who cares for Singapore’s carers? Recent deaths highlight urgent need to tackle burnout

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1231 words | 2026-07-11

When Karasi Chandramogan’s father died four years ago, the 35-year-old Singaporean was left with the overwhelming responsibility of being the sole carer for her brother, who has autism, and mother, an amputee.

“Me and my dad were a tag team,” the freelance behavioural therapist said. “When he passed on, I couldn’t mourn too much because I had to handle the funeral situation as well as my mum and brother.”

She recalled an incident in April, when her brother, Bala, suffered seizures during lunch. “It was very scary for me to handle because I was terrified that he would choke. So I try not to be away for too long, check the CCTV regularly, I’m always on the lookout,” she said.

The recent sentencing of a man who had been the long-term carer for his 56-year-old brother before strangling him to death last year hit close to home for Chandramogan and other carers in Singapore, where families are taking on increasingly complex care needs amid a rapidly ageing population.

Abdul Rani Md Ariffin, 59, had been looking after his brother, who suffered from chronic illnesses and depression. A psychiatric assessment produced in court revealed that Abdul Rani was diagnosed with adjustment disorder, a depressed mood state and irritability, as well as carer stress. He also had difficulties regulating and expressing emotions.

Initially charged with murder, Abdul Rani was sentenced to eight years in jail after he pleaded guilty to a reduced charge of culpable homicide not amounting to murder.

Karasi said such cases affected her greatly. “I think most carers would resonate with such tragic cases, because we know it can get very isolating, and they must have been stretched way beyond the point of burnout.”

She used to blame herself if Bala, who has a history of epilepsy, suffered seizures. “I would always think that it was because I didn’t take care of him properly,” she said. “It’s taken me a few years to remind myself that these things are not in my control.”

Cases where carer burnout leads to death are, unfortunately, uncommon, and highlight the immense toll and demands placed on them.

In 2022, a father killed his 11-year-old twin sons who were diagnosed with autism as he believed it would take away his wife’s burdens. He made an attempt on his own life after strangling his sons.

Such situations are often a result of stress, isolation and a lack of timely support, and highlight the “invisible burden” as carers navigate complex or long-term conditions, said Tim Oei, chief executive of Mindfull Community, a non-profit which supports carers.

“Many carers continue to cope quietly, and distress may only surface at a crisis point.”

‘Barely coping’

The cases underscore the importance of carers’ well-being as the city state transitions to a super-aged society, according to social welfare agencies and experts. They urged early identification and support for carers, and noted that while Singapore had strengthened support in recent years, lack of accessibility and awareness posed challenges.

A 2022 study by the National Council of Social Service, which surveyed over 4,500 carers in Singapore, found that more than half of the respondents were “burdened” or “barely coping” with their caregiving responsibilities and felt they had less control over their lives.

Increased financial assistance, community care services and respite options demonstrated “growing recognition of the critical role” of carers, said Steven Yeo, executive director of Caregiving Welfare Association.

Under Singapore’s Home Caregiving Grant, eligible carers can receive monthly payouts of up to S$600 (US$465) – depending on per capita household income – which can be used to hire a helper or buy healthcare items. Carers can also apply for a grant which subsidises training courses.

Millie Su, a senior lecturer at the Singapore University of Social Sciences’ SR Nathan School of Human Development, said though help was available, many carers were not aware of the policies or found the services inaccessible, she said.

She pointed to the study, which revealed that fewer than three in 10 carers used support services.

Many had difficulties navigating paperwork and applications when applying for subsidies, grants and nursing homes, she said, urging the streamlining of such processes. “Some are being ping-ponged between different agencies, and making the system more integrated and connected will make care more accessible.”

Carers should be identified early for timely support to be given, she added. “As long as someone is taking care of someone who has a chronic condition, they are vulnerable, and we’ve been ignoring this because there’s so much focus on patient care.”

As Singapore becomes a super-aged society, carer support is even more critical, experts say. The city state crossed the threshold of being super-aged this year, with more than 21 per cent of its population now aged 65 or above.

“We are seeing more and more aged couples living on their own and looking after each other, both as frail as the other,” said Oei, urging holistic and sustained support, including expanded access to respite care, and strengthened coordination across healthcare and community services.

Healthcare professionals should also be trained to identify informal carers to provide relevant information, resources and support, she added.

Yeo urged employers to foster carer-friendly workplaces through flexible work arrangements and greater understanding. “Ultimately, caregiving should never rest solely on one individual’s shoulders. Preventing such situations requires a whole-of-society approach,” he said.

Overcoming guilt

A decade ago, Edgar Tang had to step into the role as a carer for his parents after his father’s Parkinson’s disease worsened rapidly, leaving him wheelchair-bound.

“There were times I was breaking down due to work and these duties, and I felt like both ends of the candle were burning out on me,” said Tang, 50, whose mother is diagnosed with a mental condition.

He was eventually forced to move his father into a nursing home, a decision which tore him up with guilt. “It was really tough because I felt like such a horrible child,” he said. “How could I have put my father in a nursing home?”

Filial piety and a stigma associated with seeking help to care for family members – values prevalent in Asian societies such as Singapore – may also prevent carers from seeking timely support.

“We tend to not talk about it and think that caregiving is a family matter, a show of filial piety, a duty and a responsibility, and this cultural norm may prevent people from seeking help,” Su said.

Many didn’t recognise themselves as a carer in need of support. “They simply see themselves as a spouse, son, daughter or sibling doing what they should, and therefore delay seeking help until they are already overwhelmed,” Yeo said.

Tang managed to rationalise his emotions after attending a get-together session for carers organised by a welfare group. “I realised that many are feeling these emotions and it’s OK to feel this way,” he said.

His father also started to walk again in the nursing home – a development which Tang hailed as a “miracle”.

As Tang’s physical and mental health improved, he was able to be fully present for his father, allowing him to shed the guilt. “Now I’m not turning up exhausted. I’m present and we chat, walk, draw our memories, do some writing and listen to music,” he said.
