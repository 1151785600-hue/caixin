Hong Kong IPO debut delayed in rare move over missing regulatory data: sources

SCMP | FREE | 338 words | 2026-07-14

Hong Kong witnessed a rare delay of an initial public offering (IPO) debut on Wednesday, the first in more than eight months, triggered by a regulatory requirement for additional client information, according to people familiar with the matter.

On Tuesday night, just before its scheduled listing on Wednesday, DKE Holding Company postponed its IPO ceremony after it failed to provide investors with an offer price and results of its bookbuilding.

The maker of e-paper display modules said more time was needed to finalise the exchange announcement and obtain clearances from regulators, with the event now postponed to Thursday, according to a filing issued on Tuesday night.

A brokerage firm submitted share purchase documents via Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing’s (HKEX’s) FINI digital IPO platform, but was asked by the regulatory authority to provide additional client information, resulting in the delay, according to a source who requested anonymity as they were not authorised to speak publicly on the matter.

The brokerage firm was probably not familiar with the process, the source added.

The shares offered by DKE were sold out during the bookbuilding period, said another source familiar with the matter.

It was the second delay since the FINI system was launched in November 2023.

On October 16, 2025, Fujian Haixi Pharmaceuticals said it needed more time to notify the exchange about official subscription results. The company released its announcement one day later and went public on October 20.

A spokesman for HKEX said all of its systems, including FINI, were “operating normally”.

Both DKE and Haixi completed their grey market trading the evening before their official listing ceremony.

DKE, aiming to raise up to HK$517 million (US$66 million), closed around 4 per cent higher than its minimum offer price of HK$78.64 in the grey market on Tuesday evening.

Hong Kong’s IPO boom has extended into this year, with 15 companies scheduled to start trading this week. Amid the listing rush, the city’s regulators had told sponsors to monitor the quality of the application prospectus.
