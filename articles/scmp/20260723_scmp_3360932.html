Philippines’ Pax Silica AI hub plan slammed for mineral ‘plunder’

SCMP | FREE | 1065 words | 2026-07-23

On a 1,620-hectare (4,000 acres) site north of Manila, Philippine and US officials are pitching a hi-tech hub as a global gateway to AI, semiconductors and critical minerals.

For critics, however, the proposed development – part of the US-backed global Pax Silica project – is concrete proof of another infrastructure push over contested land at the expense of local communities, threatening their livelihoods and scarce water supplies.

It is, they argue, a further example of ambitious investment pitches racing ahead of environmental safeguards and proper cost-benefit analysis.

Manila is seeking to finalise a framework agreement with Washington for the hub within two to three months, according to Joshua Bingcang, president and CEO of the state-run Bases Conversion and Development Authority (BCDA).

The BCDA is tasked with turning former US military bases into centres of economic growth, including the one in New Clark City near Capas, Tarlac province – about 100km (62 miles) north of Manila – where the hub is set to be located.

Pax Silica aims to secure critical supply chains for artificial intelligence, semiconductors and other strategic technologies. The Philippines and Singapore are the only Southeast Asian members of the coalition.

Bingcang said the hub would likely start operations within two years, attract an initial US$10 billion in investment and generate “hundreds of thousands of jobs”.

The hub forms part of the Luzon Economic Corridor, a US-Japan-Philippines project linking four cities on Luzon Island that is projected to generate US$100 billion for the local economy.

Bingcang confirmed that Manila and Washington were exchanging draft agreements and denied earlier reports that the hub would operate under US legal jurisdiction despite being sited on Philippine soil.

In a television interview on Wednesday, he said Washington was aiming to conclude the deal before US President Donald Trump’s potential visit to the Philippines to attend the 49th Association of Southeast Asian Nations summit in November and mark 80 years of US-Philippine ties.

The US State Department has described Pax Silica as an effort to build “a secure, resilient” technology supply chain, as Washington looks to reduce its reliance on Chinese suppliers, especially for critical technologies.

Opposition to the planned hub has grown, with critics expressing concerns about the continued marginalisation and displacement of indigenous communities and farmers, environmental degradation and resource exploitation.

IBON Foundation has challenged claims that the hub would accelerate industrial, saying in a social media post in May that the Philippines “doesn’t have a strategy for national industrialisation”.

“Pax Silica isn’t about industrialising the Philippines but about deeper integration into US-controlled semiconductor and AI supply chains,” the non-profit think tank said, adding that industrialisation required building “national productive power” through local industries, labour, science and technology.

On Tuesday, around 3,000 members of the indigenous Aeta community in Capas marched in protest against restrictions they said authorities had imposed on their lands, including limits on access and basic supplies, which they claimed were partly related to the hub.

“The single most dangerous thing about Pax Silica is what it adds to a crisis that was already exploding before the announcement was made,” said Cathleen De Guzman, national coordinator for the Kalikasan People’s Network for the Environment, a Philippines-based environmental campaign group.

De Guzman said the Aeta community had fought for decades to secure titles to its ancestral domains, with the government repeatedly declining to issue them.

“The absence of a paper document was used to erase the existence of a community that has lived there since before the Spanish arrived,” she said, adding that an estimated 20,000 indigenous people and 15,000 farmers were at risk of displacement from the New Clark City development.

She also raised concerns about the Sacobia watershed – the primary water source for areas across New Clark City – warning that the risk of it drying up had been flagged as far back as 2020.

This Week in Asia has contacted the BCDA for comment.

Middle ground?

The backlash has exposed gaps in transparency, timing and legal process, according to environmental analysts.

Paulo Burro, an environmental policy and government affairs consultant and managing partner of Climate Balanced Business Advisors, said land conversion and the displacement of farmers, fisherfolk and indigenous people remained the biggest concerns surrounding the Pax Silica hub.

“With the drive to consolidate the supply chain, there may be increased mineral demand … questions remain around its actual economic impact on displaced farmers and fisherfolk,” Burro said.

Jonas Marie Dumdum, an energy and sustainability researcher and consultant, said such concerns were understandable given the scale of the project, noting the likely demand for minerals such as nickel, cobalt and copper to supply the hub.

Companies at the hub would not only draw an estimated 5,000 megawatts from Luzon’s coal-dependent grid, but also be located near identified watersheds and northeast of Mount Pinatubo, he said, referring to the active volcano whose 1991 eruption caused hundreds of deaths.

“The real pressure points in Capas come down to three things: carbon emissions, water and waste,” Dumdum said.

Industries would draw on the same water systems used to irrigate farms in central Tarlac, while mineral processing would produce heavy-metal waste in an area vulnerable to both flooding and volcanic eruption, he said.

“These points raise a question not just about our climate commitments but about whether there’s enough power, fresh water, and enough space and capacity to treat e-wastes in the area.”

However, Burro said that compromise remained possible, provided the interests of businesses, environmental groups and local communities were all protected.

The question was “how the project gets built and who gets a say, not necessarily over whether it should exist at all”, he said.

Dumdum said the public should be informed about the sources of the hub’s water and power supply, as well as the waste treatment process for mineral extraction.

He added that there should be a proper assessment of the potential environmental impact and “real consent” from farming and indigenous communities before the finalisation of any deal.

De Guzman said she “outrightly rejects Pax Silica”, calling it “plunder” of the country’s critical minerals and energy sources, as well as an attack on national sovereignty and patrimony.

She also questioned the authorities’ commitment to community engagement, calling the process a “pattern of consultation theatre”.

“This unequal agreement that the Philippines has joined opens our lands and vulnerable sectors to further exploitation and displacement,” she said.
