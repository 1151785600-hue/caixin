Bhutan’s heritage arts become the frontline of cultural preservation as modernity dawns

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 3367 words | 2026-07-13

Bhutan today doesn’t look quite like the Bhutan of even just four years ago.

In monasteries, laminated QR codes for digital payments are displayed next to worn donation boxes. In Thimphu, the capital, there are solar panels on roofs, Chinese-made electric cars on roads, construction projects just paces apart – six storeys sprouting from the ground in humdrum blocks. Even the hand-painted road signs, once quippy rhymes and memorable alliterations, have become mundane: “No hurry, no worry” replaced with “Don’t look at your mobile phone while driving”.

How did the Land of the Thunder Dragon, a nation whose reputation for idyllic, down-to-earth, lo-tech living was built on its reclusion from the outside world, get here?

Since it emerged from the Covid-19 pandemic, Bhutan has launched, in quick succession, a slew of initiatives designed to bring the once-withdrawn kingdom into the 21st century – and the people of the 21st century into its mountainous reaches. From the biennial Snowman Race ultra-marathon and the restoration of the ancient Trans Bhutan Trail as a hiking attraction to the opening of the Bhutan Innovation Lab and even a reduction of its Sustainable Development Fee – the daily levy paid by travellers visiting the country – from US$200 to US$100 per night, Bhutan is embracing the new, the foreign and the lucrative.

In June, it hosted the inaugural Bhutan International Travel Mart, to bolster its position as an alluring getaway, but its biggest leap is still to come: the Gelephu Mindfulness City, a 2,600 sq km (1,000 square miles) special administrative region in which governing policies and business practices from around the world will be adopted and tested; those that prove successful will eventually be rolled out across the country. Gelephu International Airport, handling up to 123 flights daily, is slated to open in 2029.

All this forward momentum raises the question: how does Bhutan balance the realities of progress with the preservation of its traditions, arguably the one quality that makes this destination stand out for travellers? One possible solution: double down on the foundations of your unique culture – with a twist.

On a balmy day in late May, Kinley Tshering sits cross-legged on the cement porch of the Exotic Art Gallery, in a village on the edge of Punakha district, a short stroll from famed hilltop fertility temple Chimi Lhakhang.

He is hunched over, with his back to the rest of the world, his attention trained on a stretched canvas frame propped against the wall, unbothered by the flies zipping around his head, landing on his bare shoulder, inching around on his paint-splattered jeans.

Kinley Tshering deftly layers white paint on top of a black background, colouring tiny, exposed slivers of negative space between delicate, embossed linework applied in shimmering gold, his brush strokes so precise and small he looks as though he is hardly moving.

He is painting a thangka. Sacred illustrated scrolls bearing motifs of divine beings, mandalas (concentric designs representing the universe) and scenes from Buddhist texts, thangkas are ubiquitous in Bhutan’s religious sites and dzongs (fortresses). Just steps away from Kinley Tshering’s in-progress Vajrakilaya – a wrathful deity of Vajrayana Buddhism, Bhutan’s state religion – the workshop’s full collection is on display, a cacophony of colour and pattern.

Leaning against the doorframe, watching Kinley Tshering at work, is his older brother Kinga Tshering, the owner of the Exotic Art Gallery. “You see those two dragons? He is combining them with Vajrakilaya for strength and purity,” explains Kinga Tshering. “He’s putting a lot of different ideas into this. He will even wait for his dreams. Whatever idea he gets, whatever dream he dreams, he will add those elements.”

Lhazo devotional painting – one of the 13 traditional arts and crafts of Bhutan – is in the brothers’ blood: the thangka trade is a family speciality that has been passed down through three generations. Kinga Tshering studied under his father from a young age and went to the National Institute for Zorig Chusum, in Thimphu, to continue his education. He opened the Exotic Art Gallery “around seven, eight years ago”, taking orders for thangkas from temples and private households; the rest of his income is earned through the gallery. Kinley Tshering is one of seven brothers and cousins he has in his employ.

Before starting a thangka, the artist must prepare their pigments. Botanical and mineral ingredients are crushed and blended with water and animal glue, to protect colour intensity and repel insects.

“Sometimes, we use pure 24-carat gold. But that costs a lot, so we only use that for paintings for temples,” says Kinga Tshering. He nods at the painting of Vajrakilaya. “We get black from the charcoal of oak trees. Gold, from crushed pyrite; we get it from the mountains.

“Once a thangka is done, it’s done; it is difficult to repeat. Colours that we got two, three years ago, it’s not always possible to replace them; it all depends on what nature can give. I can mix pink again, but not the exact same; that’s the beauty of handmade painting.”

Despite being a creative art form, thangka work does obey strict fundamentals. A mandala has to be a mandala, Kinga Tshering stresses. Depictions of deities must adhere to prescribed postures and hand gestures. Deviations are considered “bad deeds”.

Even within these restrictions, the brothers’ approach to the art is attuned to their passions, emotions, imagination and “vibes”, unlike that of their father, who didn’t paint thangkas for creation’s sake and whose practice was entirely tied to commissions.

“We cannot force ourselves to paint; the energy has to come from the heart,” says Kinga Tshering. “If your mind is too busy, if you’re in pain – you don’t paint. Good moods give the best results.

“Around 60 per cent of the artwork we make comes from us thinking about what we want to paint; we discuss, we wait for our dreams.” Motifs of mythical creatures, flora or fauna that come to them are melded with the immutable central figure of their thangkas. In their blending of the traditional, the devotional and the personal, the brothers keep lhazo relevant.

The 13 forms of arts and crafts Bhutan recognises as part of its cultural identity are known collectively as zorig chusum (zo meaning “the ability to make”, rig meaning “science” or “craft” and chusum meaning “13”). Some, like lhazo, survive thanks to continued demand from Bhutanese traditionalists. Others, like cane- and bamboo-weaving (tsharzo) and blacksmithing (garzo), are struggling to maintain their place in a rapidly modernising country.

“When it comes to zorig chusum, tourists are more inclined towards painting and [textile] weaving because they see them in our daily lives, in our buildings and monasteries, as paintings on the walls, in the national dress,” says Kuenzang Choden, marketing manager for Asia-Pacific and Oceania at the tourism department. “But our mission is to promote all 13 arts and crafts, including the ones that receive less public attention, like tsharzo, garzo, parzo [wood carving], shagzo [wood turning], lugzo [bronze casting] and dozo [stonework and masonry].”

Khampa, project management specialist for the Handicrafts Association of Bhutan, an organisation comprising some 7,500 members who practise zorig chusum, believes that the determination to preserve heritage starts from within.

“We are not looking to promote craft as a ‘thing’. We want people to understand the significance of it and educate them so they can find value in living that culture,” he says.

“Our art is alive; it lives as skills in the hands of our artisans. As an outsider, we just see them as someone who makes a ‘product’. But they practise that art because it defines their life; there is meaning in weaving a piece of cloth. It defines their survival. It defines their purpose. It defines their identity,” he adds. “For us, these artisans are how we can achieve our vision of living Bhutanese culture and traditions.”

A sharp, clacking noise rings out across a room at the Royal Textile Academy (RTA), in Thimphu, bouncing off the walls: repetitive, insistent, percussive. A calloused hand reaches for a wide wooden slat, suspended horizontally, pressing it down against rows of woven thread to tighten the weave. Rods of bamboo, threaded through the warp, clatter hollowly.

Two master weavers are teasing cloth into existence, one weft at a time, working silently save for the sound of their equipment: a pair of formidable pangthag backstrap looms, constructed of timber. A waterfall of filaments cascades from the top of the apparatus, the fibres kept taut by a belt wrapped around each of the weaver’s hips.

“Among the 13 traditional arts and crafts, weaving is considered especially important because of its integral role,” says Chimi Lhakyi Dolma, assistant programme officer of the RTA, which was established in 2005, under the patronage of queen mother Sangay Choden Wangchuck, to preserve thagzo, the art of weaving. She is referring to the national dress, gho for men and kira for women, which must be worn in schools, government offices, religious sites and dzongs, at official ceremonies and on formal occasions.

As a domestic skill, thagzo is passed down from mother to daughter, Dolma explains, and a weaver’s ultimate test is the mastery of the exquisite kishuthara, a highly intricate silk made using trima, a labour-intensive technique of hand-coiling individual threads around the base yarn to create a raised effect that mimics embroidery. Beyond being an example of ability and endurance – weaving a garment using this technique can take anywhere from eight months to two years – a treasured kishuthara kira can be handed down the matrilineal line, creating generational bonds.

However, “home-based weaving traditions are declining day by day”, says Dolma, who is also the founder of textile brand Dakini by Jyenn. “Although their mothers weave, younger generations do not take an interest in the skill; to them, weaving is a chore. [RTA’s] mission is to promote weaving as an art.”

While the museum at the RTA functions as a repository of ancient fabrics, the institute also runs educational programmes and houses a studio in which five master weavers, from eastern Bhutan, ply their craft. One of them creates textiles for the royal family while the others weave for research and development purposes, and for the gift shop.

Nowadays, the RTA is exploring how time-honoured techniques can be applied in novel ways. Craftswomen are encouraged to expand beyond tried-and-tested colour combinations and experiment with shapes and motif placement.

New thinking is also revolutionising an indispensable tool. “A traditional backstrap loom is constraining and not comfortable for weavers who work on it for a prolonged time; it affects their health,” says Dolma. “We are working with the Jigme Namgyel Engineering College to semi-mechanise the loom to ease their workload.”

Several initiatives run by the RTA, including the annual National Design and Arts Competition and community programmes such as livelihood skills training, also support the thagzo craft.

“Sometimes, [rural artisans] weave for months, but when their textiles can’t be sold, it’s discouraging,” Dolma says. “So, we buy it from them. If their textiles are good, we sell them in our gift shop. Otherwise, we transform them into pencil bags or small souvenirs, and we give them to the students who visit us.”

Dolma and the RTA are not alone in shining a light on the plight of hand-woven arts. Independent labels such as She Bhutan – the brainchild of self-taught designer Gaymit Sada Lepcha – are also presenting thagzo through a contemporary lens.

“It started as a celebration of women through fashion and stories,” says Lepcha, who launched She Bhutan in 2013. “I wanted to do something for women, support women artisans and work closely with them.”

Her vision: jackets, coats and capes that blend modern silhouettes and fashion sensibilities with yathra, a colourful weave native to Bumthang district, in central Bhutan.

Made of sheep’s wool and yak’s hair, yathra is historically woven into blankets, throws, rugs, outerwear and scarves – dense, practical items suited to higher altitudes, long winters and punishing temperatures – using the thrithag horizontal-frame loom, a mechanism introduced to Bhutan in the early 20th century. Bold, geometric motifs make up the distinctive yathra weave, and producing just one panel of fabric, around 63.5cm in width and 2.5 metres in length, can take up to two weeks.

Lepcha, who was born in Samtse, a small town in southern Bhutan, had no background in thagzo or design, so getting She Bhutan off the ground was a leap of faith.

“Initially, it was difficult to find manpower,” she says. “Most of the women knew only how to weave traditional gho and kira. I needed people who were familiar with pattern-making for Western garments.”

Eventually, she established a rapport with a yathra specialist and a community of weavers in Bumthang who could apply their traditional skills to her modern designs. Using yathra as the “hero element”, Lepcha creates patterns and swatches for her craftspeople to follow. After the panels of fabric are woven, a seamstress stitches them together to create the final product.

“Before I started combining yathra with modern jackets, people didn’t think they could wear this textile in a different shape or form, because it’s so old and traditional,” Lepcha says. “Nobody had done it before.”

But Covid-19 threw a spanner in the works and She Bhutan was forced to go on hiatus.

In October 2025, when Lepcha felt ready to return, help came from an unexpected quarter: the first-ever Bhutan Fashion Week, a government-supported event celebrating the kingdom’s textile heritage. Twenty designers and labels presented their collections; Lepcha used the opportunity and the national platform to mount She Bhutan’s comeback. In January, the brand went back to bricks and mortar, opening a boutique in downtown Thimphu, to bring the Bumthang art form to the high street.

“Preserving heritage art doesn’t mean doing things in only the strictest, most traditional way. Preservation happens when an old art form is given new life,” she says. “By adding a contemporary touch to yathra, it is kept alive because people continue to wear it.”

With all this craftsmanship in the air, it was perhaps inevitable that other practices not previously undertaken in the kingdom have entered Bhutan. In 2019, the Bhutan Wine Company brought Western vinification to the small town of Yusipang. It resulted in all 21 lots of the inaugural 2023 vintage being auctioned at Bonhams in April last year for a total of US$74,250 – an unheard-of price for an upstart wine label – expanding alcohol-making possibilities beyond the home-brewing tradition of ara, Bhutan’s answer to moonshine.

Coffee culture is also knocking on Bhutan’s door. Home-grown bean farms and roasters such as Bhutan Mountain Coffee and independent cafes, like Ambient Cafe in Thimphu and CCH2O Cafe in Bumthang’s Ura valley, are finding footholds in a country traditionally married to butter tea (suja). Most of these are entrepreneurial endeavours started by returnees who have been influenced by their time abroad.

Initiatives such as the Voluntary Artists Studio, Thimphu, aka VAST Bhutan, a non-profit organisation supporting contemporary art, creativity and innovation, and the Kabjisa artist retreat-cum-art village, led by Asha Kama Wangdi, a co-founder of VAST Bhutan and one of the kingdom’s most respected modern artists, are part of a movement to nurture “a uniquely Bhutanese voice in contemporary art”.

Choden notes opportunities for zorig chusum to flourish among this proliferation of old-meets-new ingenuity: “We’re seeing tourists – who used to come to Bhutan for sightseeing – increasingly looking for authentic and immersive experiences. Many visitors now want to understand our culture more deeply and connect with local communities, so there’s a growing interest in Bhutanese crafts. It reflects a broader global trend of travellers seeking meaningful, culturally enriching moments.”

Attention, however, can be a mixed blessing. “When tourism grows, there is pressure to produce more and adapt to market demands. We already see it happening in Lhuentse [district], with kishuthara textiles. With the demand of kishuthara exceeding what the weavers can actually produce, there are machine-made kira being sold, [diluting] the authenticity of kishuthara and the hard work that goes into it,” says Choden.

Commercialisation and commodification are notable concerns, too. “We’re not in the business of arts and culture; we don’t want to sell our arts and culture,” says Khampa. “Bhutanese arts and crafts are deeply rooted in the spiritual teachings of Buddhism. A master craftsman can become an enlightened being in that field because they have practised it, they have lived that meaning of art. But if you are learning a skill just to sell it, you’ll never understand it. You would only understand markets, money, customers, challenges.

“If you are not living the art and the culture, you are not being true to yourself.”

Cultural conservation is proving to be a struggle between generations as well.

“Like any other country, young people are focused on digital progress and pursuing further education,” says Choden. “In western Bhutan, we have more job opportunities, so they leave their villages and towns in the east.” As a result, ageing generations are unable to pass down their know-how.

“Bhutan is known for living the culture, breathing the culture. With a younger generation less likely to pursue traditional arts and crafts as a profession, it diminishes our rich cultural heritage. When a master artisan passes away, we will have difficulty reviving this craft.”

Khampa says he believes in the evolution of arts and culture: “We don’t want it to be frozen in time. We want it to flow like a river, rather than be like a reservoir. But to make people understand, to live that particular arts and culture, we need to speak their language. We don’t want them to forget their culture.”

It’s true, Bhutan is changing. For those who romanticise the Buddhist kingdom in the Himalayan mountains as a mystical Shangri-La whose appeal is intrinsically tied to its “entrenched in the past” way of life, it’s time to lose the rose-tinted glasses. But while there are practical and ideological obstacles to overcome, Bhutan’s quest for modernity hasn’t taken the shine off its national cultural heritage. If anything, it has added fuel to the fire of legacy preservation, because to understand the Bhutan of 2026, it must still be experienced through its folk arts and crafts.

People such as Choden, who promote Bhutan to the world, are keenly aware of the tightrope they must walk between the loss of identity and being left in the dust.

“We cannot compete with machines in terms of production but we can make people understand that whatever a machine can produce, hands can do it far better the traditional way. We need to blend modernity and tradition so one does not overpower the other. We need to document the traditional techniques and raise awareness among locals and tourists about the value of authentic handmade products so they can cherish them.”

When it comes to the fate of zorig chusum, Khampa takes a guarded view: “It all depends on the people, if they are committed to continuing to live that art and culture. But we are optimistic that whatever we are doing right now [to educate and promote] will have a bearing on the future passion of Bhutanese people.”

Kinga Tshering feels hopeful about the path that lies ahead for lhazo. “Now, there are a lot more painters, compared with the last 10 years. It is going to increase again; because of schools like Zorig Chusum, anyone can learn to paint. People get to learn about the religion that they practise and there is work available, so they can see a future in this kind of art.”

Lepcha sees modernisation as motivation: “It gives me encouragement to preserve our textiles and our culture because I don’t want them to vanish into thin air someday; the more modern we become, the more I want to preserve thagzo. But we can do a lot more than just preserve; we can modernise and make tradition relevant to people today.”
