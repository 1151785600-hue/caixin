Indonesia’s Tokopedia denies mass lay-offs, but ‘restructuring’ deepens tech winter fears

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1045 words | 2026-07-09

Reports of sweeping lay-offs at Tokopedia, the Indonesian e-commerce giant majority-owned by China’s ByteDance, have renewed concerns about Southeast Asia’s largest digital economy as analysts warn the country’s tech winter shows few signs of easing.

The cuts have also raised questions over the future of one of Indonesia’s best-known home-grown digital champions, with analysts saying Tokopedia’s deeper integration into ByteDance’s ecosystem could shift more technology, product and strategic decisions outside the country.

Tokopedia, which once aspired to be the Alibaba of Indonesia, cut more than 450 technology roles earlier this month, leaving just 35 such workers at the company, CNBC Indonesia reported on July 2, citing an unnamed company source.

The outlet said Tokopedia employed 1,100 engineers and developers before ByteDance’s acquisition and that the new round of lay-offs had now reduced its original workforce of 2,500 to about 10 per cent, mostly in the business, trust and safety, and technology divisions.

CNBC Indonesia also reported that ByteDance’s engineering team in China would take over Tokopedia’s technology workload, citing another unnamed source that said “it will all be done in China”.

On Monday, Stephanie Susilo, executive director of TikTok and Tokopedia, denied the mass lay-offs, describing the staff movements as “ongoing workforce restructuring and internal mobility” within the Indonesian organisation.

“In this restructuring programme, some employees have chosen to take the compensation package, choosing to work elsewhere or be placed within the TikTok Tokopedia business group,” she said, without elaborating on how many workers were affected by the reorganisation.

The company was also hiring for 100 new jobs in Indonesia, she said.

In a statement to the Indonesian bourse on Saturday, GoTo Gojek Tokopedia, the minority owner of Tokopedia, said it respected the company’s management decision and that the restructuring would have “no material impact on [GoTo]’s share of Tokopedia’s net profit or loss”.

In 2024, ByteDance acquired a 75.01 per cent share in Tokopedia for US$1.5 billion, allowing it to merge TikTok Shop with Tokopedia after the Indonesian government shut down the Chinese e-commerce platform for several months, citing economic threats to small and medium-sized businesses.

The merger has resulted in several rounds of lay-offs since June 2024 that reduced the company’s workforce by more than 800, according to news reports.

‘Sudden’ lay-off

Workers for TikTok in ByteDance’s Jakarta office say they have also been affected.

A ByteDance employee told This Week in Asia that “90 to 95 per cent” of staff working on the same floor as him, including managers, were laid off.

“I did not expect at all to be laid off. This was so sudden,” said Dimas*, who was part of ByteDance’s content moderation team.

Dimas said he received an email about the lay-off on July 1 at 7am local time as he was heading to work.

The notice gutted him. “My mind was blank all day. I was still digesting everything. My stomach was acidic for a few days,” said the 26-year-old, who joined TikTok nearly three years ago.

On July 2, ByteDance held a town hall with the affected employees. Dimas said he attended the meeting, but “I personally don’t want to hear their excuses, because I knew that their reason would be ‘business decision’.”

August 1 will be Dimas’ last day at the company. He said TikTok would give him severance pay five times his salary but remained worried that his job prospects would be “narrow” due to the rapid adoption of artificial intelligence in content moderation.

Tech winter

Analysts warn the latest shake-up at Tokopedia underlines that Indonesia’s ongoing tech winter, which started in 2022, will not end any time soon.

“The cost of funding digital start-ups is relatively higher now compared to 2020 and 2021, when funding increased significantly due to low interest rates by [American central bank] the Fed,” said Nailul Huda, director of digital economy at the think tank Centre of Economic and Law Studies.

“In the past, it might not have been a problem if a company recorded losses for 10 years, as long as they could sell a strong growth story.”

Tokopedia’s restructuring also sparked concern that other companies could follow suit by moving their operations outside Indonesia, Nailul said, pointing out that Indonesian online travel booking platform Traveloka shifted its headquarters to Singapore last year.

“If TikTok doesn’t even believe in placing its main unit in Indonesia, [investors] will ask, ‘Why would we invest here?’ he said.

However, the funding correction in Indonesia is not necessarily a bad thing for the ecosystem’s longevity, according to Jianggan Li, chief executive at Singapore-based digital market research and analytical firm Momentum Works.

“For Indonesia’s e-commerce landscape, this marks the transition from an era of building local champions at all costs to one focused on scale, efficiency and ecosystem control,” he said.

“Shopee, TikTok Shop and Lazada are competing with increasingly sophisticated regional and global capabilities. Without a clear structural advantage, it will be extremely difficult for a local platform to survive as an independent full-stack e-commerce player.”

While integration into the ByteDance ecosystem might make Tokopedia’s operations more robust, it could dilute the company’s original vision, he said.

“The more sensitive issue is not the lay-offs themselves, but the perception that Indonesia’s national e-commerce champion is becoming the local operating arm of a global platform whose core technology and product decisions are increasingly made elsewhere,” Li said.

Aside from the capital crunch, Indonesia’s tech ecosystem in recent years has also been marred by the prosecution of several prominent tech executives, such as Gojek’s co-founder Nadiem Makarim, over their perceived failed policy and business decisions.

Li said there were still reasons to be optimistic.

A report by Google, Temasek, and Bain & Co estimated Indonesia’s digital economy at nearly US$100 billion in 2025.

“Indonesia remains too large and strategically important for global investors to ignore. However, capital will become far more selective, with greater emphasis on governance, sustainable unit economics and regulatory certainty,” Li said.

“I’m also optimistic that Indonesia can produce the next generation of internationally successful consumer brands, building on the legacy of companies like Indomie and Wings.”

*Name changed to protect interviewee’s identity
