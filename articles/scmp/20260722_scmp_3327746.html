Fuchsia Dunlop tells how she fell for China’s cuisine and names her perfect dish

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 3752 words | 2026-07-22

Fuchsia Dunlop is a British author and chef specialising in Chinese cuisine, particularly Sichuanese food. She has written seven popular books and is arguably the Western world’s most knowledgeable person about the diverse food cultures and cooking techniques of China’s various regions. In this interview, Dunlop talks about the comparisons that are often made between Chinese and Western cuisines, as well as her passion for China’s food culture and views on how it is changing.

What are your thoughts on mooncakes? Do you have a favourite flavour?

I enjoy the ritual of giving and sharing mooncakes around the Mid-Autumn Festival, especially when I’m at home in London, because it feels like another connection with China. I like the rich Cantonese mooncakes in small doses: a slice of a lotus seed paste mooncake with some green tea, for example. But my favourites are the savoury minced pork mooncakes made in Shanghai, and various Yunnan mooncakes. One classic Yunnan version is made with flaky pastry and stuffed with chopped Yunnan ham and sugar. I also love the Weishan mooncakes, which are larger and more like a regular cake, made with flour and eggs and sugar, with beautiful stuffings that might include red bean paste or rose petal jam.

How did your interest and research in Chinese cuisine begin, and has your enthusiasm changed over the years?

I went to China in 1994 as a British Council student. I attended Sichuan University as a postgraduate student in the history department.

But I had loved cooking more than anything since childhood. Finding myself in Chengdu, where the food was so exciting and so fresh and so different from any Chinese food I’d had before, I started learning to cook.

I persuaded the owners of little local restaurants near the university to let me study in their kitchens. And then this famous cooking school, the Sichuan Higher Institute of Cuisine, agreed to give me and a German friend some private cooking classes.

That’s how it began.

After I finished university, the teachers at the cooking school invited me to join a chef’s training course and I spent the following three months with 50 young people from Sichuan learning the basic skills of Sichuanese cooking.

When I went back to England I was struck by the fact that there really weren’t any Sichuan cookbooks in print that were in English.

There was this incredible Chinese region that was the size of a European country, but people in Britain knew virtually nothing about it, which is hard to believe now that Sichuan food has become so popular.

So I had this idea to write a Sichuan cookbook and over the next few years I made several trips back to Sichuan and did more research.

My first book came out in English in 2001. I never expected researching and writing about Chinese food to become my life’s work. But the more I learn – and China is so huge and the food is so diverse – the more I realise that writing about it can be a fascinating way to explore the country and Chinese culture.

People in China care so much about food: at a deep cultural level this seems to be how the Chinese have always expressed themselves.

How did your interest in Sichuan food extend to other regional cuisines?

My first book was quite successful, so afterwards I thought it would be interesting to look at another region. I decided to research Hunan cookery – and discovered that there was almost no material in English about Hunan food either.

I thought it would be interesting because it was another famously spicy region and clearly readers love to read spicy recipes and cook spicy Chinese food, which is very different from the Cantonese food that almost all the Chinese restaurants in the West were serving at the time.

In 2003 I went to Hunan for a couple of months and that was the start of my second book, Revolutionary Chinese Cookbook: Recipes from Hunan Province, which came out in 2006.

But in the meantime I had also visited Shanghai for the first time and that planted a little seed in my mind of interest in the cuisine of Jiangnan [the geographic region south of the Yangtze River].

Then in 2008 I started thinking that I’d really like to write a book about the region, and that eventually led to Land of Fish and Rice: Recipes from the Culinary Heart of China.

When I was studying in London more than five years ago, most of the Chinese restaurants were serving Sichuan food. Did that happen after your first book Sichuan Cookery?

When my Sichuan cookbook came out in 2001, there were no Sichuanese restaurants in central London. And all the Chinese supermarkets were Cantonese, so you couldn’t get authentic Sichuanese ingredients such as Pi Xian broad bean paste, for example. You could not get good, zingy Sichuan peppercorns because all the ingredients available were intended to be used for Cantonese cooking.

When my book came out, a lot of readers were amazed: at that time hardly anyone in Britain, even food writers, chefs and food experts, had tasted real Sichuan peppercorns.

People had no acquaintance with the mala – numbing and spicy – flavour of Sichuanese cuisine, which combines chilli heat with the tingling sensation on the lips that you get from Sichuan pepper. It’s hard to imagine now because mapo tofu, for example, is so well known, but at that time it wasn’t.

In 2006, there was a game changer – the first Sichuan restaurant in central London, which caused a lot of excitement and got fantastic reviews in all the newspapers.

That was the beginning of this big change that ended up as you described, by the time you were in London, with Sichuan food being a real feature of the London dining scene and everyone being familiar with it, not just in London, but in Manchester, Birmingham, Nottingham, Edinburgh.

In the past, there weren’t so many Chinese people living in Britain and the vast majority of Chinese restaurants catered to people who weren’t Chinese.

And I think the hugely significant change is that now we have so many Chinese students and other Chinese residents and visitors in Britain, so there’s a whole new generation of Chinese restaurants that are designed purely to cater for Chinese tastes. They don’t have to adapt their flavours to Western tastes.

So we now have the opportunity to try the kind of Chinese food that people in China want to eat themselves. And it also reflects changing culinary fashions in China.

Sichuan spice is not a problem. Sichuan pepper, if you haven’t had it before, is quite strange and disconcerting. But one of the things I always tried to do was to explain it to people and prepare them for it. So for most people, when they try Sichuan pepper in a cool environment where they feel relaxed, it’s strange at first, but then it’s wonderful.

I mean, why wouldn’t you love Sichuan food?

According to your research, what is the biggest misconception about Chinese food in the West, and how did it come about?

There are several misconceptions that have come out of a particular history. Early Chinese food was almost exclusively Cantonese, based on a single region, but also adapted to Western tastes.

And in Britain, the most familiar kind of Chinese food was the takeaway, which was extremely simplified – no bones, no shells, not so many vegetables, lots of fried foods. It was not designed for Chinese people to eat themselves. But this style of food really shaped perceptions of Chinese cuisine, both in Britain and in a similar way in the US.

The worst stereotype of Chinese food has been that it should be cheap. People will pay a lot of money for Japanese sushi, but while they may think Chinese food is tasty, they don’t necessarily see it as the kind of sophisticated, elite cuisine that you would pay serious money for.

Another misunderstanding is the idea that it’s unhealthy – again, because popular takeaway food involved a lot of fried food, few vegetables, few soups and so on. There is also a certain amount of Western prejudice against monosodium glutamate, which is perfectly normal in China, but Westerners don’t use it in home cooking or quality cooking, and so it’s very much stigmatised.

And then the other side of that coin is the idea that the Chinese eat a lot of weird, scary stuff like chicken feet and snakes.

The Chinese certainly do eat many ingredients that are seen as unusual or strange in the West, but this has always been seen through the lens of a notion that the Chinese eat such things because they were very poor and desperate, which is a terrible simplification, because obviously very rich Chinese people also like to eat duck tongues and goose feet.

There are other reasons Chinese people eat a lot of foods that Westerners reject. And they include this pleasure of texture, the pleasure of the sensation of food in the mouth, and also the Chinese idea of unusual and exotic food – the idea that since, for example, duck tongue is very small and there is only one of them in each duck, it’s a great privilege to have that particular part.

I think for Westerners it’s been a very strange picture because on the one hand, Chinese food has been hugely successful, everybody loves it. There’s no other foreign cuisine that’s been more popular globally than Chinese in a way, but it’s also been saddled with these rather negative prejudices that are very outdated.

As someone who is Chinese, you will know very well that these stereotypes are ridiculous. Because in China, yes, you do have cheap peasant food and street food, but you also have extremely extravagant, time-consuming gongfu food or banquet cooking, which is another aspect of Chinese food culture.

And then in terms of eating “weird” things, as I said, there are gastronomic reasons for this as well as economic reasons.

Finally in terms of health, I think no other culture is as insistent on the link between diet and health as the Chinese. In China, food has been seen as medicine for more than 2,000 years. And Chinese people talk about food and health all the time.

When I learned to cook Chinese food, I learned how to make vegetables taste delicious and how to eat food that is balanced and healthy as well as delicious. I think that is one of the most impressive things about Chinese cuisine.

What misconceptions do Chinese people have about Western food?

Just as Westerners can be very dismissive of Chinese food, Chinese people can be very dismissive of Western food.

All over China, people have told me that Western food is simple and boring, and a lot of people seem to think that it just consists of hamburgers and sandwiches.

And then recently there has been all this talk about “white people food”, which is just seen as completely uncouth and boring and depressing. And then there’s a very weird stereotype about British food – “dark cuisine”.

There’s an old-fashioned British dish from Cornwall called a stargazy pie and it’s a pie with fish heads poking up through the pastry. It’s a very obscure regional dish that I’ve never tried and most British people have never tried. But an English woman wrote a blog post about it that went viral in China, so it’s now almost certainly better known in China than it is at home.

I would say that a lot of Chinese people who have not had the chance to travel abroad make these huge generalisations about Western food that are just as silly as those some Westerners make about all Chinese food.

I’ve also found it very surprising that a lot of Chinese people have told me over the years that they think Western food is healthier because there’s this sort of tradition of scientific nutrition where you’re told how many calories and how much protein and that sort of thing.

But I find that very strange because it seems to me that people in China, certainly the older generation, have a knowledge of healthy eating that is incredibly deep and incredibly useful, whereas I think a lot of Westerners are actually quite confused about how to eat healthily.

There is also this idea that Chinese food can be quite oily and Western food is not. But again, I think that’s really strange because if you think about traditional Chinese food, it involves a lot of soups and steamed dishes and boiled dishes, and traditional Chinese home cooking does not use a lot of oil.

I think it’s quite sad to see that people in China are now making a lot of the same mistakes that we’ve already made in the West of cooking less and eating less fresh food. It is one of the unfortunate side effects of modernisation and industrialisation, isn’t it?

What do you think of Westernised Chinese food, such as the fast food restaurants popular in the US, like Panda Express?

You have to remember that Chinese food is not just the food of the country of China. It’s also the food of a huge Chinese diaspora.

So Chinese food is already part of global culture and it’s a presence in every city practically all over the world. Not only Chinese people living in the West want to eat Chinese food.

Chinese food doesn’t have to be one thing. I think people are always inventing and adapting and borrowing ideas from each other. For example, I recently wrote an article about Shanghainese Western food, which is a bit like Hong Kong-style Western food.

So in China you have versions of Western food that are completely separate from what Westerners actually want to eat. They are adapted to Chinese tastes, and I think in the West you also have Chinese food that’s not designed for Chinese people. It’s made for Westerners. I don’t have a problem with that. I think it’s fun to be inventive.

The only problem is when people start to judge Chinese food by Panda Express and think that it represents the cuisine of a very large and sophisticated gastronomic nation.

We just have to be a bit careful about judging it.

In many Chinese cities, the highly rated and expensive restaurants tend to be Japanese. This seems to be the case in other major cities around the world. Why is Japanese food more likely to be considered fine dining than Chinese food?

I think the status of food is always about money.

Japan got rich before China in the late 20th century, so its food acquired high status at a time when China was quite closed to the outside world and was not rich and did not have high status.

It’s the same within China with regional cuisines. For example, when there were many rich merchants from Anhui, Anhui food was considered one of the “eight major cuisines”. But when I was a student in Sichuan in the 1990s, all of the expensive restaurants were Cantonese because places like Hong Kong and Shenzhen got rich first.

What’s interesting now is that China is getting richer and more powerful, so I think the status of China and Chinese culture is rising. The problem at the moment is that we don’t have enough chefs in the UK. We have very tight immigration rules in Britain and it’s almost impossible these days for chefs to come here and work.

If we had more free movement of chefs, Chinese food here in Britain would become more ambitious and more expensive.

Do you face any challenges in defending Chinese food in the West?

The whole environment is becoming more complicated because of geopolitical tensions. Obviously the relationship between the West and China has become more complicated.

But I think in that context it’s even more important to find ways to communicate with each other, and it’s also incredibly important to try and understand each other.

So, for example, it’s fantastic that China is now more open to foreign tourists, because foreigners will go to China and actually see something of the country, meet people and have a real experience.

At the same time, the first encounters most foreigners have with Chinese culture often involve Chinese food, and it’s an area that’s not so politically contentious, a space where you can actually learn a lot about China and have a fantastic experience of China without being so complicated.

Have there been any obstacles to cultural exchange between China and Britain over the years? Could Chinese food serve as a bridge?

The real difference is that, as I’ve described, in the 1990s, when I started doing this work, the food was all Cantonese, now it’s very regional.

So we have restaurants in London that serve Sichuan food, as well as dishes from Chongqing, Hunan, and the northeast. We have some quite wonderful Shanghai supper clubs. Although we only see a small proportion of Chinese food because China is huge, already people can see that Chinese food is not one thing, that it’s very diverse.

And we now even have more northern food, so handmade noodles and breads, like Xian street food, that can just show different sides of Chinese food culture.

Some people say that if the Chinese ate less meat it would help slow down global warming. This is very controversial in China because meat is an integral part of Chinese cuisine. What are your views?

The truth is that we should all be eating less meat if we want to tackle climate change. The situation in China is that most people didn’t eat much meat until the 1990s. And now, with the reform and opening up that followed, everyone wants to eat more meat – more like the extravagant way we eat in the West.

So objectively it is a huge problem that you have over a billion people suddenly eating more meat. But I can understand the Chinese view that it’s really unfair.

Climate change is a global problem. It would be great if Chinese people were more aware of the role that eating meat plays in climate change, but people in the West also have to change their diets and eat less meat.

And I think traditional Chinese cuisine is a fantastic resource and inspiration for that. Because in traditional Chinese cuisine there are so many ways of using a little bit of meat to make vegetarian ingredients taste fantastic – like mapo tofu and dry-fried green beans.

There are all these dishes where you have a little bit of meat and lots of vegetables that are really delicious. We can learn a lot from traditional Chinese food about how to eat less meat.

But the main thing is that it is unfair for Westerners to put the whole burden on Chinese consumers without looking at their own behaviour.

How have Chinese eating habits changed over the decades? How much of the change is political?

The first thing is that so many young people don’t really cook. The older generation, when I lived in Chengdu, were really excellent cooks. Not only did they cook daily meals, they also made pickles, sausages and other preserves. You can really see how these skills are now being lost.

It’s also quite interesting to see how Chinese people are eating more industrialised and prepared food.

At the same time in Britain, there’s a book that came out two years ago about ultra-processed foods, looking at the harmful effects of eating too much processed food, and I see China making the same mistakes we’ve made, and it’s a real shame.

China has a fantastic food culture, which is a huge resource for how to eat healthily and deliciously.

So it’s sad to see that younger people are losing touch with that and I hope that as people in China become more health conscious, which they are, and as they become more aware of the environmental impact of how we eat, which I hope will become more widespread, then they will be able to see their own culture as an invaluable resource for eating more healthily and sustainably.

But I think it is more about economic changes. When I lived in Chengdu in the 1990s, there was very little manufactured food. There were no supermarkets and everyone went to fresh markets to buy vegetables and fish.

And now there’s a huge expansion of food production and factory farming in China. There’s mass production of meat and also of ready meals, as you mentioned, and even restaurants are serving food that’s pre-prepared.

It’s all driven by the same technology and economics as in the West, and also the whole sort of takeaway food system, the fact that you can order anything at home that you don’t have to cook.

Which dish do you think best represents Chinese cuisine?

If I had to choose one, I would probably choose mapo tofu.

The reason is that it’s a really good example of a recipe that uses a little bit of meat and very delicious fermented bean spices to give an amazing flavour to a plant food. For me, it’s a really good example of how Chinese food offers ways to eat healthily and sustainably but without sacrificing pleasure, because this dish is utterly delicious.

Do you have any ongoing projects?

At the moment I’m writing another cookbook, and it’s not just about one region. It’s more of a simple homestyle Chinese cookbook, a bit like my previous book Every Grain of Rice.

So it’s designed to give readers the tools of some traditional Chinese techniques to cook wonderful food at home, not always with Chinese ingredients, but to cook food that’s really delicious and quite healthy and exciting.
