Philippines’ plan to fortify coastal defences: deterrence or target?

SCMP | FREE | 1081 words | 2026-07-17

A Philippine coastal defence project in Palawan province could complicate Chinese operations near the Spratly Islands, but is unlikely to close Manila’s military gap with Beijing and could turn one of the country’s most important naval hubs into a more prominent target in a crisis, analysts have said.

On Monday, the Puerto Princesa City Council approved a Department of National Defence proposal allowing the Armed Forces of the Philippines to establish Coastal Defence Regiment assets and logistical facilities at Oyster Bay in Bahile village, one of the Philippine Navy’s most strategic bases.

Oyster Bay is a key staging and resupply hub for Philippine outposts in the Kalayaan Island Group – Manila’s name for the features it occupies in the disputed Spratly Islands in the South China Sea.

The defence ministry has not publicly disclosed what assets will be deployed, though local officials said during council deliberations on Monday that the project could involve missile interceptor or shore-based anti-ship missile systems.

During the session, Councillor Herbert Dilig voiced support for the proposal but sought clarification on the military assets planned for the site.

“To be clear, I support this. Our country needs it, especially given the current security situation,” he said. “But the purpose is dual: to protect and to invite something that needs protection.”

The Philippines operates the Indian-made BrahMos supersonic shore-based anti-ship missile system, acquired under a US$375 million deal signed in 2022, and has also bought the Israeli-made Spyder air defence system, designed to counter aircraft, drones and incoming missiles.

Experts said deploying BrahMos missiles and interceptor systems in Palawan would strengthen deterrence without fundamentally altering the Philippines’ defence posture in the South China Sea.

“It expands it to the west, closer to the Spratlys, but will not meaningfully address the country’s asymmetric gap with China or its ability to seriously threaten Chinese military assets in the Pacific,” said Hunter Marston, director of the Lowy Institute’s Southeast Asia programme and lead on its Asia Power Index.

While Marston said the deployment would strengthen deterrence, he added that it would make a frontline location more exposed in a future crisis. He also warned it could heighten sensitivities with Vietnam, which has overlapping claims in the Spratlys, unless Manila reassured Hanoi the move was purely defensive.

Sylwia Monika Gorska, a political analyst at Britain’s University of Central Lancashire specialising in international relations, said the plan would strengthen the Philippines’ ability to put Chinese surface combatants and support vessels at greater risk around the Spratlys.

Palawan played a critical operational role as the main staging area for sustaining Philippine outposts in the Kalayaan Island Group, she said, with Oyster Bay serving as the key logistics and support hub for those missions.

Gorska added that the deployment would force China’s navy to allocate additional surveillance, targeting and strike resources so its surface forces could operate with lower risk, raising the military costs Beijing would have to weigh in any future use of force around the Spratlys.

The trade-off, she said, was that Oyster Bay itself would become a more valuable military target in a potential conflict.

“Once a conventional conflict begins, fixed infrastructure such as Oyster Bay inevitably becomes a higher-priority target. The key question then is whether the AFP [Philippine military] can restore the force’s combat effectiveness after the base comes under attack,” she said.

The Philippines is accelerating its 10-year Re-Horizon 3 military modernisation programme, prioritising an integrated air and missile defence system, advanced missile systems, mid-range launchers and enhanced radar capabilities to strengthen deterrence amid growing regional security challenges.

American and Philippine troops have previously tested the M142 High Mobility Artillery Rocket System in Palawan, which US military officials have described as “key terrain” because it controls access between the Sulu Sea and the South China Sea.

Vina Nadjibulla, vice-president of research and strategy at the Asia Pacific Foundation of Canada, said BrahMos was more relevant to deterring a high-end military escalation than managing day-to-day incidents at sea.

She said the system had to sit within a broader architecture that included coastguard capacity, maritime surveillance, legal and diplomatic pressure, alliance support, resilient resupply and credible crisis-management channels.

For Nadjibulla, the goal is not simply to place missiles closer to the front line but to create uncertainty for any adversary.

“If an adversary cannot be sure where the launchers are, whether they can be destroyed quickly, or what other assets are feeding them targeting data, then deterrence becomes more credible,” she said.

“The Philippines is still building that ecosystem. The BrahMos deployment is important, but its real strategic value will depend on whether Manila can connect missiles, sensors, logistics, command-and-control and partner support into a resilient archipelagic defence network.”

Marston agreed that additional missile deployments would enhance the Philippines’ deterrent posture, noting that while Manila already had BrahMos batteries in Luzon, closer to China’s growing presence around Scarborough Shoal, a new site in Palawan would expand the armed forces’ operational coverage across the South China Sea.

“⁠The new missile sites are a net positive for the Philippines as forward deployment and expanded deterrence outweigh the risks of high-value targets,” he said. “By spreading out its missile sites, the Philippines also makes it harder for the PLA [China’s military] to wipe out all of those potential targets.”

Gorska said the deployment’s greatest contribution might be time, because slowing Chinese naval operations could create opportunities for Manila to reinforce exposed positions, coordinate with allies and make political decisions before a crisis developed further.

She said long-range anti-ship missiles were only as effective as a commander’s ability to detect and track enemy vessels, making maritime domain awareness just as critical as the missiles themselves.

Manila’s security partners could provide this service, Gorska added, with the United States contributing intelligence sharing and alliance cooperation, and Japan strengthening Philippine surveillance through coastal radars and information sharing.

More broadly, Nadjibulla said the proposed missile deployment reflected a shift in Manila’s defence strategy towards investing in national capabilities that reinforced its legal claims and raised the costs of coercion.

“This is not a magic solution,” she said. “BrahMos changes the military equation at the margins, especially in a crisis or high-end contingency. It does not eliminate the asymmetry with China, and it does not remove the need for diplomacy, crisis-management mechanisms and careful signalling.”
