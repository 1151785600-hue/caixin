Hong Kong survivors push to close ‘consent loophole’ in reforms to sex crime laws

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 641 words | 2026-07-12

Hong Kong women’s rights advocates and sexual assault survivors have urged authorities to close a loophole that allows defendants to claim an honest but mistaken belief in consent before a sexual act, warning that it could undermine proposed reforms.

The Association Concerning Sexual Violence Against Women and survivor group We Are X said on Wednesday that the government’s updated stricter definitions of consent should also be extended to this long-standing defence.

“If we agree that the defendant has to take steps to ascertain consent, there should be guidelines in the law on what these steps mean. Otherwise, the [proposed safeguard] would be a dead letter,” Chelsea Ma Pik-kwan, vice-chairwoman of the association, said.

The Security Bureau this week launched a month-long public consultation on sweeping reforms to Hong Kong’s sexual offences laws, many of which centre on consent.

Proposals include a statutory definition, provisions on how consent may be given or withdrawn and a non-exhaustive list of 11 circumstances that would not constitute consent.

These include situations where the victim does not communicate consent, either verbally or through conduct, or is mistaken about the nature or purpose of the act.

The bureau said it would follow the Legal Reform Commission’s suggestion to draw on England and Wales’ Sexual Offences Act from 2003, requiring courts to assess “all circumstances”, including “any steps” the accused took to ascertain consent, when determining whether a mistaken belief was reasonable.

But Ma, a non-practising barrister, said this criterion was too broad to effectively prevent defendants from using subjective beliefs to persuade the jury that they were mistaken, a concern already raised in other jurisdictions.

“For example, in the context of sexual assault, if the defendant said they had observed the victim’s facial expressions, does this count as taking steps to ascertain their consent?” Ma said.

The vice-chairwoman said that current proposals had no guidelines on whether actions such as informing the victim of an intended sexual act without waiting for a response or merely considering steps to ascertain consent, would suffice as a defence.

The association urged authorities to draw on Canada’s criminal code to restrict the use of this defence.

Under Canadian law, defendants cannot invoke this defence in situations such as if their mistaken belief arose from self-induced drunkenness, recklessness or if the victim did not expressly affirm the sexual act.

Ma also cited New South Wales’ law, under which any steps taken to confirm consent must be reasonable and taken before the sexual act to invoke the “genuine but mistaken” belief defence.

A member of We Are X, who identified herself only as Jessie, said this defence could become a gaping hole in the legal overhaul.

“My biggest concern is that while the laws are comprehensively written, the ‘genuine but mistaken belief’ defence is a loophole that could cause the reform to fall short at the last mile,” Jessie said.

Another member of the concern group, who wished to be identified only as Janelle, said the proposed definitions of consent alone could not prevent perpetrators from abusing the defence.

“The point isn’t what more scenarios to add into the non-exhaustive list, but that if there are no restrictions to use this defence, no matter how long the list gets, it is still possible to be bypassed,” she said.

But the sexual assault survivors’ group agreed that the scope of the proposed overhaul was extensive, saying some of their roughly 50 members felt their trauma had been validated by the inclusion of forms of sexual assault previously left uncategorised.

“From not knowing what it was, to seeing the category of ‘spilling liquid onto the victim’ in the consultation paper, it felt like a massive affirmation [to what I had experienced],” a member, who wished to be identified only as Charlene, said.
