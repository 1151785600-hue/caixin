Employee training hours in Hong Kong hit 14-year high amid AI push: survey

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 659 words | 2026-07-12

The average number of training hours an employee in Hong Kong undergoes has hit a 14-year high as companies push for greater technology adoption and skills upgrading, a survey has found.

According to the poll released by the Hong Kong Institute of Human Resource Management on Friday, average annual training hours per employee reached 19.4 last year.

This represented a 6.8 per cent increase from 18.1 hours in 2024, marking the second consecutive year of growth and the highest level recorded since 2011, when it stood at 19.9 hours.

The study collected responses from 127 companies across 18 different industries – including business services, banking, retail, catering and information technology – from February 10 to April 30 this year, covering nearly 80,000 full-time employees.

The additional hours reflected a growing emphasis among businesses on staff training, particularly a dual focus on artificial intelligence (AI) and soft skills, according to the findings.

“The increase in training hours is also driven by the fact that AI now affects colleagues across all levels, from senior and middle management to junior staff,” said Charles Ho Long-chau, co-chairman of the institute’s learning and development committee.

Leadership development remained the top priority for employers at 50 per cent, while mastering generative AI for learning surged from sixth place to second at 49 per cent.

“While the importance of AI has certainly increased, leadership training remains crucial in areas such as communication building, teamwork development and strategic deployment,” said Chester Tsang Wing-cheong, the institute’s executive council member.

The results also showed that 73 per cent of surveyed companies had a budget for employee training and development in 2025, with actual spending accounting for 2.5 per cent of total annual base salaries.

Ho said the results reflected growing caution among some businesses in carrying out training.

“This does not mean employers are cutting down on investments and resources. Given the longer training hours, it means businesses are leveraging more efficient and cost-effective methods, such as AI-assisted or digital learning tools, to deliver more training,” he added.

According to the results of a supplementary quick poll of 96 local employers from April 27 to May 8, 29 per cent of companies had already integrated AI-assisted tools into their employee training systems. Another 14 per cent plan to introduce them by the end of 2026 and another 24 per cent aim to do so next year.

Ho projected the dual focus on AI and soft skills would remain corporate training priorities as AI technologies continued to evolve rapidly and it was crucial for employees to concurrently upgrade their soft skills.

“I also suggest that companies include more training on AI governance and security, as these factors significantly influence how broadly AI is adopted across the organisation,” he added.

Tsang said the increase in training hours might put some pressure on employees, as learning new skills could be challenging, but it was also a way to adapt to the evolving technology and business environment.

In a recent interview with the South China Morning Post, Secretary for Innovation, Technology and Industry Sun Dong warned that AI would “exceed all previous industrial revolutions” and heavily impact graduates and workers in traditional sectors.

The minister pledged to accelerate training and industry development to create new jobs, adding that Hong Kong was preparing for the paradigm shift by creating proprietary large language models, launching AI projects for public use and rolling out a HK$50 million education programme.

Sun noted that employment struggles among local fresh graduates reflected a global trend with even students from top universities in the United States feeling the pressure.

Earlier in May, Secretary for Labour and Welfare Chris Sun Yuk-han told the Legislative Council that job vacancies suitable for university graduates fell from about 80,000 in 2022 to around 31,000 in 2025 – a drop of 61 per cent.

Sun said the adoption of AI had begun to affect demand for entry-level positions, citing an International Data Corporation study that found more than 60 per cent of surveyed enterprises worldwide expected to reduce entry-level hiring within the next three years.
