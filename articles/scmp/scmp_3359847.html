Physical AI ‘space race’: can Europe compete with China and the US in humanoid robotics?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 697 words | 2026-07-08

European firms say they are fighting to secure a foothold in physical AI – the integration of artificial intelligence into robotics and machinery – as China and the United States take an early lead in the sector, with industry insiders warning the continent faces the threat of further deindustrialisation if it fails to establish a competitive industry.

“You see China and the US … because of AI … typically they are considered the leaders, but do not count out Europe,” said David Kehr, president of Humanoid Robotics, a division of German firm Schaeffler Technologies.

Traditionally focused on automotive and industrial components, the Bavaria-based company entered the sector in January through a partnership with UK start-up Humanoid. Under the deal, Schaeffler supplies the actuators – mechanical joints and motors that power a robot’s movement – and plans to deploy the British firm’s AI models across its own factories.

Speaking on the main stage at Machina – an inaugural one-day summit held in Paris on Tuesday that positions itself as the continent’s leading event dedicated to the industry – Kehr identified safety standards and faster training speeds as the industry’s primary hurdles.

Europe could lead on global safety standards, he said, but cautioned that lawmakers must strike a careful balance. Regulation must not become so heavy that it “stifles” the continental humanoid and physical AI market, he warned.

The uphill battle facing European players was starkly reflected on the summit’s main stage, where most presenting companies were headquartered in the US. European firms, by contrast, made up only a small fraction of the speaker line-up.

It’s going to be a global race... And I think it’s anyone’s to win

Jeff Cardenas, Apptronik

Chinese humanoid heavyweights, including Unitree Robotics and AgiBot, also had a strong presence across the exhibition floor.

The anxiety echoing through the summit – and across the continent – stems from fears that failing to embrace the physical AI wave could lead to another wave of European deindustrialisation.

“If we do nothing, it’s China that will take the leadership – China is in first position,” warned Olivier Scalabre, head of BCG France, during a separate economic forum in Aix-en-Provence last week. He estimated that up to 40 per cent of Europe’s industrial production could be threatened should the continent fail to adapt.

Industry group euRobotics echoed this urgency in a statement published in June, calling on the continent to establish its own robotics agenda to tackle pressing challenges that it said only capable systems could address.

On the exhibition floor, executives agreed the stakes could not be higher.

“Robots are the space race of our time,” said Jeff Cardenas, CEO of American humanoid start-up Apptronik. “Robots are going to be a part of every industry and grow over the coming decades.”

Cardenas noted that the sector was navigating its own version of the personal computer’s “spreadsheet and word-processing phase” in the 1980s, where the challenge was to prove commercial viability through real-world business cases. He projected that millions of robots would be deployed across “every facet of life” over the next decade.

Yet commercialisation faces a critical bottleneck: the immense amount of data required to train robots for complex, real-world tasks.

Kehr noted that the industry needed to train robots at a much faster pace – a challenge of unprecedented scale. “Physical AI will require 1,000 times more data than a self-driving large language model,” said Steve Xie, CEO of Chinese start-up Lightwheel, which builds data solutions to train robots.

Supply remained low despite the enormous demand, Xie stated.

Unlike Tesla, which can harvest real-world data from millions of cars on the road, the robotics sector has no equivalent fleet. This gap made software simulation – using digital environments to safely train AI models before deploying them into physical hardware – the “only scalable path” to accelerating robot training, Xie said.

However, because the challenge remained unsolved, industry insiders said the global leaderboard was far from locked in – and that Europe still had a chance to catch up.

“It’s going to be a global race,” said Cardenas of Apptronik. “And I think it’s anyone’s to win.”
