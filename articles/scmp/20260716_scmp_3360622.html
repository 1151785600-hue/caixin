China is now world’s No 1 science power, top Russian scientist Artem Oganov says

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1272 words | 2026-07-16

Leading Russian chemist and materials scientist Artem Oganov, who received China’s top international science and technology award, said the honour from the world’s No 1 science power was a sign he was “on the right track”.

Oganov, a distinguished professor at the Skolkovo Institute of Science and Technology in Moscow and long-time China collaborator, and eight other experts were awarded the International Science and Technology Cooperation Award by Chinese President Xi Jinping during a ceremony in Beijing last week.

After Oganov visited China in 2008, he told colleagues and friends in Europe that the country was “on the way” to being No 1 in both industry and science, but said this was met with scepticism at the time.

He told the South China Morning Post on Monday that, 18 years on, “China is No 1 – in industry, in economy, and indeed also in science.”

Noting that America used to be the top scientific power, he pointed to the fact that China now accounts for a third of global scientific publications, adding that it was not just numbers, but “quality”.

China holds the top spot in the 2026 Nature Index – an analysis of over 125,000 research articles published in 178 leading publications in 2025 – with the US in second place.

“I knew that [China] will rise to the top position, but I did not expect that it will be so fast and so strong,” he said.

“We live now in very dramatic times when politicians, especially Western politicians, burn bridges.

“[The] future belongs to those who build the bridges, not those who burn them. And the most natural bridge between different cultures and nations is science,” he added.

Oganov said being recognised with the “highest award” of his decorated career gave him confidence that he was on the right path, and that his journey with China was not over yet.

“I keep coming to China every year, spending at least one month there. We have new plans, new ambitions. It’s going on an interesting upward trajectory,” he said.

Oganov, born in 1975, is a theoretical crystallographer, chemist, physicist, mineralogist and materials scientist.

He graduated from Moscow State University in 1997 and received a doctorate from University College London in 2002, after which he served as a senior scientist and group leader at ETH Zurich in Switzerland, where he received a postdoctoral qualification in 2007.

From 2008 to 2017, he was a professor at Stony Brook University in New York. And in 2015, he became a professor at the Skolkovo Institute of Science and Technology, according to the Universal Scientific Education and Research Network.

Oganov currently serves as the chief scientific officer of SberUniversity and is founding chairman of the scientific committee for the Vyzov Prize, Russia’s own science award.

He is well-known for developing the USPEX crystal structure prediction program, which has been used by researchers and companies around the world to make breakthroughs in new materials discovery.

Oganov said his relationship with China began in the late 1990s during his years at University College London, when he realised that his friends were mostly Russians and Chinese.

“For many Western people, Russians and Chinese were second-class people. I’m sorry to say it, but that is what it felt [like],” he said.

“I found that a lot of stereotypes that we were fed, especially through Western press, about China and Chinese people are completely wrong.”

In 2008, he was given the opportunity to be an invited professor at the Chinese University of Hong Kong for a month. During this trip to Hong Kong and later other cities in mainland China, Oganov said he learned more about Chinese history, including the century of humiliation.

He said he also learned about how China had, through “extremely clever strategic planning of its government, regained, little by little, its former glory”.

He said China achieved this “not by humiliating and destroying others like ‘Make America Great Again’”, but by building itself up.

In 2011, Oganov applied for and was selected to be a part of China’s 1,000 talents programme, a state-sponsored recruitment scheme to attract top global scientists.

He created his own laboratory at the Northwestern Polytechnical University in Xian in 2013, which later became the International Centre for Materials Discovery. Oganov served as the international institute’s founding director before delegating the centre in 2020 to his colleague and former PhD student, Professor Niu Haiyang.

After Covid-19 restrictions ended, he signed another contract with the Xinjiang Technical Institute of Physics and Chemistry, and said he now mostly went to Urumqi when visiting China.

The whole journey with China has been one huge surprise

Artem Oganov

In 2019, he received China’s Friendship Award, which is given to foreign experts who have made contributions to the country’s economic and social progress.

According to Northwestern Polytechnical, Oganov has been involved in building transnational collaborative research platforms and has jointly trained faculty members and graduate students, particularly in the field of materials genome engineering.

Among Oganov’s former postdoctoral students is Ma Yanming, now president of Zhejiang University and one of the country’s most influential physicists.

“The whole journey with China has been one huge surprise. I have worked with remarkably talented people and I’m extremely proud of them,” he said.

In the 2026 Nature Index Research Leaders, four of the top five leading institutions by research output are in China, including the Chinese Academy of Sciences (CAS) in first place, while Harvard University took third.

CAS leads in several subject areas, including biological, earth and environmental, physical sciences, applied sciences, and chemistry. Harvard took the top spot in health sciences and social sciences.

Oganov noted that Russia also held a leading position in certain fields and had its own school of thought that was different to those of China, Europe and America.

“When we search for the truth, it’s very important for us to see nature not from one angle, but from other angles. And this is where different schools of thought come into play,” he said.

“The future belongs to international collaboration. Science cannot be isolated. If science in a given country becomes isolated, it dies.”

Oganov also noted the China Initiative, where Chinese scientists or scientists who collaborated with China were targeted and discriminated against in American academia.

He said a fellow recipient of China’s science and technology award was Zhang Shoucheng, a physicist and professor at Stanford University who died in 2018, with the official cause of death being suicide.

“Because of his strong connections with China, he was bullied by [the US Federal Bureau of Investigation] and committed suicide,” Oganov said.

“For about six years before that, every year he was named as the most likely Nobel laureate in physics.”

Oganov said he believed the world was moving towards a third world war, with some regions in an informational or economic war rather than a hot war.

“It seems that one pool of countries wants to stop the progress of another pool of countries and redistribute world power from the current status quo,” he said.

“I think it will end in the redistribution of the world map, redistribution of the world power, like every world war does.”

But he expected that, in the end, “peace will prevail”.

Oganov said that while he was “just one small human being”, small people could make big changes.

“The bridges that I’m building are not only between myself and Chinese people and Chinese scientists but also between institutions.”

China’s International Science and Technology Cooperation Award has many prestigious recipients, including multiple Nobel laureates. This year, recipients came from Russia, the US, Belgium, Portugal, Australia, Germany, the Netherlands and France – including 1987 Nobel chemistry laureate Jean-Marie Lehn.
