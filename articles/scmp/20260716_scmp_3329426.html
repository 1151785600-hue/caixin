Liu Qian on Chinese innovation, the US rivalry and women holding up half the sky

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 3764 words | 2026-07-16

Liu Qian is the founder of Wusawa Advisory and formerly the managing director of The Economist Group in Greater China. She is also a prominent advocate for gender equality, and the only Chinese representative in the core working group of the UN Women Leaders Network. In this interview, she discusses how Chinese innovation differs from Western innovation, where the US-China rivalry is headed and the critical role of women’s voices in policymaking.

This interview first appeared in SCMP Plus. For other interviews in the Open Questions series, click here.

In recent months, much of the discourse around China’s economy has centred on its external challenges, particularly on how US tariffs might impact Chinese exports. Is the rise in global trade protectionism a significant cause for concern, or is this an overemphasised aspect of China’s economic reality?

To start, it’s important to first note that the current US-China rivalry extends beyond trade and has expanded into areas such as technology, ideologies and global collaboration. If this unhealthy competition continues, it will hurt not only both countries but also the world.

That said, in terms of trade and economics, the concern has been exaggerated. At the peak of exports’ importance to the Chinese economy, they comprised around 36 per cent of China’s GDP. Today, they make up only about half of that, after accounting for roughly 19 per cent of China’s GDP last year. And within that 19 per cent, exports to the US made up only 14.7 per cent of the total, behind the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (Asean) and the European Union.

If you do the maths, that means exports to the US made up around 2.8 per cent of China’s GDP. So, even if the tariff war were to wipe out the entire US export market, it would only impact about 2 to 3 per cent of China’s economy.

China’s largest export destination is now Asean. Even though part of the exports to Asean might eventually go to the US – let’s say, hypothetically, half of the 16 per cent that goes to Asean – it would still represent only a small proportion of China’s GDP.

Obviously, there are broader and longer-term elements to consider regarding tariff wars. For instance, unemployment will hurt private consumption and social stability; uncertainties will cloud business sentiment and investment strategies; and supply chains will need to adjust to accommodate the shifting demand.

But we have seen exporters adapt resiliently and creatively – via different routes, to new markets, and with different products or even packaging. And exports have generally held steady this year.

More importantly, there are fast-emerging export destinations beyond the US. China’s exports to Brazil, for instance, grew by 23.3 per cent last year, and exports to Africa for the first eight months this year, by 25.9 per cent. The export portfolio of China has shifted significantly over the years.

So, while many people still tend to think China relies heavily on exports – especially to the US – exports are no longer as central to China’s economy as before, and the US’ importance has waned proportionally. The Chinese domestic economy now matters far more to China than exports do.

I want to argue against the concept of raising private consumption to grow an economy. It should really be the other way around

Domestically, China is pinning hopes on private consumption being a key economic growth driver, with the government rolling out a series of initiatives to boost spending. How effective has this been?

Not very, and I think focusing on private consumption for the sake of consumption itself is the wrong strategy. Yes, private consumption is now a major engine for China’s economy, but understanding why it hasn’t been growing as fast as expected in recent years is crucial. I’d say there are three reasons.

The first one is income. People were used to disposable income growing by double digits, or at least above 8 per cent, for two decades – until Covid hit. Nowadays, the growth is no longer as much as before.

The second reason is unemployment, which is quite a worry and concern for China at the moment. The government even stopped publishing the unemployment rate for young adults in 2023 [for five months while recalculating the figure to exclude students]. The official number was above 20 per cent then, but some labour economists said the actual number may have been much higher.

The third reason is the wealth effect. More than 90 per cent of Chinese households own homes, and more than 20 per cent of Chinese households own multiple homes.

Previously, when the property market was growing well, even if you weren’t working, you were becoming wealthier every day just by owning a house. Now, with property prices down, many lack that sense of wealth and security to spend.

For consumers, if their income is not growing as fast, if they have a much higher chance of becoming unemployed, and if their wealth is shrinking, why would they have the confidence to spend? Lots of policy suggestions involve giving people subsidies to encourage them to spend, but if the fundamentals aren’t there, then direct stimulus and subsidies would only be scratching the surface.

And on a deeper level, I want to argue against the concept of raising private consumption to grow an economy. It should really be the other way around, that people will spend more if a country and its people are richer. Here’s an easy way to think about this: no household can become richer by spending more. When a family makes more money, then it will spend more – not the other way around. Higher private consumption as a proportion of GDP is the result – not the cause – of a country’s growth.

If we look at World Bank data from 2023, countries such as Somalia and Haiti have the highest ratio of household consumption as a percentage of GDP, above 100 per cent, while developed countries such as Ireland and Singapore have some of the lowest ratios of private consumption to GDP. Thus, spending more to grow a country is not a universal formula for success. We shouldn’t spend for the sake of spending; rather, when a country grows richer, its people can afford to spend more.

If we place excessive emphasis on mistakes, then the safest thing is to not do anything, and thus, it’s guaranteed that no mistakes will be made

So, what do you see as the most significant and promising bright spot in China’s economy?

There are four elements in a classic macroeconomic model of growth: land, capital, people and technology. The technology factor is the exponential element – and China’s tech sector and innovation can give us a lot of hope. Behind this are the very hardworking and resilient Chinese people. And the labour productivity in certain private sectors is particularly impressive.

In a way, I think of China’s innovation as if the country is an enormous venture capital field. For instance, Silicon Valley is characterised by failing fast and pivoting fast. There’s a lot of competition, there’s a lot of money supporting that, and entrepreneurs are very much encouraged to try. If it’s right, brilliant – they make it. If it’s wrong, they fail, but then they pivot very quickly, and they move on to something new and hopefully right the next time.

China, in a sense, operates similarly. It has so many hardworking and innovative people, and they’re trying all these new things. But the problem now is that the prevailing mentality is focused on avoiding mistakes rather than celebrating successes.

Obviously, we need to prevent and punish careless and irresponsible mistakes. But a balance is needed – if we place excessive emphasis on mistakes, then the safest thing is to not do anything, and thus, it’s guaranteed that no mistakes will be made.

The current macro and policy environment is not yet friendly enough for entrepreneurs in China. They still have many concerns, with many uncertainties, and they don’t feel fully empowered. I would really hope to see the macro policy environment shift more towards celebrating successes – that it’s OK to fail, as long as you have good intentions and keep trying to succeed – rather than stressing the avoidance of mistakes.

Some critics argue that Chinese innovation mainly re-engineers existing global technologies rather than achieving foundational breakthroughs. Does China truly innovate? How would you characterise China’s innovation style?

Chinese innovation differs markedly from Western innovation, but that doesn’t mean China isn’t innovative. In fact, China is very innovative.

The Western notion of innovation – as outlined in Peter Thiel’s book, Zero to One – is about breakthroughs and creating something brand new. Chinese innovation takes a different approach. Let’s consider the comparison of “zero” to “one”, and I’ll outline four types of Chinese innovation.

First, China often takes a “one” and turns it into “1.1”, but on a massive scale. China has a population of more than 1.4 billion and can sell to the rest of the world, which has a population of more than 8 billion – this is a large market. So, if China achieves “1.1”, it can potentially scale that to “8.8 billion”.

A prime example is Ant Group. It originated from the 2004 launch of Alipay, six years after PayPal. But then it began adding its own characteristics, services and products, and then scale – the idea being, as the name “Ant” suggests, that it’s small but abundant. [Ant is an affiliate of Alibaba Group Holding, owner of the Post.]

Alipay was integrated into Ant Group, which was founded in October 2014. Within just six years, the company’s estimated market cap when it was initially going for IPO in 2020 was US$310 billion, which was huge for a relatively young company. It began not with a new concept, but by adding its own innovative characteristics and applying them to the mass market. It wasn’t “zero” to “one”, but it was “one” to “1.1”, then to over a billion, and that’s also innovation.

The second type of innovation involves taking an existing “one” and making it in a different and possibly better way. A good example is DeepSeek. It was founded only two years ago, but in January, everyone was talking about it – it swept the world away. Basically, it uses a very different computational method than ChatGPT. It’s more efficient and it does it cheaper. So, yes, OpenAI started ChatGPT – amazingly significant – but DeepSeek does the “one” differently, and possibly better.

The third type of innovation is taking different “ones” and combining them into a “10”. A good example would be WeChat. In the beginning, WeChat’s parent company, Tencent, started with QQ, and QQ was learning from ICQ. And then, over the years, while Silicon Valley produced products such as Facebook, WhatsApp and Instagram, WeChat became a combination of all of them, and you can even do online shopping like on Amazon, and hire taxis like on Uber.

It almost serves as a Windows system – a platform for everything. Many people don’t even use Baidu to search for things any more; they will just use WeChat, because it’s now this information universe. You go there, and you can get everything done. This is a third form of Chinese innovation, starting with different “ones” and merging them into a “10”.

The fourth type of innovation focuses intensely on “0.1” within a “one”, making that element the best in its class and applying it across sectors. An example would be CATL, which doesn’t make entire electric cars, but specialises solely on the “0.1” – the batteries. It’s the largest firm in the industry and powers everything. It already supplies batteries for a third of the electric vehicles around the world, with Tesla also being an important client. Last year, CATL successfully tested a flight for an electric plane, and has even launched electric cruise ships.

Now, CATL’s ambition is not just to work on batteries – their ambition is to electrify whatever can be electrified. They just focus on the “0.1”, but they do it so well that they empower all the “ones” in different sectors. This is absolutely also innovation, which is why I say China just innovates differently.

The two [US] political parties can’t agree on anything, but the containment of China is one thing they can agree on

The first administration of US President Donald Trump tried to curb China’s tech advancements, but some say it accelerated China’s self-reliance instead. As we navigate a second Trump term, what new dynamics do you foresee?

I wouldn’t say it accelerated self-reliance. Rather, it forced China to pivot. For instance, China is denied access to the most premium chips, but China can use chips that are good enough and focus on their applications and business.

And China can pivot to other markets. Like I said earlier when we were talking about trade, it’s not just the US and European markets any more. We’re now in a very different phase of globalisation. Traditionally, we think about trade as China selling to the US and Europe – these were the big markets. But nowadays, the Global South is the emerging market.

And entrepreneurs always find a way. Chinese manufacturers are innovating to sell better and cheaper products, and to more people and different countries. At the core of economics, it’s all about incentives. Money motivates, and people will find a way.

This time, though, President Trump has accumulated more power. At the moment, it’s mostly related to trade and economics, but I am concerned that it will escalate to other areas as well, such as security and finance realms. And it’s important to note that it’s not just about China itself; we also have to look at American domestic politics.

Politically, it’s a more divided country at the moment. And I joke – but I mean it – that with all the political, economic and social divisions in the US right now, the only thing that unites the US is China. The two political parties can’t agree on anything, but the containment of China is one thing they can agree on. So, I am worried that there will be more attacks on China, and they won’t just be trade and technology related.

You’ve previously alluded to the Thucydides Trap in the context of US-China relations. Is conflict inevitable, and what can the US, China and the international community do to avert such an outcome?

Professor Graham Allison’s Thucydides Trap theory provides a very insightful, alarming and powerful historical reference point for US-China dynamics. Out of 16 past cases when there was one existing dominant power and one emerging power, 12 resulted in war. So, it’s telling us that there is a very high likelihood of conflict, and we need to be alarmed, but at the same time, war is not inevitable.

For instance, it’s very important to note that when Sparta and Athens went to war, neither of the kings actually wanted to go to war. It was really the very hawkish people on both sides who made it impossible for the kings not to go to war. In a way, the kings had to give in to public sentiment.

On this, ironically, China can have better cards to play. Relatively speaking, China can more effectively manage and steer public opinion. This offers some hope – that what makes the US and China different from the Thucydides Trap is that China’s side could avoid being hijacked by irrational public sentiment.

The Thucydides Trap is an important theory and stark warning, but psychology suggests that what we focus on can actually lead to what we get. So, while it’s important we are fully alert to the risks of war and do everything we can to prevent it, overemphasising risks, on the other hand, could lead us to act in ways that increase the possibility of war, as both sides start to act more defensively and inward-looking rather than exploring more possibilities of collaboration and joint interests. I don’t know which of the two is better, but averting conflict will require very self-disciplined and wise world leaders to collectively make the right, informed choices.

And this is not just about the US and China. In physics, the most stable shape is a triangle, but we currently lack a third pillar that can stabilise the two countries. Before, I had hoped Europe could play that role, given its economic power and global influence, but European nations are also stuck at many fronts and they can’t, unfortunately, reach an agreement among themselves on many key issues. So, I’ve shifted my hopes to the emerging markets. Instead of being stuck between the geopolitical rivalries of the US and China and forced to choose a side, perhaps those nations could come together and broker a path forward by pressuring the two superpowers to work together.

Curiosity and personal experience are our best tools to bridge perception gaps … come to China and see first-hand

Your insights today reveal a more nuanced reality of China than is often portrayed in mainstream media. How can we correct this perception gap?

It will take quite some time to fix this, as the lenses are different, misperceptions are too prevalent, and many have moved beyond facts and rational discussions to emotions.

A lot of it is about trust building – otherwise, the two sides will only push each other away. Whether through bridges of businesses or cultural exchanges, one of the most effective ways to build understanding is for outsiders to visit China.

Even if you don’t like China at the moment, it’s worth recognising that China is important enough for you to get out of your comfort zone and see for yourself. Curiosity and personal experience are our best tools to bridge perception gaps. So, I’d say come to China and see first-hand – it’s very different.

As a woman who has navigated leadership roles in the workplace and now runs your own advisory, what are the most significant barriers limiting the career advancement of women?

There are two types of barriers: those that we can work on ourselves and those that are rooted in systemic gender inequality.

For example, globally and including in China, there’s about a 25 per cent gender income gap. Traditional wisdom says that women don’t negotiate for salaries enough. For instance, a survey in Linda Babcock’s book, Women Don’t Ask, found that men negotiate their salaries eight times more often than women, on average. This is something that, as women, we ourselves can do better, because traditionally we have been brought up to be more amicable, accepting and kind, as opposed to being assertive and firm in asking for what we want.

But a study from UC Berkeley two years ago found that women graduates from a top MBA programme were actually negotiating more often than their male counterparts – yet were rejected more frequently. This highlights the systemic barriers: even when we adopt behaviours traditionally seen as successful, we might still be met with resistance.

Another critical barrier is the disproportionate burden of domestic work and childcare. In China, while women are now pursuing higher education in greater numbers than men and maintain a relatively high participation in the labour force, women still do 2.6 times more housework and childcare after marriage. This imbalance will have to be changed for women to be able to contribute more to economic growth and social development.

But despite the barriers, I think we’re already making big advancements overall. I believe that the rise of female power – and the rise of China – are the two key defining themes of this century.

We must ensure our voices are heard – not just for ourselves, but for the nations’ economic growth and social progress

What unique opportunities do you see for women today?

Women view the world, and change the world, differently.

Our lenses are different and can be the key ones. Take the field of economics as an example – in the Chinese version of Paul Samuelson’s classic textbook, Economics, only less than half a page, out of 1,187, was dedicated to discussing women’s issues. But female economists look at economics differently.

For example, in 2022, a group of Chinese male economists argued in a report that the reason Chinese people were no longer having as many babies was that it was too expensive. They calculated the direct costs of raising a child in China, from birth to 18 years old – including milk powder, housing and education – to be around US$66,000.

I have complex emotions for this study. On one hand, it’s an important study, and I much appreciate their work in calculating this and the attention it drew from government and society and the widespread debate it sparked. But on the other hand, it was a classic example of how male economists view the world differently – they looked only at the direct out of pocket costs.

This approach overlooks a critical concept in economics: opportunity cost. Leading international economic studies show that, on average, a woman sacrifices about 20 per cent of her lifetime income to have a child. This is known as the parenthood penalty, also referred to as the motherhood penalty, since it disproportionately affects mothers.

So, I worked with professors Zhao Yaohui and Zhang Xiaobo from Peking University to examine this issue in China. We estimated that the motherhood penalty alone costs US$78,000 – more than all the direct costs of raising a child combined.

Fertility policies need to be focused in the right direction. If they consider only the direct costs studied by male economists, rather than the more important indirect costs, i.e. the motherhood penalty, then the policy direction would be wrong, resources misallocated and results not effective.

That’s why it is important to have more women, whether as government officials, economists or business managers, at the table. We must ensure our voices are heard – not just for ourselves, but for the nations’ economic growth and social progress.

China’s famous saying, that women hold up half the sky, captures this well. Ultimately, we’re all carrying the torch from previous generations on to the next. While there are times we take two steps forward and one step back, I’m convinced that – especially given rising education levels and economic opportunities – we will find our way.
