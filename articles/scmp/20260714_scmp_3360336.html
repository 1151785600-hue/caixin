Malaysia’s top court clears Syed Saddiq of corruption, ending 6-year legal drama

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 881 words | 2026-07-14

Malaysian lawmaker Syed Saddiq Syed Abdul Rahman walked free on Monday after the Federal Court dismissed the prosecutors’ final appeal in his corruption case, ending a six-year legal battle that had threatened to derail the future of one of the country’s most recognisable young politicians.

The decision was reached by a 2-1 majority, with Federal Court judges Che Mohd Ruzima Ghazali and Collin Lawrence Sequerah upholding last year’s acquittal of the former youth minister on four charges linked to funds belonging to Armada, the youth wing of former prime minister Muhyiddin Yassin’s Bersatu party.

Court of Appeal president Abu Bakar Jais, who chaired the panel, dissented and would have convicted Syed Saddiq on three of the four charges: the alleged misappropriation of 120,000 ringgit (US$29,400) and two subsequent money laundering transactions.

Delivering the outcome, Abu Bakar said the majority had found “no appealable error” in the Court of Appeal’s decision, making his proposed convictions and sentences immaterial.

In his majority judgment, Ruzima said the prosecution had failed to establish the principal offence of criminal breach of trust involving the withdrawal of 1 million ringgit from Armada’s account.

“The Bersatu constitution is not law,” he said, adding that any breach of the party’s internal rules should attract disciplinary, not legal, action unless it was also a criminal offence.

Sequerah agreed with Ruzima’s findings, giving the panel the majority required to dismiss the prosecution’s appeal.

The ruling means Syed Saddiq remains clear of the Kuala Lumpur High Court conviction that carried a seven-year jail term, two strokes of the cane and a 10 million ringgit fine.

The Muar MP, who was accompanied by his parents, supporters and fiancee, singer-actress Bella Astillah, later embraced his family and prayed before speaking to reporters.

He said the prolonged case had taken a heavy toll and that he hoped neither political allies nor adversaries would have to endure a similar ordeal.

Asked about his future in politics, Syed Saddiq said he needed time with his family before deciding his next move, but added that “the fire to help rebuild Malaysia will never end”.

Room to rebuild

The acquittal removes the threat to Syed Saddiq’s Muar parliamentary seat and gives him room to rebuild his political career. But it comes against a bleak backdrop for the Malaysian United Democratic Alliance (Muda), the youth-centred party he founded after leaving Bersatu in 2020.

Muda was wiped out in Saturday’s Johor state election – losing all four seats it contested, including Puteri Wangsa, its sole seat in the state assembly. The constituency was won by former education minister Maszlee Malik of Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s Pakatan Harapan (PH) coalition.

The legal case had followed Syed Saddiq through one of the most turbulent periods of his political career – from his exit from Bersatu after the collapse of the PH government in 2020, to his attempt to build Muda as an alternative for younger Malaysians after the voting age was lowered to 18.

Syed Saddiq was accused of abetting former Armada assistant treasurer Rafiq Hakim Razali in criminal breach of trust involving 1 million ringgit at CIMB Bank in Kuala Lumpur in March 2020.

He was also accused of misappropriating 120,000 ringgit raised through two fundraising dinners for his 2018 election campaign and faced two money-laundering charges involving transfers of 50,000 ringgit each into his personal accounts.

The Kuala Lumpur High Court convicted him in November 2023, rejecting his defence as unreasonable. The Court of Appeal overturned the conviction in June last year, ruling unanimously that the trial judge had seriously misdirected himself and failed to consider crucial parts of the defence.

It found that the prosecution had not proven Syed Saddiq acted dishonestly when the 1 million ringgit was withdrawn. It also found that the 120,000 ringgit had been raised for his election campaign and that there was no evidence showing it belonged to Armada or the company that was temporarily holding it.

Syed Saddiq maintained throughout the case that he was innocent, saying the charges were politically motivated after he refused to back Muhyiddin’s administration following the collapse of the PH government.

He stepped down as Muda president after his 2023 conviction, as he said he did not deserve to lead the party while fighting to clear his name.

In an unusual aside from the bench on Monday, Abu Bakar said the judges knew Syed Saddiq had plans after the ruling and hoped those plans could now proceed, an apparent reference to his coming marriage.

The 33-year-old’s engagement to Bella, 32, in March pushed the Johor-born politician deeper into Malaysia’s pop-culture spotlight, with fans dubbing the couple “BASS” and treating their relationship as one of the country’s biggest celebrity-political moments of the year.

He thanked Bella in his statement to reporters and hinted at marriage plans but asked for the spotlight to remain on the acquittal.

“It’s not easy to be with me as when we hooked up I had been found guilty. I have the green light from the elders to proceed but to comment further, I’ll do it out of court,” he said. “I want to respect the courts and I don’t want this to be about me and my personal life. I want it to be bigger than that.”
