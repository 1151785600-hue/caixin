How South Korean hypermarket chain’s fall reflects ‘time-poor’ consumer demands

SCMP | FREE | 1027 words | 2026-07-19

Homeplus was once a retail haven for millions of South Korean families who shopped for groceries and household goods regularly at its cavernous hypermarkets.

But that ritual over almost three decades ended this week when the company closed all its remaining 67 operating outlets nationwide after suspending 37 others earlier as part of its restructuring.

The sudden closure of South Korea’s second-largest hypermarket chain reflects not only its financial distress, but also a decline of the shopping model it helped popularise.

A 200 billion won (US$135 million) emergency loan approved on Thursday has given Homeplus a temporary lifeline, allowing the company to challenge a court decision that had pushed it closer to liquidation. However, it still faces questions about unpaid wages, supplier payments and above all, its business status.

The Homeplus crisis signals how online shopping and smaller households are adversely affecting the hypermarket model, according to retail analysts.

Nearly 80 per cent of South Koreans shop online, according to a 2025 survey by Statista. A separate report in May by the country’s trade ministry shows that online platforms accounted for more than 60 per cent of total sales of the country’s major retailers earlier this year.

Evolving shopping experience

Lee Do-hyeon lives with his wife and one-year-old daughter in Unjeong, a suburb north of Seoul populated mostly by young families. A Homeplus megastore in the suburb’s central area used to be the neighbourhood’s main shopping destination.

“While the news was shocking because I’ve been going to Homeplus since I was a child, in another way it felt inevitable. It wouldn’t really affect our lives if it closed today. My wife and I don’t have much time, so we do most of our shopping online,” said the 30-year-old employee at a logistics company.

Lee and his family have increasingly spent their weekends at the nearby Starfield Village Unjeong, a sprawling shopping and entertainment complex operated by department store giant Shinsegae.

The complex attracted more than 2.1 million visitors in its first 100 days after opening in December, with visitors drawn to its attractions including trendy restaurants, cooking-class studios and children’s libraries.

“If we have to choose between the two, we’d rather spend time at Starfield. There’s much more to do,” Lee said.

Jeon Da-hyen, a researcher and co-author of the bestselling Trend Korea series, said the physical retail model had transformed in response to changing shopping patterns.

“Rather than simply buying products, shoppers now visit physical stores for experiences, entertainment and atmosphere. Retailers have to give consumers a reason to visit,” she said.

Promotional events such as pop-up stores and exclusive product launches have also become increasingly important for retailers.

“Because opportunities for families to spend time together have become much rarer, people increasingly choose destinations that offer broader leisure experiences rather than simply visiting a hypermarket,” Jeon said.

Recent data shows the changing fortunes of South Korea’s retail segments.

Department stores in the country were the only major retail segment to post higher sales in 2025, according to data from the Korean Statistical Information Service. In comparison, hypermarkets recorded their first annual sales decline while convenience stores also slipped.

According to data from the trade ministry, department store sales rose 14.7 per cent while online sales grew 8.1 per cent in March from the same month a year earlier. Hypermarkets and supermarkets posted a decrease in sales of 15.2 per cent and 8.6 per cent, respectively.

Tech-driven retail

Suh Yong-gu, a marketing professor at Sookmyung Women’s University in Seoul, said the Homeplus experience partly reflected the changing demographics of South Korean society.

“The decline of the hypermarket is, in many ways, the decline of the traditional family. As family structures have changed, the hypermarket model has become much less relevant,” Suh said.

South Korea has the world’s lowest birth rate, with one-person households accounting for 36 per cent of all households, followed by two-person households at 29 per cent.

Spending patterns have also been affected by rising inequality.

Suh noted income growth did not rise across all households while the wealthiest 20 per cent recorded a strong rise in their income and assets.

Nonetheless, South Korea’s appetite for retail consumption remains strong. The country’s spending in high-end segments such as food, clothing and accessories was among the highest in the world, according to a report by Deloitte Korea’s Consumer Signals that was released in April 2025 and based on that year’s first-quarter data.

“South Korea has a large and growing population of what we call ‘money-rich, time-poor’ consumers – people who have purchasing power but very little time”, Suh said, citing the success of Coupang, the largest online retailer in South Korea.

Coupang controls about one-third of South Korea’s e-commerce market and accounts for more than half of online grocery sales, according to several surveys. The company’s other major market is in Taiwan, where its delivery service now covers 70 per cent of its geography.

Suh said South Korea was a “global laboratory” reflecting how retail trends were evolving in Asia.

“Many Asian countries are becoming increasingly urbanised, household sizes are shrinking and online retail is increasingly becoming the norm even in traditionally family-oriented societies,” he added.

Despite the rise of online sales, major South Korean retailers are still expanding their physical presence in the region, with the likes of hypermarket and discount store giant Lotte Mart and convenience-store chain Emart expanding in markets such as Indonesia, Vietnam, Thailand and Mongolia.

One market that has caught the attention of retail analysts like Jeon is China, where she notes drone deliveries have become part of daily life in cities such as Shenzhen.

“China has taken delivery culture to another level. In some cities, delivery times are measured in minutes, with companies facing financial penalties if they miss strict deadlines,” Jeon said.

Similarly, Jeon believed it was a matter of time that South Korea’s retail scene would be revolutionised by technology.

“I expect South Korea’s delivery ecosystem to become increasingly integrated with advanced technologies. You may see robots delivering your groceries to your doorsteps”.
