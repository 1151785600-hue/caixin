People’s Daily joins Beijing blitz against Philippines over 2016 South China Sea ruling

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 612 words | 2026-07-11

China’s flagship Communist Party newspaper has joined a sweeping rhetorical offensive against rival maritime claimant the Philippines, days ahead of the 10th anniversary of a South China Sea arbitration ruling rejected by Beijing as “illegal”.

In twin commentaries published on Friday, the People’s Daily branded the July 12 decision of 2016 a “destabilising force” and demanded that Manila pivot to direct talks.

The commentaries from official media cap a multi-agency initiative from Beijing in recent days, including successive critiques of Philippine maritime claims by a Ministry of Natural Resources think tank and a June 30 state assessment of the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (Unclos) warning of “abusive practices”.

China did not take part in the arbitration case at The Hague initiated by the Philippines in 2013, nor does it recognise the verdict, which invalidated key elements of Beijing’s claims in the contested waters.

Yet the ruling remains a permanent friction point, souring Beijing’s relations with Manila and other rival South China Sea claimants.

China “would never be bound” by the ruling “under any circumstances”, one of Friday’s People’s Daily commentaries asserted.

China’s non-compliance actually defended the “sanctity” of international law, said the article attributed to Huang Yu Ping, a collective pen name the newspaper uses for official commentaries on major international affairs.

“Conversely, the Philippines’ repeated exploitation of this illegal and invalid ruling is fundamentally a severe departure from and disruption of the spirit of international law, reducing the law to a mere tool for political manipulation.”

In a separate commentary, a maritime expert writing under the pseudonym Zong Haiping called the ruling a “political farce in legal disguise”.

He accused the United States – a treaty ally of the Philippines – of “single-handedly instigating, planning and manipulating” the arbitration, while blaming Manila for acting in “bad faith” to undermine Beijing’s sovereignty in the region rather than resolve the dispute.

“The Philippines has never entered into any negotiations with China regarding the matters raised in its claims, such as historic rights, the status of islands and reefs, or traditional fishing rights,” the article said.

“Instead, it deliberately misrepresented general diplomatic consultations between China and the Philippines as negotiations conducted on these specific matters, falsely claiming that it had exhausted all bilateral negotiation channels.”

The author went on to accuse the tribunal judges of bias, arguing the court overstepped its mandate by expanding Manila’s narrow complaint into a sweeping judgment on the entire Spratlys archipelago.

China’s claims in the resource-rich chain – which it calls Nansha Islands – overlap with those of several other Southeast Asian nations, including Vietnam and Malaysia.

“A tribunal is supposed to be an objective and impartial judicial body, yet it acted more like the plaintiff than the Philippines itself,” the maritime expert alleged.

Sunday’s landmark anniversary of the ruling comes at a time when maritime tensions between China and the Philippines have sharply escalated after a string of high-seas confrontations.

While Manila accuses Beijing of defying international law, Beijing says the Philippines has jeopardised mutual trust by weaponising the ruling.

There is also growing concern that these maritime frictions could further derail long-stalled talks between Beijing and Asean on a South China Sea code of conduct.

In Beijing on Friday, foreign ministry spokeswoman Mao Ning urged Manila not to weaponise the 2016 decision. “The so-called ruling has absolutely nothing to do with the code of conduct,” Mao said, warning the Philippines against using the award “to create obstacles to achieving the code”.

As the 2026 chair of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations, the Philippines has pushed for the negotiations to be concluded within the year.
