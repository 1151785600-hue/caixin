How Bersama could cost Malaysia’s Anwar the Johor poll

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 819 words | 2026-07-08

Malaysia’s former economy minister Rafizi Ramli and his breakaway party, Bersama, are unlikely to emerge as a credible third force in the country’s political landscape, but they could still siphon votes away from the reformist Pakatan Harapan (PH) coalition led by Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim.

Bersama’s first electoral test, in Johor on Saturday, will be watched less for the number of seats it wins than for the votes it might peel away from PH in multi-cornered contests, where analysts warn Bersama could act as a spoiler rather than a serious contender for power.

“For Bersama to make a difference in Johor by appealing to urban or semiurban Malay voters, yes they can do it, but they will have very minimal effect on the whole political scenario,” said Azmi Hassan, a senior fellow at the Nusantara Academy for Strategic Research.

Johor voters were pragmatic, he said, adding that Bersama, in its current form, could not be considered a credible challenger to the more established parties because it lacked the strength to succeed without cooperating with major coalitions.

“If not, they are just a spoiler in the election,” he said.

The Johor state election promises to be a tough-fought contest between the incumbent Barisan Nasional (BN) bloc and PH. The two coalitions are allies at the federal level but are both fielding candidates for all 56 seats in Johor, while the opposition Perikatan Nasional (PN) is eyeing 33. Bersama is contesting 15.

Led by former PKR stalwarts Rafizi and Nik Nazmi Nik Ahmad, both of whom held cabinet posts under Anwar, Bersama is filling a reformist vacuum while also functioning as a protest vehicle, according to Bridget Welsh, a political analyst and honorary research associate at the University of Nottingham’s Asia Research Institute-Malaysia.

Though still in its infancy, with around 20,000 members as of late May, the party was attracting attention from pro-reform voters, Welsh said, while also tapping into frustration among former PH supporters who felt the coalition had failed to deliver meaningful reform since taking federal power.

“Johor is essentially a branding, breaking-the-ground exercise for Bersama,” she said.

Johor is essentially a branding, breaking-the-ground exercise for Bersama

Bridget Welsh, Asia Research Institute-Malaysia

Success would be measured not by seats won but by exposure gained and deposits saved, and perhaps a strong showing in split contests. “Success may end up spoiling PH’s chances, but that is part of the branding as well,” Welsh added.

Rafizi, once deputy president of Anwar’s PKR, was widely regarded as one of PH’s key campaign strategists, building his political brand on data-driven attacks, viral messaging and bread-and-butter issues that resonated with urban voters.

He rose through the ranks to become one of the coalition’s most recognisable reformist figures before falling out with the party establishment after losing the PKR deputy presidency last year.

In May, Rafizi and Nik Nazmi – the former natural resources and environmental sustainability minister – announced their exit from Anwar’s People’s Justice Party that is a key component of the ruling PH coalition, vacated their parliamentary seats and took over Parti Bersama Malaysia, a small party founded in Penang in 2016.

Analysts say Bersama is unlikely to swing the overall result, but its impact may extend beyond Johor into the next general election, since its support base overlaps more closely with PH’s than with its rivals’ and it could chip away at Anwar’s coalition specifically.

“I think that is the main purpose of Rafizi,” Azmi said. “He established Bersama to hurt Pakatan Harapan more than Barisan Nasional, the Malay-led bloc, or the opposition bloc, Perikatan Nasional.”

Oh Ei Sun, a political analyst and senior fellow at the Singapore Institute of International Affairs, said Bersama and PH were competing for the same shrinking pool of reform-minded voters, many of whom were unconvinced by the contrast between Rafizi’s image as a reform champion and his record as economy minister.

At the end of the day, PH and Bersama will just cannibalise each other electorally

Oh Ei Sun, Singapore Institute of International Affairs

A Malaysian electorate growing more conservative and less receptive to reform-oriented parties would only add to the challenge, he said.

“At the end of the day, PH and Bersama will just cannibalise each other electorally,” Oh said.

On Monday, while campaigning in the Puteri Wangsa constituency, Rafizi told supporters that although voter sentiment appeared to be muted, small actions could make all the difference in an electoral race.

“No matter how difficult life is, we do what is best and small actions are what make a difference in an election and this is what I want to do with Bersama,” he said. “Trust the process because changes start with small steps and it starts for us.”

This Week in Asia has reached out to Rafizi and Nik Nazmi for comment.
