Why is a Chinese-made portable AC selling out across heat-stricken Europe?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 599 words | 2026-07-08

Amid weeks of intense heat across Europe, a niche Chinese air conditioner has become one of the hardest-to-buy products in the region this summer, selling out within just a few weeks.

A type of portable air conditioner (AC) made by Chinese home appliance giant Midea – the PortaSplit – has sold out across multiple e-commerce platforms as temperatures in parts of Europe have exceeded 40 degrees Celsius (104 degrees Fahrenheit).

The unit has gained traction in the region, in part, because it appears to sidestep some regulatory and structural constraints, including strict historic-preservation rules in many cities that restrict exterior-wall modifications such as drilling for external AC units.

Unlike conventional split-system ACs, the Chinese model requires no permanent installation or wall penetration, using a window bracket to suspend the outdoor unit externally.

In southern France, Christopher De Bruyn installed three air-conditioning units at his home in Ceret in 2024, describing the process as “quite painful”.

He said that the initial quotes from a local installer reached about €8,000 (US$9,160), with long delivery and installation delays. Having previously lived in Spain, he returned to check the prices and found that “the models were only half the price”, adding that French installers effectively charged double.

However, he said that since installers in France typically refuse to fit equipment bought abroad, he was unable to import cheaper units.

He eventually purchased units in France for about €4,000 from one appliance store. But he said their installation was delayed by a mandatory on-site assessment and a long waiting list, meaning that the system – paid for in May 2024 – was not installed until that September, “so I missed the whole summer”.

De Bruyn added that, while most European flats require approval from a homeowners’ association to install air conditioning, he was able to proceed more easily, as he owns a private house.

Priced at around €900 last year, the PortaSplit unit has reportedly been resold on secondary markets for thousands of euros, and the official retail price has since risen to €1,199.

Several social media users have shared accounts of driving across multiple cities to secure stock, posting lines such as “this will be the best investment of your lifetime” and “rarer than Pokemon cards – a brand-new, unopened Midea PortaSplit in stock at my place.”

There are dedicated websites, including one called MideaFinder, that track real-time stock availability of the units.

Beyond regulatory approvals and costs, Europe is also a predominantly rental market: nearly one-third of EU residents live in rented accommodation, a share that rises above half in Germany, which has been coping with recent heatwaves, according to Eurostat data. Portable AC units can also be taken with renters when they move, raising the units’ appeal.

Against this backdrop, the PortaSplit’s user-installable design reduces many local regulatory and building constraints.

Beyond manufacturers, China’s broader supply chain is also responding rapidly. On Chinese social media, logistics providers have begun advertising dedicated “AC air lanes”, using expedited air freight to speed up deliveries to Europe.

A Shenzhen-based logistics operator said that, after launching the service two weeks ago, orders from France, Belgium and the UK surged.

“My warehouse is packed with air conditioners now,” he said, adding that it appeared as though “overseas Chinese students are overheating”.

Historically, Europe’s temperate climate led buildings to prioritise insulation over cooling. Thick, airtight structures that improve winter efficiency can trap heat during extreme heatwaves.

According to the European Commission, around 85 per cent of EU buildings were built before 2000, with much of the stock designed for heating-dominated climates rather than summer cooling.
