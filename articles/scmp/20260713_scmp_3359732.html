Northern Metropolis’ science and education focus key to Hong Kong’s future

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 951 words | 2026-07-13

China’s new development narrative places science and education at the centre of long-term competitiveness. This is a systemic project powered by technology, grounded in education and driven by talent as the country’s foremost resource.

Hong Kong’s traditional identity as an international financial centre is no longer sufficient to sustain the city’s competitiveness. To remain relevant, Hong Kong must articulate a platform that structurally aligns with China’s national strategy while retaining its own institutional distinctiveness.

The Northern Metropolis is essentially a “science and education city” – a fusion of a technology park and a university town. It integrates the San Tin Technopole, the university town and the Hetao Shenzhen-Hong Kong Science and Technology Innovation Cooperation Zone. For the first time, Hong Kong possesses a platform that can be clearly positioned within China’s science and education strategy.

The focus is not on building campuses or factories. It is about creating an institutional loop that integrates technology, education and research. San Tin carries the science dimension, with innovative industries, prototyping facilities and advanced manufacturing that will help fill Hong Kong’s long-standing gap in research commercialisation.

The university town carries the education dimension, expanding post-secondary capacity, strengthening talent pipelines and embedding collaboration between academic, research and industry. Hetao covers both research and regulation, enabling cross-boundary research collaboration, data flows, intellectual property protection and regulatory innovation. Together, these three components have a structural resonance that makes the Northern Metropolis a concrete embodiment of China’s strategy.

Hong Kong is not alone in seeking an identity founded in science and education. Beijing, Shenzhen and Shanghai have already shown how integrated ecosystems can reshape a city’s long-term competitiveness.

Beijing’s Zhongguancun-Haidian corridor is China’s most mature example. Anchored by Peking University and Tsinghua University, it forms a dense cluster of national laboratories, artificial intelligence (AI) firms and semiconductor companies. It is not merely a tech district but a national engine of science and education.

Shenzhen’s Nanshan-Xili belt shows how institutional flexibility can accelerate innovation. Shenzhen University and the Southern University of Science and Technology sit alongside leading biotech and AI firms, supported by a regulatory environment designed for rapid experimentation.

Shanghai’s Zhangjiang Science City shows how research, regulation and industry can be integrated at scale. Its life sciences cluster is supported by regulatory pilots, cross-institutional research platforms and a talent pipeline linked to Fudan University and ShanghaiTech University.

Hong Kong’s Northern Metropolis must absorb the logic of these clusters. Science and education ecosystems only succeed when technology, education, research and regulation are integrated into a single institutional space.

The significance of a science and education city goes beyond boosting gross domestic product and involves raising gross national income (GNI). The essence of China’s national strategy is to enhance long-term income capacity and human capital, not simply expand production.

San Tin can increase the income of Hong Kong residents and enterprises through innovation output and industrial value. The university town can raise long-term income capacity through improved education quality and talent development. Hetao can increase external income through cross-border research, intellectual property and international collaboration.

Together, they can form a GNI-oriented institutional space that shifts Hong Kong’s economic narrative. This represents a move from a production-driven economy to an income-driven one, adding an international science and education centre to its expertise as a financial centre.

Institutionally, Hong Kong’s science and education city is not a sum of three projects but a single governance space. Hetao’s model of “one river, two banks” and “one zone, two parks” requires Shenzhen and Hong Kong to collaborate in planning, regulation, data governance, research and finance – a direct manifestation of the national strategy’s emphasis on institutional reform and regional coordination.

San Tin must develop a distinct institutional package for intellectual property protection, research commercialisation, innovation finance and regulatory sandboxes. The university town must redesign academic structures, industry-academia collaboration, cross-boundary academic mobility and vocational education to form a closed loop with the city’s innovation and research needs.

From a governance perspective, the science and education city is the core node of Shenzhen-Hong Kong collaboration. The national strategy requires not only coordination among mainland cities but synchronising different systems. Shenzhen and Hong Kong differ in legal systems, regulatory frameworks, capital markets and data governance.

Hetao is the institutional estuary where these differences can be transformed into advantages. If a replicable cross-boundary research and data governance model can be built in Hetao, the science and education city will become Hong Kong’s innovation and education hub as well as a national model for institutional innovation.

To truly align the Northern Metropolis with China’s national strategy, Hong Kong must shift from a narrative focused on GDP to one centred on GNI, measuring success through resident income and talent returns. It must shift from construction projects to institutional projects, treating San Tin, the university town and Hetao as a single institutional space that rebuilds Hong Kong’s long-term competitiveness.

Under this framework, the science and education city is not merely a new name for the Northern Metropolis. It is a strategic concept that can be understood by Beijing, adopted by Hong Kong’s policy community and debated by academia and the media.

It translates China’s national strategy into Hong Kong’s institutional space, turns abstract strategy into concrete urban form and expands Hong Kong’s future from a single financial narrative into a multidimensional narrative of technology, education and talent. The science and education city is a critical step for Hong Kong to redefine its role in the new era.
