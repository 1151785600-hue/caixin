China’s StepFun claims it has unveiled the world’s first AI smartphone

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 525 words | 2026-07-15

As Apple’s latest legal battle with OpenAI complicates the Silicon Valley race to reshape the consumer tech market, one Chinese start-up is taking a leap forward after unveiling what it claims to be the world’s first agentic smartphone.

At a launch event in Shanghai on Monday, Tencent Holdings-backed StepFun introduced the StepX Neo. The device runs on Step AOS, an operating system designed specifically for artificial intelligence agents, and was launched under StepX – which the firm billed as the world’s first AI terminal brand built natively around large language models.

Unlike traditional smartphones that required users to manually open individual apps, the device was built around AI agents, according to StepFun. The device could interpret user requests, coordinate across multiple services, and execute complex tasks autonomously, the company added.

The smartphone features Amoo, a new personal AI assistant native to Step AOS.

StepFun also announced integrations with major Chinese digital services including Alipay for payments, Trip.com for travel bookings, Didi Chuxing for ride-hailing and Meituan for on-demand services, which would allow Amoo to seamlessly complete tasks across different platforms without user intervention.

The launch highlights a growing global push by AI firms to control the entire product stack, spanning foundation models, operating systems and hardware.

The race to dominate AI hardware has already sparked friction among the US tech giants. In a lawsuit filed last week, Apple accused OpenAI of recruiting its former employees and prospective candidates to steal trade secrets regarding unreleased Apple products.

OpenAI announced a partnership with former Apple design chief Jony Ive last year to develop its own hardware for ChatGPT.

But while the US giants tangle in court, StepFun is aiming to prove that the next frontier of mobile software instead belongs to independent AI ecosystems.

Speaking at the launch event, StepFun founder and CEO Yin Qi said AI agents required a ground-up operating system rather than AI features layered onto existing mobile platforms, arguing that conventional smartphone software was never built for autonomous task execution.

Yin added that StepFun was not planning to rely on traditional revenue streams like hardware sales, pre-installed app fees or advertising. Instead, the company – which did not specify when the smartphone would be available or its sales price – would continue to explore alternative monetisation models for AI-native devices.

Meanwhile, industry analysts suggest the true disruption of AI-native hardware lies in data collection rather than immediate hardware sales.

The primary value of AI-native smartphones was their capacity to continuously gather user context, allowing AI assistants to become highly personalised over time, said Zebin Wang, an investment manager at JG Investment.

“Smartphones remain the richest source of contextual data among AI devices because they integrate the broadest range of user activities and modalities, making them a more powerful foundation for AI agents than emerging wearable devices,” he said.

Founded in 2023 by former Microsoft Research Asia executive Jiang Daxin, StepFun has attracted backing from Tencent Holdings and hardware manufacturers like ZTE. The company raised nearly US$2.5 billion in pre-initial public offering financing in May, valuing the AI start-up at about US$10 billion.
