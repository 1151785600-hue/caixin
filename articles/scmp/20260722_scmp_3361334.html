China’s policy playbook took its EV sector from zero to hero – can other countries follow?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1643 words | 2026-07-22

China’s electric vehicle (EV) industry sits at the centre of numerous economic and geopolitical trends: consumer spending, environmental protection, trade policy, high-end manufacturing, technology and more. In this short series, we assess the current state of the sector within these broader contexts. Here, Yeon Woo Lee asks whether Beijing has written a blueprint for developing countries to kick-start their own contenders in the field.

Emerging markets once seemed like also-rans in the global car industry. In the days when petrol vehicles predominated, manufacturers required vast supplier networks and hard-won expertise in engines, transmissions and other core power train technologies to compete – two advantages among many for established, wealthier players.

But with a global shift towards electric engines and China’s consequent rise from automotive latecomer to EV powerhouse, that calculus has changed.

Many emerging-market governments have taken cues from China’s state-led playbook, using incentives to create demand while pushing companies to localise production and supply chains.

As the thinking goes, electrification can accelerate industrial catch-up and help them build domestic champions before global EV markets become more firmly dominated by a handful of large firms from a few early adopter countries.

“China’s rapid rise in the EV sector demonstrates that disruptive innovation can dismantle entrenched technology barriers – such as internal combustion engine – and unlock viable pathways to industrial upgrading,” said Claire Yuan, director at S&P Global Ratings. “Established international players could risk losing their competitiveness as local, state-backed national champions expand.”

Electrification has lowered some traditional barriers to entry by reducing the importance of engines and transmissions. At the same time, China’s vast EV supply chain has given aspiring carmakers a faster foothold by making key components easier to source.

But the same supply chain, though it has made entry easier, has also raised a difficult question: without full integration, how much value can these countries ultimately retain at home?

National champions take shape

Vietnam offers one of the clearest examples of a domestic EV industry built, at least partially, in China’s image, with policymakers leveraging state support to help transform a domestic firm into a national champion. At the centre is VinFast, founded in 2017 by Vingroup, one of the country’s largest private conglomerates.

According to state news agency Viet Nam News, by 2025 VinFast had become the country’s bestselling car brand, delivering 175,099 vehicles and nearly doubling the previous annual sales record set by foreign carmakers.

But its rise has not been driven solely by market forces. It has been underpinned by a strong government campaign to build a domestic EV industry, with Hanoi using policy support to localise production, strengthen supply chains and accelerate adoption. Vietnam has set a target for all road vehicles to be electric by 2050.

Incentives introduced under a July 2025 decree are among the strongest Vietnam has offered, according to Vietnam Plus, a website operated by the state-run Vietnam News Agency. The measures cover taxes, land use and rental fees. Foreign-invested companies seeking incentives are also required to include at least one Vietnamese company in their supply chains, a rule intended to deepen local industrial capacity.

The government has also helped to open another fast-growing market for VinFast: public transport. Vietnam wants taxis, buses and other vehicular forms of urban transport in major cities to shift to EVs by 2030, and VinFast has already secured a commanding position in that segment.

VinFast is now trying to turn its home-market momentum into a regional springboard. It launched electric taxi services in Indonesia in 2024 and the Philippines in 2025, while also building factories in India and Indonesia.

“There are some questions … but it [VinFast] does appear to have rapidly transformed the domestic four-wheeler market and, if successful, would be an impressive example of a non-Chinese company applying the lessons from China to succeed in technological leapfrogging,” the Centre for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS), a Washington-based think tank, wrote in an October report.

A short cut with long odds

But even with its proven track record, China’s model is not easy to emulate. Not every emerging economy has the industrial base or fiscal capacity to nurture a domestic EV champion. Smaller markets with limited automotive manufacturing bases, such as Nepal and Costa Rica, have largely relied on imports to electrify their roads, according to CSIS.

Building a domestic EV champion is slower and costlier than relying on imports or assembly, but if it works, electrification offers the chance to build domestic manufacturing capacity and move up the value chain.

“EV sales are where all the growth is happening. It’s pretty clear what the long-term bet is,” said Euan Graham, senior electricity and data analyst at energy think tank Ember. Global sales of petrol and diesel cars, he noted, had already peaked in 2017.

Turkey is among the countries trying to seize that opening, drawing on its manufacturing base that global carmakers have long used as a hub for exports to Europe.

To meet its target of putting 2 million EVs on the road by 2030, Ankara is offering a wide range of support, including tax breaks, charging infrastructure expansion and multibillion-dollar manufacturing incentives. It is also seeking to localise battery production, with a target of 80 gigawatt-hours of annual capacity by the end of the decade.

At the centre of the push is Togg, a domestic EV maker formed in 2018 by five major Turkish industrial groups – Anadolu Group, BMC, Kok Group, Turkcell and Zorlu Holding – together with the Union of Chambers and Commodity Exchanges of Turkey, a confederation of private-sector groups.

Togg became Turkey’s top-selling electric car brand last year, selling 39,020 units and ranking ahead of global rivals such as Tesla and BYD in the domestic market, according to Daily Sabah, a Turkish newspaper with links to the government.

India’s bet, meanwhile, is bigger and more protective, reflecting the scale of its domestic car market and the strength of its existing manufacturing base.

New Delhi has focused on production-linked incentive schemes for advanced batteries, automobiles and auto components, while the states of Tamil Nadu and Maharashtra have developed their own strategies to attract EV investment.

Those incentives are reinforced by high tariffs. India maintains some of the world’s highest duties on imported EVs, with rates reaching around 100 per cent for some models, giving domestic manufacturers additional room to scale up.

That protected and policy-backed market has helped local players take a commanding position. Tata Motors and Mahindra & Mahindra, along with JSW MG Motor – a joint venture established in 2023 between India’s JSW Group and China’s SAIC Motor – accounted for 87.3 per cent of the country’s passenger EV market in the 2025-26 financial year, according to the Federation of Automobile Dealers Associations of India.

“Some domestic manufacturers have seen extreme success in some local markets,” Graham said. “The challenge they face now is being able to replicate this domestic success abroad, which would be key to scaling further.”

The dependence dilemma

Analysts say the policy logic is broadly similar across these countries. Like China, governments are using subsidies and tax incentives to create an early EV market before gradually pushing companies towards more local production and component sourcing.

The harder question is how much of the higher-value EV supply chain they can capture at home, particularly as their path into the industry increasingly runs through China.

Ryan Featherston, an associate fellow at CSIS’ Trustee Chair in Chinese Business and Economics, said the major uncertainty was whether emerging-market carmakers could move into higher-value parts of the EV supply chain, especially batteries, where Chinese firms remain the most competitive.

“I do think for countries that have that domestic market scale and industrial policy commitment, they have a shot at developing a real industrial base,” said Featherston, who wrote the CSIS report from October. “For many of those countries, though, that base is also going to come in part from investment from Chinese companies.”

That dependence is already visible. VinFast procures batteries from Chinese battery maker Contemporary Ampex Technology Ltd, commonly known as CATL, while Togg has teamed up with Farasis Energy to operate Siro, a battery joint venture. JSW MG Motor India is partly backed by China’s SAIC Motor, BYD supplies batteries for some Mahindra models and some Tata models rely on batteries from Chinese firm Gotion.

“Consequently, fully replicating China’s EV development model is difficult for most emerging countries in the short-run,” said Yuan of S&P Global Ratings.

That reliance creates a difficult trade-off. Chinese investment and technology can help emerging economies build EV capacity faster, but heavy dependence on foreign suppliers could limit how much value they ultimately capture at home.

The challenge is sharper for smaller markets. Limited domestic volumes may not be enough to justify the heavy R&D spending required for batteries, software and vehicle platforms.

Industrial policies that encourage too many domestic players also risk creating more capacity than local demand can absorb, said Michael Smitka, an emeritus professor of economics at Washington and Lee University who specialises in the global auto industry.

That could push some governments towards a more defensive approach. Yuan said some emerging economies may initially rely on imported components to launch domestic brands, then seek to protect those national champions through high import tariffs or local standards.

But the bigger test, the CSIS report said, is whether governments can turn Chinese investment into deeper local capacity by pushing firms to localise production, train local staff, support domestic suppliers and transfer some technology and manufacturing know-how.

Attracting non-Chinese investment would also be crucial to reducing both geopolitical and economic dependence on China, it added.
