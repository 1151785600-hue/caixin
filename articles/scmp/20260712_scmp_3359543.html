Europe could prosper as China’s trade ally rather than adversary

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 914 words | 2026-07-12

The European Commission has been clearly signalling its intention to wage a trade war against China. Brussels’ intent to counter the so-called China shock 2.0 – the wave of subsidised Chinese exports such as electric vehicles (EVs), solar panels and batteries – is no longer merely a verbal threat but appears to be an imminent decision.

Comparing current trade tensions between the European Union and China with the trade conflict between Europe and Japan in the 1970s and 1980s reveals striking similarities but also significant differences in terms of geopolitics, technology and state intervention. In both instances, European industry feared that imports from emerging Asian manufacturing hubs would undermine domestic production.

During the 1970s and 1980s, Europe faced fierce competition from Japanese exports. Companies from Japan captured market share through high-quality, efficient production and competitive pricing. European policymakers were concerned about the deindustrialisation of sectors such as automobiles, consumer electronics and machine tools.

Today, European manufacturers of EVs, batteries, solar panels, steel and other industrial products face intense competition from Chinese companies, which have become global leaders in strategic sectors. The loss of production capabilities and technological leadership is raising concern. Europe has responded with anti-dumping and protectionist measures.

Against Japan, these measures included import quotas, “voluntary” export restraints (informal restrictions) and anti-dumping investigations. Against China, they have included anti-dumping duties, anti-subsidy investigations, tariffs on electric vehicles and other products, and increased scrutiny of Chinese investments.

The crucial difference lies in the fact that, while Japanese companies benefited from industrial policies and government support in the 1970s and 1980s, they were also political allies of the West. Japan enjoyed a favourable tariff policy in the US, for example. China, by contrast, is viewed as a “systemic rival”.

Trade policy is increasingly linked to security issues. Matters such as technology transfer, cybersecurity, critical infrastructure and supply chain resilience are shaping public policy. Consequently, current conflicts are more highly politicised.

Trade tensions between Europe and Japan eventually subsided due to transatlantic solidarity and the escalating trade conflict between the United States and Japan, which forced the latter to sign the Plaza Accord, leading to a prolonged economic crisis for Japan.

To gain the upper hand in the trade war with China, the EU requires a favourable international environment, solid economic and trade policies, and unity – both within the EU and between the EU and the US. Currently, none of these factors appears to be in place.

First, European leaders ignored the profound shifts in Sino-American relations and repeatedly failed to truly understand US President Donald Trump. The EU missed a crucial point when Chinese President Xi Jinping backed the convergence of US and Chinese national strategies, “make America great again” and “China’s great rejuvenation”.

European political elites made strategic errors: they viewed the Trump presidency as a one-off event; hence they were not prepared for Trump’s trade war against the EU. They also believed that European strategy towards China, framed through the rhetoric of a systemic rivalry, would enjoy enduring support from Washington. None of these have come to pass.

Second, the EU’s leverage in the trade war pales in comparison with that of the US, given that the global trade landscape generally favours China. The EU is attempting to deploy new instruments in this conflict. Europe seeks to use cross-sectoral trade defence tools to fight “systemic and structural industrial overcapacity”, essentially aimed at Chinese firms that Brussels perceives as a threat to European strategic industries.

These instruments will operate alongside the EU’s “anti-coercion instrument” and cybersecurity regulations, which are set to further limit Chinese suppliers’ access to the digital economy’s critical infrastructure. But China has more retaliatory tools against the EU.

Third, the EU years ago squandered a great strategic opportunity to approve and activate the hard-bargained treaty with China, the Comprehensive Agreement on Investment. In it, China agreed to eliminate joint venture requirements, forced technology transfer, equity caps and quantitative restrictions in a number of sectors in which European firms in China do business.

The deal would have protected European investments in China. Ultimately, the deal was killed by a dispute over human rights. If the deal had existed today, the EU’s fear of deindustrialisation would have been much alleviated. More jobs may have been created. European companies in China would have been more profitable. Perhaps, the corresponding prosperity would have stifled the rise of the far-right.

The EU’s weakness is obvious, but China has no reason to be arrogant. China must also acknowledge that such a massive trade surplus with the EU is unsustainable. Voices advocating a “wolf warrior” trade stance and demanding harsh retaliation against the EU are becoming louder.

But has Europe actually “failed” in competing with China in a broad range of sectors? That depends on the metric. Europe remains highly competitive in luxury goods, aerospace, pharmaceuticals, industrial machinery and precision engineering.

It is more accurate to say that the EU has had difficulty matching China’s manufacturing scale and speed than saying Europe has broadly failed to compete. In fact, the larger concern among European policymakers is how to remain competitive simultaneously against both China’s manufacturing strength and the technological leadership of the US.

Therefore, as Washington withdraws from its global responsibility, Beijing needs the EU even more as a key market. It should consider using incentives to allay its trading partners’ real concerns, rather than relying solely on heavy-handed tactics.
