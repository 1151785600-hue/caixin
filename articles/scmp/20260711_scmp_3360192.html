Renewed US-China AI dialogue serves US security interests: former officials

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 731 words | 2026-07-11

A new round of US-China dialogue on the risks of artificial intelligence, even just for the sake of dialogue, will be important for furthering US national security, according to former senior US officials responsible for China affairs.

The assessment comes after the world’s two leading AI superpowers agreed to a new round of intergovernmental talks on AI safety following the Trump-Xi summit in Beijing in May, though few details about the dialogue have been announced.

Jeannette Chu, one of the longest-serving US diplomats in China who was senior export control attache at the US embassy in Beijing between 2005 and 2010, said that dialogue with Chinese officials on the fast-moving technology should be seen as furthering “self-preservation from a national security standpoint” for the US.

“It’s really important that we keep talking because it’s … how we get visibility into where China is going [with AI], how are they doing it, what are their successes and failures,” she said at an Asia Society event in San Francisco on Thursday.

“Do you want to be shut out of what they’re doing … [so] you’re cutting yourself off from the ability … to understand [their] vulnerabilities, much less how to exploit it?”

High-level concerns over AI risks have heightened in both the US and China after the release of US AI company Anthropic’s Mythos model in April, which showed unprecedented cyberattack capabilities.

The US and China had previously engaged in a round of high-level talks on AI risks in Geneva, Switzerland, in May 2024.

Though the talks did not immediately lead to any tangible outcomes, President Joe Biden and President Xi Jinping later that year announced an agreement on ensuring that humans, not AI, would retain decision-making power over the use of nuclear weapons.

Still, there has been long-standing scepticism in the US about the merits of engaging in dialogue with China as relations between the two countries have steadily worsened in the past decade.

As AI has emerged as the centrepiece of US-China tech competition, some US national security analysts have warned that China could use AI safety dialogue as leverage to push the US to relax other areas of its tech policy towards China, such as export controls on advanced semiconductor chips, citing experiences in previous dialogues with China on issues such as military affairs.

While acknowledging that dialogues with China on a range of issues were not all productive in previous eras, Mark Cohen, senior intellectual property attache at the US embassy in Beijing between 2004 and 2008, said the previous problem of having “too many” dialogues with China was now a problem of “too few”.

“This is one of the few situations where I see dialogue for the purpose only of dialogue as being good,” said Cohen, now a senior technology fellow at Asia Society Northern California.

“In a critical area like [AI], I believe dialogue is useful, although there are some questions about what it can legitimately cover and where it can be most effective.”

Alvin Wang Graylin, senior fellow at the Asia Society Policy Institute who also spoke at the event, said that some of the most critical areas the US and China should discuss are the use of AI in nuclear weapons, bioweapon development and cyberattacks.

However, he added that a major challenge was the lack of technical expertise in both Washington and Beijing among the government agencies likely to lead the talks.

“Right now, the problem is that both the US and China do not have a key owner for regulating and managing international dialogue around AI and AI safety,” he said.

“When it’s spread between over a dozen organisations in both countries, it really creates a lot of confusion.”

Neither Washington nor Beijing have confirmed which government agency will lead the talks, though US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has said that he is one of the “point people” for US AI policy and its economic relationship with China.

In a statement, Liu Chang, a spokesperson for the Chinese embassy in Washington, said that China and the United States, as major AI powers, should “join hands to advance AI development and governance, ensuring that AI better serves the progress of human civilisation and the shared well-being of the international community”.

The embassy did not say which Chinese government agency would represent Beijing in the talks.
