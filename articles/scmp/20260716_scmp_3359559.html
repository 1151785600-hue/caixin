Mujun Planet: one man’s quest to revive Japan’s artisanal legacy

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1954 words | 2026-07-16

“There’s a snake.”

Shinya Kobayashi points his phone camera at a sleek, black reptile, creating a trinity of S-shapes in the dirt, before calmly walking on. It is a remarkably cheerful day, with blue skies overhead and dense, emerald forest all around.

The 39-year-old designer, artist and father is taking me on a virtual tour of his own mountain, in Japan’s Shimane prefecture, along Honshu island’s western fringe. Nearby are a recently built blacksmith’s forge, sawmill, woodworking workshop, charcoal kilns and a renovated 200-year-old farmhouse, currently serving as storage space.

Mujun Planet, as Kobayashi has named the mountain and its tiny “village”, has been six years in the making. While the hardware might seem modest, he hopes its impact on the neighbouring town of Yunotsu, and the broader realm of Japanese craftsmanship, will be profound.

Metalworking is his focus for now. Blades from the forge made into knives are sold in Japan and around the world through Coelacanth Shokudo, his design company that, among other activities, manages the wholesale distribution of artisanal products.

All this and more are chronicled in the Hyogo native’s recently released, self-published book, Why Did Mujun’s Shinya Kobayashi Buy a Mountain?

“I have long worked to resolve the issue of artisan succession,” he tells me. “However, there are numerous problems that cannot be solved without entering the mountains to work, and I want people to understand why.”

To appreciate the seemingly tenuous connection between the two is to plunge into the depths of Kobayashi’s world – an adventure he has chosen for himself. On the surface, his mission revolves around self-sufficiency, breaking free from the dependence on imported tools necessary for various artisanal endeavours. His mountain and its surroundings serve as a natural reservoir of raw materials.

Take the knife – an essential tool across all crafts. It is why a forge was among the first components at Mujun Planet, operated by 29-year-old blacksmith Koharu Yamaguchi. As Kobayashi enters her workspace during our virtual tour, visible behind her is a tray of neatly arranged blades, their sharp edges catching the light.

Yamaguchi receives a steady supply of iron sand and charcoal from Yunotsu, historically renowned for tatara, the ancient Japanese method of ironmaking. For the knife handles, wood is harvested from the large branches of trees, as part of ongoing forest maintenance.

“The decline of craftsmanship has accelerated dramatically in recent years,” says Kobayashi. “I have always believed that nurturing successors for steelmaking artisans is vital to stemming this tide, and this conviction has now become a tangible reality.”

He is not alone in his beliefs. Growing numbers of young Japanese are eager to pursue careers in artisanal trades, and many are modernising traditional processes and finding innovative ways to share their stories. But few have adopted Kobayashi’s maverick methods.

His decision to buy a mountain is closely tied to his mission of promoting satoyama. Literally meaning “village” (sato) and “mountain” (yama), it originally described the landscape between these two realms.

Over time, it has evolved into a philosophy that deeply respects nature and tradition, advocating sustainability and living in rhythm with the seasons.

The past six years have been dedicated to laying a foundation for this vision. At Mujun Planet, overgrown trees have been cleared, mountain streams rejuvenated and neglected agriculture plots revived and nurtured.

As much as possible, local cedar is used, such as for the construction of buildings. In the farmhouse, Kobayashi’s pride is palpable as he highlights a smooth, 4.5-metre-long beam – crafted from a single trunk – supporting the roof. Plans are under way to turn the space into an atelier for emerging artisans.

“Sustainability is most crucial in resolving the issue of succession,” Kobayashi says. “It exists only through cycles and it is precisely because they have been practised for so long that we have traditional crafts. Within them lies an unbroken culture and way of life.

“That’s why I believe it is essential to revive the spirit of satoyama, which has been forgotten over the last 100 years or so.”

Compounding this challenge is society’s diminishing appreciation of Japan’s mountains. The costs associated with their management and maintenance have soared, leading many families to hesitate at the prospect of passing down or, indeed, inheriting such legacies.

Kobayashi says that after World War II, conifers such as cedar and cypress were planted as part of a new national policy for post-war reconstruction. Unfortunately, the initiative was quickly overshadowed by the influx of cheap, uniform meranti wood from Southeast Asia. This coincided with a population explosion in Japan that spurred the construction of inexpensive, simple houses throughout the country.

As a result, the conifers were left to languish and are now severely degraded. Undeterred by these challenges, however, Kobayashi seized an opportunity in 2020, when he learned from a local temple that a nearby 200,000 square metre mountain was being sold for just 1 million yen (US$6,230 at that time).

Today, the Mujun Planet community comprises seven members, three of whom are full-time “residents”, including Kobayashi. Their lodgings are a two-minute drive away, between the mountain and Yunotsu, and visitors are accommodated in a guest house behind his home.

Their workday begins at 8.30am and ends at sunset. In between caring for free-range chickens, the members engage in woodworking, grass cutting and tree-felling. Kobayashi himself travels far and wide occasionally – such as to design events in Europe and, recently, Singapore – to attract others to join him on his planet.

He also gives talks and holds workshops, including hosting a training and team-building retreat for a high-end blade-making brand.

Already, his efforts are beginning to bear fruit. Kobayashi speaks of young, like-minded nationals and non-Japanese finding their way to Mujun Planet, eager to become craftspeople or to experience satoyama. One, in particular, has embraced the lifestyle wholeheartedly: a Canadian-Chinese construction worker from the United States, who wants to build a house on the mountain, is currently working with Kobayashi on its design.

Then there’s the elderly Japanese blade maker whose speciality is scissor-making. Kobayashi tells how the man had given up on finding his successor only to change his mind after learning about Mujun Planet. He now plans to help train someone to take over his business.

While it might be easy to blame the dismal state of artisan succession on youthful indifference or the inability of senior craftspeople to think outside the box, the causes run deeper.

“The root of the problem lies in the social structure itself,” Kobayashi says, adding that one consequence is the homogeneity of many Japanese regions. “Japan became an economic powerhouse with astonishing speed, alongside its post-war reconstruction. Massive capitalist economies, and [the allure of] convenience, have ultimately devoured the regions and their cultures. Most Japanese regions now look identical – the same houses, the same shops, the same scenery.”

Moreover, issues of low profitability persist, stemming from inadequate distribution channels and ignorance around branding and marketing. Kobayashi has also heard of parents who choose not to support the continuation of their crafts. These elderly artisans want their children ­­– whose university education they funded ­– to find well-paid, city-based jobs, he says.

Kobayashi understands the push and pull personally, having witnessed similar issues in his own family’s traditional paper-mounting business in Ono city, Hyogo prefecture. He recalls a bustling business during his primary school years, in the 1990s, before Japan’s economic bubble burst and fortunes declined in the 2000s. “I increasingly saw my father looking rather idle. I’d often chime in with suggestions such as, ‘Why don’t you try sales?’ ­which led to frequent arguments.”

After his father, Michiaki, took over what would become a third-generation business, he made little effort to attract more customers, believing that they would appear if he simply waited. “I felt a strong sense of crisis seeing him just waiting like that,” recalls Kobayashi. “At the same time, I gradually came to understand that it was very difficult for him to suddenly adopt new ideas or put them into action.”

External factors compounded their challenges. Paper mounting involves replacing washi paper on sliding doors, folding screens and hanging scrolls marking significant ceremonies, such as weddings and funerals.

However, many of these events have moved away from the traditional settings of homes. “Culture has disappeared from communities,” Kobayashi says, adding that most Japanese now aspire to Western-style residences, rendering such time-worn Japanese features obsolete.

“The decline of regional culture, economy and local craftsmanship feels like just the tip of the iceberg. Perhaps I still want to do something about this today – or perhaps I just wanted to help my father. He passed away in March [2025].”

Kobayashi has the foundations for action. He studied product design at the Osaka University of Arts, graduating in 2010. The following year, he founded Coelacanth Shokudo to help revitalise traditional crafts.

“I was a young man who didn’t know his right from his left, a know-it-all with a head full of design education only applicable in the city,” he says. “But my enthusiasm was second to none. I was confident that with passion, ideas and skills, I could overcome any obstacle. My sense of urgency stemmed from a feeling that if I didn’t act quickly, it would be too late.”

Since 2013, Coelacanth Shokudo has focused on developing sales channels in Europe and America, collaborating with artisan businesses to generate income. This was formalised with the launch of the wholesale brand “Mujun” (meaning “contradiction”) in 2016.

From this initiative emerged a diverse range of products, including homeware, scissors, bags and wrapping cloth (furoshiki) made from vintage kimono fabric. Two years later, Mujun Workshop was established as a platform to nurture young craftspeople who would succeed retiring artisans.

Then there are the products he has designed for others, Kinjo Japan included. Kobayashi’s silicone cup collection, sold in Hong Kong at Wing On department stores, consists of silicone-rubber drinking vessels as transparent as regular glass and inspired by the Japanese kiriko tradition of glass cutting.

Kobayashi appears to have all his ducks in a row. Back on the mountain, he swings his phone from side to side, enthusiastically discussing upcoming projects.

Coils of hosepipes await connection to a small river about 200 metres up the mountain, promising an independent water supply. A barely visible track between tall hedges will soon become a road leading to fields where rice will be cultivated. He is also expecting the arrival of a Chinese-made dynamo to provide stable, independent power.

After the tour, he sits cross-legged on a dirt road with a colleague, who multitasks as his interpreter.

“Good internet connection here,” he says, when asked if he’d prefer moving to a more comfortable spot, such as the cedar-wood picnic bench and table built by the community.

He hopes to continue nurturing the satoyama way of life through Mujun Planet, while forging new connections domestically and internationally. On the family front, his wife, Misato, is learning the art of creating hanging scrolls from his mother, Misako, and he is exploring introducing the technique into his own art. Although his family’s paper-mounting enterprise ended with his father, some of the skills promise to live on.

“My ultimate goal is to sustain this satoyama life with my family. My dream is to be a sculptor, carving stone and wood using chisels I forge myself from iron sand,” he says. “I want to create a future where families engaged in various crafts live in these mountains, where children roam freely through the hills, drink mountain water and catch insects.”
