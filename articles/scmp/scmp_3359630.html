Japan weighs Myanmar aid restart to counter China’s growing clout

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 885 words | 2026-07-08

Japan is facing mounting calls to decide whether its long-frozen development aid to Myanmar should stay that way, as rights advocates warn that any resumption could ease pressure on a regime accused of widespread abuses.

The decision is a delicate one for Tokyo, which has tried to retain influence in Myanmar without appearing to legitimise the junta that seized power in 2021 – all while watching China deepen its own engagement with the country.

Human Rights Watch has urged Japan to resist Myanmar’s calls to lift its ban on new development assistance, after the military-controlled parliament claimed “ordinary citizens” were suffering as a result of the suspension.

At a press conference in Naypyidaw on June 30, parliamentary spokeswoman Khaing Khaing Soe thanked Japan for its past support but called for more, warning that “ordinary citizens will suffer” if Tokyo kept new aid on hold.

“We have benefited enormously from infrastructure development, such as bridges and roads,” she said. “Even if new projects are not feasible immediately, resuming projects that have already been agreed upon would be a great help to the people.”

Tokyo halted new official development assistance for long-term government projects after the February 2021 coup, though it has continued to provide urgent humanitarian aid through international organisations and NGOs.

It has stopped short of harsher sanctions, however – partly because it regards itself as having a “special relationship” with Myanmar and partly out of concern that taking a tougher line could push the junta further towards China, analysts say.

Teppei Kasai, senior programme officer at Human Rights Watch’s Tokyo office, said he “hopes Tokyo is not considering restarting development aid”.

“The spokesperson’s expressed concern for the well-being of Myanmar’s people bears no relation to the junta’s actions,” he said. “Since the coup, the junta has committed widespread and systematic human rights abuses, including extrajudicial killings, torture and attacks on civilians that amount to crimes against humanity and war crimes.”

Kasai noted that in September 2022, Myanmar’s military allegedly misused civilian vessels donated by Japan, deploying them to transport soldiers and weapons in its campaign against the separatist Arakan Army in Rakhine state.

Human Rights Watch has also accused military-owned conglomerates of profiting from the Japan-funded construction of a bridge over the Bago River, and the junta of repeatedly obstructing the delivery of humanitarian aid to civilians after natural disasters.

“The spokesperson’s comments are mind-boggling. It is the Myanmar junta that is hurting the ordinary people of the country through years of indiscriminate attacks against civilians,” Kasai said.

“If they are serious about Japan resuming development assistance, then the very first step they need to take is to halt all abuses of the civilian population.”

Japan’s foreign ministry has been contacted for comment on Myanmar’s request.

Kasai said he was disappointed by Tokyo’s “lukewarm” approach to the crisis.

“The Japanese government has not sufficiently called out serious abuses by the military and my sense is that Japan does not have the leverage that it believed it had,” he said, pointing out that efforts to pressure Naypyidaw had “not [produced] the desired results”.

Jeff Kingston, director of Asian Studies at Temple University’s Tokyo campus, said Japan had long considered itself to have a “special relationship” with Myanmar, based in part on war guilt.

“Japan may have a ‘special relationship’, but it’s based on whoever is in power. When it’s a more democratic government then the relationship blossoms, but when it’s like it is today, then it gets a bit more chilly. But Japan is consistently flexible because it does not want to be on the outside,” he said.

“Japan wants access to the resources that Myanmar has and, even more importantly, it does not cede any of its influence to China.”

Kingston said he expected Japan to “muddle along” in response to the aid request.

“Japan usually takes its foreign policy cues from Washington and I do not think the Trump administration cares about the human rights situation in Myanmar, but I do not believe the Japanese government will do anything that could be seen as supportive of the government there,” he said.

Even so, analysts told This Week in Asia that despite Tokyo’s concerns about pushing Naypyidaw further towards Beijing, Myanmar and China have their own differences – and the junta would likely prefer Japanese assistance anyway.

Stephen Nagy, a professor of international relations at Tokyo’s International Christian University, said Japanese aid would come with fewer long-term diplomatic strings attached, adding that Tokyo could be willing to at least explore the possibility of restarting aid.

“It’s less a question of the leverage that Japan can apply and more about Myanmar understanding that it needs aid to build some stability because it cannot go on suppressing its people constantly,” he said.

“The aid is the leverage and, if there is progress, Japan could theoretically advocate for other countries to also shift on the pressure that they are exerting.”

Nagy said a “middle power like Japan, [when] facing superpowers like China and Russia”, would need to engage countries such as Myanmar.

“Japan has to engage and offer assistance, trade and investment to build the relationship back up and to keep China’s influence to a minimum,” he said.
