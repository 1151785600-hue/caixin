Indonesia’s anti-LGBT education push raises concerns about stigmatising youth

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1035 words | 2026-07-14

Indonesia’s Ministry of Religious Affairs is drafting educational material aimed at discouraging what officials call the spread of “LGBT culture”, after a presidential regulation listed the issue among the country’s non-military security threats.

Officials said the content, which was still under discussion, could be included in religious education in regular and Islamic schools, as well as Friday prayer sermons, family development programmes and other religious events.

The government has said the presidential regulation is not meant to be the basis for criminalising any group, but experts and queer activists note that bringing the issue into classrooms risks deepening stigma around sexual orientation.

They also argue there is no scientific proof that anti-LGBT education can change a person’s sexual orientation, while warning that such teaching could harm children’s mental health.

Same-sex relations are not criminalised nationally in Indonesia, though LGBT people face deep social stigma. Aceh, the only province with special autonomy to enforce sharia law, punishes same-sex conduct.

Romo Muhammad Syafi’i, vice-minister of religion, said the material was a follow-up to the 2025 presidential regulation that included the spread of homosexuality as a non-military threat to national security, alongside separatism, terrorism and online gambling.

Coordinating Minister for Law, Human Rights, Immigration, and Corrections Yusril Ihza Mahendra has said the regulation is not a special rule targeting LGBT people and should not be read as a basis for criminalising any group.

“Currently, in accordance with the leadership’s directive, the Ministry of Religious Affairs is developing content related to LGBT in Indonesia. Discussions are still ongoing. We haven’t yet focused on more detailed content,” Abu Rokhmad, director general of Islamic community guidance at the ministry, told This Week in Asia.

“We must truly understand that LGBT [is caused] by many variations. Some are hormonal, meaning they have a hormonal predisposition [to being queer]. Some are influenced by their environment. Some are [gay] because of their belief related to freedom of sexual orientation. This requires different approaches.”

According to Rokhmad, there are suggestions that the anti-LGBT material should be taught starting in elementary school. He said teachers would also be trained on the matter but did not provide further details.

He vowed to view the issue “comprehensively”, particularly from the perspective of religious leaders.

“We must certainly be consistent with the views of religious scholars and figures regarding LGBT. Many religions generally prohibit it. We already have a regulation on this matter. Our culture also tends not to normalise LGBT,” Abu said.

No scientific proof

Social scientists and members of the LGBT community have criticised the decision, warning it could backfire with consequences for young people.

“The ministry’s position is based on the consensus of five religious figures, whom they claim disapprove of LGBT. In Indonesian society, where the majority [believes] in God, this religious-based argument has strong legitimacy and cannot be ignored,” said Wida Ayu Puspitosari, a sociologist at Brawijaya University.

“However, sexual orientation is not something that arises from exposure to information, it’s not something that can be prevented through education or counselling. Conversion efforts have also repeatedly been deemed ineffective and risk causing psychological harm.”

Wida warned that anti-LGBT teaching in schools could instead increase self-isolation among children.

“The debate about nature versus nurture is not yet fully resolved in the scientific realm, but today’s evidence does not support the idea that curriculum material can change a person’s [sexual] orientation,” Wida said.

Arisdo Gonzales, outreach director and campaigner at Pelangi Nusantara, an NGO that advocates for the LGBT community, called the anti-gay education plan an “inappropriate” approach because it discriminated against individuals with different sexual orientations.

“LGBT is an identity factor … and it cannot be spread, it is not a disease that when I poke someone, they will become gay,” he said.

For Andi*, a gay man living in Jakarta, the anti-LGBT teachings will “not have an impact and [are] simply not relevant” to an individual’s sexual orientation.

“As a gay man born into a devout Muslim family, I attended religious school from childhood to adolescence, but that doesn’t [alter] the fact that I am gay,” Andi said.

“And from my experience, there’s no connection between religion and my biological development. I continue to practise religious rituals, while at the same time, I’m gay.”

Digital persecution and raids

The anti-LGBT rhetoric has resurfaced with intensity in Indonesia in recent weeks, as members of the community faced raids, online harassment and public shaming.

Last month, a student body for psychology majors at the University of Indonesia published a post on social media, citing a 2008 study from the American Psychological Association that said homosexuality was neither a mental illness nor a psychological deviation. The post was removed after it went viral and sparked public backlash.

Also last month, two men – one of them a student – were caught kissing at a university in Jakarta. This led to them being paraded around and subjected to an informal, open “trial” in the middle of the campus courtyard.

Muhammad Cholid Nafis, vice-chairman of the Indonesian Council of Ulama, told news magazine Tempo last week that the council was drafting an academic manuscript to support a bill that would criminalise the LGBT community, to “prevent more LGBT activities in public”.

Arisdo was critical of the “digital persecution of LGBT friends” that he said had been ongoing since the start of the year. “Even my account has been attacked. Almost every minute I open social media, I see hateful messages with threats.”

He urged the government to reconsider its anti-gay stance, which fails to address the needs of today’s youth.

“Personally, I experienced what it was like to be extremely flamboyant and feminine in school. I experienced discrimination through bullying by my peers. I wasn’t focused on whether I was gay at the time, but whether I would be safe expressing myself femininely,” Arisdo said.

“What the children need is a safe space, peers who won’t judge them. They should also be able to learn without the fear of being bullied at school.”

*Name changed to protect interviewee’s identity
