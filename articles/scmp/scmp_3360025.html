How AI is changing the nature of war and conflict

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 886 words | 2026-07-10

As US President Donald Trump flew home from a fractious Nato summit in Turkey, he was poised to resume the war with Iran, whose leaders he labelled “sick” and “scum”. Trump also complained about European leaders’ failure to spend enough on arms, support him in Iran and recognise the need for the US to take control of Greenland. The sense of rising global conflict has been palpable this week.

What clearly showed at the summit of the transatlantic security alliance was confirmed by the latest Global Peace Index, released last month by the Sydney-based Institute for Economics and Peace. The report, which covers 163 economies and has been published every year since 2008, calculates that 119 economies have become less peaceful since 2008, with 103 involved in at least one external conflict over the past five years, and the number of active, state-based conflicts reaching 61 – the highest since the second world war.

More than 181,000 people were killed in conflicts in 2025, with some 117 million displaced. Global military spending reached a record US$2.9 trillion. The report estimates the total economic cost of the violence at US$21.8 trillion, or 10.5 per cent of the global gross domestic product.

More alarmingly still, the report predicts a steady deterioration in the coming decades, not just because of Trumpian bellicosity and chronic conflict across sub-Saharan Africa, but because of the rapid deployment of artificial intelligence (AI) in shaping how conflicts evolve.

In the chilling language of war, it sees AI “compressing the kill chain, lowering the marginal cost of lethality and concentrating compute and capital in a handful of firms and states”. In short, AI is enabling armies to kill more people more quickly and more cheaply.

While seven of the world’s 10 most peaceful countries are in Europe, much of the increase in worldwide military spending was also in Europe. This seeming paradox is explained by the region’s sharp re-militarisation since Russia’s war in Ukraine and Trump’s pressure on Nato members to lift military spending.

Also paradoxically, even though the US was ranked among the world’s least peaceful countries (down four places to 134th), its military expenditure in 2025 was 7.5 per cent lower than in 2024, mainly due to a sharp reduction in financial military assistance overseas. Trump’s America has also become significantly more troubled, with the indicator for violent demonstrations up by 37.5 per cent.

Despite the sharp rise in military spending, Western and Central Europe remains the world’s most peaceful region, followed by the Asia-Pacific, where the report expressed concern about China (ranked 118th and criticised for 163 “separate activities” across the South China Sea) and Myanmar (ranked 151st, where a raging civil war since 2021 and a drug economy that has more than tripled to US$35 billion over the past decade have displaced over 3.5 million people and killed an estimated 90,000).

Keen global concern is focused on Africa, in particular the five interlocked countries in the Horn of Africa – Sudan, South Sudan, Ethiopia, Eritrea and Somalia – where endemic conflict has deepened to create a “single conflict system” that has disrupted at least eight of the countries surrounding them.

The report’s keenest area of concern is how AI is changing the nature of war: “Artificial intelligence is reshaping how peace and conflict are waged, observed, and analysed,” it said. “In this environment Positive Peace – the attitudes, institutions and structures that sustain peaceful societies – becomes both more important and harder to build, because the technologies of conflict now spread faster than the institutions of peace.”

The widespread “underdog” deployment of drones and AI-assisted target detection, in particular in Ukraine and Iran, is also radically altering the balance of conventional military power. The assumption of the overwhelming “shock and awe” power of the US (and Russia) has been overturned, making clear-cut military victories increasingly elusive, and conflicts easily spread. The report says only 4 per cent of military conflicts in the past decade have ended in clear peace agreements, with most left to drift with what it describes as “low activity”.

Drones sit at the heart of AI’s radical impact on how future wars will be fought. The report notes that drones are increasingly deployed in conflict, nowhere more than in Ukraine, which deployed around 2.4 million drones last year against Russia and has spawned 500 companies that can produce 5 million drones a year.

AI is also enabling attacks of unprecedented speed. While in the 1980s it took armies a day to identify and fire at a target, AI has compressed these target-to-fire times to just seconds. In the first 12 hours of the US-Israeli Operation Epic Fury against Iran, 200 Israeli aircraft struck 500 targets, and the US recorded 900 strikes.

In Gaza, Israel’s Lavender enemy targeting system can identify and destroy an enemy in just 20 seconds, reportedly flagging around 37,000 Palestinians as suspected militants. It boasted an “error rate” of just 10 per cent – which means “just” 3,700 innocents were killed. “Military capabilities are evolving faster than the legal or governance frameworks meant to regulate them,” the report observed.

This all brings to mind the “Doomsday Clock”, published every January by the Bulletin of the Atomic Scientists, which gauges how close humanity is to self-inflicted catastrophe. The clock says we are just 85 seconds short of midnight. I wonder what further conflict needs to happen to push us to that apocalyptic time?
