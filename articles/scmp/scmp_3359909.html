Malaysians in Hong Kong locked out of state elections amid tight voting deadlines

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 763 words | 2026-07-09

Jack Yu wants to vote in Saturday’s crucial election in his home state of Johor, Malaysia, but being based in Hong Kong has left him frozen out after he missed the short window to register for a postal vote.

Hong Kong is home to an estimated 15,000 Malaysians working across finance to hospitality, according to the Southeast Asian nation’s consulate general. Many remain closely engaged with politics back home – more so when the result could reshape the balance of power in Putrajaya.

Yu, 36, missed the Johor registration, despite having voted by post before and helping other Malaysians in Hong Kong return ballots during the 2022 general election.

The singer and bartender, who has lived in Hong Kong since 2009, said the problem was a lack of official communication, meaning overseas voters like himself were left to track and keep abreast of election timelines pretty much by themselves.

“Throughout my years in Hong Kong, I have never received any updates from the Election Commission or the consulate about elections at all,” he said, adding that his information often came through Malaysian community networks.

If voters were informed early enough, the official 10-day registration period could still be workable in Hong Kong, where courier services were efficient, he said.

The process, though, can be stressful. In the 2022 election, Yu spent nearly two days helping other Malaysians collect their ballot slips to ensure they made the deadline. Even then, he said, there had been anxiety over delayed ballots, witness requirements and whether votes would arrive in time.

But the experience of Hong Kong-based Johor voters is not all bad, pointing to patchy communication across the city’s Malaysian diaspora.

Finance executive Y.H. Tan, 33, said his ballot arrived three days after candidates were nominated on June 27, the fastest he had ever received one.

Tan, who votes in the Johor Jaya state seat, sent his completed ballot back by registered courier.

Still, he said Malaysia’s Election Commission (EC) could improve the process by lengthening the period between nomination and polling days and by giving voters confirmation that their ballots had been received.

“Every time, it’s like throwing a stone into the sea, I don’t know if my vote has been received or not,” he said.

The Consulate General of Malaysia in Hong Kong said it did not track or keep statistics on how many Hong Kong-based Malaysians had applied for postal voting in the Johor and Negeri Sembilan state elections, but said it had observed strong engagement from the Malaysian community.

“Since the EC digitised the process, all Category 1B (Overseas Voter) postal vote applications are submitted directly by citizens via the centralised MySPR Daftar online portal. The Consulate General does not act as an intermediary in this registration process,” it replied.

Voter disconnect

Electoral consulting organisation Tindak Malaysia said the share of overseas Malaysians who registered for postal voting in the Johor state polls fell from 21.2 per cent in 2022 to 4.2 per cent this year.

The apparent tail-off in engagement may reflect a wider disconnect between Malaysian expatriates and politics at home.

“The core problem is not only the length of the application window,” said Nirmala Devi Windgaetter, president of electoral reform group Global Bersih, the Geneva-based international arm of Malaysia’s Bersih electoral reform movement.

‘’The more fundamental issue is the lack of overseas voter mobilisation and voter education.’’

She said extending the registration period alone would not be enough without earlier outreach and better voter education on steps such as checking voter eligibility, registering an account on the EC’s voter services portal, applying as an overseas postal voter and tracking relevant deadlines.

Global Bersih had also received questions from Malaysians based in other countries, such as Dubai, she said, suggesting that confusion and a last-minute scramble to exercise voting rights was a wider issue.

A Hong Kong-based worker in the medical devices industry, who asked to be identified only as Calvin, similarly said no announcements about the election in his home state of Negeri Sembilan had reached him. Postal voting registration for the state election opened on June 12 and closes on Friday.

State elections should be given the same overseas voting support as general elections, Windgaetter said.

With Johor and Negeri Sembilan both running on 14-day campaign periods, overseas voters were being given too little time to receive and return ballots, she said, calling for campaign periods of 21 to 25 days.

The Malaysian consulate said it had also focused on outreach, including advisories as well as through community groups such as the Malaysian Association of Hong Kong.
