To boost manufacturing, India need not choose between Japan and China

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 888 words | 2026-07-16

Last week, Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi led a business delegation to New Delhi for the 16th India-Japan annual summit. During last year’s summit in Tokyo, Takaichi’s predecessor, Shigeru Ishiba, and Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi outlined the Japan-India Joint Vision for the next decade – during which period India may see up to 10 trillion yen (US$62 billion) in investment from Japan.

Some of Japan’s most notable investments in India have been in the financial sector. Japanese financial group MUFG’s US$4.4 billion stake in Shriram Finance announced last December is one example.

Japan has been a major investor in India, with a cumulative foreign direct investment (FDI) of over US$48 billion this century. Leaving aside Singapore and Mauritius, which serve as domiciles for channelling investments to India, Japan was second only to the US in the scale of FDI investment in India – with US$3.2 billion in the 2025-26 financial year.

However, India’s trade with Japan was a modest US$27.5 billion in the 2025-26 financial year, compared with US$151 billion with China in that period. While Japan is only ranked 10th in trade with India, China is India’s top trading partner in goods – pointing to how complementary the two economies are. Japan’s industrial investments in India often need imported components from China.

FDI is a key driver of economic development. Upon its entry into the World Trade Organization in 2001, China saw brisk growth in the inflow of FDI. According to World Bank data, the country’s net FDI intake grew from US$42 billion in 2000 to almost US$244 billion in 2010. After a peak of US$290 billion in 2013, China saw another peak of US$344 billion in 2021, before dropping to US$43 billion in 2024. At the end of 2023, China’s inward FDI stock stood at US$3.7 trillion.

In comparison, India’s net FDI inflow was US$3.6 billion in 2000, peaking at US$64 billion in 2020 and falling to US$27 billion in 2024. While the cumulative inflow of FDI into India has approached US$1.2 trillion since 2000, India’s FDI stock was only about half a trillion in 2023.

India has taken a path distinct from the East Asian manufacturing model. India’s manufacturing value added as a percentage of gross domestic product declined from 17 per cent in 2006 to 13 per cent in 2025. Bangladesh’s increased from 15 per cent to 22 per cent in the period. Vietnam’s grew from 19 per cent to 25 per cent.

This pattern may be seen in the composition of FDI into India. In 2025-26, 17 per cent of FDI was directed at the service sector, far outpacing automotive (4 per cent) and pharmaceuticals (3 per cent). The leading FDI category was computer software and hardware (representing 24 per cent) – a sector historically dominated by software.

With these sectors facing pressure from artificial intelligence, India must urgently turn towards manufacturing. Will industrial investments from Japan materialise soon enough to close the gap India faces?

Japan is a key supplier of critical components while China is a leading supplier of basic components. With the unrivalled breadth and depth of its supply chain, China is an anchor partner of choice for most nations seeking to industrialise.

Without Chinese suppliers on the ground, potential Japanese investments in India will struggle to reach the scale or speed the moment demands. As China is the world’s manufacturing powerhouse, its firms can make indispensable contributions to upgrading India’s supply chain– building ecosystems where Japanese investments can thrive.

Tata Semiconductor Assembly and Test (TSAT) has adopted the outsourced semiconductor assembly and test (OSAT) model. OSAT capacity today is concentrated in mainland China and Taiwan. While Tokyo Electron has signed an agreement to supply equipment to Tata Electronics, a more structurally important equipment provider to TSAT is Singapore-headquartered and Hong Kong-listed ASMPT, in which Dutch semiconductor equipment maker ASM International is a major shareholder.

Ishiba’s bold vision, now carried by his successor, will be realised only if India makes itself an attractive investment destination. In IMD’s World Competitiveness Ranking, mainland China is ranked 12th and India 44th. In the Kearney FDI Confidence Index, Japan and China are ranked third and fourth while India is ranked 22nd.

India’s manufacturing investment prospects face challenges in terms of infrastructure, logistics and regulatory frameworks. Without resolving these bottlenecks, how will the promised investment from the 120 business deals signed with Japanese companies over the past year be realised?

In the flying geese configuration of East Asian industrialisation, many mature industries flowed from Japan to South Korea, and then to China and Southeast Asia. As the earliest Asian economy to reindustrialise after World War II, Japan is today a deindustrialising advanced economy – its manufacturing value-added declined from 24 per cent in 1995 to 19 per cent in 2024. Many labour-intensive industries which would be a good fit for India left Japan decades ago.

In contrast, China is industrially comparable to India – world-leading in some sectors, down-to-earth in others. The Chinese mainland started its industrialisation process with labour-intensive industries from Hong Kong and Taiwan before moving to more sophisticated manufacturing outsourced from Japan and the US, climbing the development ladder step-by-step.

Given the scale of its development challenge, India would need all the FDI it can get from both Japan and China. Both countries are necessary. New Delhi skips the China rung of its industrialisation ladder at its own peril.
