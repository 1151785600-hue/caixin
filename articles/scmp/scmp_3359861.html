Nato, Indo-Pacific 4 pledge defence, tech boost as deeper China-Russia ties spark alarm

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 925 words | 2026-07-08

Nato and its Indo-Pacific partners have pledged to expand cooperation in defence industry and advanced technology as the transatlantic security alliance boosts its military build-up in the face of deepening Russia-China ties.

Secretary General Mark Rutte on Tuesday met with top officials from the Indo-Pacific Four (IP4) partner nations – Australia, Japan, New Zealand and South Korea – on the sidelines of the Nato summit in Ankara.

The talks featured South Korean President Lee Jae Myung and Foreign Minister Cho Hyun, Japanese Foreign Minister Toshimitsu Motegi and Defence Minister Shinjiro Koizumi, New Zealand Defence Minister Chris Penk and the Australian defence industry minister, Pat Conroy.

Lee is the only IP4 state leader attending the two-day summit, which closes today.

According to the Japanese foreign ministry, Rutte and the IP4 leaders shared “candid” views on the international situation, including Russia’s aggression against Ukraine, the situation in the Indo-Pacific such as issues related to China, their policies towards North Korea and the situation in Iran. They agreed to strengthen cooperation in areas such as the defence industry, cyberspace and technology, it said.

Nato intended to strengthen its relations with the IP4 to “appropriately address various challenges facing the international community”, Rutte was quoted as saying.

Motegi said: “The security of the Euro-Atlantic and the Indo-Pacific is inseparable as seen in the military cooperation between Russia and North Korea in Ukraine and … it is highly important for the IP4 and Nato to gather and confirm their solidarity in this manner.”

“A robust Nato-IP4 relationship also contributes to the peace and stability of the international community as a whole,” the Japanese foreign minister added, while expressing his “intention to further build up practical cooperation with Nato and further strengthen [the] strategic relationship”.

The IP4 partner group was formed for Nato to address interconnected security dynamics between the Euro-Atlantic and Indo-Pacific regions, and the four non-member nations have been invited to the alliance’s annual summits as guests since 2022.

Delivering a keynote speech to open the Nato Summit Defence Industry Forum earlier on Tuesday, Rutte said that Russia was pouring half of its national budget into its “war machine” in Ukraine, and “increasingly” working together with China, North Korea and Iran.

He said China had continued to modernise its armed forces and expand its nuclear capabilities “without transparency”, while North Korea continued to expand its nuclear programme and supply Russia. This “should concern us all”, Rutte cautioned, calling for increased cooperation within the alliance as well as with Indo-Pacific partners.

While Beijing maintains it is neutral on the war in Ukraine, it has been accused of supporting Russia with dual-use technology and equipment, such as microelectronics, advanced machine tools, drone components and raw materials.

China and Russia also regularly carry out joint military exercises, including a joint bomber fleet patrol last month and naval drills off eastern China that began this week.

A Reuters report last week citing a classified Russian document said that a Russian military delegation took part in training exercises at a People’s Liberation Army facility in Beijing in November, focusing on radiological, chemical and biological warfare. China denies training Russian troops.

Meanwhile, Beijing is also advancing its military build-up. On Monday, China said it had test-fired a “strategic missile” carrying a dummy warhead towards the Pacific from a nuclear-powered submarine – in the first known submarine-based missile test by the PLA since 1982.

Rutte said the missile test was “evidence” that Nato could not be “naive” about China, telling a pre-summit press conference in the Turkish capital on Monday that “what happens in the Indo-Pacific is relevant to what is happening in the transatlantic”. He also described China, North Korea and Iran as “key enablers of Russia’s unprovoked war of aggression against Ukraine”.

With Nato aiming to raise defence spending to 5 per cent of gross domestic product by 2035, industrial collaboration has emerged as the central theme of this year’s summit, alongside growing interest in how the IP4 countries could support the alliance’s military build-up.

According to the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute, South Korea was the second-largest exporter of weapons to European Nato members between 2021 and 2025, behind the United States.

Korean President Lee also held a separate meeting with Rutte before the IP4 talks, where they started negotiations to sign a basic bilateral procurement agreement.

Lee said such an agreement would provide a “solid foundation” for South Korea’s defence companies to expand further into the Nato defence market.

“I look forward to seeing our companies – possessing world-class technology and competitiveness – fully demonstrate their capabilities on a larger stage and create new opportunities for growth,” Lee posted on social media.

South Korea’s national security director, Wi Sung-lac, said in Ankara that the agreement would mean “securing a foothold to enter the world’s largest defence procurement market”, providing access to Nato’s joint procurement market worth around 15 trillion won (US$9.9 billion) annually.

On the meeting with other IP4 representatives, Lee said that the participants agreed to expand cooperation in defence industries, cutting-edge technologies and innovation, by leveraging the strengths of the partners in Europe and the Indo-Pacific. This could further bolster “collective security and solidify global peace and stability”, he said in his post, while warning that no country could handle the situation on its own.

“The current security landscape is evolving so rapidly and is of such complexity that no single nation can address the challenges alone.”
