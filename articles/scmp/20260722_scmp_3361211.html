‘Payback’: why China’s world-beating car industry faces plunging sales at home

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1817 words | 2026-07-22

China’s electric vehicle (EV) industry sits at the centre of numerous economic and geopolitical trends: consumer spending, environmental protection, trade policy, high-end manufacturing, technology and more. In this short series, we assess the current state of the sector within these broader contexts. Here, Emma Ma examines the forces driving higher exports and lower domestic sales.

Wang Wenfei, a 30-year-old chef from central China’s Henan province, had long dreamed of buying a new car. So, when he received his annual bonus in February, he decided to splash out as a gift to himself after a decade of hard work at a restaurant in Shanghai.

“A comfortable new car that I can drive home to Henan for Chinese New Year was all I wanted,” he said, setting himself a budget of 100,000 yuan (US$14,700).

However, just weeks later, Wang received an unpleasant surprise: the government had slashed its subsidy for new car purchases by 33 per cent to 10,000 yuan. Meanwhile, petrol prices were rising amid the war in the Middle East – and his employer had declined to give him a pay rise.

Reluctantly, he decided to put his dream on hold.

Wang is far from alone. China is now the world’s largest car exporter, but sales at home have hit the skids in recent months, as the government scales back its subsidy programme and consumers remain reluctant to spend amid a shaky job market and prolonged property downturn.

The trend is creating a headache for Chinese carmakers like BYD and Chery Automobile – pushing them to lean more heavily on overseas markets at a time of rising trade tensions – as well as complicating Beijing’s efforts to rebalance China’s economy towards domestic consumption.

The trade-in ‘payback’ effect

The subsidy change is a major reason behind the fall. China introduced a generous trade-in programme for new cars in 2024 as part of a drive to rev up the domestic economy, which offered buyers 15,000 yuan off any purchase.

The scheme proved hugely successful and was extended last year, when China invested a whopping 300 billion yuan (US$44 billion) to provide subsidies on an array of consumer goods to help the economy ride out a trade war with the United States.

But with China and the US reaching a trade truce and the trade-in programme showing signs of diminishing returns, Beijing trimmed its subsidies earlier this year. The total funding for the programme was reduced by 50 billion yuan, and discounts for new car purchases were adjusted downwards.

Previously, buyers received a flat 15,000 yuan subsidy on any new car regardless of price. Now, they receive a subsidy equivalent to 10 per cent of the purchase price, which is capped at 15,000 yuan. That means the subsidy for a 100,000 yuan petrol car – like the one Wang hoped to buy – has fallen by 5,000 yuan.

The reduction has had a direct impact on car sales, according to analysts. China was always going to experience a form of “payback” for the trade-in programme, as the subsidies effectively front-loaded future demand by convincing large numbers of consumers to replace their cars earlier than needed, Beijing-based research firm Gavekal Dragonomics said in a research report in late June.

Now, with the subsidies scaled back, the payback effect has kicked in and may continue for some time, with Gavekal Dragonomics predicting that it could “depress retail sales growth” for “as long as two years”.

Sales of passenger vehicles in China fell 20.2 per cent year on year during the first half of 2026, data from the China Passenger Car Association (CPCA) showed. The decline accelerated over the past two months, with sales tumbling more than 23 per cent in June – the second-steepest decline recorded so far this year.

Why consumers expect another price war

The outbreak of the Iran war has compounded the problem, with higher petrol prices making consumers less willing to invest in a car. Though China imposed some price controls, petrol prices on the mainland still climbed over 25 per cent between late February and early April, and remained elevated into July.

“The biggest drag on May and June’s weak sales performance was the collapse of consumers’ demand for petrol vehicles, a direct fallout from elevated pump prices,” said Cui Dongshu, secretary general of the CPCA.

The sales decline also has a self-reinforcing dynamic, as Chinese consumers widely expect carmakers to respond by slashing prices. China’s car industry has been gripped by vicious price wars for years, especially in the electric vehicle (EV) segment, where Beijing has repeatedly railed against “involutionary” competition, policymakers’ term for the race to the bottom in certain sectors.

Now, many buyers believe brands will soon be forced to cut their prices once again, and they are deliberately delaying purchases as a result.

“Consumers now have become less inclined to pay premiums for owning foreign-branded cars,” Cui said. “Instead, they widely expect petrol car prices to slide further.”

That was exactly what Wang had in mind. “The vehicle market is seeing a sales decline, and we bet carmakers will have to offer big discounts later this year to draw new buyers. I will not make a purchase decision unless prices appear to be attractive,” he said.

Wu Jinling, a Shanghai-based consumer, made a similar point. She is looking to buy an electric vehicle to replace her petrol car, but believes she might get a better deal later this year.

“Competition among new models will get fiercer in the coming months,” Wu said. “Price cuts can be expected, as all the car brands struggle to reduce their inventories.”

Juchen Auto Trade, a car showroom in Shanghai, is already feeling the pressure. “Most buyers appear to be budget-conscious and want us to offer steeper discounts,” said Steve Shi, a manager at the firm. “Consumer sentiment is extremely weak, and we are pessimistic about the sales outlook this year.”

The subsidies will shore up domestic entry-level auto consumption in the short term

Cui Dongshu, China Passenger Car Association

How Beijing aims to boost demand

The Chinese government has already rolled out several measures in an attempt to shore up the car market, including a fresh round of subsidies for vehicle purchases in rural areas.

The rural subsidy programme dates back to 2009, and since 2020 it has focused solely on supporting electric car purchases. According to figures from the China Association of Automobile Manufacturers, the policy has helped generate more than 20 million EV sales in rural China over the past six years.

This year, the programme has expanded to cover 155 vehicle models, 31 more than last year. The subsidies also cover cars in a wider range of price brackets, applying to models costing from 30,000 yuan to 500,000 yuan, according to a report by Kaiyuan Securities released in late June.

“The subsidies will shore up domestic entry-level auto consumption in the short term,” said Cui of the CPCA. “This year’s subsidies also cover EV charging infrastructure construction across rural areas, which will eventually stimulate fresh demand for trading in old petrol cars for EVs.”

But he warned that any sales bounce would not match those seen over the previous few years.

Analysts have echoed that view. In late June, Goldman Sachs lowered its forecast for passenger vehicle sales on the Chinese mainland over the next five years, after demand turned out to be weaker than expected in the first five months of 2026.

The pivot to exports – and growing pushback

Sluggish demand at home is driving China’s carmakers to focus more on expanding their overseas sales.

China has rapidly emerged as a car export powerhouse over the past few years, driven by the breakout success of the country’s EV industry, and the trend accelerated further in May.

The country’s passenger vehicle exports reached 800,000 in the first five months of the year, up 73 per cent year on year, according to a report by HSBC released in June. Electric cars were a primary driver of the boom, with EVs accounting for more than half of China’s car exports during the period, up from 42 per cent over the same period in 2025, the report added.

Macquarie Equity Research, a global financial services group based in Australia, recently lifted its forecast for China’s automotive export growth in 2026 from 13.5 per cent year on year to 38 per cent.

The rise of China’s carmakers has been particularly dramatic in Europe, where Chinese brands outsold Japanese marques for the first time in May, according to data from the European Automobile Manufacturers’ Association. The trend is all the more impressive given Chinese battery electric vehicles have faced tariffs in the European Union since 2024.

Chen Zhiyi, an employee at a Chinese retailer based in Germany, said Chinese-branded cars were still a rare sight on the streets of Munich – the home of German auto giant BMW Group – but local consumer interest in Chinese EVs was steadily rising.

“A neighbour of mine recently rented a BYD electric car at a dealership. Chinese car models offer a far wider range of colour options, but what ultimately swayed her was the discounted monthly rental cost of less than €300 (US$342.5), much cheaper than local brands,” Chen said. “Chinese cars are still a new thing to Germans, but I think their advantages have been evident. They will become more popular for sure.”

However, China’s rapid gains in the European car market have sparked alarm on the continent, with Brussels facing growing calls to take stronger action to protect domestic industries.

In June, German business newspaper Handelsblatt reported the European Commission planned to impose tariffs on Chinese plug-in hybrid electric vehicles – a measure that could be painful for China’s carmakers, which pivoted to hybrids after the EU imposed tariffs on their pure electric models.

If the tariffs go ahead, “prices for Chinese-made vehicles would surge in European markets, heaping heavy pressure on domestic carmakers”, said Sun Xiaohong, secretary general of the automotive branch of the China Chamber of Commerce for Import and Export of Machinery and Electronic Products.

But Sun added that several Chinese carmakers were already working on projects to build manufacturing facilities across the EU, allowing them to avoid the levies. “So, I think they will be able to swiftly adjust their strategies and ramp up local production,” she said.

BYD, the world’s largest EV maker, is building a factory in Hungary and is in talks to take over an existing European plant, Reuters reported in July. SAIC – the owner of MG – plans to build a facility in Spain, according to another Reuters report in June.

Chery is discussing a deal with Nissan to use spare capacity at the Japanese firm’s plant in the United Kingdom, while Leapmotor is looking to leverage its partnership with global auto giant Stellantis to begin production in Spain.
