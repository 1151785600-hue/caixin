Sexual offences law review a chance to make clear what constitutes abuse

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 695 words | 2026-07-13

In recent years, Hong Kong society has repeatedly confronted the realities of sexual violence, sexual harassment and online sexual exploitation. Survivors’ experiences have made it clear that our sexual offences law is no longer adequate for the world we live in today.

Against this backdrop, the government has issued a consultation paper on improving the laws on sexual offences in Hong Kong. It aims to clarify and give a concrete definition to “consent” and to build a modern framework for non‑consensual sexual offences that reflects gender equality and contemporary understandings of sexual autonomy.

This requires recognising that both women and men can be victims of rape and other serious sexual offences, rejecting outdated notions that equate the absence of resistance with consent and focusing instead on free and voluntary agreement in the context of fear, coercion and power imbalances.

At the same time, it calls for strengthening protection for those who are particularly vulnerable – including children under 16, people with mental impairment, people of different sexual orientations and other marginalised groups – so harmful conduct is properly captured and those most at risk of exploitation or abuse receive effective and meaningful protection.

It is important to emphasise that this reform exercise is not a knee‑jerk response to recent headlines. The Law Reform Commission started work on sexual offences many years ago, setting up a sub‑committee in 2006 and issuing four consultation papers between 2012 and 2020 that invited detailed input from stakeholders and the public.

On the basis of that process, the commission published reports in 2019 and 2022, making a series of final recommendations which now form the backbone of the government’s proposals. What we see today is therefore the product of more than a decade of research by eminent jurists, comparative study and public engagement, rather than short‑term political or media pressure.

Viewed from an international perspective, the case for reform in Hong Kong is clear. Our sexual offences provisions remain largely based on the English Sexual Offences Act 1956, even though social attitudes to sexuality, gender and personal autonomy have changed dramatically, especially the non-consensual creation and sharing of intimate images.

Many comparable common law jurisdictions have already overhauled their laws to meet these challenges. England and Wales replaced the 1956 act with the Sexual Offences Act 2003, recalibrating the law around a modern concept of consent and expanding offences to reflect current realities. Canada, Australia and New Zealand have similarly revised their frameworks to strengthen the level of protection for children and other vulnerable people.

Hong Kong now has an opportunity to draw on the experience of others while tailoring solutions that will fit its own context. It can ensure that reforms both enhance protection for victims of sexual offences and bring our legal framework into line with international practice and the expectations of a modern, rights‑respecting society.

However, this is not only about alignment with international trends or refining legal definitions. It is about the everyday safety and dignity of people living in Hong Kong. From intimate relationships and family life to workplace interactions and online communication, we all navigate situations where bodily autonomy, privacy and power imbalances come into play.

Children, people with mental impairment, sexual minorities and other vulnerable groups can often bear the greatest risks, so they require enhanced protection. A clearer and more contemporary legal framework for sexual offences can provide a common language for what counts as respect and what constitutes abuse.

It can make it easier for survivors to seek help and for bystanders to understand when intervention is warranted. If the current reform can be implemented thoughtfully with genuine public participation, it should help to reduce incidents of sexual violence and exploitation and encourage us to re-examine our practices in education, workplace culture and institutional design.

Different stakeholders in society will inevitably hold divergent views on the details set out in the consultation paper. Whether one agrees with the consultation document in its entirety or wishes to see amendments to particular aspects, the reform of laws that have been in force for more than 50 years is, in any event, the only way forward if we are to safeguard the interests of society as a whole.
