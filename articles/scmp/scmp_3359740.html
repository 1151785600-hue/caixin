Indian ambassador to China urges deeper economic ties, touts AIIB as model

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 758 words | 2026-07-08

Creating “new institutional architecture” like the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB) could ease “security-first” economics globally, New Delhi’s new top envoy to Beijing has suggested, as both countries work to mend ties.

Vikram Doraiswami, who took up the post in May, also called for greater access for Indian products to China and more Chinese investment in India, efforts that he said would benefit relations between the two Himalayan neighbours.

The senior diplomat said countries were now “less willing to look at preferential trading arrangements” around the world, adding that beyond tariffs, the global economy faced other barriers in certification processes, export controls, weaponisation of finance, technology and digital infrastructure.

Doraiswami also pointed to a global trend of looking increasingly at “moving away from efficiency-first economics to security-first production”, with many pursuing “competitive advantage rather than comparative advantage” for a “unilateral self-reliant system”.

The Indian ambassador made the comments on Saturday during a panel discussion at the World Peace Forum in Beijing, an event jointly organised by Tsinghua University and the state-backed Chinese People’s Institute of Foreign Affairs.

Different economies were reluctant to open up areas where they had shortcomings and often sought free trade in sectors where they were strongly positioned, Doraiswami said.

To deal with resistance to global economic connectivity, staying receptive to the idea of building up multiple nodes of cooperation could be an “excellent” solution, he argued.

“If we are not willing to democratise or modernise the Bretton Woods institutions”, Doraiswami said, referring to agencies such as the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund, “creating more options has become a viable alternative”.

He cited the AIIB in Beijing as one of the “best” examples of working together.

“The fact that … India and China could work together as leading partners to start with, this has become one of the excellent examples in which countries across a region can set aside every other kind of difference and focus on the opportunity created by new institutional architecture to offer developmental advantages for more people.”

China and India are among the 57 founding nations of the Beijing-initiated multilateral development bank, which opened its doors in 2016. Delhi is the AIIB’s second-largest shareholder.

Doraiswami said that India’s position as a top borrower for infrastructure funding and a reliable credit risk for the AIIB showed “what can be created if we are willing to look beyond the headlines”.

His remarks come as developing countries, particularly China, raise concerns about the global governance system’s ability to address mounting challenges, including persistent protectionism.

Both Beijing and Delhi have also been vocal advocates for granting emerging-market economies a greater voice in international organisations to match their rising economic clout.

Despite long-standing border disputes and perceived rivalry over leadership of the Global South, the two countries have upheld their commitments to new multilateral platforms such as the AIIB and the Brics mechanism, of which they are also founding members.

Trade between the world’s two most populous countries has kept expanding even amid a multi-year freeze in relations following the Galwan Valley clash of 2020, a skirmish between Chinese and Indian troops along the Line of Actual Control, the countries’ de facto border. It was the deadliest confrontation between India and China in 45 years.

Still, as one of the world’s most active users of anti-dumping tools against China, India tightened restrictions that year on inbound investment by Chinese companies. The restrictions were only partially eased in March during a thaw in ties.

Asked about India’s trade deficit with China, Doraiswami described it as “a matter of considerable interest” to Delhi, noting the two sides had discussed it over several years.

“We would like to be able to export more to China,” he said. “There is nothing unreasonable about suggesting that, particularly in areas where we believe we have [a] competitive advantage, like [in] pharmaceuticals, for instance.”

In addition, India “would love” to expand the conversation with China on structural issues, including trade remedy measures.

“As the relationship is moving towards normalisation, the government of India has taken steps to re-establish [opportunities] for Chinese businesses to invest in the Indian market,” he added. “We would like that connection to be better.”

Doraiswami said the Indian embassy would listen to the concerns of Chinese businesses and give more hands-on assistance to help them enter the Indian market.

“That, I think, is good for the economics of the relationship. It’s also good for the larger country-to-country relationship.”
