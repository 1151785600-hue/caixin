‘Cherry-picked’ crimes: call for legal reforms to cover long-term child sexual abuse

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 889 words | 2026-07-08

While most Hong Kong children find sanctuary at home, Bobo* spent her primary and secondary school years constantly on high alert, always looking for a new place to hide in her flat.

“Every single day I had to figure out how to avoid that person, how to find another safe spot inside the flat,” said the young woman, who suffered repeated sexual abuse by a family member.

Another girl, Mui*, said nights and school holidays were the most terrifying times. She would often jolt awake with the realisation that the rest of the family was either asleep or out, leaving her alone with her stepfather.

In addition to sexually abusing her from the age of 13, Mui’s stepfather would scold and beat her, and at times even force her out of the home as punishment for disobedience.

“It felt like I had completely lost my freedom,” said the 25-year-old, who remained silent in her younger years for fear that speaking out might cost her and her mother their home.

Sexual abuse survivors such as Bobo and Mui are calling on Hong Kong to introduce a new offence of persistent child sexual abuse, which would address repeated abuse over a prolonged period. Their appeal comes as the Security Bureau prepares to launch a public consultation on reforming sexual offence laws that have remained largely unchanged since the 1950s.

The proposed reform comes two decades after the Law Reform Commission (LRC) set up a subcommittee in 2006 to study the issue and is based on its recommendations.

A consultation paper submitted by the bureau to the Legislative Council on Monday outlined the amendments to existing sexual offences and introduced dozens of new crimes, including the criminalisation of grooming a child.

However, it stopped short of proposing a specific offence covering long-term child sexual abuse, leaving existing prosecution gaps unaddressed, said Cheryl Yip Cheuk-yi, advocacy officer at the Association Concerning Sexual Violence Against Women.

Under current laws, victims such as Mui and Bobo must isolate a small number of incidents for prosecution from what may be dozens or even hundreds of episodes over the years.

This “fails to accurately reflect the severity of a long-term relationship of sexual exploitation”, Yip said.

Both women eventually went to authorities but felt their horrific experiences were “cherry-picked” or filtered.

“Our legal system has to respond to the unique nature of these sexual abuses that are highly repetitive and marked by delayed reporting,” Yip said.

Mui spent years piecing together details of the abuse and speaking to her family before filing a police report against her stepfather in 2024. She provided four accounts, although she estimated she endured more than 100 instances of sexual abuse over the years.

An old iPod she used as a teenager helped support her case. She had recorded some incidents in the Notes app with timestamps, and copies of those entries were used as evidence.

In May, her stepfather was convicted of two counts of indecent assault, one count of rape and one count of committing an indecent act with a child under 16. He was sentenced to 13 years in prison.

But Mui said the charges did not reflect the sustained pattern of abuse.

“Sexual violence does not just happen at a single point in time, but it’s a long-term harassment using verbal abuse and physical actions,” Mui said. “He did not suddenly ambush me, but had always screamed at me and beaten me.”

However, these actions were not accepted as evidence in court to prove her case.

Yip said that requiring victims to recall precise details of abuse from childhood fails to recognise how trauma affects memory. Repeated abuse can blur recollection and make it difficult for survivors to distinguish between individual incidents.

Bobo, who also reported her case to police, described the distress of recounting events in “meticulous detail”.

“Especially every single one of his movements, what time it was, and how long it lasted … it is not that I did not want to remember what happened, but that the memory itself is simply impossible to retrieve,” she said.

More than half of 500 child sexual abuse cases involved ongoing and repeated incidents, according to data from from 2019 to 2023 from the sexual violence crisis centre Rainlily.

The proposal to introduce a specific offence of “persistent sexual abuse of a child” was first raised by the Department of Justice in 2000 to address loopholes that could provide “a charter of immunity” to offenders.

However, the legislation was later shelved amid concerns from legal professionals about potentially “draconian changes in the substantive law”.

Elsewhere, jurisdictions in Australia have introduced offences targeting persistent child sexual abuse, with variations across states, focusing on patterns of unlawful sexual relationships.

Hong Kong could draw on that experience to “craft an offence that reflects the reality of repeated abuse while preserving fair trial protections”, said Darcy Davison-Roberts, a senior lecturer at the University of Hong Kong’s faculty of law.

Bobo said she used to grieve her stolen childhood.

“But at least I can live a normal life now, and this alone means a lot to me,” she said.

Mui urged others not to be afraid.

“Take your time to speak up, and there is nothing you cannot overcome,” she said.

“There will definitely be someone who can help.”

*Names changed at interviewees’ request.
