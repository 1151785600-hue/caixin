China’s tax crackdown shakes A-share and Hong Kong-listed firms with major payouts

SCMP | FREE | 490 words | 2026-07-08

China’s anti-tax evasion campaign is sweeping across the A-share and Hong Kong stock markets, with at least 80 listed firms already having been ordered to pay back corporate income taxes and late fees to local authorities.

The number of listed companies involved in the first half of the year is likely to soon surpass the total of 89 for full-year 2025, raising concerns that Beijing’s taxation drive could be extended amid local debt black holes, which would threaten corporate earnings, cash flows and investor sentiment.

Pharmaceutical, new materials, chemicals, environmental protection, agriculture, and information technology firms accounted for most of the cases, according to Shanghai-based Wind, a Chinese financial market data provider.

“The surge in supplementary tax payments reflects China’s efforts to reduce system leakages amid pressure on the fiscal deficit,” said Gary Ng, senior economist for Asia-Pacific at Natixis.

“The main drag now falls on A-shares, but there could be spillover to Hong Kong-traded shares as well.”

Global oncology developer BeOne Medicines, triple-listed in mainland China, Hong Kong and the US, was among the high-profile names caught up in the compliance wave. Its Chinese subsidiary agreed to pay about 446 million yuan (US$65.6 million) after receiving a notice from a local tax authority, according to a June 26 filing with the Hong Kong stock exchange.

The payment could potentially affect the company’s operating income this financial year, according to Zhang Jialin, head of China healthcare research at Nomura.

The tax bill is equivalent to about 8 to 9 per cent of BeOne’s estimated full-year operating income in 2026, said Zhang. However, “it is not a reason for investors to worry about the company’s fundamentals”.

State-owned Heilongjiang Agriculture was among the hardest hit. The company was ordered to pay back corporate income tax of 1.02 billion yuan and late-payment surcharges of 386 million yuan for the period from 2021 to 2025, revealed by a filing with the Shanghai Stock Exchange on June 23.

The payment was expected to reduce net profit attributable to Heilongjiang Agriculture’s shareholders by 1.41 billion yuan in financial year 2026. In Shanghai over the past two weeks, the company’s shares hit rock bottom at 10.69 yuan apiece on June 25, before rebounding to 11.03 yuan on July 1.

Other companies on the list included Guangzhou Jinzhong Auto Parts Manufacturing, an industrial components maker; Chengdu Olymvax Biopharmaceuticals, a human vaccines producer; and Lopal Tech, which manufactures automotive fine chemicals and lithium-ion battery cathode materials.

These payments could weigh on investor sentiment in the near term, according to Ng. “Sectors that have previously enjoyed tax benefits may now face greater reversal risks unless the firms are critical to supply chain security,” he said.

“China’s financial market is moving towards stricter compliance rules and tax enforcement, which is not bad if it comes with transparency. But the question is how much leakage needs to be recovered during the transition, which could affect corporate profitability,” Ng added.
