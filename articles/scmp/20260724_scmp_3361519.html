‘She’s blown it’: support frays for Japan PM Takaichi over controversial laws

SCMP | FREE | 1019 words | 2026-07-24

Public support for Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi appears to be fraying, with two new opinion polls suggesting voters are losing patience with a leader who won a sweeping mandate in February but has since spent political capital on conservative causes instead of tackling cost-of-living pressures.

Analysts said the problem was not simply that Takaichi’s honeymoon period was ending, but that she had used her commanding position to push through legislation dear to the most conservative elements of the ruling Liberal Democratic Party while making little visible progress on day-to-day issues affecting the public’s well-being – chiefly, rising prices.

“She was given an immense mandate with a two-thirds majority for her own party, so there should have been nothing to obstruct her policies and no need to extend the Diet session, which they have just done,” said Michael Cucek, a professor of Asian Studies at Temple University’s Tokyo campus.

According to Cucek, Takaichi has failed to get her “key policy ducks in a line”, her credibility has been “eaten away by her obsequiousness” to US President Donald Trump and she has focused on fringe issues that may have pleased the far-right but have done nothing to improve the lives of ordinary people.

“In short, she has blown it and I see these opinion polls as more of a performance review than a sign that Takaichi’s ‘honeymoon period’ has come to an end.”

Two pieces of legislation pushed through the Diet this month made it a crime to “desecrate” the national flag – a law that critics say violates the constitutional right to free speech – and a revision to the Imperial Household Law that solidifies the long-standing rule of only allowing a man to ascend the Chrysanthemum Throne.

By preserving this restriction, the law makes it impossible for 24-year-old Princess Aiko, the only child of Emperor Naruhito, to become empress. A Kyodo News poll in May showed that 83 per cent of Japanese favoured altering the law to allow an empress on the throne, with just 13.1 per cent opposed.

Cucek said it was notable that both opinion polls were conducted immediately after revisions to the imperial law were passed, reflecting the public’s disdain for the prime minister’s priorities.

A poll published in the Asahi newspaper on Tuesday showed Takaichi’s support slipping 7 percentage points in the last month and her disapproval rate rising to 35 per cent from 27 per cent.

A Mainichi survey released on Wednesday, meanwhile, showed her backing plunging 10 percentage points over the same period to 41 per cent – below the 50 per cent threshold for the first time since her election in October.

“People are very disappointed in her administration, especially in her economic policies,” said Toshimitsu Shigemura, a professor of politics at Tokyo’s Waseda University.

“The sense is that she has no economic policies and that means prices continue to rise and the yen continues to weaken. Instead, she has made the new imperial law the priority. People are unhappy that there was so little discussion in the Diet and that the public’s wishes were ignored.”

Shigemura said Japan’s business leaders were concerned about worsening relations with China and the impact that could have on trade, noting there were whispers of discontent within the LDP that would only grow louder if public support continued to decline.

He added that Taro Aso, the party’s deeply conservative vice-president who is widely regarded as its kingmaker, had achieved his personal aim of reinforcing the prohibition on a female emperor and might no longer feel such a close allegiance to Takaichi.

Hiromi Murakami, a political-science professor at Temple University, also sees the empress vote as driving the sudden downturn in Takaichi’s popularity.

“It has caused huge controversy and people are angry that there was such little debate and that their opinions on the matter were ignored. There have been many polls that show the public is in favour of having an empress, but this decision only makes the conservatives happy,” she said.

“At a time when people need help with prices that seem to be constantly rising … Takaichi instead put priority on a new law to make it illegal to damage the flag. People are asking why and there do not seem to be any answers,” Murakami added.

“People are pretty angry about these peculiar bills.”

Takaichi is a firm follower of former prime minister Shinzo Abe, according to Cucek, but has failed to learn from his mistakes.

Abe opened his first term in 2006 “with all guns blazing”, Cucek said, but immediately ran into a crisis in the national pension system when the now defunct Social Insurance Agency lost about 50 million pension records. Abe vowed to solve the issue immediately but could not keep his word as the pressure mounted and he resigned exactly one year after taking office, citing ill health.

When he returned for a second – far more successful – spell as prime minister in December 2012, Abe approached the task far more pragmatically, Cucek said. “Abe put all of his focus on staying in office to effect policy over the longer term.”

While prime minister, Abe mostly avoided visiting Tokyo’s controversial Yasukuni Shrine, – where Japan’s war dead, including convicted war criminals, are honoured – after a visit in 2013 drew an international backlash. He also failed to revise the pacifist constitution, even though it was a stated ambition and he had the votes in the Diet.

“Takaichi is constantly looking back at Abe, but she is making the mistakes he made in his first term,” Cucek said.

He warns the omens are not good for Takaichi, who recently admitted she gets by on less than three hours of sleep every night.

“She is trying to show how hard she is working, but that’s a stupid thing for a leader to do,” Cucek said. “I can only see her burning out and crashing. That’s unfortunate because this is Japan’s first female prime minister, but I expect more opinion polls in the coming weeks and months to replicate these two recent polls.”
