As China’s tech rises, companies must rethink their R&D strategy

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 945 words | 2026-07-08

For years, many foreign companies treated China as a vast consumer base or a low-cost manufacturing hub. Now that paradigm is shifting dramatically. China is not just where multinationals scale production or sell at volume; it is increasingly where they make strategic decisions, engage in serious research and development and innovate in ways that will have a global impact.

For example, take Toyota’s R&D centre in Changshu, Jiangsu province. Led by local engineers, it has helped develop electric vehicles such as the bZ3X, a compact SUV, and the bZ7 sedan, with Chinese-designed components and in-cabin systems. Notably, the engineers did so as a fully owned car venture, with likely a reasonable amount of independence from Toyota’s headquarters in Aichi, Japan.

These are not just China-specific models; they feed into the car maker’s global EV strategy, signalling the country’s new role as a key piece of multinationals’ innovation jigsaw puzzle.

This shift demands more than lip service, though. The mantra “in China, for China, for the world” can no longer be empty flattery or sugar-coated sloganeering. Today, multinationals need to recalibrate their China strategy, primarily to treat that pledge as a binding commitment.

This pivot requires real R&D localisation and genuine decision-making power for Chinese engineers and product teams. It also calls for the creation of Chinese-led innovations that can be scaled globally.

Long-term conviction underpins this transformation. China remains uniquely attractive: its market size, consumer diversity, mature industrial ecosystem and world-class engineering talent offer structural advantages few countries can match.

Foreign companies in China will either have to adapt to the new playbook faster or risk being knocked out of a relentless game of “involution”.

“For foreign enterprises, it is essential to recognise that China is transitioning from a ‘world factory’ to a ‘consumption-driven economy’, a shift that is reshaping their role and positioning in the market,” Professor Jin Liyin of Fudan University told me in a recent interview.

As he sees it, the shift to prioritising “local first” is no longer limited to product localisation; it now extends into capital partnerships, operating models and supply chain management.

Nowhere is this transition more visible than in automotive innovation, where a number of partnerships between legacy carmakers and Chinese EV upstarts highlight their growing synergies. Volkswagen has teamed up with Xpeng to jointly develop EVs with “state-of-the-art software and hardware” and self-driving capabilities. The partnership is slated to blend Volkswagen’s global scale and brand strength with Xpeng’s local agility and digital expertise.

Volkswagen is also collaborating with Horizon Robotics, an autonomous-driving chipmaker, to power smart driving systems for future models in the Chinese market through the joint venture Carizon established in November.

The reality is sinking in: foreign car makers are co-creating with China, gaining critical consumer insight and building the next generation of globally competitive vehicles.

To drive this point home, Volkswagen Group China chairman and CEO Ralf Brandstatter recently warned: “Anyone who isn’t involved here and doesn’t acquire the necessary expertise will inevitably find it difficult to compete outside of China.” His words mark an update of Volkswagen CEO Oliver Blume’s description of the cutthroat Chinese market as “a kind of fitness centre”.

China’s value today lies not just in cost or supply-side flexibility, but in innovation itself. Years ago, some multinationals began to diversify supply chains to Southeast Asia under the “China plus one” strategy. Many have now realised, often after sustaining productivity losses, that the benefits of local integration outweigh cost concerns and geopolitical anxieties.

Putting too much emphasis on supply chain relocation risks missing what may be the biggest source of future competitive advantage: China’s innovation ecosystem.

Forward-looking foreign enterprises should ask themselves how they can build deeper collaborations with China’s leading companies and research institutions, whether through joint R&D centres or jointly funded laboratories, to explore next-generation technologies.

This new localisation push did not happen overnight. Confronted with harsher market conditions and regulatory squeezes like home-grown substitution and volume-based procurement of medical products, foreign companies now recognise that embracing local integration cannot be half-hearted or superficial. It requires a sincere approach.

China’s rising position in the global R&D value chain shows that the country’s “independent innovation” strategy is bearing fruit. As Chinese companies develop world-class capabilities, the balance of power is shifting. Foreign companies once charged a premium – a kind of tech tax – for their superior technology. Chinese innovation is closing the gap.

In many domains, domestic firms offer comparable solutions at markedly lower costs. Aside from critical bottlenecks such as advanced artificial intelligence chips and high-end industrial machinery, the premium for imported technology is shrinking. The tide is turning.

While “cost-effectiveness” is a label often attached to Chinese innovations, there are strong reasons to believe such a competitive strategy will not last indefinitely. There will come a time when Chinese firms levy their own tech tax on Western buyers: exporting not only products but value-added intellectual property, design standards and innovation models.

This is the broader context for understanding why foreign companies need to rethink their China strategy. Future success will go to those who treat China not merely as a place to sell or manufacture, but as a co-creator, a source of innovation and a springboard for global competitiveness.

The era of superficial localisation is over, giving way to deep, systemic and globally consequential collaboration. Foreign firms in China must rise to meet this moment, not just in word but in deed.
