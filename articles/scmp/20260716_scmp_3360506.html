Climate disasters compete with wars, markets for distracted world’s money

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 858 words | 2026-07-16

Capital is finite, as is political bandwidth and public concern. So far, this year has asked all three to stretch further than usual, split between security spending, market volatility and now simultaneous disasters unfolding on opposite ends of the planet. Climate change did not wait for global crises to subside. It forced its way onto the front page anyway.

Last week supplied the clearest illustration yet. In southern Spain, a wildfire near Almeria killed at least 12 people and left 23 more missing, one of the deadliest fires in the country’s history. Some victims tried to outrun the flames along roads that turned into traps. It followed Spain seeing its hottest June days ever recorded, part of a blistering summer that has Europe on track for another brutal wildfire season after 2025 became the European Union’s worst on record, with more than a million hectares (2.5 million acres) burned.

On the other side of the planet, floods told the same story in a different register. Typhoon Bavi forced the evacuation of about 2.2 million people in Zhejiang province alone, days after it had killed at least 17 people in landslides across the southern Philippines and injured more than 130 people in Taiwan on its way there.

In the coastal city of Yueqing in Zhejiang province, more than 1,300 trees fell and over 700 of those were uprooted entirely. Bavi was the second typhoon to make landfall in China in just over a week, arriving after Typhoon Maysak struck the country’s south on July 3.

Fire on one side of the world, floods on the other, arriving within days of each other. That is not a coincidence of timing so much as a description of what a warming planet now looks like most weeks.

The harder question is what happens when disasters like these have to compete for a finite amount of money and attention. That is the essence of what analysts have started calling a polycrisis: not one emergency but several of them arriving at once, each with a legitimate claim on the same limited pool of capital.

Markets have had their own difficult year to absorb. The closure of the Strait of Hormuz during the US-Israel war against Iran pushed the price of Brent crude oil above US$100 a barrel at the peak of the crisis earlier this year, rattling everything from airline ticket prices to fertiliser costs for farmers, before easing back below US$70 a barrel following a ceasefire in June. It rose to more than US$84 a barrel this week after US President Donald Trump reinstated the Iran blockade.

That kind of volatility pulls political attention and financial hedging capacity in its own direction before anything has been spent on defence or disaster recovery.

The United States alone is set to spend just over US$1 trillion on national defence in 2026, a jump of more than 17 per cent from the year before. The White House has already proposed going further, requesting US$1.5 trillion for 2027. If Congress approves the request, it would be the highest level of military spending in modern US history.

The US is not alone in ramping up defence spending. Last year, Nato members agreed to increase their defence spending to 5 per cent of gross domestic product annually by 2035. Poland is already spending close to 5 per cent of its GDP on defence, while Germany amended its constitution to ease the debt brake that had constrained military spending for decades. Meanwhile, China plans to raise defence spending by 7 per cent this year while Japan’s cabinet approved a 9.4 per cent increase in the country’s defence budget for 2026.

None of this money was earmarked as an alternative to climate finance, but the contrast is instructive regardless. The Fund for Responding to Loss and Damage, the main global mechanism for helping climate-vulnerable countries recover from disasters like these, had roughly US$250 million to distribute this year. The US alone spends more than that most days just on its own military. This is what competing for finite attention and capital actually looks like in practice.

Not every country has had its own Almeria or its own Zhejiang yet, the kind of disaster large enough to dominate a global news cycle for days on end. Many have already had smaller, less-covered previews of the same thing, such as storms and floods and heatwaves that stopped short of the headlines but pointed in the same direction. The difference between the countries watching this week’s disasters from a comfortable distance and the ones now cleaning up after them is mostly a matter of scale and timing, not immunity.

None of this is a prediction of doom, and it does not need to be. It is simply an argument about timing. The countries watching this year’s fires and floods from a comfortable distance still have a choice about how ready they want to be before their own turn comes.

That window narrows a little more with every disaster that joins the queue competing for the world’s attention. The sooner money and planning shift towards preparation rather than recovery, the smaller the bill becomes once the weather stops being someone else’s problem.
