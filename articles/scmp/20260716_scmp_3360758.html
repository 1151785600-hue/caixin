China’s imports from Africa surge after Beijing expands zero-tariff policy

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 564 words | 2026-07-16

Africa’s exports to China have surged since Beijing expanded its zero-tariff policy across the continent in May, as customs data showed signs of emerging trade links in a range of new sectors alongside traditional categories such as critical minerals.

China’s imports from Africa soared by 21.1 per cent and 40.2 per cent year on year in May and June, respectively – outpacing the overall growth in Chinese imports over the same period, customs data showed.

The increase was partly driven by China’s growing hunger for critical minerals, as the world’s second-largest economy invests heavily in clean energy, semiconductors and data centres to power its fast-growing artificial intelligence industry.

Imports of unwrought copper from Africa increased by more than 110 per cent in May from a year earlier to US$1.65 billion, while purchases of strategic minerals such as platinum and spodumene – used to produce batteries and fuel cells – also rose sharply.

Inbound shipments of rhodium powder – another precious metal with a range of industrial applications – surged nearly fivefold in May to surpass US$125 million in value, according to the customs data.

China’s crude oil imports from Africa also rose 21 per cent in May to US$3.11 billion, making it China’s largest import category from the region, as the US-Israel war on Iran continued to disrupt oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz.

But the tariff-free scheme also appears to be opening up new opportunities for African exporters in several other sectors, especially agricultural products.

China expanded its zero-tariff policy to include all 53 African countries with which Beijing has diplomatic relations on May 1, up from 33 previously. The change slashed import duties for several major economies, including South Africa, Egypt, Nigeria, Algeria and Kenya.

The measure – set to remain in place until April 2028 – is widely viewed as part of Beijing’s broader efforts to narrow its trade imbalance with Africa, after logging another huge surplus last year.

It is also seen as part of China’s push to strengthen ties with the Global South at a time of rising global protectionism, especially after US President Donald Trump raised tariffs on both China and several African economies last year.

On Monday, the Communist Party-run People’s Daily newspaper published a report highlighting a rise in African agricultural products entering the Chinese market under the tariff-free scheme.

The trend is also reflected in customs data released by Beijing. In May, China’s imports of several agricultural products from Africa surged, with shipments of shelled peanuts jumping 15-fold from a year earlier to reach US$13.68 million.

Imports of virgin peanut oil and non-genetically modified soybeans also increased sharply, rising more than 30-fold and eightfold, respectively.

Shipments of coffee and apples from Africa rose by 52 per cent and 85 per cent respectively, while sugar imports exceeded US$1 million – up from just US$200 during May 2025.

China also began importing a range of goods from Africa at scale for the first time, including cane sugar, frozen cuttlefish, cloves and orchid seeds.

China-Africa trade hit a record US$348 billion last year, driven largely by China’s exports of machinery, vehicles and ships, as well as its imports of African crude oil and minerals.

However, the expansion was uneven: Chinese exports to Africa rose by about 25.8 per cent to US$225 billion, while imports from the continent increased by only around 5 per cent to US$123 billion.
