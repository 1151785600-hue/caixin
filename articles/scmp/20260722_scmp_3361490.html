China, Philippines should avoid South China Sea ‘miscalculation’: top diplomats

SCMP | FREE | 1222 words | 2026-07-22

Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi met his Philippine counterpart in Manila on Wednesday, with both sides highlighting the need for “reducing the risk of miscalculation” and maintaining stability in the South China Sea.

The meeting on the sidelines of the Asean foreign ministers’ gathering marked the first formal talks between the top diplomats since Maria Theresa Lazaro assumed office in July last year.

It also came days after fresh tensions in the disputed waters, with Chinese coastguard and Philippine Navy vessels clashing on Monday near the Second Thomas Shoal, the first serious maritime encounter in months between the rival claimants.

The Philippine foreign ministry described Wednesday’s meeting between Wang and Lazaro as “candid, comprehensive and constructive”.

“The two sides underscored the importance of maintaining peace, security, and stability in the region, reducing the risk of miscalculation, and sustaining dialogue on matters of mutual concern,” it said in a read-out.

The talks covered a wide range of topics, including maritime disputes, security, law enforcement, trade and investment cooperation as well as tourism and regional issues, according to the ministry.

Lazaro reiterated Manila’s protest against “violent actions” by China Coast Guard personnel near the Second Thomas Shoal, known as Renai Jiao in China and Ayungin Shoal in the Philippines.

She also reaffirmed the Philippines’ position on the maritime dispute and raised concerns over Chinese actions that “have infringed on the maritime rights and entitlements, including incidents affecting the safety of Filipino personnel and fishermen”.

Beijing has accused the Philippines of instigating Monday’s incident by sending small rubber boats to approach its coastguard vessel, while Manila says one of its navy personnel was struck on the head with a wooden baton.

Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jnr summoned the Chinese ambassador on Tuesday, condemning the incident as a matter of national security.

Hours earlier, the Philippine envoy in Beijing had been summoned by the Chinese foreign ministry, which accused Manila of provocation.

The Wang-Lazaro meeting also came days after a diplomatic row over a Chinese state media video mocking the 10th anniversary of a South China Sea arbitral ruling favouring Manila.

The video, published by China Daily on July 10, showed a monkey dressed in traditional Philippine attire being manipulated by figures representing the United States and Japan before being blasted by water cannon, something China has been alleged to have deployed against Philippine vessels in contested waters.

Manila strongly condemned the depiction as racist propaganda and lodged a formal diplomatic protest.

Beijing distanced itself from the video, saying it did not represent an official position, while asserting its territorial claims.

Lazaro raised the issue with Wang, according to her ministry.

“Disagreements over legal and political issues do not justify resorting to disturbing imagery,” she was quoted as saying.

She also described the video as “deeply offensive, distressing, and unacceptable”.

“The Philippines emphasised the need to exercise restraint, avoid actions that increase tensions, and resolve disputes peacefully in accordance with international law, particularly the 1982 United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea and the 2016 South China Sea Arbitral Award,” the read-out said.

Lazaro also reiterated Manila’s one-China policy, it added.

Additionally, both sides underscored the importance of “maintaining open channels of communication, enhancing mutual understanding, and strengthening confidence-building measures”.

According to the read-out, both foreign ministers highlighted progress on the South China Sea Code of Conduct and reaffirmed their commitment to wrapping up related negotiations this year.

The Philippines is this year’s rotating chair of the 11-member Association of Southeast Asian Nations. Manila is hosting regional ministers this week for discussions on regional flashpoints ranging from the South China Sea to Myanmar and the economic impact of the Middle East conflict.

In separate meetings on Monday with Asean Secretary General Kao Kim Hourn of Cambodia and Malaysian Foreign Minister Mohamad Hasan, Wang emphasised that maritime disputes should not derail ties and called for joint efforts to exclude perceived external meddling.

During his talks with Kao, Wang described the South China Sea as a shared home for regional countries while hailing Beijing as “the most important anchor of stability in the region”.

“Certain forces within the Philippine military and maritime law enforcement ranks have deliberately provoked incidents in the South China Sea to serve the interests of external forces, undermining the process of improving China-Philippines relations as well as peace and stability in the South China Sea,” Wang told Kao.

On July 12, the Philippines marked the anniversary of the 2016 arbitral ruling with a joint statement alongside the US, Japan and 11 other nations. Beijing denounced the declaration as “a distortion of facts and an act of vilification against China”.

The Wang-Lazaro meeting would be significant, Asean spokesman Dominic Xavier Imperial of the Philippine foreign ministry had said on Monday, noting it would cover not only bilateral relations but also regional issues.

“We believe that this engagement is very important because not only will it address … the bilateral relations between the Philippines and China, but it will also have something to do with … the impact of what’s happening globally to the region, to Asean,” he said, according to the Manila Bulletin.

Imperial added that negotiations on the South China Sea code of conduct, ongoing for nearly 17 years, would “always be part of the discussions” during both bilateral and Asean meetings.

In 2023, Asean and China agreed to speed up talks with the goal of concluding the agreement within three years, but progress has been slow as both sides still dispute the legal basis of Beijing’s claims in the strategic waterway.

Despite expanding trade and Chinese investment in the Philippines, the South China Sea dispute remains central to their relations, which have fluctuated between tension and engagement since Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jnr took office four years ago.

Encounters between Chinese coastguard vessels and Philippine ships have become more frequent, particularly around Scarborough Shoal and Second Thomas Shoal, over which Beijing asserts “indisputable sovereignty” alongside much of the South China Sea waters.

While China sees Asean as a critical arena to push back against what it considers “external interference” led by the US and Japan, the Philippines has hardened its stance in recent months, accusing the Chinese of aggressive manoeuvres.

Defence Secretary Gilberto Teodoro Jnr, sanctioned by Beijing last month over “erroneous remarks”, has asserted that the Philippines is justified in avoiding defence engagements with China.

Manila has strengthened military ties with the US, Japan and their allies over the years, while maintaining bilateral channels with Beijing to manage disputes and avoid escalation.

Both sides have also kept communication channels open, despite frequent clashes and maritime incidents.

During a working-level dialogue in Manila on June 30, Liu Jinsong, director general of the Chinese foreign ministry’s Asian affairs department, urged the Philippines to “honour its commitments and match words with deeds”, while expressing “serious concerns” over what Beijing saw as negative moves.

Philippine officials at the meeting, led by Marshall Louis Alferez of the foreign ministry, reaffirmed a commitment to dialogue and the one-China principle, while expressing a readiness to manage disputes constructively and avoid unintended incidents.

Philippine read-outs described the talks as building on earlier bilateral sessions this year in Cebu, Beijing and Quanzhou, where officials discussed South China Sea issues, provisional arrangements to reduce incidents during resupply missions and potential practical cooperation.
