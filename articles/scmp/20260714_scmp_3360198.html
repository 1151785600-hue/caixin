UK deal to serve as testing ground for India’s Western trade ambitions

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1048 words | 2026-07-14

India’s free-trade agreement with the United Kingdom takes effect on Wednesday, delivering immediate tariff relief to businesses on both sides while giving New Delhi an early test of the concessions, compliance demands and implementation challenges it may face in future deals with other Western powers.

UK exports in sectors such as luxury vehicles and spirits are expected to benefit, while Indian textiles and leather products will gain as the agreement eliminates tariffs on 99 per cent of exports from India and reduces duties on 90 per cent of British goods, analysts say.

“The earliest benefits will be tariff reductions on goods, simplified customs procedures, and improved market access for businesses in the UK and India,” said Christopher Blackburn, a London-based analyst.

“Bureaucratic hurdles that have caused headaches in the past will be removed or reduced.”

But analysts caution that the India-UK Free Trade Agreement’s longer-term value will depend on how effectively both governments and businesses handle customs compliance, product standards and other practical barriers to trade.

The agreement offered a “practical blueprint” for resolving issues such as tariffs, services, mobility of people and regulatory cooperation, Blackburn said. “It’s good news for both sides.”

Total bilateral trade rose 10 per cent year on year to £47.9 billion (US$64.2 billion) by the end of 2025, making India the UK’s 11th-largest trading partner, according to the British government.

The deal is being implemented at a crucial juncture for India as a long-pending agreement with the United States, one of its largest trading partners, is still being finalised.

For the UK, the deal is equally important strategically because it eases access to one of the world’s largest consumer markets, analysts say.

“Businesses on both sides will benefit at a moment when the global economy is growing increasingly fragmented and unpredictable. The main challenge will be implementation,” said Uday Chandra, a political-science professor at India’s Ashoka University.

Indian industry has had a mixed record with free-trade agreements: labour-intensive sectors such as textiles and pharmaceuticals secured greater market access, while manufacturing industries dealt with import pressures.

The main challenge for smaller Indian companies with the UK agreement would be adapting to new criteria such as determining product origin and customs procedures concerning the application of tariffs and export quotas, Chandra said.

“Indian exporters will also have to meet British regulatory, environmental and quality standards. British firms will have to work through the complexity of the Indian market,” he said.

India’s trade and foreign investment regulations are often seen as intricate, requiring navigation of sector-specific caps and strict foreign exchange rules.

“The success of the [free-trade agreement] will, therefore, depend on the extent to which both governments resolve everyday obstacles to trade,” Chandra said.

Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi and his British counterpart Keir Starmer signed the deal in July last year. British government estimates at the time suggested the pact could add £4.8 billion, or about 0.13 per cent, to the country’s gross domestic product.

Starmer resigned as UK prime minister and leader of the ruling Labour Party last month, but has continued to serve as a caretaker prime minister until a new Labour chief is selected.

Analysts say the political changes are unlikely to affect the trade agreement because it enjoys broad cross-party support in the UK.

Starmer has cited the trade pact as an important political achievement with one of the world’s largest and fastest-growing economies, Chandra pointed out.

Despite this, Chandra said Labour would still need to show that the deal “produces investments, export orders and jobs” to boost support for the party after its recent defeats in local elections.

Biswajit Dhar, an economics professor at Delhi’s Council for Social Development, said how the agreement was implemented would be equally or more important for India, though “on the face of it there are a lot of market opportunities for both countries”.

More importantly, he said Indian industry had to be ready to meet environmental and labour standards to fully tap the market opportunities offered by the trade deal.

India’s exports to the UK will be subject to a Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism – a charge on carbon-intensive imports such as cement – that is scheduled to launch next year and scale up gradually, similar to the one implemented by the European Union.

Dhar said sectors in India such as iron and steel, fertilisers, cement, hydrogen and aluminium could be affected.

“We don’t have the preparedness. Our industries have not really looked at the global emission norms very seriously,” he said.

For Dhar, such readiness is particularly important because India and the EU are also expected to implement their free-trade agreement soon. The two sides concluded negotiations in January and are reviewing the treaty’s legal provisions.

Indian exports to the UK and the EU could also increasingly face stringent labour standards and sustainability requirements, driven by recent free-trade agreements and other laws, Dhar said.

The local industry would need to be sufficiently prepared to deal with such issues because India was set to conclude trade pacts with other major powers such as Canada and Australia that could enforce similar standards, Dhar said.

“All these issues have implications for other markets as well,” he said.

Delhi and Canberra have already implemented an interim deal called the Australia-India Economic Cooperation and Trade Agreement and announced plans for a broader free-trade pact.

Earlier in March, India and Canada formally relaunched trade negotiations for their Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement, following a diplomatic thaw after ties were strained three years ago over Ottawa’s accusations of Indian involvement in the assassination of Canadian Sikh activist Hardeep Singh Nijjar.

Chandra said the UK-India trade agreement would help Indian industry assess what it needed to do to take advantage of such deals.

“The UK agreement gives India valuable experience in implementing a comprehensive trade deal with an advanced Western economy. It also demonstrates that Delhi is willing to make significant concessions on tariffs, services, and government procurement when it receives meaningful market access in return,” he said.

“For British firms, there is a potential first-mover advantage. They will receive preferential access to India before the EU agreement is likely to enter into force later in 2026-27.”
