Chinese paratrooper saves unconscious comrade in dramatic mid-air chute emergency

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 642 words | 2026-07-09

A Chinese paratrooper rescued an unconscious colleague after their parachutes became entangled in mid-air, the military’s official newspaper reported on Thursday.

The PLA Daily said the incident happened during a scheduled parachute refresher training exercise conducted by a People’s Liberation Army Air Force brigade on May 28.

First Lieutenant Wang Rui, a team leader in the unspecified brigade, was making his 16th-ever jump, at an altitude of 800 metres (2,600 feet).

Wang jumped from the transport aircraft but right before his parachute was to deploy, he was struck by another jumper, Corporal Chen Sijie, and flipped upside down, according to the PLA Daily.

After regaining his bearings, Wang realised his parachute was entangled with Chen’s chute – one of the most dangerous emergencies in military parachuting.

In such an emergency, the two jumpers would usually work together to free their chutes but Chen appeared to be unconscious and was not responding to Wang’s repeated calls.

A PLA Daily photo showed Chen’s still mostly undeployed parachute chute directly above Wang’s chute, with the canopy fabric and suspension lines of the two rigs entangled.

Wang tried to reach for his reserve parachute ripcord, but he found himself tangled in his own chute and could not see. If Wang had pulled the ripcord handle forcefully, it might have broken without deploying the reserve.

Wang curled up his body and tried to kick the canopy fabric of his chute away from him. He failed in his first attempt and tried to adjust his angle.

In Wang’s second kick, his foot was able to catch the edge of the fabric. In his final kick, the canopy fabric and suspension lines that wrapped around him were released.

He pulled the ripcord of the reserve chute, and it deployed with a bang.

It was not until after landing that Wang found out his main chute had also been inflated and deployed.

Still entwined, Wang eventually crashed into a bush before rolling into a nearby ditch, while Chen landed on the grass nearby.

When the ground duty officer found Wang, half of his body was submerged in the ditch water, his camouflage uniform was torn in several places, and his face and arms were covered in blood.

The suspension lines were wrapped around Wang’s legs, and his hands were holding tight onto the canopy fabric.

Chen regained consciousness on his way to the hospital. He had sustained a compression fracture to the mid-section of his spine. The brigade said it had not yet determined why Chen lost consciousness during the jump.

Wang fractured his fifth through 10th ribs on his right side as well as his right shoulder. He had multiple soft-tissue injuries across his body.

For his life-saving actions during the exercise, Wang was awarded a Third-Class Merit in early June. He was discharged from hospital at the end of June and has since returned to his unit but is unable to take part in training.

A free fall lasts only a matter of seconds from an altitude of 800 metres, and under a normal parachute descent, it takes about 2½ minutes.

Wang was able to identify the emergency, free himself from the tangled canopy, deploy his reserve chute and hold on to his fellow jumper to execute a coordinated landing, according to a post-incident analysis reported by the PLA Daily.

“To be honest, there simply wasn’t time to be afraid while I was dealing with the emergency,” the PLA Daily quoted Wang as saying.

“It wasn’t until I was lying in the ambulance and finally relaxed that I began to feel scared. But I think that if I had to do it again, I would make the same choice.”

According to the brigade, Chen is an experienced ram-air parachute specialist, having completed more than 40 parachute jumps.
