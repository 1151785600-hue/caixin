Why factories in southern China are feeling the squeeze despite pickup in orders

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 725 words | 2026-07-12

As China prepares to release its second-quarter and first-half economic data this week – with markets generally expecting performance to have softened – manufacturers in its southern Pearl River Delta region offer a nuanced picture.

While official manufacturing data for June showed continued expansion, electronics exporters in the region – one of China’s biggest industrial areas and a bellwether for the national economy – said last week that a pickup in orders had yet to lift their bottom lines.

Rising raw material costs, tougher payment terms set by vendors, and persistent price competition were squeezing profits, they said.

Manufacturers said that despite strong export demand, particularly from the US, those pressures could stymie efforts to increase sales and fund new investments or hiring.

The question being asked on factory floors is whether stimulus measures could ease the cost pressures eating into margins, with all eyes on the midyear Politburo meeting, expected to be held later this month.

At the July meeting of the Politburo – a major decision-making body of the ruling Communist Party – policymakers typically assess the country’s first-half economic performance and chart the course for the remainder of the year.

“The schism between rising costs and export strength has gripped much of China’s manufacturing sector, frustrating the search for profits,” said Liu Kaiming, the founder of the Shenzhen-based Institute of Contemporary Observation and a long-time analyst of trade supply chains.

Liang Xiong, co-founder of electronics and lighting producer A Delight Group in Guangzhou, said US orders had seen a modest increase in recent weeks, partly because improving China-US trade conditions had encouraged buyers to place orders with Chinese suppliers. But he said margins remained under pressure, with raw material prices surging.

“Material prices are rising every day,” Liang said. “Some specialised electronic components have gone up four, five or even 10 times, from capacitors, resistors to other electronic components.”

Rising upstream costs, along with stronger artificial intelligence-related demand for some electronic components, had pushed prices higher, he added.

China’s official manufacturing purchasing managers’ index returned to expansionary territory in June, with production and new orders both improving. But the employment index still contracted, suggesting the improvement remains uneven.

Liang said cash flow was also under pressure, with suppliers that previously offered credit terms increasingly demanding payment before delivery to avoid risks from price volatility.

“More suppliers are asking for cash payment,” he said. “That has made cash flow much tighter than before. Our quotes used to be valid for weeks. Now, they are valid for only a couple of days because raw material prices are changing so quickly.”

Many export orders signed before the latest round of price increases were now being fulfilled at a loss, he added.

Pressure is also visible further down the supply chain.

Tan Bo, who owns the Huihong packaging and printing firm in Dongguan, said customers were pushing harder on pricing, with that push reflecting weak downstream demand.

In the appliance manufacturing hub of Zhongshan, industry insiders said they saw uneven recovery across product lines.

“The European air-conditioner and fan markets are growing strongly, and demand is expected to increase significantly this year and next,” said Chen Haibo, general manager of Crossbow Brand Electric.

But he said most other product lines remained under pressure due to weak domestic demand and overcapacity.

Lawrence Tan, who runs a mould design company in Zhongshan, said monthly factory and office rents had hit a low of 7 yuan per square metre (roughly 10 US cents per square foot), almost half the level last year, which was a boon to companies facing other cost pressures.

Liu, from the Institute of Contemporary Observation, said exports had become the main driver of growth for many Chinese manufacturers as domestic demand remained weak, but any gains for Pearl River Delta factories were unevenly distributed.

“Export growth has mainly benefited higher-value industries and companies with stronger overseas channels, while many traditional manufacturers, which employ the bulk of the industrial workforce, continue to face thin margins and cost pressures,” he said.

“That is why strong exports have yet to translate into a broad recovery in hiring, investment or domestic consumption.”
