In Philippines, solar power becomes ‘practical necessity’ as energy costs soar

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1034 words | 2026-07-08

Joab Jorge runs Dream Latte Cafe, a speciality coffee shop and small-batch roastery, with his mother Ces out of their old ancestral home in Pilar, a town in Bataan province some 180km (112 miles) northwest of Manila.

Rising electricity costs and frequent blackouts have put a strain on the business, which has already had to raise prices by 10 per cent to cover higher costs for goods and imported coffee beans since the energy crisis triggered by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz in February.

The squeeze pushed Jorge and his mother, 60, to consider installing solar panels to guard against the outages, which Ces said were “bad for business” because they could shut the cafe for hours at a time. “Who’s going to come to you then?”

The pair have seen promising results since installing a hybrid solar system at home, where they carry out part of their business, including roasting coffee beans and baking the pastries sold at the cafe.

“Before using solar panels, we couldn’t run everything at the same time. If she was baking and I was roasting, the lights would dim. We’d have to take turns. Now, there is no problem,” said Jorge, 30.

Their experience mirrors that of many Filipinos, who are turning to rooftop solar to soften the blow of soaring electricity costs, which have climbed even further in the wake of the US-Iran war.

Between March and May, the Philippines imported US$407 million worth of solar panels from China, a 145 per cent increase from the previous year, Reuters reported, as households and businesses sought to shield themselves from high power prices, unreliable supply and exposure to fuel-import shocks.

Jorge said his father had initially balked at the cost of solar panels, but came round once he saw how far prices had fallen.

A hybrid system of 10 panels and a 6 kilowatt battery set them back 400,000 pesos (US$6,500), a price Jorge reckons would have been nearly double that just a few years ago.

“Ours are Chinese-made; everything is Chinese now. China has ramped up production, so costs have dropped across all kinds of equipment, solar included. Our installer said the same thing. When he first started installing, panels were expensive. Now they’re much cheaper and more efficient,” he said.

Rush to rooftop solar

Residential power costs in the Philippines are among the highest in the region, and with further price rises and scant subsidies, solar has become an increasingly attractive way for households to cut their monthly bills.

Meralco, the country’s largest power distributor, has raised prices by 10 per cent since the Gulf conflict began. Reuters estimates that a median Filipino household now spends around 12 per cent of its monthly income on electricity.

On social media, content creator Pat Quinto said his household’s electricity bill fell from 8,000 pesos to just 300 pesos after he installed solar panels in May.

For installers, the shift is visible in both the volume and nature of inquiries they receive.

Jay Agbayani, founder of local solar installer Solar Home, said his company used to receive around 10 inquiries a day before the conflict.

“Since February, we have been receiving around 100 inquiries per day, representing a tenfold increase in customer interest,” Agbayani said, adding that most came from homeowners keen on hybrid systems that pair solar panels with battery storage.

He expects demand to keep climbing, driven by high electricity costs, worries over energy reliability and growing awareness of solar technology.

“Filipino homeowners today are much more educated about solar,” he said. “They understand that solar is not simply about saving money on electricity … for more Filipino families and businesses, it is becoming a practical necessity and a long-term investment in energy independence.”

Hedging against price increases

While the Gulf conflict has sharpened the case for solar in the Philippines, energy analysts say the demand is not solely a product of the current crisis.

“People are realising the value of solar in their homes, so an uptake in solar installations in the country is expected,” said Vince Pacanot, lead energy consultant at Access to Energy Philippines, though he cautioned that cost could still be a barrier to wider adoption.

Still, solar adoption lets consumers hedge against the risks of price spikes and unreliable power, according to Jonas Marie Dumdum, a Philippines-based energy and sustainability researcher and consultant.

Dumdum described the country’s demand for solar as “structural”, since electricity rates are exposed to factors largely outside consumers’ control, including imported fuel prices, spot market conditions and currency swings.

“What consumers are discovering is that solar, particularly rooftop solar, lets them hedge against these risks – and we see this in the volume of people now coming forward, online and in person, saying they wish they had made the switch sooner,” he said.

“Growth may moderate if rates ease, but the demand will persist well beyond the current crisis, because the exposure that created it does not quickly go away when this particular conflict ends.”

While Dumdum said solar uptake was currently concentrated among middle and upper-income households because of the upfront cost, he added that soaring electricity rates had shortened the residential payback period to just three years, down from an estimated four in 2025.

“Whether adoption broadens beyond the current demographic will depend on how quickly financing innovations like rent-to-own and zero-down payment models scale, and on whether the industry continues to build consumer confidence.”

As the Philippines looks to diversify its energy mix and wean itself off imported fossil fuels, Pacanot urged the country to look beyond solar alone.

He noted that the Philippine Energy Plan 2023–2050 projects solar will contribute between 10 and 20 per cent of the country’s total power mix, while renewables such as wind could account for as much as 46 per cent.

“Solar could definitely help the country become more sustainable or shift away from fossil fuels, but renewables development must also be diversified – wind, geothermal, hydropower especially,” Pacanot said.

“The government is doing a lot for solar already. Recent policies have been towards solar. For utility scale, what they must ensure is the support for the development of a battery energy and storage system to complement the variability of solar.”
