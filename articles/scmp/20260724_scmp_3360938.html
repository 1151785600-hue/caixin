Why buyout funds, not IPOs, may define China’s next capital market cycle: Zhang Yichen

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 2831 words | 2026-07-24

Zhang Yichen is chairman and CEO of Citic Capital Holdings, one of the leading players in China’s capital market, and the chairman of Trustar Capital. He heads several companies, including the McDonald’s master franchise business in mainland China and Hong Kong, as well as Harbin Pharmaceutical Group. He also sits on the board of the Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing (HKEX) as an independent non-executive director.

In this interview, conducted during the “two sessions” – the annual meetings of China’s top legislature and advisory body – the veteran investor and government adviser discussed the role of buyout funds, the impact of geopolitical tensions on cross-border deals and why global brands are increasingly relying on local partners to succeed in China. He also shared his views on Hong Kong’s strategic role as a global financial centre.

Regulators have recently stressed that government-backed investment funds should lead the way in serving as “patient capital” – funding provided from a more long-term perspective and with a higher tolerance for risk – as a contrast to what they view as the pursuit of rapid and maximum returns. In your opinion, how should buyout funds respond to this?

Patient capital is not simply about holding assets for longer. It requires both long-term capital and the capability to create value within companies.

China has historically lacked long-duration capital due to its reliance on bank-led, indirect financing. Increasing the share of direct and equity financing is essential to building a foundation for patient capital.

At the same time, institutions managing this capital must be able to empower businesses through governance improvements, operational support and strategic guidance.

China’s market evolves rapidly, and remote control from global headquarters is often too slow and insufficiently attuned to local dynamics

You are a member of the national committee of the Chinese People’s Political Consultative Conference – the country’s top political advisory body. At this year’s two sessions, you said the role of buyout funds in capital market restructuring is an area of focus for you. With the private equity industry holding more than 10 trillion yuan (US$1.47 trillion) in legacy assets and facing mounting exit pressures, what role should buyout funds play in addressing this challenge? How do you assess the long-term significance of the new national-level buyout funds?

Relying solely on initial public offerings (IPOs) to exit such a large pool of private equity assets is unrealistic. Even with the recent recovery in the A-share and Hong Kong markets, IPO capacity remains limited. In mature markets, nearly 80 per cent of exits are achieved through buyout funds rather than IPOs.

China’s mergers and acquisitions (M&A) market is still underdeveloped. Many companies continue to focus on listing, while the market needs more consolidation to support industrial upgrades and innovation. Buyout funds could play a key role by integrating fragmented assets and linking the primary and capital markets.

The launch of national-level buyout funds sends a clear policy signal: the government is placing greater emphasis on asset consolidation after years of “early-stage, small-scale, innovation-focused” investments. These funds are complementary to market-driven buyout funds and have a stronger strategic orientation.

The Citic Capital-led buyout of McDonald’s China under your leadership is widely regarded as a landmark deal. In carve-outs involving the China operations of multinationals, what are the critical factors for success? Do you expect more deals of a similar scale in the coming years?

Multinational companies in China are facing two main pressures: intensifying local competition and geopolitical headwinds, which have made China a more challenging and, in some cases, a riskier market in the eyes of global investors.

Against this backdrop, many have come to realise the core issue is not operational capability, but decision-making. The core lessons are threefold: the importance of a localised strategy, aligned incentive mechanisms and speed of execution. In many cases, multinational operations in China are fundamentally sound. The issue is not the “body”, but the “brain” – decision-making that is not sufficiently localised.

China’s market evolves rapidly, and remote control from global headquarters is often too slow and insufficiently attuned to local dynamics, particularly given the country’s highly digitalised and distinct ecosystem. As a result, there’s a growing need for more localised decision-making at the strategic level.

This is why more multinational companies are turning to local partners. In China, many have chosen to work with financial investors rather than industry players, partly because successful cases such as McDonald’s have shown this model can be more straightforward.

Bringing in a strategic partner often introduces additional complexities, such as potential competition and management integration issues, whereas working with an investment fund allows companies to retain operational continuity while improving strategic alignment.

In our experience, multinational businesses in China are generally well run, with capable local teams. What is needed is not a wholesale overhaul of operations but a shift in strategy, stronger management incentives and faster execution.

The McDonald’s China transaction illustrates this approach. After our investment, we largely retained the existing management team and focused instead on strategic adjustments. One key change was upgrading the company’s digital capabilities, including appointing a new chief technology officer to build systems tailored to China’s unique IT environment. This significantly improved efficiency and responsiveness, without disrupting the core organisation.

Looking ahead, as competition intensifies and geopolitical factors persist, we expect more multinational companies to consider similar carve-out transactions or partnerships with local investors.

You mentioned earlier that AI is being integrated into your operations. Could you explain how the technology is reshaping your overall strategy and share specific examples of its use?

AI is already being applied across both customer-facing and operational functions. It is used to analyse consumer behaviour, enhance customer engagement and improve membership activity.

On the operational side, AI supports decision-making in areas such as demand forecasting, staffing and inventory management. For example, predicting peak-hour traffic helps optimise food preparation, reducing waste while maintaining efficiency.

Rather than relying on a single large model, the company uses multiple models in parallel and continuously tests and refines them to improve performance.

How do you assess the outlook for China’s M&A market over the next two to three years? As Chinese companies accelerate their global expansion, how is Citic Capital positioning itself to support outbound M&A and global market development, and what are the biggest opportunities and challenges for Chinese companies pursuing overseas acquisitions?

We remain highly optimistic about the outlook for China’s M&A market over the next two to three years. This is largely driven by a structural shift in the economy, as China moves from a phase of high-speed growth to one focused on high-quality development.

At this stage, economic growth increasingly depends on upgrading existing industries rather than simply creating new ones. While emerging sectors are still driven by venture capital, a much larger part of the economy now consists of mature companies that require consolidation, restructuring and efficiency improvements – which is where M&A becomes essential.

In that sense, M&A is not just an option but a natural outcome of this stage of development. Policy support is also reinforcing this trend. Regulators have introduced measures to facilitate M&A activity, including financing tools such as M&A loans, while the establishment of state-backed buyout funds signals a stronger role for government guidance in driving consolidation. Taken together, both the economic fundamentals and the policy environment point to a more active M&A market ahead.

On outbound M&A, we’ve been involved in cross-border transactions for more than two decades, primarily in the United States, Europe and Japan. In recent years, however, geopolitical factors have made deals in the US increasingly difficult. Europe has also been affected, though to a lesser extent, while Japan remains relatively more accessible.

There are also structural challenges beyond geopolitics. In cross-border acquisitions, financial investors and strategic buyers often have different priorities – particularly regarding exit timing and long-term objectives – which can make alignment difficult. This is not unique to China, but a global issue, and it partly explains why successful joint acquisitions remain relatively limited.

For Chinese companies going global, the biggest challenge is still geopolitics. At the same time, many companies are still in the process of building [the full capabilities of a multinational] – particularly in branding, product development and end-to-end operations –rather than relying primarily on manufacturing [strength].

As more companies expand overseas, they also face cultural differences and operational mismatches when applying domestic business models in foreign markets. In addition, as developed markets become more difficult to enter, many are turning to emerging markets, which come with higher risks.

That being said, the opportunities are significant. China’s manufacturing and industrial capabilities are highly competitive globally. In many sectors, Chinese companies have clear advantages in efficiency and execution. The potential for international expansion is therefore substantial – but it will take time to realise fully.

Building China into a “financial powerhouse”, a major policy goal, requires attracting more long-term foreign capital to Chinese assets. From your communications with overseas investors, how do they currently view the China market and what factors matter most in their assessment? In the current geopolitical environment, how can China stabilise expectations and strengthen long-term investor confidence? Do you see a reversal of an earlier view that China was “uninvestible”?

Foreign investors’ views on China have undergone a significant adjustment over the past few years, but it’s important to differentiate by region.

US investors, particularly large long-term institutions, have sharply reduced their exposure to China, largely for geopolitical reasons. This has also affected capital from countries closely aligned with the US, such as Canada and Australia. European and Japanese investors have also been affected, though to a lesser extent.

Asian investors, by contrast, are less constrained by geopolitical considerations. But their allocation to China has also declined in recent years due to factors such as the pandemic, the property sector downturn and policy uncertainties, making it harder for them to fully understand the market. In addition, weak exit conditions have limited their ability to redeploy capital.

That being said, sentiment has begun to improve since last year alongside the recovery in capital markets. We are seeing renewed interest from Asian investors, particularly in the Middle East, where allocations to China have historically been low compared with those to the US. As a result, there has been a noticeable increase in collaboration with these investors in recent transactions.

More broadly, we are also seeing a reversal of that earlier view that China was “uninvestible”. Investors are once again recognising that China remains investible and that it continues to have strong economic fundamentals. But geopolitical constraints mean that capital flows are unlikely to return to pre-2020 levels.

In terms of what investors are focused on today, geopolitics remains the key overarching risk, but it is not easily controlled. More fundamentally, investors are concerned about the balance and sustainability of China’s economic growth – in particular, the weakness in domestic demand and consumption.

Much of this is linked to the property sector. As a result, a clear and sustained stabilisation of the real estate market would be a critical turning point. Once investors see that the property sector has genuinely bottomed out and begun to recover, confidence in China’s broader economic outlook – and in turn, long-term capital allocation – is likely to strengthen significantly.

As more mainland Chinese institutions expand into Hong Kong, how can the city strengthen its role as a “superconnector”? What further steps can Hong Kong take to bridge mainland capital with global assets and attract more international capital into China?

Hong Kong should make full use of its “one country, two systems” advantage and continue to reinforce its position as an international financial centre, while integrating more closely with the country’s broader development agenda, including initiatives such as the 15th five-year plan.

Its role as an international financial hub is irreplaceable and represents a unique advantage for both Hong Kong and the country. This advantage has become particularly evident over the past year, as Hong Kong has helped revive its own economy while also supporting the development of mainland China’s capital markets. To sustain this role, it is important to preserve and further strengthen the institutional and legal foundations under “one country, two systems”.

At the same time, Hong Kong is beginning to explore a more proactive development model. The establishment of the Hong Kong Investment Corporation (HKIC) is a useful step in this direction. The city has long relied on a “small government” approach, but some of the deeper structural challenges it faces today cannot be addressed by the free market alone. While the HKIC is still relatively small and should not be expected to do too much too quickly, it can serve as an important starting point.

As Hong Kong deepens its integration into national development and works more closely with mainland institutions – particularly large state-owned enterprises – there’s a need for a platform similar to Singapore’s Temasek. In this sense, the HKIC could evolve into such an institution over time.

More broadly, Hong Kong’s most important role lies in supporting the internationalisation of the yuan. The city should actively, and in some areas even boldly, introduce policies that strengthen its position as a true offshore yuan centre. This requires balancing financial stability – given that the yuan is not yet fully convertible – with greater openness, while leveraging Hong Kong’s advantages as a free port and international financial hub.

If China is to meaningfully increase the share of direct financing, a well-functioning bond market is essential

What do you consider to be the most pressing issue in China’s capital markets today, and why? What solutions do you envision?

China’s capital markets remain relatively underdeveloped, particularly in terms of structure. The financial system has long been dominated by banks, with a heavy reliance on indirect financing.

For many years, there’s been a policy push to increase the share of direct financing – especially equity financing – but progress has been relatively limited. As a result, much of the economic growth has been funded by debt, leading to high leverage across the system.

The key challenge, therefore, is how to shift more capital from debt-based financing into true direct investment, particularly equity. There’s been some progress on the equity side, especially in the stock market, where development has gradually moved onto a more stable track.

The next critical step is the bond market. In previous years, disruptions in the credit environment led to a loss of confidence and numerous defaults. However, this should not derail its development. If China is to meaningfully increase the share of direct financing, a well-functioning bond market is essential.

What is needed now is to establish a more complete institutional and regulatory framework tailored to the bond market, so that it can develop in a more stable and sustainable way. This is one of the most urgent priorities for the future development of China’s capital markets.

The recent AI boom has reignited investor interest in the sector, as well as downstream industries. How is this changing the way Citic Capital evaluates investment opportunities? Are you seeing more M&A or buyout opportunities emerging as companies race to adopt AI, or are you adopting a “wait and see” approach?

Driven by strong market sentiment, valuations in certain AI segments have risen rapidly. In response, we have maintained a relatively cautious stance, believing that both solid fundamentals and reasonable valuations are essential.

We have not been overly aggressive in deploying capital at the earlier infrastructure stage. Instead, we are more inclined to wait until commercial opportunities at the AI application layer gradually emerge and achieve initial validation before investing. Ultimately, we hope the market does not become overheated, allowing us to preserve room for continued deployment in the second half of the year.

Looking ahead to the second half of this year, what do you see as the biggest opportunities and challenges for China’s capital markets? How will these shape the outlook for M&A and private equity investment?

China’s capital market is shifting from “scale expansion” to “quality enhancement.” The greatest opportunities lie in policy-supported industry consolidation and controlled buyouts. Buyout funds are becoming a key bridge connecting patient capital, the upgrading of listed companies, and new quality productive forces.

Specific opportunities include M&A and restructuring of listed companies, carve-outs by multinational corporations, scaling up of small and medium-sized enterprises, and so on. The main challenge is the shortage of long-term capital and the need to further optimise the fundraising structure.
