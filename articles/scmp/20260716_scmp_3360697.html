UK grooming gangs scandal deepens Labour’s woes amid voter backlash

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 887 words | 2026-07-16

When Shabir Ahmed, a convicted leader in one of Britain’s grooming gang cases, was released from prison this month and allowed to stay in the country rather than being deported to Pakistan, the political fallout extended well beyond a single immigration dispute.

The case has become a test for the ruling Labour party, which is under pressure both as the government responsible for deportation policy and over criticism of failures by some local authorities to protect vulnerable girls.

Analysts said Ahmed’s release had revived public anger over group-based child sexual exploitation – known in Britain as the grooming gangs scandal – and claims that officials failed to act because they feared accusations of racism.

Ahmed was convicted in 2012 of multiple counts of rape and sexual offences against girls, but could not be deported to Pakistan because of a 55-year-old law that prevented his removal.

Provisions under the Immigration Act 1971 bar the removal of Commonwealth citizens who arrived in the United Kingdom before 1973 and had been in the country for five years.

Ahmed held dual British-Pakistani citizenship before losing the former following his 2012 conviction, but was still exempt from deportation.

Pakistan has reportedly linked Ahmed’s deportation to its demands for the extradition of several UK-based political dissidents and critics of the Pakistani military.

Andy Burnham, who is widely expected to replace Keir Starmer as prime minister this month, was among those calling for Ahmed to be deported.

UK Home Secretary Shabana Mahmood told parliament on Monday that the government intended to amend laws to make it easier to deport serious criminals such as Ahmed.

The case has highlighted public concern over neglect and institutional lapses in the high-profile group-based child sexual exploitation cases, many of which involved white British girls.

“It has undermined Labour because many voters believe authorities failed to protect victims and were reluctant to confront the wider issues,” said Christopher Blackburn, a London-based independent political analyst.

Less than two years after securing a landslide victory in the July 2024 national elections, Labour suffered a historic collapse and lost seats in municipal polls, ultimately leading Starmer to announce his resignation last month.

Analysts said key reasons included voter dissatisfaction over a stagnant economy and law and order issues such as the failure to deal effectively with grooming gangs.

Investigations, including a major 2026 Rape Gang Inquiry – a crowdfunded, survivor-led investigation into child sexual exploitation across the UK – cited institutional failures and avoiding action due to fear of allegations of racism.

Observers said the issue showed why right-wing populism had dramatically risen in recent months, with Nigel Farage’s Reform UK party, which promised to target illegal immigrants, making huge electoral inroads.

The grooming gangs controversy – with several involving men of Pakistani heritage – has sparked intense debate over immigration and criminal justice, though victims’ advocates have emphasised the need to put child protection at the centre.

“It [grooming gangs] has fuelled anti-immigration sentiment among some voters. The actions of these criminal gangs will be used to stigmatise the wider British Pakistani community and others,” Blackburn said.

Yashwant Deshmukh, an independent Dubai-based political analyst, said he was not surprised that the issue of criminal gangs targeting schoolgirls – primarily those of British white ethnicity – had touched a raw nerve because political parties had sought to cash in on “vote banks” by not taking effective action.

“They were simply taking the white majoritarian sentiment for granted. Britain on the whole has been a liberal society,” he said, adding that the issue was one of the reasons the nation was leaning towards “ultra-conservative” or right-wing political parties.

“This issue is bothering them [the population’s majority British whites] because it is ending up on their doorsteps.”

Harsh Pant, professor of international relations at King’s College London, concurred that the grooming gangs scandal generated significant public concern, particularly because several high-profile cases involved offenders predominantly of Pakistani heritage.

“Although I think the reasons are varied and the extent of its electoral impact can be debated, there are perceptions that Labour-controlled local authorities failed the victims and that party figures have been reluctant to address sensitive aspects of the cases,” he said.

However, the cause of the failure to deal with the issue was “institutional and multi-agency rather than the responsibility of any single political party”, he noted.

“It is also true that for some people it has contributed to anti-migrant sentiment in the UK. But I would say that it is one factor among several others, rather than the sole driver.”

Pant suggested that cooperation between the UK and Pakistan on deporting serious criminals could help improve relations.

“For example, if there are suspects, witnesses, victims or financial assets connected to Pakistan, the UK can seek assistance through mutual legal assistance treaties,” he said.

Police forces on both sides could also share information related to tracking or pursuing criminals who might have fled from one country to another, Pant added.

“The UK can pursue their return using the legal frameworks and bilateral cooperation. The two countries can exchange intelligence on transnational criminal networks, including those involved in human trafficking, child exploitation [or] money laundering,” he said.

Pant also urged Britain to use broader diplomatic and economic pressure to get Pakistan to take such issues seriously.
