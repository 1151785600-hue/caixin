The human faces of China’s belt and road strategy, through Liu Heung-shing’s lens

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1718 words | 2026-07-24

It is barely light when Liu Heung-shing starts the day in his art-filled Happy Valley apartment. But then, the photojournalist has always believed in getting to the story before everyone else.

Liu, 74, wakes at 5am to spend three hours reading the news, coffee in hand. Despite being “long retired”, the Pulitzer Prize-winning photographer takes note of where he might want to visit next.

“There are so many ways to tell a story. Sometimes, the most difficult decision is when not to press the shutter, rather than when to press the shutter,” he says, while cooking and sipping a glass of Chablis. He has invited me to his home for dinner and to discuss his most recent book, Old Road New Connections: China Builds a Different World, for which his shutter charted a global odyssey following the tentacles of China’s Belt and Road Initiative. If the initiative sounds abstract in policy papers – six economic corridors, hundreds of billions in cumulative investment, more than 150 participating countries and 230 cooperation agreements – Liu’s book is an attempt to humanise that scale.

“I knew it was also a human story of Chinese going around the world, doing things, changing things and putting a uniquely Chinese stamp on different parts of the world.”

In many ways, the project is typical Liu: ambitious, self‑assigned and pursued with a mixture of stubbornness and curiosity.

“I think I’m somebody whose brain works 24 hours a day,” he laughs.

He likes to say that his books all begin with a simple editorial question: “If this is going to be one of the defining stories of the 21st century, and nobody has framed it properly yet, what can I do?”

Born in Hong Kong in 1951, Liu spent his early childhood in Fuzhou, Fujian province, with his mother, witnessing famine during the Great Leap Forward. His family eventually returned to Hong Kong.

In 1969, Liu left for New York, in the United States, where he studied politics at Hunter College. He remembers the year clearly because it was then that he saw Jimi Hendrix and Janis Joplin perform up close at the Woodstock festival.

More specific education came when his mentor, Albanian‑born photographer Gjon Mili, took him to the 28th floor of the Time & Life Building, which was where the photographers at Life magazine and other Time Inc titles were based.

“On that floor,” Liu says, “the world unfolded through photography.”

It was for Time Inc that Liu first returned to mainland China, in 1976, to photograph a nation coming to terms with the death of Mao Zedong.

As a foreign correspondent in China until the early 90s, he documented the pivotal decades that followed. Later, as the executive vice-president of News Corp China, Liu shuttled between tycoon Rupert Murdoch and party cadres.

Fundamentally, the photographer divides his understanding of modern China into three eras: China 1.0, the Maoist times he glimpsed as a child; China 2.0, launched by Deng Xiaoping’s 1978 reforms; and China 3.0, the country that is developing at breakneck speed, with the belt and road a defining initiative.

“This book is my exploration of that new world,” he writes in Old Road New Connections. “I used my camera to capture a resurgent nation spreading its wings.”

Doing so involved more than 270,000km of travel, “roughly the distance of seven times around the globe”, on multiple self-funded journeys over a three-year period. He covered five continents, 21 countries and 54 regions, spending a great deal of time in factories, mines, ports, deserts and train drivers’ cabins, as well as on the road, and that was after months of negotiation for access to the businesses, state-owned and otherwise, operating along the routes.

The resulting book turns one of this century’s most ambitious infrastructure projects into something unexpectedly intimate and personal: a portrait of a world quietly reordering at China’s direction.

“It’s capturing the contradictions sometimes, the smallest details that lend poetic meaning to life,” says Liu. “That is much more enduring than what’s in the latest news cycle.”

Following new lines of steel in Indonesia, he rode the Jakarta-Bandung high‑speed Whoosh, photographing Chinese and Indonesian operators shoulder to shoulder in the cab as Southeast Asia’s first bullet train, launched in 2023, zipped at 350km/h past villages and roads still heaving with traffic jams.

“You can show [a picture of] a high‑speed train once,” he says. “You show it twice, it’s already a repeat. After that, what interests me is who’s riding it, who’s driving it, who’s being moved and who finds their village cut in half.”

His journey through western China began on the old Silk Road: Xian’s terracotta warriors; camel caravans re‑enacted in Dunhuang. In complete contrast, he then visited the country’s first 100-megawatt molten‑salt solar thermal power station, which uses 12,000 mirrors and looks like a sci‑fi sun.

Some of Liu’s favourite photos from this project emerged along the sand ridges of the Gobi: young dancers from the Beijing Modern Dance Company performing in the desert, their body language, movement and skin tones coalescing with the sand dunes.

In Thailand, he watched workers construct concrete pillars that will one day connect Kunming, in China’s Yunnan province, to Bangkok and then Malaysia and Singapore, a rail artery replacing old Maritime Silk Road routes.

In Kazakhstan’s Aktau port, Liu slept uncomfortably “in a small converted shipping container”, he recalls, while sharing a worksite with engineers of the Citic Environmental Group, who were trying – not without problems – to hand over a water-treatment plant to a local team.

He met a couple in the old city of Kashgar, in the Xinjiang Uygur autonomous region; Hadiya Msham Abdulla from Tanzania studied at Fujian Medical University and now runs a cafe with her Uygur partner, Dilshat Tursun. As their staff made a latte for Liu, the couple proudly introduced him to their baby daughter.

“What is the most important factor in storytelling? I believe it’s trust,” he says. “I’m a humanist to the core, because I’m interested in everything. I’m interested in why this woman wants to look beautiful. I’m interested in athletes, how this man can run so fast.”

He’s also interested in the Kenyan manager at the Mombasa-Nairobi Standard Gauge Railway control centre who dispatches instructions in fluent Mandarin. In the Democratic Republic of Congo, Liu and his camera descended down a suffocating 1,150-metre-deep underground lift into the Kamoa-Kakula copper mine, which has Chinese investors. He snapped portraits of local miners, South African technicians and Angolan labourers working side by side with Chinese engineers.

Elsewhere in Africa, he encountered a Chinese lion conservationist, Xing Ba, in the Masai Mara wildlife reserve and a young Chinese engineer who has no plans to “go back” – Africa, for her, had become home.

His photographs from Iran date from just before the current conflict in the Middle East. While in the country, his interpreter asked him when more much-needed trains would be coming from China for Tehran’s metro. China helped build several of the city’s lines.

Liu captured a ladies’ Zumba class in Xinjiang; Brazilian cowboys; and a courier making AliExpress deliveries on horseback in a satellite town outside Moscow, Russia. He even peeked inside a Shanghai factory producing satellites deployed in the US-China space race.

A post-dinner snoop around his apartment finds a small framed picture of Liu’s, above his desk. It’s of Richard Nixon holding a bucket of Tsingtao beers and play-acting as a waiter on a Hangzhou-to-Shanghai train. The former US president was acting goofy on returning to China in 1982, to commemorate the 10th anniversary of the signing of the Shanghai Communiqué, which paved the way to normalising bilateral relations between China and the US.

On another wall is a picture that helped earn Liu his shared Pulitzer – as well as a punch from a KGB guard inside the Kremlin: a 1991 shot for Associated Press of Mikhail Gorbachev announcing his resignation and the dissolution of the Soviet Union. It shows the departing president sitting at a table, his shoulders slightly slumped, a blur of paper – an empire dissolving between one breath and the next.

“I was the only foreign photojournalist inside the great hall at the time,” recalls Liu. “I was really running around like crazy that day.”

His contributions to culture grew in 2015, when he founded the Shanghai Centre of Photography, which quickly became a mainstay of the West Bund art district before closing in December 2023. Running a non-profit, fundraising and training 10-plus staff proved rewarding but ultimately too taxing. He now divides his time between Hong Kong, Europe and mainland China.

With Old Road New Connections, Liu shows he is still more interested in being on the road than at the podium. Although conceived as a China 3.0 travelogue of sorts, a book on how China’s infrastructure push translates into lived experience is perhaps a logical addendum to a lifelong project of bearing witness to world-defining tipping points.

His photographs and anecdotes do not resolve into a neat verdict on the belt and road, or China’s far-spanning cross-border development. Instead, they register the speed of progress and the human ways by which capital and culture, Chinese or otherwise, flow. It’s not the panoramas of bridges, trains or ports that are most poignant, but the images of people absorbed in daily life as the world around them is being rewired.

“If the 21st century is to be China’s, as the last was America’s, I wanted to see the making of that century where it was happening, no matter how far away,” he writes.

“I’ve photographed so many people, in so many stories, in so many different countries, but I’m still interested,” he says, finishing the Chablis, “because my next picture could just be my best.”
