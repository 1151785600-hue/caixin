Japan treads diplomatic fine line over US campaign to end ICC ‘threat’

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 973 words | 2026-07-16

Japan has voiced concern about the United States’ campaign against the International Criminal Court (ICC), exposing a diplomatic bind for Tokyo as it seeks to defend a tribunal it has long championed without provoking its most important security ally.

Chief Cabinet Secretary Minoru Kihara told a press conference in Tokyo on Tuesday that Japan “places great importance on the eradication and prevention of serious crimes, as well as the upholding of the rule of law”.

He added that Japan, the largest financial contributor to the court, “has consistently supported the ICC in its capacity as a permanent international criminal tribunal”.

The ICC’s current president, Tomoko Akane, is Japanese, and analysts point out that Tokyo has also strongly advocated for the court and the rule of law in the international community.

While Japan was likely to avoid a direct clash with Washington, observers warned the episode would deepen doubts about US reliability and force Tokyo to weigh its international-law commitments against its dependence on Washington’s security umbrella.

“I am sure that Prime Minister [Sanae] Takaichi is going to gingerly try to avoid confrontation with the US and hope that the problem just goes away,” said Jeff Kingston, director of Asian Studies at the Tokyo branch of Temple University.

“They will not react, play for time and have their fingers crossed that something else comes up that catches President [Donald] Trump’s attention and that he forgets about this,” he told This Week in Asia.

“But yes, Japan will be very worried by this announcement.”

The US policy towards the ICC was outlined in an opinion article by Secretary of State Marco Rubio published in The Wall Street Journal on Monday, in which he accused the court of “waging a war against our country, not with bullets or missiles”, but with “the force of so-called international law”.

The ICC, he said, was “backed and run by a powerful network of leftist non-government organisations, smug globalists, and hostile Third World governments united by their enmity towards the US”.

A statement published on Monday on the State Department website claimed the ICC “poses an intolerable threat to US sovereignty” and warned there would be “increased scrutiny of nations that refuse to reject the ICC’s false authority while relying on US assistance”.

The ICC, based in The Hague, is a permanent international tribunal that prosecutes individuals accused of genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity and the crime of aggression.

Hostility towards the court goes back to Trump’s first term in office, when it announced plans to investigate allegations of war crimes committed by US forces in Afghanistan.

The second Trump White House has imposed a range of sanctions on the ICC’s officials, including its chief prosecutor and eight judges, for efforts to investigate both the US and Israel.

The risks attached to Akane’s role have already become personal.

In 2023, while Akane was temporarily in Japan, she received a call from ICC headquarters in The Hague telling her to change her flight plans to return to Europe.

Her original route would have taken her over China and some of the Central Asian republics, and there was concern that the flight would be forced to land because the Russian government had issued an arrest warrant for her.

A few months earlier, the ICC had put out arrest warrants for Russian President Vladimir Putin and Maria Lvova-Belova, accusing them of responsibility for the war crimes of unlawfully deporting and transferring Ukrainian children from occupied areas of Ukraine to Russia.

Threats to the court are the antithesis of what Japan stands for, according to Kingston.

“Japan is a country that believes in the rule of law and a rules-based international order,” he said. “There is already deep apprehension in Tokyo about the credibility of the US security umbrella and this is just going to reinforce the perception that the US is not a reliable ally any more.”

For Kingston, the ICC dispute is not isolated but part of a wider erosion of confidence in Washington’s security commitments.

That concern, he suggested, could lead Japan to conclude that its best self-defence strategy would be to develop an independent nuclear weapons capability, thereby also encouraging South Korea to take similar steps and potentially “fuel a regional nuclear arms race”.

Kingston said other long-standing US allies in the region were now looking to Tokyo for closer security partnerships.

Japan has recently announced that it will provide the Philippines with five former Maritime Self-Defence Force warships, while Tokyo and Vietnam reached an agreement this week to jointly develop fast military landing craft.

Toshimitsu Shigemura, a professor of international relations at Tokyo’s Waseda University, said Japan was likely to keep a low profile hoping the matter would blow over, but would ultimately prioritise its security over the ICC if the pressure ramped up.

“The ICC has accused Israel and Putin of crimes and Trump is not happy because those are his friends,” he said.

“I think the pressure on Japan is going to increase and I expect Japan to stay quiet and hope things calm down again.”

Shigemura said the proposal for Japan to host the ICC’s Asian branch, first outlined in early 2024, was likely to be quietly dropped, which would be a significant blow.

“Japan recognises that it has very few senior officials in the leading posts at global organisations, such as the United Nations, which means that its voice is often not heard,” he said, noting that the ICC was one of Japan’s few successes.

“Japan is very eager to maintain the ICC and the Japanese judge who presides over it, but if the US threatened to draw down the security alliance, then I believe Japan would put priority on the defence of the nation,” he said.
