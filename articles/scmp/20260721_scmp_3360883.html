China’s survival strategies written in flavours

SCMP | FREE | 1473 words | 2026-07-21

The first time I ate proper Sichuan food in Chengdu, my mouth went numb. Not spicy-numb. Anaesthetised numb, like a dentist had been at my tongue with lignocaine. The waiter saw my face and laughed. “Málà,” he said. Numbing spicy: the specific sensation of Sichuan peppercorn meeting chilli oil, a flavour combination so particular to this basin-locked province that there’s no English word for it.

I look Chinese and I should appreciate this, except I am not quite. What I didn’t understand then, eating my way through a bowl of mapo tofu while my face leaked involuntary tears, was that I wasn’t experiencing cuisine. I was experiencing climate adaptation disguised as dinner.

Sichuan is a basin. Humid, isolated, subtropical. Before refrigeration, before modern preservation, the question wasn’t “what tastes good?” The question was “how do we stop food rotting in 90 per cent humidity while we’re eating it?” The answer: you overwhelm. You use so much chilli and peppercorn that bacteria doesn’t stand a chance. You pickle everything that can be pickled. You dry what can be dried. You ferment what’s left. The intensity isn’t indulgence, it’s necessity turned into culture over 2,000 years.

This is what Chinese food actually is, once you strip away the romanticisation and the “gastroporn” and the idea that cuisine is primarily about pleasure. It’s a historical record of what people needed to survive, written in flavour.

The basin: Sichuan’s humidity problem

Sichuan sits in a depression surrounded by mountains, with the Tibetan Plateau to the west, the Qinling range to the north. Moisture gets trapped. The air stays thick. In summer, it’s a greenhouse. In winter, it’s a damp cold that seeps into your bones and won’t leave. This creates a preservation crisis that coastal and northern China don’t face. Food spoils faster, mould is constant, storage is a battle against biology. So, Sichuan cooking became aggressive: doubanjiang (fermented broad-bean paste) by the handful, and hotpot, where everything gets boiled in chilli oil hot enough to sterilise on contact.

The food overwhelms your senses because it had to overwhelm microbes first.

I’ve eaten mapo tofu in London, in Singapore, in Chinese restaurants across three continents. It’s never the same as eating it in Chengdu, where the humidity is 85 per cent and your clothes never quite dry and the málà makes sense as something other than a flavour preference. Twice-cooked pork, dan dan noodles, fish-fragrant aubergine – every signature Sichuan dish follows the same logic: bold flavours that mask spoilage, high heat that kills bacteria, preservation techniques that buy time.

This is survival cuisine that became so successful it convinced the world it was always about pleasure.

The coast: Cantonese confidence

If Sichuan cuisine is about fighting humidity, Cantonese food is about not needing to fight anything.

Guangzhou sits at the Pearl River Delta, where river meets ocean, where trade brought wealth and wealth brought abundance, and abundance produced restraint. When you can get fresh fish daily, when vegetables grow year-round, you don’t need to preserve anything because tomorrow will bring more.

Cantonese cooking is quiet. Steaming, not frying. Light soy, not heavy bean paste. Ginger and spring onion, not chilli oil. The food doesn’t shout because it doesn’t need to. I’ve watched how Cantonese cooks prepare fish; steamed whole, soy sauce drizzled, nothing else. The point is showing off the fish, proving it’s so fresh that anything more would be vandalism. This is prosperity cuisine, abundance cuisine, cuisine that says, “we’re so secure, we can afford to do nothing”.

Dim sum, the elaborately folded dumplings, the delicate shrimp har gow, the translucent rice rolls, is restraint dressed as complexity. It is maximum technique applied to minimal ingredients, showing skill through precision rather than force. The Cantonese saying goes, “eat in Guangzhou”, not because the flavours are loudest, but because they’re truest. Coastal confidence written in steamed fish and morning yum cha.

The plateau: Shaanxi’s arid survival

Shaanxi province is characterised by yellow earth, cold winters, not enough water, wheat instead of rice because rice needs irrigation, the plateau can’t provide. This is northern China and the food reflects it: heavy, durable, built to fuel physical labour in cold, dry air where every calorie counts.

It gives us biang biang noodles, hand-pulled, wide as belts, served with chilli oil and vinegar because that’s what you’ve got and that’s what stores; roujiamo, flatbread stuffed with slow-cooked meat, eaten by hand, portable fuel; yangrou paomo, mutton soup with torn bread, eaten for breakfast when you need 2,000 calories before you start work.

I ate biang biang noodles in a tiny restaurant in Xian’s Muslim Quarter, watching the cook slap dough against a metal table until it stretched into noodles 60cm (two feet) long. The sound echoed off the walls – biang biang biang – which is supposedly how the dish got its name. The character for biang (𰻝) has 58 strokes and isn’t in standard dictionaries because it was invented by illiterate noodle sellers who needed a word for the sound. The noodles were thick, chewy, coated in chilli oil and vinegar, topped with vegetables and a bit of pork. Not delicate or subtle. Just: here’s wheat, here’s heat, here’s what keeps you upright when the plateau cold tries to put you down.

Shaanxi food is northern Chinese pragmatism rendered edible. It values sustenance over pleasure, durability over delicacy, survival over refinement.

The delta: Jiangnan’s sweet abundance

South of Shaanxi, east of Sichuan, you get Jiangnan, which sits “south of the Yangtze”, centred on Suzhou and Hangzhou – waterways, fertile land, relative peace for centuries. The scholar-official class developed here. The imperial examination system produced more successful candidates from Jiangnan than anywhere else. This is where Chinese literati culture comprising painting, poetry, gardens and aesthetic refinement grew the deepest roots.

The food reflects that. Jiangnan cuisine is subtly sweet, using sugar to balance rather than dominate. Think of xiaolongbao – soup dumplings in which the broth is delicately flavoured, not aggressively seasoned, and West Lake vinegar fish from Hangzhou – sweet-sour, restrained. This is cuisine developed by people who had time to think about aesthetics, who had enough security to value harmony and balance over intensity. When you’re not fighting for survival, you can afford elegance.

I ate xiaolongbao in a Shanghai restaurant where each dumpling was folded with 18 pleats, not because 18 is functionally necessary, but because 18 is the traditional number and tradition matters when you’re performing refinement rather than just feeding people.

Jiangnan food is what happens when prosperity lasts long enough to become culture.

The freeze: Dongbei’s preservation imperative

Finally, there’s the northeast: Manchuria, where winter lasts six months and temperatures hit minus 40 degrees Celsius (minus 40 degrees Fahrenheit), and the question isn’t “how do we keep food fresh?” but “how do we keep food at all?” The answer: you pickle. You ferment. You preserve everything possible in autumn because nothing grows between November and April.

Pickled cabbage, fermented in massive jars, stored for winter, the base of dozens of Dongbei dishes. Stews that cook all day because when it’s minus 30 degrees Celsius outside, the stove stays on anyway. Dumplings filled with pork and cabbage, boiled in vast quantities, frozen outside, retrieved as needed.

This is the cuisine of cold-climate survival. The food is heavy, repetitive, calorically dense. You’re not eating for pleasure, you’re eating to generate body heat, to maintain function, to survive until spring.

I visited Harbin in January, when everything was frozen solid. Beautiful and profoundly cold. The locals were eating hotpot at outdoor tables, wearing three layers, drinking baijiu to stay warm. The food was pure fuel. When I asked a Harbin native what defined Dongbei cuisine, she said: “We eat what doesn’t freeze.”

What the food reveals

Together, these five cuisines cover the fundamental climates and geographies that shaped how a billion people eat.

Each cuisine is a survival strategy that worked so well it became culture. Once it’s culture, wrapped in tradition and nostalgia and regional pride, it stops looking like adaptation and starts looking like identity.

Chinese food isn’t mysterious. It’s just geography rendered edible, climate written in flavour, history you can taste if you know what you’re tasting for. Every dish becomes a climate map, every regional speciality a survival footnote, every meal a reminder that culture is just successful adaptation given enough time to forget it was adaptation in the first place.

This is an exclusive essay by Lu-Ann Ong, whose forthcoming book, Experience China: What Maps Don’t Tell, explores contemporary China through landscape, cuisine, history and memory.
