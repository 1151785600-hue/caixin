China report warns against European-led Nato 3.0 expansion in the Asia-Pacific

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 654 words | 2026-07-15

The transition of the North Atlantic military alliance to a more Europe-led “Nato 3.0” will fail to heal deep-seated strategic rifts between Washington and the European capitals, according to a leading Chinese think tank.

In a report published on Monday, the China Institutes of Contemporary International Relations (CICIR) also warned that Nato’s expanding military and defence cooperation with Asia-Pacific nations such as Japan would leave the bloc “increasingly at odds with an accelerating multipolar world order”.

The report follows the transatlantic security alliance’s summit in Ankara last week where the Nato 3.0 concept was formally embraced as its latest strategic direction.

What is Nato 3.0?

The term broadly refers to a structural overhaul that shifts greater responsibility for conventional defence on to Europe, while the United States recalibrates its global commitments.

According to the CICIR, this marks a new phase in Nato’s evolution: from Cold War-era territorial defence against the Soviet Union, through out-of-area interventions and crisis management, to a Europe-led model centred on home defence and deterrence.

The shift is being driven in large part by the Trump administration’s renewed emphasis on burden-sharing.

Washington’s National Security Strategy, released in late 2025, gives priority to the western hemisphere, putting added pressure on European allies to take “primary responsibility” for their own conventional defence.

But the burden-sharing push is exposing a more fundamental conflict of interest, according to the Chinese think tank.

While Washington sees the transition as a way to reduce its security costs and keep Europe tied to American weapons systems, European governments are using the moment to try to pursue genuine strategic autonomy and rebuild their defence supply chains.

The US and European goals “diverge from the very beginning”, the CICIR report said.

Washington’s preferred “made by Nato” production model and Europe’s desire to “buy European” reflected a chronic industrial struggle over who controlled the continent’s defence future, it said.

Tensions were not limited to procurement. The summit’s show of unity could not hide growing European unease over Washington’s hard-edged approach, including US President Donald Trump’s renewed Greenland rhetoric and his continuing criticism of Nato on the opening day, the CICIR said.

The report also took aim at the increasingly active military outreach in Asia by some Nato members, especially their defence ties with Japan and South Korea.

‘Asian version of Nato’

The alliance’s geography has historically confined it to the North Atlantic and its Article 5 collective defence clause applies only to Europe and North America.

Beijing has long viewed the bloc’s deepening footprint in Asia as part of a US effort to build an “Asian version of Nato” to contain China, a concern underscored by alliance Secretary General Mark Rutte’s meeting in Ankara with leaders from the Indo-Pacific.

The Indo-Pacific Four, or IP4 – Australia, Japan, New Zealand and South Korea – have become regular guests at Nato summits since the bloc began stepping up its outreach to the region in 2022.

In the meeting with Rutte on the summit’s sidelines, they agreed to deepen practical cooperation in defence industrial supply chains, cyberspace and advanced technologies.

According to Japan’s foreign ministry, the leaders also exchanged candid views on global flashpoints, including Ukraine, as well as regional tensions around China, North Korea and Iran.

The CICIR said the projection of force beyond Nato’s traditional transatlantic domain was “severely destabilising” security elsewhere. “It not only undermines European security but also threatens global stability,” the report said.

The think tank also noted that Nato’s evolving global posture did not alter its underlying nature as “a tool to preserve and protect American hegemony”, and accused the alliance of clinging to a zero-sum Cold War mindset.

“Nato 3.0 … will further expose the contradiction between US hegemony and European autonomy, leaving the alliance increasingly out of step with a multipolar world,” the report said.
