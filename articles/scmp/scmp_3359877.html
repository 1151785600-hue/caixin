Beijing slams Manila over South China Sea claims ahead of 2016 Hague anniversary

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 797 words | 2026-07-08

Beijing has issued a lengthy critique of the Philippines’ territorial claims in the South China Sea, condemning its actions as “unlawful” expansions that pose a “grave threat” to the post-war global order.

Wednesday’s report by a Ministry of Natural Resources think tank – its third within days – marks the latest move in China’s campaign to reinforce its position in the disputed waters.

The push underlines Beijing’s rejection of the Philippines’ stance just days before the 10th anniversary of a landmark international tribunal ruling that invalidated key elements of China’s claims. China did not take part in the arbitration and does not recognise the ruling.

Manila had offered “shifting” justification for its claims over the decades, the China Institute for Marine Affairs (CIMA) in Beijing said in its report, citing successive territorial claims to Scarborough Shoal and parts of the Spratly Islands. The features are also claimed by China, which calls them Huangyan Island and the Nansha Islands, respectively.

Manila’s “attempt … to extend its territory” to those maritime features was “devoid of historical and legal foundation”, the 62-page English-language version of the report argued.

It said “a comprehensive analysis of credible historical sources and generally recognised rules of international law” showed that Manila had no sovereignty over Scarborough or any of the islands and reefs in the Spratlys.

Manila was also accused of advancing arguments that misinterpreted and distorted historical sources, carried little legal weight, and were inconsistent and at times even contradictory.

“The Philippines’ actions have revealed an intention to expand its territorial limits beyond those laid down in various treaties and accepted upon independence,” said the report, which was compiled with the help of researchers from another government-affiliated think tank and five Chinese universities.

“It is necessary to clearly recognise that the Philippines’ unlawful acts, while challenging the international legal order, constitute a grave threat to regional peace and stability and the maintenance of the international order.”

Titled “Historical and Legal Critique of the Philippines’ Territorial Claims in the South China Sea”, it is the CIMA’s third report on maritime disputes in just over a week.

It follows a July 3 legal warning to Japan and the Philippines over their planned boundary talks and a June 30 assessment of the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (Unclos).

The flurry of legal opinions – largely targeting Manila – comes days before the 10th anniversary of a Hague ruling that found no legal basis for Beijing’s sweeping South China Sea claims in a case brought by the Philippines. Beijing dismissed the July 12 ruling of 2016 as “illegal, null and void”.

However, Chinese observers have warned of sustained pressure in the international discourse, noting that some countries that once backed Beijing now sat on the sidelines or leaned towards Manila. They have Beijing to sharpen its legal counter-strategy and more forcefully rebut the ruling.

Beijing claims most of the islands and rocks in the South China Sea and rights over their adjacent waters, leading to territorial disputes with several neighbouring countries. Tensions have flared most sharply with the Philippines in recent years.

The latest CIMA report said that after gaining independence in 1946, the Philippines inherited and confirmed territorial limits formed during the Spanish and American colonial periods, defined by the treaties of 1898, 1900 and 1930.

“None of the islands or reefs of the Nansha Qundao or Huangyan Dao were incorporated,” it added, using the Chinese names for the features.

According to the report, Manila repeatedly shifted its justification for claiming Scarborough Shoal – moving from an exclusive economic zone argument to “discovery and occupation” and finally to a reinterpretation of those historical treaties.

Those claims violated Unclos, the report said, adding that an 18th century map cited by the Philippines was unreliable, and noting that Manila had previously acknowledged the atoll lay outside its sovereignty.

It also dismissed Manila’s claims to a part of the Spratlys called Kalayaan Island Group as legally invalid. It argued that the claim, resting on geographical proximity, the disputed Allied trusteeship under the San Francisco Treaty after World War II and Filipino explorer Tomas Cloma’s private expedition from 1947 to 1950, had no legal validity.

“The Philippines’ current territorial expansion activities stand in sharp contrast to its historical commitment to maintaining the treaty limits it accepted and respecting the post-war arrangements,” the report said.

Beyond accusing the Philippines of “unlawful occupation”, the document blamed it for escalating tensions through coastguard patrols, rammings, close-in reconnaissance and other “radical” moves, as well as blocking talks on a South China Sea code of conduct.

The Philippines had introduced “unwelcome and complex external factors” to back its claims in the South China Sea, the report said, in an apparent reference to the United States and its allies such as Japan and Australia.
