Sometimes, it pays to be a backward country

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 771 words | 2026-07-11

Who would have thought that Leon Trotsky inadvertently offered a theory to explain China’s economic leapfrog into the 21st century after bouncing back from history’s worst famine and the Cultural Revolution?

In recent decades, some people thought China was done for and would eventually collapse. They might have done better if they had read at least the first chapter of Trotsky’s The History of the Russian Revolution. Here, he expounds on what he calls “the privilege of historic backwardness”, which seems to apply quite well to China’s economic and technological leapfrogging.

“A backward country assimilates the material and intellectual conquests of the advanced countries,” he wrote. “But this does not mean that it follows them slavishly, reproduces all the stages of their past.” Does that sound a lot like China in the past few decades?

Trotsky continues: “Although compelled to follow after the advanced countries, a backward country does not take things in the same order. The privilege of historic backwardness – and such a privilege exists – permits, or rather compels, the adoption of whatever is ready in advance of any specified date, skipping a whole series of intermediate stages … Their development as a whole acquires a planless, complex, combined character.

“The possibility of skipping over intermediate steps is of course by no means absolute. Its degree is determined in the long run by the economic and cultural capacities of the country.”

Witness fast-growing economies in Africa that have leapfrogged to 5G networks and e-commerce without the intermediate development stages.

Here, I have to thank a reader, Alberto, who referred me to Brazilian political economist Leonardo Burlamaqui. He wrote a Substack post titled, “Unpacking Leapfrogging: Towards an Explanation From Trotsky and Gerschenkron to Schumpeter and China.”

Trotsky is actually trying to explain why the communist revolution occurred in Russia, the most politically and economically backward of the European great powers. This contradicted Karl Marx’s theory that communism would be the final stage of development in advanced capitalist economies.

In his piece, Burlamaqui combines Trotsky’s inverted theory of revolution with the economic theories of Alexander Gerschenkron and Joseph Schumpeter. He quotes Gerschenkron: “The more backward a country, the greater the likelihood that industrialisation would proceed through special institutions capable of organising and accelerating the process.”

Note the key word “institutions”. Without proper state direction, a backward country might not develop at all, let alone leapfrog economically or technologically.

Commenting on Schumpeter, Burlamaqui writes: “A socialist system, properly organised, might not suppress innovation. Under certain political and institutional conditions, it could organise and coordinate innovation more effectively than corporate capitalism itself.”

I have already said too much about Burlamaqui, as I wrote this column as a signpost so more readers can read his insightful posts directly. However, I will mention another piece, “Why China got rich and India didn’t: The human roots of the Sino-Indian divergence” by economics blogger David Oks.

Earlier, I mentioned Mao Zedong’s infamous Cultural Revolution, the Great Leap Forward and the resulting famine. These were unmitigated disasters, but as Oks argues, China’s subsequent success is because of Mao’s brutal rule, not in spite of it.

Between 1949 and 1976, the Chinese communist state essentially dismantled traditional kinship structures, replacing them with a modernised social framework focused on mass education and public health. This ideological shift resulted in massive leaps in human development, making China socially prepared for rapid industrial capitalism well before its economic liberalisation.

Much like German unification and industrialisation under Otto von Bismarck, post-Mao China inherited a massive educated, young and healthy labour force, including many women, enabling industrialisation and urbanisation to take off under increasingly institutionalised, state-directed development.

“And this meant that by the late 1970s, even before the ‘reform and opening up’ process started, China was perfectly prepared for industrial capitalism,” Oks writes. “The old constraints – kinship, tenancy, female seclusion – had been swept away; the Chinese workforce was mobile, trainable and cheap. That mismatch, between China’s level of human development and its level of wealth, was bound to be resolved by rapid economic growth.”

None of this is new. Social scientists such as Wang Shaoguang of the Chinese University of Hong Kong and Hu Angang of Tsinghua University have long articulated similar views. However, Oks’ succinct summary is worth mentioning as anti-China discourse in the West tends to focus on claims about the China threat and its alleged subversion of the international order.

Though ideologically motivated, the official verdict on Mao as being 70 per cent right and 30 per cent wrong has turned out to be not too far off the mark.
