Global attractions that ooze innovation and imagination

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1721 words | 2026-07-08

By their very nature, museums, heritage sites and other tourist favourites tend to be backwards-looking, but that doesn’t mean there aren’t global attractions that flip the script to imagine what lies ahead – or what could exist in alternate universes.

From interactive museums to cities built on tomorrow’s green tech and imagination-firing immersive-art zones, the following destinations offer a passport to innovative worlds, be they future or imaginary ones.

Shenzhen Science & Technology Museum

Inspired by a “colossal interstellar spacecraft” and located in the Guangming Science City, the Shenzhen Science & Technology Museum is an eight-storey (two subterranean) architectural masterpiece designed by Zaha Hadid Architects that opened last May. It features an eye-catching U-shaped stainless-steel facade and this year was named one of the world’s seven most beautiful museums by the compilers of the Prix Versailles lists.

This immersive, tech-driven playground is packed with hands-on artificially intelligent robotics, simulation technologies and state-of-the-art cinemas. Outdoor terraces and a sunlit central atrium integrate the building with the surrounding Science Park and the museum is easy to reach across a walkway from the Guangming Metro Station, a mere 90 minutes from Hong Kong’s Central MTR station (depending on connections and border-crossing queues).

Chengdu Science Fiction Museum

Another Zaha Hadid Architects masterpiece, the Chengdu Science Fiction Museum “floats” on Jingrong Lake and boasts a “fluid starburst” roof that reflects the expanding nebula of the universe to viewers on the outside.

Enter and you’re treated to a deep dive into the history and future of Chinese and global science-fiction in multimedia-heavy exhibition spaces, art galleries and a multifunctional theatre that in 2023 hosted the Hugo Awards, widely considered the Oscars of the sci-fi literary world. Look out for the life-size replica of Benben, the hyper-intelligent, easily frightened utility droid/mechanical dog from Andy Lau Tak-wah’s sci-fi blockbuster The Wandering Earth 2 (2023).

The surrounding complex blends the futuristic, fluid indoor spaces with lakefront pedestrian pathways, green parklands and outdoor viewing platforms complete with panoramic photo opportunities.

The quickest way to get to the Chengdu Science Fiction Museum is to set your time machine for the year 2100, then be whisked over to Sichuan province from any of Hong Kong’s major teleport stations.

City in Nature, Singapore

Singapore’s City in Nature initiative aims to weave rich, functional ecosystems directly into the urban fabric, the goals being to boost biodiversity, manage climate resilience and improve human well-being.

At the heart of this vision is the Supertree Grove at Gardens by the Bay. Despite being 14 years old, these 12 structures are still considered futuristic and are fully functional biophilic systems. Standing up to 50 metres tall, they serve as vertical beds for more than 160,000 plants, use photovoltaic cells to harvest solar energy and act as exhaust and cooling receptacles for the park’s conservatories.

As in every good ecosystem, the Supertrees do not exist in a vacuum, and are connected to an island-wide network of living infrastructure, exemplified by three other tourist-friendly sites that can be easily reached from Gardens by the Bay on public transport: the Singapore Botanic Gardens, the CapitaSpring Green Oasis and Sky Garden – two huge green spaces that break up the density of the 280-metre-tall skyscraper – and the Forest Walk, part of the 10km Southern Ridges Trail, which links several hilltop parks and crosses the Henderson Waves, Singapore’s highest pedestrian bridge.

Toyota’s Woven City, Japan

The Woven City is “weaving the future”, according to the website. Located at the foot of Mount Fuji, the 175-acre “living laboratory” is designed to test technologies such as autonomous driving, robotics, smart homes and AI in a real-world community.

The city gets its name from its layout and the weaving together of three distinct street types: one dedicated to fast automated vehicles, one for lower-speed personal mobility and pedestrians, and a third consisting of park-like paths exclusively for walkers.

Toyota is planning to open public tours as early as this year, on which visitors can enter the community as “one-day weavers” to test out the city’s mobility tech and interact with smart-home features. They will also be encouraged to provide feedback to the resident inventors.

A key stop on these tours is likely to be the Inventor Garage, which was opened in April to serve as the primary hub for tech showcases, AI demonstrations and collaborative innovation events.

Museu de les Ciències, Valencia, Spain

The Science Museum opened in 2000 as a focal point of Valencia’s City of Arts and Sciences and is housed in a building that is still considered futuristic, even though it resembles the skeleton of a prehistoric whale. Spanish architect Santiago Calatrava’s innovation here involved making a heavy concrete and steel structure look fluid, weightless and alive. Roughly 220 metres long, 80 metres wide and 55 metres high, the vast exhibition halls are largely free of internal supporting pillars.

Operating under the motto “Forbidden not to touch, not to feel, not to think”, the museum is 26,000 square metres of interactive exhibits devoted to everything from genetics and space exploration to climate change.

Echoing Singapore’s Supertrees, here there is the Chromosome Forest, 23 glowing “trees” each representing a pair of chromosomes in the human genome. Visitors have to pull levers, press buttons and trigger interactive modules built directly into the “bark” of a tree to discover what specific genes control.

Outside a curtain wall consisting of 4,000 panes of glass – the entire north facade of the museum – seasonal concerts take place around turquoise reflection pools. Next door is the Hemisfèric IMAX cinema and the Oceanogràfic, Europe’s largest aquarium.

Futurium, Berlin, Germany

Standing on the banks of the River Spree, in central Berlin, the energy-sustainable Futurium is a space designed for both public debates and lectures and as an exhibition hall in which the possible worlds of tomorrow are viewed through the lenses of humanities, nature and technology.

The Futurium’s sculptural exterior features more than 8,000 diamond-shaped cassettes made of cast glass and hidden metal reflectors, and admission to its three floors of interactive exhibits, hands-on subterranean lab and AI installations is free of charge.

In the centre of the building is an energy-storage system packed with paraffin-candle wax combined with water. As the building absorbs heat from the “solar sea” of panels on the roof, the wax melts, soaking up energy. When the building cools down at night, the wax solidifies again, releasing that stored energy back into the museum. This giant wax battery is housed behind glass that allows visitors to check the state of the melting or hardening wax to see exactly how hard the building is working to power itself.

The highlight for many visitors, though, is the Skywalk on the roof, which pairs an inspection of the building’s solar-energy collectors with panoramic views of the Berlin skyline, including the Reichstag dome and the Federal Chancellery.

TeamLab, Tokyo

Created by an interdisciplinary international art collective that came together in 2001, Tokyo’s permanent teamLab exhibitions – including “Borderless”, in Azabudai Hills, and “Planets”, in Toyosu – are large-scale, multisensory digital art museums that dissolve the boundaries between the viewer and the artwork.

The spaces are designed to be “museums without a map”, allowing visitors to freely explore shifting, digital landscapes that change in real time based on human interaction – a walk barefoot through knee-deep water filled with digital projections of koi fish, a wander through a mirror maze of thousands of hanging LED crystal lights or a lie-down under a dome of floating orchids – and even sample flower-infused vegan ramen inside an active art installation.

Not visiting Tokyo? TeamLab operates between 15 and 20 long-term or permanent exhibitions globally, including full-scale museums, permanent collaborations and open-air parks. The nearest to Hong Kong is teamLab SuperNature Macao, a permanent, 5,000-square-metre immersive digital museum inside The Venetian Macao.

Meow Wolf, various locations across the United States

Another outfit riding the modern immersive-art wave is Meow Wolf.

Part-escape room (or, more accurately, linger room), part-immersive exhibition, Meow Wolf started in 2008 as a DIY punk artist collective in Santa Fe, New Mexico, and now operates five experiences across the US, with a sixth to open soon, in Los Angeles.

The company hires hundreds of local painters, sculptors, technologists and writers to build permanent, larger-than-life dreamscapes in which visitors are encouraged to touch everything, explore freely and play.

Each is unique. Santa Fe’s House of Eternal Return is based on a multidimensional Victorian house in which a family has vanished after performing an experiment with time and space; Omega Mart, in Las Vegas, takes its cue from a weird American supermarket that serves as a front for a corrupt, interdimensional corporation harvesting a mysterious cosmic substance. Similar freakiness unfolds at the Convergence Station in Denver, Colorado, and two experiences in Texas, The Real Unreal and Radio Tave.

There are narrative puzzles to solve in each experience, says Meow Wolf public relations manager Michael Duffield, but visitors don’t have to do so to fully enjoy the experience, which lasts as long as they wish. Some may just want to find the well-hidden speakeasy bar.

T-PARK, Hong Kong

Sludge may not sound like everyone’s idea of fun, but T-Park, a state-of-the-art waste-to-energy treatment facility in Tsang Tsui, Tuen Mun, is a must for the eco-minded.

One of the largest and most technologically advanced self-sustained facilities of its kind in the world, T-Park processes the concentrated, semi-solid by-product left after waste water is treated elsewhere in the city. That sludge is fired in incinerators that in part heat the water for a free public bathhouse, T-Spa, featuring three indoor pools maintained at different temperatures. Also on-site are 9,800 square metres of landscaped regenerated outdoor wetlands designed to support biodiversity, and a self-service cafe.

It’s a bit of a trek out to Tsang Tsui, granted – even if you take the free shuttle from the Tuen Mun MTR station – but as far as green days out go, it rivals another hike along the Dragon’s Back trail.
