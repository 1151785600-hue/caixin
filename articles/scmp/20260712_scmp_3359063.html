The quiet grind behind the wig and gown

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 808 words | 2026-07-12

If ViuTV’s Court! feels unusually grounded for a legal drama, it is because the series dares to show what the public rarely sees – and what the profession has perhaps been too polite to say out loud.

There are no dramatic ambushes or theatrical monologues that turn the tide at the last minute. Instead, we watch young barristers navigate the far less cinematic realities of practice: waiting hours for a case that is adjourned in minutes; case preparation with leading counsel; and dealing with challenging interjections from judges. The series captures, with uncomfortable accuracy, the gap between how the Bar is imagined and how it actually operates.

One of the show’s most telling choices is to focus not only on courtroom performance, but also on preparation. A barrister’s real work happens at a desk – drafting, researching, recalibrating strategy. This is not incidental, it is the profession. Advocacy, for most junior counsel, is less about eloquence in court and more about disciplined, often invisible, intellectual labour. Court! gets this right, and in doing so, quietly challenges the mythology that has long surrounded the Bar.

That mythology, however, remains stubbornly enduring.

There is still a persistent belief that a call to the Bar confers immediate status and financial security. It does not. The early years of practice are defined by uncertainty. Income is uneven, work is not guaranteed, and the effort required to build a sustainable practice is significant.

Many young barristers take on legally aided or lower-fee work not as a matter of choice, but out of necessity. The notion that all barristers are comfortably remunerated is not just outdated, it is misleading.

Equally misplaced is the idea that courtroom advocacy is the central, daily function of a barrister. In reality, much of a junior’s time is spent outside court – drafting pleadings, advising clients and grappling with procedural complexities that rarely make for compelling television. These are not peripheral tasks; they are the foundation of competent advocacy. Yet they remain largely invisible to the public, reinforcing a skewed perception of what the profession demands.

Perhaps the most unhelpful misconception is that the Bar is, or should be, a solitary pursuit. Independence is a defining feature of the profession, but it should not be confused with isolation.

The challenges facing young barristers today, from financial sustainability to professional development, are very real. As a young barrister myself, I have seen both the resilience of young barristers and the strain on them.

This is where the Young Barristers’ Committee (YBC) has a role that goes beyond representation. Our work is not about preserving tradition for its own sake, but about ensuring that the profession remains viable for the next generation.

The YBC engages directly with the Bar Council and other stakeholders on issues that materially affect young practitioners, participates in events with other professional bodies, and provides a platform for young barristers to contribute to the community in meaningful ways.

We also invest in practical support. Advocacy training, mentorship initiatives and peer networks are not optional extras – they are essential infrastructure for a profession that expects excellence from the outset.

The transition from pupillage to independent practice is tough, and without structured support, the risk is not only individual burnout but the loss of capable practitioners who might otherwise have contributed meaningfully to the Bar.

Just as importantly, we see a responsibility to address public understanding. If Court! has opened a window into the realities of practice, it should not be the only voice shaping that narrative. A more accurate picture of the profession does not diminish its standing; it enhances it.

The YBC regularly holds talks for local and overseas university students to give them an insight into the Bar, and in particular the multitude of challenges that we face.

This outreach allows prospective entrants to make informed decisions, and it fosters a more honest conversation about the conditions necessary for the rule of law to be upheld in practice, not just in principle.

The young Bar in Hong Kong remains committed, capable, and adaptable. But commitment cannot be a substitute for sustainability. If the profession is to continue attracting and retaining talent, it must confront, rather than conceal, the realities faced by its newest members.

Court! may be fiction, but its restraint is its strength. By showing less spectacle and more substance, it invites a reassessment of what it truly means to practise at the Bar. The question is whether we are prepared to continue that reassessment beyond the screen.

Felix Tang is a current council member of the Hong Kong Bar Association and chairs its young barristers’ committee. He maintains a mixed practice. He shares views from a young barrister’s perspective.
