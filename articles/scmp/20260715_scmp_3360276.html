Why some Hongkongers take a 6-hour train ride for a Zhangjiajie holiday

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 677 words | 2026-07-15

Hongkonger Lam Chun and his family hopped onto a high-speed train for the first time last month for a five-day holiday in Zhangjiajie in Hunan province, finding the experience more suitable for travelling with elderly relatives.

The 54-year-old medical sector professional said he had accomplished a mission of travelling with his 90-year-old father and 85-year-old mother, along with a domestic helper, on a sightseeing trip.

He chose Zhangjiajie because the train journey took about six hours, giving his parents a sense of travelling “far away” from Hong Kong by their standards.

“It worked out well because I don’t think they would have managed to handle the hassle of flying, and if anything unexpected happened, touch wood, they would be able to get help sooner,” Lam said. He added that his parents had not travelled for years.

Travel agents in Hong Kong have observed strong growth in interest among residents in travelling across the border, bolstered by the expansion of direct train services from the city to mainland China destinations.

Travellers departing from the West Kowloon terminus can now reach 113 mainland destinations without transfers, including the new additions of Guangzhoubei in Guangdong province, Yiwu in Zhejiang province and Fuqingxi in Fujian province as of this month.

Among the many locations vying for visitors, Zhangjiajie has emerged as one of the most popular destinations for Hong Kong travellers since the direct line opened in June 2024.

The city in Hunan province is known for distinctive natural landscape that inspired the scenery in the Hollywood film franchise Avatar, as well as Tianmen Mountain, which has an opening in the middle large enough for a small plane to fly through.

Timothy Chui Ting-pong, executive director of the Hong Kong Tourism Association, said the interest in high-speed rail tour groups to the mainland from the city rose by 50 per cent between 2023 and 2025.

“Many tourist attractions, especially those south of the Yangtze River, really upped their game during the pandemic. When we were closed off to the world for a few years, they kept operating and became very mature,” Chui said.

He added that the MTR Corporation, which runs parts of the high-speed rail, had been proactive in working with travel agents to secure ticket bookings, and that the mainland attractions offered good value for money.

In a reply to the South China Morning Post, the MTR Corp, which operates the Hong Kong section of the Guangzhou-Shenzhen-Hong Kong high-speed rail, said Zhangjiajiexi – or West Zhangjiajie – station had become “one of the more popular destinations”.

“In the first half of this year, patronage to and from Zhangjiajiexi station increased by more than 20 per cent compared with 2024, when services were first launched,” a corporation spokesman said, bolstered by collaboration with the travel industry on high-speed rail travel products.

Other popular destinations included Shenzhenbei, Futian, Guangzhounan, Xiamen and Wuhan, the MTR Corp said.

Hongkonger Susanna Cheung and her husband, who run an electronics business together, returned from a trip to Zhangjiajie with a group of neighbours in mid-June.

Her trip was organised by her local district council and subsidised by Zhangjiajie’s tourism agency, which explained the low price tag of about HK$3,000 (US$383) for a five-day visit covering transport, accommodation, food and tips, she said.

The 65-year-old said the trade-off was an early 8am train departure, but she only needed to arrive 45 minutes beforehand, compared with the two hours typically required for flights, with no fuel surcharges or airport tax.

“The high-speed rail is much more comfortable. The seats are more spacious and it’s easier to walk around. It’s also easier to grab a bite and use the toilets,” Cheung said.

For Lam’s family, he said he regarded the Zhangjiajie trip as a rehearsal for their next journey to Shanghai, where his father was born and raised before moving to Hong Kong in the 1960s.

“I am planning for a homecoming trip for my father, on a sleeper train to Shanghai in autumn,” he said.
