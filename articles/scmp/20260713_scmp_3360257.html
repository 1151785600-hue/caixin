Flights and rail services resume across eastern China as Typhoon Bavi weakens

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1065 words | 2026-07-13

Rail and air services began returning to normal across large areas of eastern China on Sunday as Typhoon Bavi weakened and moved inland, away from some of the country’s largest cities.

As of 9pm on Sunday, the storm was over Chuzhou city in the central province of Anhui, according to the National Meteorological Centre. Bavi, which had been downgraded to a tropical storm earlier in the day, was slowly moving to the north at speeds of around 15km/h (9mph), the centre said.

The system is expected to veer northeast over Anhui province on Monday, cross the Shandong Peninsula into the northern waters of the Yellow Sea on Tuesday, and eventually transition into an extratropical cyclone.

The storm had earlier brought strong winds and heavy rains to large swathes of the east coast, prompting the evacuation of around 2.4 million people in the path of the storm and causing thousands of flights and rail services to be cancelled.

But as the conditions began to ease along the east coast, and some local authorities downgraded their weather warnings, services slowly began to return to normal. However, high winds and heavy rainfall were expected to continue across many parts of the country.

High-speed rail services along several major corridors connecting cities from Beijing to Hong Kong – as well as major eastern transport hubs such as Shanghai, Nanjing and Hangzhou – began a phased resumption on Sunday afternoon.

In Shanghai, a total of 653 flights were expected to be cancelled on Sunday, 458 at Pudong International Airport and 195 at Hongqiao International Airport, the local airport authority said earlier.

By Sunday afternoon, planes had started arriving at and departing from both hubs as Bavi moved inland. Flights also resumed at major airports in Hangzhou and Wenzhou in Zhejiang, and in Hefei, the capital of Anhui.

Xuanzhou Airport in Wuhu, the city at the centre of the storm on Sunday afternoon, cancelled all flights except one.

Air services were also returning to normal in Taiwan, which experienced heavy rainfall on Saturday when the storm brushed by the northern tip of the island.

The Taoyuan airport transit line – which connects major cities to the island’s primary international airport – also resumed full operations

Spanning 1,000km (600 miles) at its widest point – roughly the width of mainland France – Typhoon Bavi first came ashore in the coastal city of Taizhou in Zhejiang just after 11.10pm on Saturday. It made a second landfall in Wenzhou city around midnight, with maximum sustained winds near its centre of around 108–117km/h (67–72mph).

Regular bus, taxi and ride-hailing services in both cities gradually resumed from Sunday afternoon, with only a few routes still suspended due to the weather.

The National Meteorological Centre warned on Sunday that parts of the country would experience heavy to torrential rain, with Anhui receiving up to 250-350mm (10-14 inches) in a 24-hour period.

Provinces likely to be affected stretched from Fujian in the southeast to Liaoning in the northeast, taking in large parts of the east coast, including Zhejiang and Jiangxi, as well as Hebei in northern China.

Strong winds with sustained speeds of 75-88 km/h, and gusts reaching 89-117 km/h are expected in the Yangtze River estuary, along with coastal areas of central and southern Jiangsu, Shanghai, and Zhoushan in Zhejiang.

Local authorities in affected areas remain on high alert for flooding.

On Sunday afternoon, the Ministry of Water Resources issued a Level IV flood prevention alert – the lowest level – for Jiangsu, Anhui, Jiangxi and Shandong, with the heaviest rains expected over the next two days.

It said torrential rain was expected in several areas and significant rises in water levels were expected on major rivers, while smaller ones may also see dangerous levels of flooding.

So far no local authorities have reported serious casualties or economic damage, but many videos of severe flooding in Wenzhou, where Bavi made its landfall, have been circulating online.

Some of the footage showed rescuers using inflatable boats to evacuate residents after the ground floors of many houses were inundated by floodwaters.

Other videos showed that strong winds had caused serious damage to the windows in many residential buildings in Zhejiang and Fujian, leaving rooms in a mess.

Rescuers were also seen braving the rain and wind to clear fallen tree branches and clear roads.

In just one part of Wenzhou, Yueqing city, the winds had downed more than 1,300 trees, according to the China News Service.

More than 2.2 million people were evacuated in Zhejiang and nearly 200,000 in Fujian, along with thousands of others in the neighbouring provinces and Shanghai, according to state media.

Schools, work and outdoor activities have been suspended in the affected provinces.

As of Sunday morning, the authorities had not reported any casualties.

The National Marine Forecasting Centre issued a red alert – the highest level in a four-tier system – for high waves and an amber alert for storm and tidal surges on Saturday, warning local governments to stay alert to these risks.

According to CCTV, strong winds caused huge waves up to 8 metres (26 feet) high along Fujian’s coast.

But as Bavi headed north on Sunday, some areas began to lower their weather warnings.

Fujian’s provincial meteorological observatory downgraded its typhoon and rainstorm warning to Level IV. The provincial government also officially ended its flood response emergency preparations and services in the provincial capital Fuzhou resumed.

Zhejiang province also lowered its typhoon emergency response at noon and subway trains started running again in major cities such as Hangzhou, Ningbo and Shaoxing.

Bavi began as a super typhoon, battering Guam and the Northern Mariana Islands last Monday with wind speeds of 290km/h.

After sweeping through a chain of remote Japanese islands, it brought heavy rainfall to Taiwan.

No fatalities or severe injuries have been reported on the island so far, but 113 people were injured – mainly as a result of falling off motorbikes or bicycles due to strong winds and slippery roads, or from accidents while preparing for the typhoon.

The storm also knocked out power supplies to 234,481 households as of 8pm yesterday, according to local government reports.

Landslides triggered by the storm have also killed at least 17 people in the Philippines.

Parts of southern China are still reeling from the devastation brought by Typhoon Maysak earlier this week.

Maysak left at least 39 people dead and killed large numbers of livestock. It also caused two rare tornadoes in the landlocked central province of Hubei.
