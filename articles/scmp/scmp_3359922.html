Philippines gets economic upgrade, yet many Filipinos feel it’s no ‘big deal’

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 971 words | 2026-07-09

The World Bank recently upgraded the Philippines to an upper-middle-income country, but that classification makes little difference to many Filipinos grappling with rising cost of living, debt and stagnant wages.

Manila resident Ann Michelle Federez-Abato, 35, said her entire salary goes towards her parents’ mortgage, while she and her two-year-old daughter subsist on a monthly remittance of 20,000 pesos (US$324) from her husband, who works abroad as a production officer at a factory.

Much of the public high-school teacher’s money is spent on bills, utilities, groceries and transport, a situation she described as “not really financially comforting, but more of surviving” in Metro Manila – especially with electricity costs up 10 per cent and national inflation at 6.4 per cent.

“I laughed actually. Like, for real? Where did the data come from?” Federez-Abato said of the July 1 announcement that her country’s gross national income (GNI) per capita had reached US$4,850 in 2025, surpassing the US$4,636 cut-off for upper-middle-income economies.

“Filipinos are already at a huge loss with inflation, coupled with their daily expenses and with many sinking further into debt just to get by,” she said, reflecting on a milestone that took nearly 40 years to reach and does not match the nation’s economic reality.

For Hans Bautista, executive director of Inklusibo, a non-profit research centre advocating for the country’s impoverished sector, the World Bank’s upgrade “should not be considered a big deal” when millions of Filipinos are still struggling with “meagre wages, attacks on livelihoods and inaccessible basic necessities”.

“If we look at the bigger picture, the Philippines ranks 130 out of 201 countries, and is far from the global GNI average,” he said, adding that the category is used by the multilateral lender to determine a country’s eligibility to access more funding.

Bautista said the country’s inequality gap remained wide, with wealth distribution “highly skewed”.

Citing IBON Foundation, he said 44.1 per cent of Filipinos fell within the middle-income bracket, while middle- and low-income earners stood at 41.4 per cent. Only 0.2 per cent possessed a majority of the country’s wealth.

Bautista called on the government to re-evaluate the metrics used to measure income, including how food and non-food expenses were computed, suggesting officials use actual data rather than “crude estimates and minimum values” in the process.

He said that food variety and “dignified nutritional standards” should be incorporated when assessing food expenditure and consumption patterns.

Progress, not the destination

The apparent disconnect between the World Bank upgrade and the financial strain felt by many Filipinos lies in what the classification measures: national income averaged across the population, not whether households feel richer, more secure or closer to the middle class.

“It indicates that the country’s average income level has risen enough to meet the World Bank’s threshold for upper-middle-income economies,” said Ruben Carlo Asuncion, chief economist of the Union Bank of the Philippines.

Asuncion said the new label reflected years of sustained growth, increased productive capacity and improved economic resilience, signalling that the Philippines is “gradually moving up the development ladder and is better positioned to attract investment and create more economic opportunities”.

But the country-level measure “does not mean that most Filipinos are now middle class, nor does it guarantee” that the benefits of growth have been shared evenly across households, regions and sectors, he said.

“Upper-middle-income status is not our destination. It marks another stage in our development journey,” Department of Economy, Planning, and Development Secretary Arsenio Balisacan told reporters on Monday.

“Development is not measured by average income alone. It’s measured by whether growth creates quality jobs, increases productivity, expands opportunities, reduces poverty and improves the quality of life of our people.”

John Paolo Rivera, a senior research fellow at the Philippine Institute for Development Studies, said the new status was “an important milestone that reflects the country’s long-term economic progress”.

“It recognises that the Philippine economy has grown over time and that average incomes have improved,” Rivera said, adding that the next challenge is ensuring that the benefits of growth are broadly shared through better jobs, higher productivity and improved public services.

Rivera noted that the World Bank classification and the everyday experiences of many Filipinos are not contradictory, saying the “milestone should be viewed as an opportunity to accelerate reforms that make growth more inclusive”.

“In fact, macroeconomic stability and household welfare are complementary perspectives in understanding the Philippine economy. The classification is based on average national income, while household welfare depends on purchasing power, employment and the cost of living,” he said.

But despite the upgrade, he said many middle-class households that keep the economy humming “are only a few shocks away from financial stress because they carry housing, education, transport, health and debt obligations”.

“High inflation and weak purchasing power mean that many families are still vulnerable to falling back into poverty after a major shock,” Rivera said.

“They may not be poor but they are exposed,” he said, urging the government to strengthen the cohort’s purchasing power and resilience through quality jobs and affordable public services to help sustain the country’s upper-middle-income status.

According to Asuncion, the milestone was possible due to gains accumulated over many years. However, inflation, governance concerns and weaker investor sentiment can weigh on growth and affect how households and businesses perceive the economy in the near term, he said.

“These challenges remind us that income classification upgrades and current economic conditions are not always the same thing,” Asuncion said.

While the progress was “an achievement worth acknowledging”, he said it should be viewed as “a milestone rather than a destination”.

“The real challenge is ensuring that stronger economic performance translates into tangible improvements in the lives of ordinary Filipinos.”
