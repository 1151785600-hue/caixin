China accuses US of ignoring Trump, Xi rapport and targeting Chinese firms

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 565 words | 2026-07-11

China on Friday accused the United States of misusing its national power by blacklisting more than 60 Chinese companies, alleging that Washington has “disregarded the consensus” reached between US President Donald Trump and Chinese leader Xi Jinping in Beijing in May.

In early June, the Pentagon blacklisted dozens of Chinese companies – including Alibaba, Baidu and BYD – as “Chinese military companies operating in the United States”. Days later, China announced export controls against leading US rare earth firms.

“The US has disregarded the consensus reached by the leaders of China and the US during their meeting in Beijing,” the Chinese embassy in Washington said.

It also added that the Trump administration has “excessively expanded the concept of national security, misused state power and unjustifiably suppressed Chinese enterprises, seriously undermining their legitimate rights and interests”.

Sources familiar with the matter told the South China Morning Post that while the latest tit-for-tat actions are unhelpful, they are unlikely to adversely impact Xi’s likely visit to the United States in September.

Amid fragile stability, affected Chinese tech firms have taken legal steps to fight the Pentagon’s designation.

Last week, Alibaba secured a temporary relief from a California court allowing it to resume lobbying in the US, days after it sued the US Department of Defence, arguing that it was added to the Pentagon’s Section 1260G list without substantial evidence or explanation.

The court on Sunday blocked the Defence Department from enforcing a lobbying ban on Alibaba while it reviews the company’s constitutional challenge to the law.

“We are pleased that, for purposes of the lobbyist-contracting ban, Alibaba will not be treated as a Chinese military company and will have proper channels to communicate our views and address concerns,” a spokesperson for the company said on Monday.

Alibaba owns the South China Morning Post.

The Chinese embassy also defended Beijing’s recent export controls on American firms, calling it a direct response to the Pentagon’s “inappropriate practice of adding so-called Chinese military companies to its list”.

It maintained that the export ban on dual-use items was necessary to “safeguard national security and interests and fulfil international obligations such as non-proliferation”.

Last month, Beijing placed two American rare earth giants – MP Materials and USA Rare Earth – along with eight other American firms on its export control list and barred 46 US firms from government procurement.

The new rules prohibited exporters from supplying dual-use goods to the 10 firms. They extended the ban to any organisation or individual worldwide that transfers or provides China-origin dual-use items to those companies, highlighting the measure’s extraterritorial reach.

The reprisals marked one of the most serious escalations since Washington and Beijing agreed to a temporary truce in Busan last October, a move later reaffirmed by Trump and Xi at their summit in Beijing last month.

After the visit, a White House fact sheet said that China had agreed to “address US concerns” regarding supply chain shortages of rare earths and other critical minerals.

“China will also address US concerns regarding prohibitions or restrictions on the sale of rare earth production and processing equipment and technologies,” the fact sheet added.

China has a near-monopoly on rare earth processing, particularly heavy rare earths – a group of 17 critical elements used to produce the high-performance magnets found in electric vehicles, wind turbines, robotics and defence systems.
