Rail and road: the benefits of China’s full-system transport makeovers

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1214 words | 2026-07-16

I visited China after the pandemic. The thing that struck me was how different it sounded, and then, once I started paying attention, how different it moved. Every Didi I called in Shanghai was a different make. A BYD, then a Zeekr, then an Avatr I had never heard of. I started waiting for the app to reveal its hand, a small thrill each time, and got a quiet burst of joy whenever it was a Hongqi.

The variety of electric vehicle (EV) brands is in every first-tier Chinese city, but Shanghai is where it gets faintly ridiculous. The city is home to SAIC, one of China’s oldest state-owned carmakers, and to Tesla’s Shanghai Gigafactory. For years, its notoriously brutal licence-plate lottery has waved EV buyers straight through. The result is the highest EV adoption rate of any Chinese city, and a fleet to match: a different marque every ride.

Once, a sleek blue saloon slid past me, and I mistook it for a Porsche – the silhouette, the sloping roofline, the whole stance. A closer look and it was a Xiaomi SU7; Chinese social media got there before I did and nicknamed it “Mi Porsche”. When you think of a phone company building cars that look suspiciously like a six-figure German sports saloon, the mix-up says plenty about how far the design has come.

In Chengdu, Sichuan province, I walked into a swanky Huawei shop intending to browse phones and laptops. I found what I was expecting – and then full-sized electric cars on display, like an expensive impulse purchase by the till. This is uniquely Chinese: the brand that sold you your phone also sells you the thing you drive it around in.

How the cars got everywhere

In 2020, EVs made up about 6 per cent of new car sales in China. By 2026, that figure had climbed past 60 per cent.

This did not happen by accident. It was by design, through purchase subsidies, mandated sales quotas in big cities, free green licence plates in places where an ordinary plate is auctioned off for a small fortune and a charging network built ahead of demand rather than behind it.

China now has more than 12 million public charging points, more than the rest of the world combined, built mostly within the last decade. That solved the chicken-and-egg problem that has slowed EV adoption everywhere else: nobody buys an electric car if there’s nowhere to charge it, and nobody builds chargers for cars that don’t exist yet. China built both at once and let everyone else argue about which should have come first.

The other surprise was how far down the price ladder this goes. The Wuling Mini EV costs not much more than a decent scooter. Counties have run subsidy schemes for rural buyers and the result is farming towns where EVs are now charged outside vegetable markets. As someone who lives in Singapore, a country that has cornered the market on the world’s most expensive cars, a comparable car and kilowatt-hour cost a sum I cannot mention, for it gives me a genuine heartache just thinking about it.

The railway that keeps on going

If the EVs crept up gradually, the trains did something more dramatic: they opened doors to places I’d wanted to reach for years that simply had not been accessible in any sensible amount of time.

Jiuzhaigou, in Sichuan province, was the first. The only way in used to be a road journey of about 10 hours from Chengdu, through mountain passes, or a flight. The Huanglong-Jiuzhaigou line opened in 2024, making the journey to the legendary turquoise lakes from Chengdu East station less than two hours.

Yanan was next, and it carries its own piece of history. This is the town in the loess hills of Shaanxi province where Mao Zedong and the Communist Party regrouped after the Long March, governing from a network of cave dwellings for over a decade before taking the rest of the country. The American journalist Edgar Snow made the same journey in 1936, and it took him nearly two days by truck. The high-speed line that opened in 2025 does it in about an hour, which finally makes Yanan’s revolutionary sites, the caves, the museums – the whole “red tourism” itinerary – something you can see and do without needing the constitution of a 1930s war correspondent.

Then there was Xingping, in the Guangxi Zhuang autonomous region, and the cormorant fishermen I’d always wanted to get up close and personal with. I did it via a direct high-speed line from Guangzhou South to Guilin. The line’s Yangshuo stop, despite the name, is in Xingping itself, so I went straight from the train to the riverbank, to the exact scene I’d always wanted to capture.

This is a part of China’s rail network that doesn’t get talked about as much as the headline speeds: reaching into places previously cut off by geography rather than by lack of ambition. The whole system runs on the backbone of trunk lines, eight running broadly north to south and another eight running broadly east to west, with regional lines branching off the spine to feed smaller cities. The trains keep getting faster regardless; the CR450 has already hit 400km/h, making it the fastest on the planet.

The seat that puts business class to shame

China’s high-speed trains run four classes: second, first, a premium first on newer routes, and business, the one worth knowing about. On the newest Fuxing trains, business-class seats sit two and one across the aisle, with two metres of legroom, and each reclines fully flat. It is, in essence, a bed. You get a personal screen, a reading light, slippers and, on longer journeys, an attendant who treats the cabin the way a flight attendant might treat first class.

I have sat in first class on the Shinkansen in Japan and on the high-speed lines in Europe, and neither comes near a flatbed for the price. Japan gives you a wider seat and an admirably straight spine. Europe gives you a bit more elbow room and a quieter carriage. Neither lets you lie down, and the fare for trying is nothing like the modest premium business class commands here, where it runs at two to four times the price of second class, not the eye-watering sums you’d hand over to go flat on a flight.

Two systems, one habit of mind

What connects the trains and the cars isn’t just the technology, but the willingness to build the whole system at once. Decide where the chargers go before everyone owns an EV. Decide where the railway goes before the towns at the end of it have grown into the decision. Decide that business class ought to put everyone else’s first class to shame, and sort out the pricing later.

None of it announces itself. You only really notice when the sheer geographic cheek of the whole thing catches up with you all at once when you come back.

Lu-Ann Ong’s forthcoming book, Experience China: What Maps Don’t Tell, explores contemporary China through landscape, cuisine, history and memory.
