Hong Kong doctor issued over 3,700 fraudulent Covid vaccine exemptions, court hears

SCMP | FREE | 497 words | 2026-07-08

A Hong Kong doctor fraudulently issued more than 3,700 Covid-19 vaccination exemption certificates over seven months during the pandemic, allowing some residents to avoid inoculation on the grounds of skin allergies, chest pain and other conditions, a court has heard.

Dr Tai Kong-shing, 80, stood trial at the District Court on Monday over allegations that he generated the certificates en masse for personal gain.

Prosecutors said the Western medical practitioner failed to make a proper diagnosis before issuing 3,713 notices via the government’s electronic health record system at his clinic between February 10 and September 20, 2022.

They further accused Tai of laundering HK$10.96 million (US$1.39 million) in cash and bank deposits, which represented in whole or in part the proceeds of the fraudulent scheme.

The doctor denied one count of accessing a computer with dishonest intent, 13 alternative counts of the same offence, and three counts of money laundering.

Merchant Kriselle Young, 61, also stood trial on a count of accessing a computer with dishonest intent.

She allegedly introduced patients to Tai and gave instructions to him and his nurses regarding the amount of consultation fees to be charged and ways to expedite the certification process.

Police arrested six doctors and put a seventh on a wanted list in September 2022, accusing them of failing to observe the Department of Health’s guidelines when they issued more than 20,000 vaccination exemption certificates, more than half of all valid exemptions at the time.

The certificates were part of the government’s vaccine pass scheme to boost the inoculation rate by limiting the mobility of those who were unvaccinated. The scheme ran from February to December 2022.

Those granted exemptions were allowed to enter restricted premises such as restaurants, supermarkets and shopping malls. The exemption periods ranged from 90 to 180 days.

Tai was arrested on September 20 that year after three undercover officers were deployed to his clinic at Bright Growth Medical Centre in Yau Ma Tei to gather evidence.

Two of the officers were granted exemptions after claiming they either feared vaccination or felt unwell upon receiving the first dose. One of them also got a waiver for her purported son, who did not attend the consultation session.

A pile of consultation notes seized from Tai during a body search led police to 12 patients who allegedly benefited from the scheme.

Prosecutors said those patients secured 180-day exemptions at prices ranging between HK$1,000 and HK$4,000 without providing valid reasons, citing conditions such as skin allergies, chest pain, respiratory issues, acid reflux and malfunction in the lymphatic system.

One patient received a certificate after stating she experienced an increased heart rate due to a habit of smoking and drinking coffee 10 years earlier, the court heard.

Although police were unable to persuade two of the 12 patients to give live evidence, prosecutors argued that the court could still draw the irresistible inference that Tai had instructed his staff to grant the exemptions to patients regardless of their ineligibility.

The trial before Judge Ernest Michael Lin Kam-hung is scheduled to last 20 days.
