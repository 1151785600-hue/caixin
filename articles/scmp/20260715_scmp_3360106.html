Hong Kong proposes job-related injury compensation for gig delivery workers

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 902 words | 2026-07-15

Hong Kong’s army of gig workers delivering food and goods will be entitled to compensation for job-related injuries under proposed legislation aimed at improving labour protection for those working for digital platforms.

According to a paper by the Labour and Welfare Bureau, the proposals will cover gig workers who sustain injuries or die “as a result of an accident arising out of and in the course of performing platform work”.

Occupational diseases would not be covered, the paper said.

The proposed compensation mechanism would apply only to those in food and goods delivery services, as these two sectors employed most gig workers in Hong Kong, the bureau said.

In cases of injury, gig workers would be entitled to payments for sick leave, medical expenses, compensation for permanent incapacity, and the cost of surgical appliances.

In fatal cases, compensation would cover funeral and medical attendance expenses, as well as death compensation.

“In the calculation of the amount of compensation for death and compensation for permanent total incapacity, the minimum amounts of compensation – HK$514,510 and HK$584,220, respectively – and the monthly earnings ceiling – HK$38,670 – under [the Employees’ Compensation Ordinance] are applicable to compensation payable to platform workers,” said the paper, to be tabled for discussion at a meeting of the Legislative Council’s manpower panel next Friday.

“This will ensure that platform workers who suffer from permanent total or partial incapacity, or die as a result of a work accident, are entitled to compensation comparable to those of employees.”

The legislation would cover workers from when they set off for the designated location to pick up the food or goods until delivery at the designated destination, the bureau said.

It would also cover the period when workers were on their way back to the vehicle used after delivering food or goods, or a specified period of time in the event of order cancellation, the paper added.

Platform workers could also claim compensation for injuries sustained in an accident while commuting home within four hours of finishing work if the No 8 typhoon signal or higher, or a red or black rainstorm warning signal, was in force at the time of the accident.

The bureau also said the government would adopt a “no-fault” principle, stressing that “the liability to pay compensation by a platform operator will not be exempted under normal circumstances, even if a platform worker is found to have negligence or to have breached any legislation at the time of the work accident”.

The operator would bear no responsibility, however, if there was proof that the worker’s injury was “deliberately self-inflicted”, or that the worker was under the influence of drugs or alcohol at the time of the accident, the paper said.

Under the proposals, a platform operator would have to notify the Labour Department of its operations. The department will compile a list for public reference.

But the bureau stressed: “For the avoidance of doubt, whether or not platform operators have notified [the department] … as required does not affect their obligations to comply with the legislation.”

The proposals follow an announcement by Chief Executive John Lee Ka-chiu in his policy address last year to improve the legal framework for work injury compensation for digital platform workers engaged in food and goods delivery services.

Hong Kong had an estimated 114,000 delivery couriers and ride-hailing drivers in 2023, accounting for around 3 per cent of local employment, according to a paper prepared by the Legco secretariat for lawmakers last year.

In general, they are classified as self-employed and are not entitled to employee benefits such as paid annual leave and sick leave, compensation for work-related injuries and death, or minimum wage protection.

In mainland China, official directives provide basic protections to gig workers, including minimum wage standards and work-related injury insurance.

Labour rights advocate and former legislator Frankie Ngan Man-yu hailed the Hong Kong government’s move as “a historic step”.

“The government is breaking away from the traditional framework and establishing a new legal status for gig workers to protect their basic labour rights,” he said.

But he urged the government to set up a licensing system for operators, rather than compiling a list of such operators under the legislation. There should also be an official register for platform workers, Ngan added.

He said the law should be further expanded in the future to cover ride-hailing drivers, carers and pet sitters.

Unionist legislator Dennis Leung Tsz-wing shared similar views but agreed with the government’s “step-by-step” approach to deal with work injury compensation first.

“It fills the gaps in current laws and policies and enhances the safety net for employee protection,” Leung said.

A spokesman for Foodpanda, a leading food and grocery delivery platform, said it had “always been committed to the rights and welfare of platform workers, as well as the development of the relevant legislative framework”.

It said it would “continue to maintain constructive conversations with the government and various stakeholders, working together to explore a long-term framework solution that best suits the Hong Kong market, one that balances the protection needs of platform workers and the sustainable development of the platform economy”.

The government hopes to introduce the legislative proposals into Legco this year.

The South China Morning Post has approached the other major food delivery platform in Hong Kong, Keeta, a unit of mainland giant Meituan, for comment.
