Why did only 2 Hong Kong developers put up their hands for Northern Metropolis project?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 2025 words | 2026-07-11

Stretches of green farmland and shimmering ponds, winding village roads and sprawling brownfield logistics yards all lend Hung Shui Kiu in the New Territories a patchwork look of rustic Hong Kong standing in stark contrast to its glistening skyscrapers.

This rural landscape is slated for radical transformation under the government’s ambitious Northern Metropolis blueprint and is primed to be reinvented as a modernised logistics hub in the near future.

However, the premise of this transformation relies heavily on market buy-in. The project faced its first litmus test with the closing of the site tender last Friday, which was also the government’s first new large-scale land disposal tender.

The tender has defied conservative expectations by drawing two submissions, comprising one solo bid and one massive joint venture.

“Even with only one bid, the government will treat it as a success,” said Hannah Jeong, head of valuation and advisory services at CBRE Hong Kong.

Others have hailed the turnout as a success given the current property downturn and noted that large consortiums made up of local and mainland Chinese firms would become the norm for Northern Metropolis developments due to the sheer scale and complexity of these multi-use plots.

This outcome, they said, had also set a reassuring precedent for the government’s coming mega-tenders, while providing a vital blueprint that would bolster investor confidence as future development parcels were rolled out in San Tin and Fanling North.

The results raise questions about whether momentum can be sustained for future tenders and what permutations may work well to optimise development. Will more players be encouraged to take on the risk or will they continue to hold back? Will the government adapt the terms of future tenders based on the recent outcome? Analysts say these are issues that will decide the development of the megaproject.

No market precedent

The Hung Shui Kiu pilot site marked the first land sale under the government’s new large-scale land disposal model for the Northern Metropolis. The authorities’ blueprint is to transform 30,000 hectares (74,130 acres) of land near the mainland border into an economic and residential hub.

As part of the first batch of the most construction-ready land within the megaproject, the roughly 11-hectare pilot site is designed for double duty, boosting the city’s housing supply while anchoring innovation and technology (I&T) industries right at the border.

Beyond this specific tender, the wider Hung Shui Kiu area is being strategically positioned as a high-end professional services and logistics hub. The master blueprint features dedicated zones for commerce, recreation and conservation, alongside a newly announced roughly 22-hectare university town area.

The cluster as a whole is designed to cultivate a mature, self-sustaining community by seamlessly connecting different sites within the megaproject.

The Northern Metropolis has been touted repeatedly as a top priority for the current administration, capped most recently by a visit by Xia Baolong, director of the Hong Kong and Macau Affairs Office (HKMAO), who underscored Beijing’s call to accelerate construction.

The initiative serves as a critical strategic test of whether Hong Kong can successfully reinvent its role within the country’s broader I&T landscape. City leader John Lee Ka-chiu is in the midst of a large-scale consultation exercise to draft Hong Kong’s own five-year plan that will align with national development, specifically Beijing’s blueprint covering 2026 to 2030.

Hence, last week’s tender was closely watched as a test of whether the government can persuade property companies to take on a broader role in delivering its economic development agenda.

“The central government was watching how the developers play their part,” an establishment source told the South China Morning Post, without elaborating.

The 10.5-hectare site under the tender comprises three residential plots and three enterprise and technology park sites, with capacity for about 3,120 homes and 280,000 square metres (3.01 million sq ft) of industrial floor space.

Unlike traditional land sales, bids are assessed under a two-envelope system, with 30 per cent weighting given to the land premium offered and 70 per cent to non-price factors, including proposals for technology park development and plans to attract strategic industries, investment and jobs.

Henderson Land Development submitted a stand-alone bid, while Sino Land partnered with five companies – China Merchants Land, China Resources Land (Overseas), China Overseas Land & Investment, CTG Investment and Chinese e-commerce firm JD.com.

Other major developers, including Sun Hung Kai Properties, New World Development and CK Asset Holdings, did not take part.

Against the backdrop of a sluggish economic recovery, industry observers had low expectations for the tender before the closing deadline, the SCMP learned.

Jeong, of CBRE Hong Kong, said the firm had expected all along a maximum of two bidders for the plot of land, adding that limited commercial feasibility and operational difficulties acted as the key deterrents for potential bidders.

“The three land sites in Hung Shui Kiu are either triangular or irregularly shaped,” she said, noting that these irregular layouts posed development challenges for firms.

She said that a major “operator gap” existed in meeting the enterprise and technology (E&T) requirements, noting that residential developers were struggling to identify suitable partners with a proven track record and the financial strength to meet the government’s stringent criteria, especially since the tender required the development of a site spanning about 10,190 square metres.

She added that overall returns became marginal once potential E&T losses were factored in, meaning the risk-adjusted return simply did not justify the massive capital and time commitment required.

Industry analysts and government officials viewed the final turnout of only two bidders as a clear success.

“Two bids for a project of this complexity and scale should be viewed as a qualified success, not a disappointment,” Vincent Cheung, managing director at Vincorn Consulting and Appraisal, said, noting that the pilot area was the first of its kind under an entirely new large-scale land disposal framework.

“There is no market precedent, no comparable benchmark transaction.”

He said the formation of a multiparty consortium bid showed that “the pairing of mainland state-owned enterprise capital, local developer execution capability and industrial anchors has now been validated as a workable structure”.

“It [the consortium bid] reflects a calibrated, rather than fearful, risk posture … in an environment of elevated interest rates and subdued residential sentiment, aggregating complementary competencies across housing, technology and logistics is a highly sophisticated response,” he said.

He added that rather than a sign of corporate weakness, multiparty risk distribution was a rational way to meet the government’s complex scoring criteria.

Conversely, he said, Henderson Land’s decision to bid unilaterally was strategically consistent with its position as one of the largest private landowners in the New Territories.

“This allows the developer to maximise the government’s premium offset mechanisms by leveraging its massive local land bank,” he said.

Cheung said that the broader outcome provided a constructive, if cautious, signal that the Northern Metropolis framework could successfully attract well-resourced consortiums.

Commenting a day after the tender closed, Secretary for Development Bernadette Linn Hon-ho said that unlike previous stand-alone residential or commercial land tenders, the new Northern Metropolis model had achieved the government’s goal of pairing traditional developers with industrial operators.

She said she was particularly pleased that the bids differed from past tenders for residential or commercial sites, which often attracted “homogeneous” bidders.

Government as matchmaker

However, asked whether the tender results had met Beijing’s expectations, a source said that the business sector had not faced much pressure as it was a free market and companies simply bid after calculating costs to ensure profitability.

The source added that the government was likely to have faced far more pressure than the private sector and authorities had been acting as matchmakers behind the scenes.

“They provided developers with lists of mainland artificial intelligence companies that have expressed interest in partnering on the project,” he said.

However, he said that because many of these tech firms were not household names and remained unfamiliar to the local commercial property sector, developers needed more time to assess them.

“The developers simply need more time to vet and get comfortable with these prospective tech partners,” the insider said.

He said that several major developers had conducted site visits and expressed initial interest in bidding, but many hit a bottleneck during discussions to create joint ventures.

He said that joint ventures with unfamiliar AI firms involved massive capital distribution, complex profit-sharing models and intricate legal agreements, processes that some consortiums simply could not finalise before the tight deadline.

He noted that some mainland AI firms were equally unfamiliar with Hong Kong’s large-scale land disposal frameworks, so many had adopted a “wait-and-see” approach, choosing to observe how this initial tender performed before committing to future tenders in the region.

Economist Simon Lee Siu-po said it was difficult for traditional developers to bid alone, adding that forming consortiums helped share the risk, especially for megaprojects.

He noted that back in the 2000s, several property developers tried to diversify and transform their businesses but ultimately failed.

“It is very difficult to get developers to pivot into industrial sectors because they are so accustomed to the traditional model of buying land, building properties and selling them,” he said, noting that developers were only now beginning to explore new business models.

Only the big boys will play?

Looking ahead to Fanling North and San Tin, the other two pilot areas earmarked for large-scale land disposal, the market expected the government to roll out the former first, given its more mature ancillary infrastructure.

“The objective conditions in San Tin are simply not mature enough yet,” another insider said, pointing out that critical transport infrastructure, such as some of the nearby proposed Northern Link railway stations, would not be ready until at least 2032.

He added that this prolonged infrastructure lag was expected to temporarily dampen developers’ appetite for bidding on San Tin plots.

The source said he expected the government would closely monitor the market’s response to the Hung Shui Kiu results and work to modify future tendering terms to better align with developer interest and financial realities.

“It was a highly, highly technical bid. Small players won’t have the appetite not just for risk but complexity; so it’s up to the big players to do their part,” the source said.

Kathy Lee, head of research and retail consultancy at Colliers, said the tender had successfully forced a structural evolution, drawing out cross-border joint ventures that were capable of executing long-term ecosystem building rather than conventional land banking.

Another source also warned that the market should brace for relatively few bidders, citing a total of fewer than five in the coming Northern Metropolis land sales, noting that the new tendering model represented a radical shift from traditional practices.

He added that historically, developers were only required to bid for land to build and sell flats. However, the current framework introduced a new, industry-driven style that bundles residential plots with information technology and industrial land, requiring property firms to partner directly with industrial operators.

“The industry needs time to adapt to this new model,” the source said.

The insider added that the sheer scale of these multi-use sites made it incredibly difficult for small to medium-sized developers to participate.

“These developers typically lack the immense capital required for such long-term, large-scale projects,” the source said, noting that the payback period for advanced industrial hubs was significantly longer compared with traditional residential developments, where profits were realised much faster through flat sales.

While forming consortiums offered a potential avenue for smaller developers to enter the Northern Metropolis market, the source noted they were likely to have very little say in the decision-making process.

As a result, smaller players were expected to pivot back to traditional investments such as urban redevelopment projects, where the development cycle was familiar and they could maintain full control over the asset.

“Only the major conglomerates can afford to enter the game now,” he said.
