Markets, not politics, will make China’s yuan a global currency

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 920 words | 2026-07-14

The yuan’s next global push will fall short if it is treated as persuasion. The test is not whether Beijing wants an international currency or whether Hong Kong can host summits around it. It is whether a treasurer in Jakarta, a bank in Riyadh or a Gulf central bank can fund, hedge, invest and exit yuan positions at a predictable cost. Until then, yuan internationalisation will look stronger in policy papers than on balance sheets.

Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA) Chief Executive Eddie Yue Wai-man is right to put yuan internationalisation at the centre of Hong Kong’s financial agenda and to highlight liquidity and direct conversion costs. These are not technical details. They are the language of market doubt, asking why the yuan can be used to settle invoices yet still be avoided in financing, reserves and risk management.

The progress is real, but so are the limits. Hong Kong’s yuan deposits reached 1.13 trillion yuan (US$166.3 billion) at the end of May, with monthly cross-border trade settlement remittances above 1.12 trillion yuan. In May, however, the yuan accounted for only 2.75 per cent of global payments by value on the Swift international payment system, while the US dollar took 50.73 per cent.

In trade finance, the yuan’s 7 per cent share looked stronger, but the US dollar still commanded 82.5 per cent. International Monetary Fund data put the yuan at just 1.99 per cent of global foreign exchange reserves in the first quarter. The yuan is growing, but it is not yet indispensable.

Settlement is not currency power. A true international currency finances projects, backs collateral, anchors hedging markets and sits in reserves without unnerving investors. The yuan has entered the first category, but it is still fighting for the second.

The problem begins with offshore liquidity. A company can receive yuan, but it must also borrow it, roll it over and hedge it when markets tighten. If offshore yuan funding becomes unstable or more expensive than US dollar funding, corporate loyalty disappears. Treasurers do not make geopolitical statements with working capital – they choose the cheapest reliable instrument.

Hong Kong has moved in the right direction. The HKMA’s decision to increase its RMB Business Facility to 500 billion yuan gives banks a steadier, lower-cost funding channel. However, a facility is not a market. Hong Kong’s larger task is to build a full offshore yuan funding curve: longer-dated instruments, reliable term liquidity and bank balance sheets to support trade finance and supply chain credit.

The next friction is direct conversion. Too many yuan transactions with Asian and Middle Eastern currencies still face thin liquidity, wide spreads and incomplete hedging markets. Some trades still pass through the US dollar. If a Saudi, Malaysian or Indonesian firm must route exposure through the dollar to manage yuan risk, the yuan is operating inside the dollar system, not reducing reliance.

Hong Kong should judge success less by yuan-dollar activity and more by whether other markets become deep enough for daily treasury use. That requires market makers, transparent pricing, forwards, swaps and options. The recent agreement between the HKMA, People’s Bank of China and Bank Indonesia on cooperation on offshore yuan and rupiah use is the right practical experiment. It should become a template, not an exception.

The asset side is just as important. Firms holding yuan need a credible home. Banks need to deploy it. Fund managers need liquid exits. Central banks need assets usable in stress. The question is whether yuan bonds can become collateral.

Investors need a reliable yield curve, active secondary trading, efficient custody, clear tax treatment and the ability to reuse bonds in financing and margin operations. Otherwise, yuan bonds remain products, not the plumbing of a currency system. The priority is not another symbolic offshore bond sale. It is a market where yuan can be borrowed, hedged, pledged and exited at predictable cost.

The deepest constraint is trust. Investors care about returns, but they care more about exit. They want stable rules, credible legal recourse, capital mobility, hedges that work under stress and confidence that policy will not suddenly rewrite a trade. The more international the yuan becomes, the less China can expect every major financial risk to be domestically managed.

This is Hong Kong’s value. It speaks the language of global capital: contracts, disclosure, custody, enforceability, collateral, risk management and exit. Hong Kong handles about three-quarters of offshore yuan payment traffic, but dominance can become complacency. Singapore, London and Gulf centres also chase flows linked to Asian trade, wealth and energy. Hong Kong must be the architect of the yuan ecosystem, not just the gateway through which transactions pass.

Geopolitics gives the yuan an opening, but not a free pass. Many emerging economies want US dollar alternatives, yet dissatisfaction with the dollar does not equal confidence in the yuan. Markets move when the alternative becomes cheaper, safer and easier to use. The yuan does not need to defeat the dollar to matter; it needs to become a serious regional currency for trade, financing and reserves across Asia, the Middle East and parts of the Global South.

The immediate agenda is simple: reduce the cost of using the yuan. The cost of funding, conversion, hedging, collateral and exit matter more than official enthusiasm. If Hong Kong can lower those frictions, the yuan’s rise will be decided by market adoption. If it cannot, headline numbers might improve while the currency remains peripheral where it matters most: in balance sheets, reserves and risk systems.
