In China’s electronics hub, a memory chip crisis is hitting consumers hard

SCMP | FREE | 489 words | 2026-07-15

For Cai, a trader in Shenzhen’s vast Huaqiangbei electronics hub, the business of assembling computers for gamers and corporate clients has suddenly become dramatically more expensive.

The price of memory products in Huaqiangbei has tripled over the past year amid the global artificial intelligence boom – and the spike has hit the world’s largest wholesale electronics market hard.

“Right now, memory and SSDs are the biggest cost drivers in a personal computer build,” Cai said, referring to solid-state drives – devices that can store data on a computer at high speed.

The memory supply squeeze is forcing vendors in Huaqiangbei to introduce significant price increases, which are putting off buyers, according to Cai. Only customers with an urgent need for a computer are currently willing to pay the premium, he said.

To make matters worse, Cai said he expected prices to climb even higher over the coming months.

Other traders echoed that view. Inside SEG Plaza, the skyscraper at the heart of Huaqiangbei, a vendor surnamed Ye said memory prices had in some cases risen fivefold in the past 12 months.

“The prices just kept rising since last year, and will continue to climb,” Ye said. “Those who stockpiled last year were able to make a big fortune.”

Record-high contract prices mean customers from consumer markets, such as PCs and smartphones, are reaching their affordability limit

TrendForce

The scale of the surge is laid bare on Ye’s own price sheets. On Tuesday, he was selling a Kingston DDR5 – a high-performance memory product known as “double data rate 5” – kit for 2,980 yuan (US$438), five times the price he was charging last September.

Prices for the vast majority of mainstream memory kits have surged three- to fivefold over the past year, while SSDs have doubled or tripled over the same period, according to Ye’s records.

The upheaval in Huaqiangbei is being driven by skyrocketing demand from the AI industry, as global tech giants place vast orders for memory products to build servers and data centres.

As memory chipmakers prioritise servicing these heavyweight clients, the supply of conventional memory for consumer electronics is undergoing a severe squeeze. That is forcing computer and smartphone makers worldwide to raise their retail prices to absorb the rising memory and storage costs.

For consumers, the good news is that the price rises are expected to slow down – though not stop – in the second half of the year.

Contract prices for DRAM – a common memory product – are projected to rise by 13 to 18 per cent in the third quarter, compared with 58 to 63 per cent in the second quarter, according to a report by TrendForce released last week. NAND flash memory prices are expected to increase by 10 to 15 per cent, down from 60 per cent.

“Record-high contract prices mean customers from consumer markets, such as PCs and smartphones, are reaching their affordability limit, leading to more moderate price increases in the third quarter,” the report said.
