Could Canada use Chinese electric vehicle firms to reboot its auto manufacturing?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 925 words | 2026-07-11

Following a January deal on Chinese electric vehicles (EVs) makers’ imports into Canada, there was a rush to buy Polestar EVs at an Oakville showroom in Canada’s Ontario province. But potential buyers say there are now too few Chinese EVs in the Canadian market.

During Prime Minister Mark Carney’s visit to Beijing earlier this year, Canada agreed to allow 49,000 Chinese EV imports annually at a 6.1 per cent tariff rate. But potential Canadian buyers are now gauging how long it will take the policy to become a reality, and a lot depends on Canada’s tightly-controlled import licensing.

“We got about 40 off-lease Polestar 2s that sold very quickly, with the attractive price point of these pre-owned EVs,” said Jeevin Pal, a product specialist at the showroom in Ontario. “We’re expecting another set in October, and I think they’ll go quickly too; the cars are very popular.”

An import permit from the Canadian foreign ministry is required to bring in a Chinese EV, and permits are issued based on the annual quota.

“Once quota is reached, permits will no longer be issued,” a Canadian customs notice read on March 1.

An increase is not likely until 2030, when the import ceiling is set to be raised to 70,000 vehicles per year.

As a result, only a limited number of Chinese EVs have so far come into Canada under the deal, leaving a growing list of keen potential buyers. The biggest beneficiary to the quota has been US EV maker Tesla, which shipped a line-up of its cheaper, Chinese-made models to Canada.

Polestar and Lotus Technology, two Geely subsidiaries that already had a footprint in Canada, have also brought in some cars under the new quota. Other Chinese EV giants are slowly unfurling their Canadian plans.

BYD, the world’s biggest EV-maker, is reportedly setting up dealerships in some major Canadian cities, with its first cars expected to arrive in the third quarter of the year.

Chery Automobile will introduce two of its models into the Canadian market in early 2027, with Xpeng and Nio entering the market by the end of that year. ChinaEV.Ca, a website dedicated to tracking the arrival and sales of Chinese EVs in Canada, has projected a Xiaomi entry in 2027 or 2028.

When asked about Xiaomi’s overseas expansion plans, a spokeswoman said Xiaomi EV expected to enter Europe in 2027, without commenting on other markets.

While import volumes are small under the current arrangement, both the Canadian government and EV firms appear to see it as a first step toward setting up manufacturing plants.

For Chinese EV giants, this is a valuable opportunity in the North American market. Meanwhile, the Canadian government sees potential to reboot its domestic car manufacturing industry after some carmakers shelved expansion plans or closed plants under tariff pressure from US President Donald Trump.

During a mid-June visit to China, Canada’s Minister of Industry Melanie Joly met with top executives at BYD, Chery, Geely and Shanghai Launch Automotive Technology.

Their talks centred on the potential for Chinese companies to set up manufacturing plants in Canada.

Operations must be joint ventures, majority-owned by Canadians, align with domestic labour standards, use local parts and ensure vehicle data is secure with no backdoors to China, according to reports following Joly’s visit on the terms specified by Canada.

The companies must “build where they sell”, Joly said, adding that the government would aim to create more domestic jobs and build new skills for its autoworkers. Meanwhile, cheaper and more technologically advanced vehicles would enter the Canadian market.

“The issue is not only whether working with Chinese carmakers can preserve employment and provide Canadians with more affordable, technologically advanced vehicles,” said Vina Nadjibulla, vice-president for research and strategy at the Asia-Pacific Foundation Canada in an analysis published on the organisation’s website in June 17.

“It is whether Chinese participation in Canadian production would strengthen Canada’s ability to design and produce the technologies at the heart of the next automotive economy, or leave the country assembling vehicles whose most valuable systems, intellectual property and supply chains remain controlled elsewhere.”

Canada’s biggest trading partner is the US, where most Canadian-made cars are shipped, which has placed restrictions on Chinese EVs.

Canada must decide whether to have Chinese EV make products strictly for its domestic market or reach an agreement that ultimately allows the cars to be imported into the US and elsewhere abroad.

At a meeting to discuss the future of the US-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) on July 1, the US said it will not be renewing the pact. Annual reviews by signatories could lead to its winding down within a decade.

“Canada-China engagement complicates the [USMCA] renegotiation,” said Alejandro Reyes, a senior fellow at the Centre on Contemporary China and the World, at the University of Hong Kong.

“Washington’s core concern is that Canadian production could become a back door for Chinese content to access the US market tariff-free.”

Imports of Chinese EVs to Canada are likely to come in a slow trickle, and the waiting list of buyers will likely grow.

Some potential buyers may also be discouraged because any rebates only apply to vehicles made locally or in a free trade area.

“I thought I was just going to walk into a showroom and drive out with a new Chinese EV,” said Osas Samuel, a Toronto resident who has joined a waiting list at a showroom. “Now it turns out it is a long wait.”
