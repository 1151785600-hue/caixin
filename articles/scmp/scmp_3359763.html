Is Johor ready for new power balance? Malaysia’s Loke implores voters to curb BN dominance

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1253 words | 2026-07-08

Malaysian Transport Minister Anthony Loke says success in Johor’s state polls will be measured by how many seats can be clawed away from Barisan Nasional (BN), imploring voters to seek a better power balance if they want a surer footing with Singapore, the economic powerhouse across the causeway that has soaked up its workers and industry.

Loke leads the Chinese-majority Democratic Action Party (DAP), a small but punchy partner in Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s Pakatan Harapan (PH) coalition that broke the Malay-led BN’s decades-long grip on Malaysian politics.

Crucial state polls on Saturday in Johor, where BN holds 40 of the 56 seats, may offer flickers of hope for a return to national power for the once all-conquering Malay umbrella party, which has grown increasingly restless with its own junior partner status inside Anwar’s government.

Loke wants to ensure BN does not have it all its way on Saturday.

His DAP is contesting 17 of the state’s seats hoping a message about returning quality jobs, investment and industry from Singapore may cut through in the Malay bastion.

“We know it is a tall order for Pakatan Harapan to form the state government,” Loke told This Week in Asia in an exclusive interview in the MPs’ lounge at Malaysia’s parliament in Kuala Lumpur.

“But we need to have some sort of balance of power in Johor, and to ensure that Johor will not return to the era of one-party dominance like in the past.”

To get there, he is urging voters to flood the polls and defy fears of low turnout that helped BN candidates in 2022.

A good result for DAP on Saturday would be to keep the 10 legislators it won in 2022, said Loke, 49, who holds the key transport portfolio and is one of PH’s more visible and adroit operators, adding “any gain would be a bonus”.

Causeway blues

Johor, long regarded as the birthplace and fortress of Umno, the dominant Malay party in BN, is being closely watched as a test of whether Malaysia’s post-2022 national power-sharing model can survive fierce competition at state level.

BN and PH are contesting all seats, turning the election into a shoot-out between the two frontline forces in Malaysia’s complex and fragmented political scene.

A third force is also in the mix: the opposition bloc Perikatan Nasional (PN) which is fighting for 33 of those seats, according to the Election Commission, with the potential to eat into BN’s share from the conservative right of politics.

Loke says the state badly needs power to be shared to stem the bleeding of Johor jobs, skilled workers, industry and investment to Singapore. “Over the past many decades, sometimes due to policies, sometimes due to our lack of competitiveness, we have been losing a lot to Singapore.”

A stronger PH presence in the state assembly would be “the best scenario for Johoreans”, he said.

With many of Johor’s biggest development projects falling under federal authority, a strong state presence could translate into greater leverage with the national government, Loke said, citing big-ticket transport projects that would benefit from close links to stable government in Putrajaya.

The Johor Bahru-Singapore RTS Link, the Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone, rail upgrades and proposed transit projects all depend heavily on federal ministries, from transport to home affairs.

PH’s Johor manifesto, launched last week under the theme Johor Untuk Semua (“Johor for All”), pitches the state as the future “Shenzhen of Southeast Asia”.

That is a reference to the mainland Chinese city that grew from a border town near Hong Kong into one of the world’s most important technology and manufacturing hubs.

In its election vision, PH has promised Johor 250,000 high-paying jobs, a 30 per cent rise in median wages, 80,000 affordable homes, health coverage and measures to halve waiting times at Malaysia-Singapore checkpoints.

The improvements should seduce people into returning, and PH has launched a Balik Johor programme to bring home Johorean professionals working overseas and especially Singapore, where tens of thousands cross each day for work.

“A lot of industries and a lot of sectors and businesses actually can be fully utilised and can be held in Malaysia,” Loke said.

‘’But somehow we have been losing a lot, manufacturing, the service sector, shipping, even bunkering activities are all held in Singapore.”

Federal friends, Johor rivals

Saturday’s election has put Loke and DAP in a bind.

BN is both a governing partner in Putrajaya and the main obstacle to PH in Johor.

Loke said the situation was “not ideal” – a function of the awkward coalition glued together by Anwar after the 2022 general election failed to produce a clear winner.

Johor’s BN-led government dissolved the assembly last month, steering the timing of the contest.

Then its leaders swiftly put open water between BN state allegiance and the national government – in which it serves.

“Once the state assembly is dissolved, and the state Barisan Nasional has taken a position where they do not want to talk about cooperation, they want to contest on their own, then what choice do we have?” Loke said. “We have to put up a challenge.”

Joining Anwar’s coalition of rivals has not been all smooth sailing.

DAP has had to navigate thorny issues that once defined its fight against BN, including Umno’s continued campaign for leniency for former prime minister Najib Razak, who was jailed over the 1MDB state sovereign wealth fund scandal.

Najib’s sentence was reduced from 12 years to six by the Pardons Board in 2024, a decision that angered many inside DAP who had for years fought corruption.

“Najib has been sentenced. He should serve his sentence. Period,” Loke said.

Whatever the outcome on Saturday, Loke insists it will not destabilise Anwar’s government.

“We still govern the country as a coalition. The cabinet functions as usual. The federal government still functions as usual,” he said.

As for his own party, Loke insists DAP is squarely behind Anwar with no immediate reason to withdraw support.

DAP was formally registered in 1966 as a social democratic party built around freedom, social justice and equal opportunity.

It contested its first general election in 1969, winning 13 parliamentary and 31 state assembly seats.

Six decades later, it remains one of Malaysia’s most successful multiracial opposition-born parties, but also an easy target in Malay nationalist campaigning.

Its support base has long been associated with ethnic Chinese voters, although DAP constantly rejects that framing and has pushed to field more Malay candidates in mixed and Malay-majority seats.

Chinese voters remain crucial to DAP’s survival in Johor, especially in urban and mixed seats. But Loke bristles at the suggestion that any votes can be taken for granted, or as “fixed deposits” in Malaysian political vernacular.

“Voters have a choice, they have the right to decide their destiny,” he said. “Support must be earned.”

Aware of the ethnic bias attacks, in Johor DAP is fielding Nor Zulaila Abd Ghani in Tiram, a Malay-majority seat where around 60 per cent of voters are Malay, as well as Mohamad Shafwan Ani in Bukit Permai and Shazwan Zdainal in Parit Raja.

Loke said DAP had long been cast as “the enemy of the Malays”, a “regressive” political playbook deployed by rivals to stir resentment among the largest population – and voter base.

“We take it in our stride,” he said. “If you are being targeted by the opponents, it means we are a force to be reckoned with.”
