Scott Pelley’s firing from 60 Minutes offers Hong Kong food for thought

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 807 words | 2026-07-08

Before Scott Pelley was fired from 60 Minutes last week, he accused CBS’s editor-in-chief of “murdering” the programme he had served for over two decades. For a show that once defined hard-edged journalism, his dismissal raises questions about what institutional independence means.

The split followed a tense staff meeting in which Pelley confronted the show’s new executive producer. In a statement after his firing, Pelley said the organisation was becoming more politicised and tried to get him to inject bias into his work. Two other employees who were dismissed have also spoken of corporate and political pressure being felt in the newsroom – claims CBS denies.

These concerns are not abstract; 60 Minutes covered the protests in Hong Kong in 2019, profiling Jimmy Lai Chee-ying as a global symbol of resistance to Beijing. The story was often framed as a straightforward struggle for democracy.

In reality, the picture was more complex. Closer integration with the Chinese mainland brought economic pressures – fiercer job competition, higher rents and rising living costs. Those who highlighted these concerns remained largely at the margins.

I was interviewed for that report, but the narrative seemed predetermined. Only a small portion of what I said aired, with much of the context missing.

It left me wondering how much room there is for independent judgment – not only in newsrooms, but in other institutions.

Back when I sat in the Legislative Council for the insurance functional constituency, the atmosphere was very different from what it is today. Opposition and pro-establishment members vied for the media’s attention and competed to criticise the administration; the main dividing line was in how they spoke about Beijing.

That dynamic is worth recalling. Legco is often discussed in broadly comparable terms with the news media, as another institution governed by checks and balances. On paper, both institutions have procedures and checks in place. The question, as with journalism, is how those safeguards are put into practice.

I felt that pressure during 9/11. As the insurance sector’s representative in Legco, reporters asked me about how claims would play out. As the situation was evolving, it would have been irresponsible to go on record.

Legco has set out its own safeguards. Official notes outline four main duties: attending council meetings, taking part in committee work, handling public complaints and liaising with visitors and local bodies.

These duties now operate under a revamped electoral system introduced in 2021 to improve governance and policy debate and create a more stable environment.

Members must raise questions, debate issues and help scrutinise and amend bills. They also rotate through the Redress System to hear complaints and decide which matters should be referred to committees or brought before the chamber.

Stricter rules on attendance, voting and reporting are meant to enhance transparency and accountability. The aim is to set a baseline for diligence in an executive-led system, where the legislature still enacts laws as well as approves budgets and public expenditure.

Newer members are discovering that getting elected is only the beginning; the real challenge is to speak on difficult issues and use the platform effectively. Yet in a chamber where members no longer oppose for the sake of opposing, how should voters decide whether their representatives are doing a good job?

Those expectations are reinforced by specific requirements. Any member whose unexplained absence causes a meeting to lapse for lack of a quorum is liable to a fine. Those unable to attend are expected to provide reasons in advance. Members must report if they will be out of the city for more than 48 hours during the council’s session, maintain a district or constituency office and file an annual work report.

That scrutiny creates its own tension. A balance must be struck as legislators need time to work in their districts and build trust. However, without clearer records of meetings and votes, it is harder to show effectiveness. Relying on metrics such as speeches or media clips risks mistaking visibility for substance.

This tension was reflected in commentary surrounding social welfare sector legislator Grace Chan Man-yee. Months into her term, some questioned whether she was sufficiently visible, apparently judging her performance largely by media exposure and public-facing engagement rather than by the substance of her legislative work.

Chan said that she and her team had been working continuously on child protection and welfare issues. She conceded that while she had focused on the policy grind, she had not explained her work clearly or quickly enough, and would do better.

In newsrooms as in legislatures, what counts is the ability of people inside the institution to question decisions and discuss uncomfortable issues in the public interest.

That is how institutions earn trust – not through procedure alone, but through the quieter work of scrutiny, explanation and steady engagement with the public they serve.
