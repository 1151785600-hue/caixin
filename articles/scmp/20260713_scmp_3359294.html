After hooking China on coffee, Starbucks ramps up consumer strategy

SCMP | FREE | 470 words | 2026-07-13

When Anne walked into a Starbucks branch in Seattle more than a decade ago, she knew exactly what to expect: a latte, a comfortable chair and a place to work.

Now, after living in the technology hub of Hangzhou, the capital of the eastern province of Zhejiang, and working as an English teacher for three years, the 34-year-old American still visits Starbucks regularly, but says the local Starbucks stores are increasingly different from the ones she remembers back home.

“It’s not really just a coffee shop any more,” she said. “Sometimes it feels more like a community centre.”

While Starbucks in the United States often serves as a pit stop for takeaway coffee or for a convenient break between work meetings, its China outlets are increasingly positioning themselves as venues for social connection and cultural experiences.

The trend seems to go beyond localisation, and some analysts attribute it to intensifying competition in China’s consumer market and the growing need for foreign brands to become part of local consumer culture, rather than simply selling products.

“China’s coffee market has evolved from an education stage to a competition stage. Consumers today are no longer choosing Starbucks simply because it is a foreign coffee brand,” said Jason Yu, the general manager of CTR Market Research.

“They are looking for brands that reflect their lifestyles, interests and cultural identity. That is pushing multinational brands to localise more deeply than before,” he added.

Bottled and canned Starbucks products are now sold in grocery stores in China, and Starbucks energy drinks are set to be sold at Sam’s Club. After Boyu Capital acquired a 60 per cent stake in Starbucks China in May, the coffee company launched Bearista Town apparel collections, featuring utility vests, T-shirts and hooded jackets.

Starbucks is not alone in embracing change for Chinese consumers.

McDonald’s China has accelerated menu localisation and expansion into lower-tier cities since selling control of its mainland business to a consortium led by Citic Capital Partners in 2017. And KFC, operated by Yum China, has long adapted its offerings with products ranging from congee breakfasts to region-specific menu items and festival-themed promotions.

Some analysts pointed to the red-hot competitive environment.

Starbucks, which entered China in 1999, operated about 8,000 stores nationwide at the time of the Boyu Capital joint venture. It faces growing Chinese rivals: Luckin Coffee ended the first quarter of 2026 with 33,419 stores across mainland China and Hong Kong, while Cotti Coffee said it had roughly 16,000 stores in China in the same period.

“Starbucks can no longer rely solely on its premium positioning to maintain customer loyalty,” said Yu.

“Chinese consumers have become more sophisticated and have many alternatives. Building communities, embracing local culture and creating emotional connections are increasingly important ways for Starbucks to differentiate itself from domestic competitors.”
