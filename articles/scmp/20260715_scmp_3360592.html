China’s second-quarter GDP growth misses mark, with lowest reading since end of 2022

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 819 words | 2026-07-15

China reported 4.3 per cent gross domestic product growth in the second quarter of 2026 shortly after recording strong, sustained momentum in exports – a contrast underscoring the need for policy support to address weak domestic demand and persistent economic imbalances.

The closely watched GDP metrics, released by the National Bureau of Statistics (NBS) on Wednesday, marked the slowest quarterly growth since the end of 2022. The figure fell below the 4.48 per cent forecast from economists surveyed by Chinese financial data provider Wind.

The second-quarter growth – which marked a deceleration from the 5 per cent expansion recorded in the first three months of 2026 – brought China’s first-half growth to 4.7 per cent, according to the bureau.

China’s exports remained resilient despite disruptions to global shipping routes and energy supplies caused by the Iran war, with outbound shipments rising 27 per cent in June and 17.6 per cent for the first half of 2026, both on a year-on-year basis.

Beijing has set this year’s growth target at “4.5 to 5 per cent” – a slight shift from the “around 5 per cent” goal that had been the recent norm – as the economy grapples with numerous headwinds, including weak domestic demand, a prolonged downturn in the property market and rising external risks.

Officials said that the GDP growth in the first half of the year laid the groundwork for achieving China’s full-year target.

“The central government will also introduce targeted, more proactive and effective policies in response to changing circumstances,” said Mao Shengyong, deputy director of the statistics bureau.

“There are solid conditions and strong support for achieving the main annual targets, including the economic growth target.”

This rebound can’t be sustained without swifter policy action

Tan Junyu, Coface

Retail sales, a major gauge of consumption, rose by 1 per cent year on year in June, beating the 0.09 per cent growth projection from the Wind poll. The metric fell by 0.6 per cent year on year in May, the first decline since China lifted Covid-19 lockdowns at the end of 2022.

Overall fixed-asset investment – covering major items such as infrastructure, manufacturing and property construction – dropped by 5.7 per cent year on year in the first six months of 2026, widening the 4.1 per cent decline recorded in the January-May period.

“Overall, the second-quarter data highlights a deepening divergence between domestic and external demand,” said Tan Junyu, regional economist for North Asia at trade credit insurer Coface.

While export performance was being underpinned by artificial intelligence hardware and green-tech demand, he said, domestic demand was being weighed on by the property slump. However, he added, last month’s improvement in retail sales could be a sign of stabilisation, with consumption supported by accelerated trade-in subsidies.

“But this rebound can’t be sustained without swifter policy action,” Tan said.

Zhang Zhiwei, president and chief economist at Pinpoint Asset Management, said he was not sure whether the slower economic growth in the second quarter would prompt the government to “significantly” change its policy stance in the coming months.

Citing robust growth in the first quarter and China’s resilient exports, he said Beijing was “still on track to deliver growth in line with the official target.”

Zhang said this month’s meeting of the Politburo, a high-level body of the ruling Communist Party, would reveal policymakers’ outlook on the economy for the remainder of the year.

“The Politburo meeting in the last week of July will shed light on the policymakers’ guidance,” he said.

Su Jian, an economics professor at Peking University, said Beijing was unlikely to roll out broad-based, aggressive stimulus measures in the coming months.

That said, as investment growth continues to weaken and authorities seek to prevent systemic risks, Beijing could unveil further support measures centred on “new infrastructure” in the second half of the year, he added.

Property investment, an entrenched drag on economic growth, fell 18 per cent in the first half of the year, compared with a fall of 16.2 per cent in the first five months, according to the NBS.

Industrial output grew by 5.3 per cent year on year in June, compared with an increase of 4.5 per cent in May.

Elsewhere, China’s overall urban unemployment rate stood at 5 per cent in June, compared with 5.1 per cent in May.

“We expect China’s growth to strengthen modestly in the second half of the year, supported by a more proactive fiscal stance,” said Su Yue, principal economist for China with the Economist Intelligence Unit.

She said reconstruction spending after recent floods and a favourable base effect from last year should help stabilise growth, while technology-related exports are likely to remain resilient despite mounting external headwinds.

Su said “rebuilding household confidence will become increasingly important” in the second half of the year and in early 2027 – the year of the party’s next national congress, a high-profile event in the political calendar – adding she expected policymakers to place greater emphasis on boosting consumption.
