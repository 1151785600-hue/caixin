Taiwan’s new military outsourcing plan: a pragmatic fix or security risk?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 906 words | 2026-07-13

Taiwan’s armed forces plan to outsource guard duties at military academies to private security firms in a pilot programme aimed at freeing up limited personnel for core combat missions.

But the plan has triggered concerns that handing frontline access to civilian contractors could create new security vulnerabilities as the island grapples with a rise in espionage cases that the Taiwanese government claims are linked to Beijing.

Under the two-stage programme, private guards will take over routine duties at nine military academy sites, including identity and vehicle checks, visitor registration and general access control, according to the island’s defence ministry on Monday.

The first phase will begin on September 1 at five sites – the National Defence University’s three campuses, the army’s military academy and the naval academy.

Four more sites – the Army Academy, two Air Force Institute of Technology campuses and Chung Cheng Armed Forces Preparatory School – will join the programme from January 1 next year.

The ministry said the scheme was intended to ease the military’s manpower burden and allow troops to return to their units to focus on combat training and core missions.

The move comes as Taiwan confronts a deepening manpower shortage caused largely by its declining birth rate.

In 2023, the number of men eligible for military service fell below 100,000 for the first time, dropping to 97,828. According to the Taiwanese legislature’s Budget Centre, the figure is expected to fall to around 79,742 next year.

The island’s armed forces are only about 79 per cent staffed despite years of recruitment incentives.

This is tantamount to tearing down your own walls

Lee Shying-jow, former security bureau chief

The outsourcing plan drew a mixed response online, with some arguing it was a pragmatic way to reduce the number of troops tied up in routine duties.

Others questioned whether civilian guards could adequately protect military compounds from infiltration or attacks.

“Military personnel stand guard to prevent bases from being attacked or infiltrated. If you can’t even defend your own base and have to outsource the job, how are you going to fight a war?” one critic wrote.

“Should we outsource future wars to mercenaries?” another said.

Others asked whether the defence ministry itself should be outsourced, while one commenter warned that the change could make infiltration “even easier”.

Lee Shying-jow, former director general of the island’s National Security Bureau, said the policy appeared at odds with the government’s repeated warnings about growing infiltration threats from Beijing.

He noted that 64 people were prosecuted in espionage cases linked to Beijing in 2024 – three times the number in 2021 – with serving and retired military personnel increasingly targeted.

“Under such infiltration pressure, the correct logic should be to tighten and reinforce access and personnel controls, rather than hand the most basic line of security defence to private firms whose national security vetting standards are far lower than those for active-duty military personnel,” Lee said.

Beijing, which sees Taiwan as part of China to be reunited by force if necessary, has repeatedly denied the espionage accusations. Mainland China’s counter-espionage agency has also warned of infiltration and influence efforts from Taipei.

Most countries, including the United States, do not recognise Taiwan as an independent state, but Washington is opposed to any attempt to take the self-ruled island by force and is committed to supplying it with weapons.

Lee said that on the one hand, Taiwan’s government stressed that the Communist Party was a hostile force and called on the public to remain vigilant.

“On the other, it is handing the physical gateways to military academies to people outside the military system, where staff turnover is high and loyalty screening is relatively weak,” he said. “This is tantamount to tearing down your own walls.”

Lee warned that civilian security workers, whose salaries and benefits were generally lower than those of military personnel, could also present attractive targets for mainland Chinese intelligence operatives seeking to exploit financial vulnerabilities.

Military analyst Lu De-yun, a former Taiwanese defence ministry press secretary, offered a more cautious assessment, saying it would be wrong to assume private guards were inherently vulnerable to infiltration.

“You can’t simply say there will be enemy agents or Chinese communist spies among them,” Lu said, noting that many retired military personnel worked in the private security industry.

“There should be some companies that maintain quite high standards and discipline.”

In response to the controversy, the island’s defence ministry said private guards would only help with routine access control, vehicle and personnel checks and visitor registration.

The protection of important military facilities, weapons and ammunition depots and core security zones, as well as emergency responses and combat readiness missions, would remain the responsibility of military personnel, the ministry added.

It said responsibility for base security, command authority and security standards would also remain with the armed forces.

All contracted guards will undergo qualification screening, background checks and professional training and be subject to military supervision, according to the ministry.

It added that they would not be allowed access to sensitive defence information or carry out military security missions, and the pilot scheme would be comprehensively assessed for security risks, manpower use and effectiveness before any further changes were made.

The ministry said the US and other armed forces sometimes employed a similar division of labour between military personnel and professional security guards, depending on the base and the nature of the duties.
