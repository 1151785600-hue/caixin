Philippines looks to space for bird’s-eye view of South China Sea threats

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1051 words | 2026-07-11

The Philippines is preparing to create a military space centre by 2028, a move that could help Manila strengthen surveillance, communications and command across its archipelago and in contested areas of the South China Sea.

But analysts said the ambition would be constrained by the technical, financial and personnel hurdles of turning space assets into military capability.

Philippine military chief General Romeo Brawner Jnr said on Tuesday that the initiative would enhance communications, command and control, missile systems and drone operations, laying the groundwork for a future space command.

The proposal was presented to President Ferdinand Marcos Jnr at the military’s midyear command conference, following the president’s directive in December for the armed forces to develop their own satellite capability.

“It will be a centre. We need to start small first, and later on, we will expand into a space command or space force,” Brawner told reporters, adding that the plan envisaged the use of military satellites.

A space command would give the military a sovereign platform for persistent surveillance not only of the South China Sea but across the entire Philippine archipelago, said Rommel Jude Ong, a retired Philippine Navy rear admiral who now lectures at the Ateneo School of Government.

“A notional space capability requires a satellite platform, a ground station to receive data, a processing centre and a secure distribution system to the end users. But more important is the ‘humanware’. It takes time to train people to operate such systems efficiently,” Ong said.

While he said defence cooperation arrangements with partners remained strong, sovereign capability would grant Philippine security forces greater operational flexibility.

“That would be particularly valuable if the space programme were integrated into the [military’s] kill chain,” he said, referring to the systems used to find, track and, if needed, strike a target.

Manila has not publicly outlined a formal kill-chain doctrine comparable to those of the United States or South Korea. But analysts say its modernisation drive – including coastal missile batteries, long-range radars, drones, maritime patrol aircraft, secure command-and-control networks and the proposed space command – could underpin a more integrated targeting and response system.

Own or collaborate?

Experts said the 2028 target was achievable provided the project’s scope stayed modest and Manila pursued inter-agency cooperation alongside partnerships with countries that had established commercial space capabilities.

Given the timeline, the military has two broad options: launch satellites of its own or contract the use of allied or commercial systems, according to Vincent Kyle Parada, a former defence analyst for the Philippine Navy.

“Both options have their trade-offs. Launching satellites would require a greater initial investment, both in terms of resources as well as the expertise required to develop and maintain them,” Parada said.

“Using existing satellites may fast track space capability development in the short term, but the level of dependency that entails may lead to issues of availability in the long run.”

Parada doubted the military would establish a dedicated “space force” akin to those of the US or China. Instead, he said the proposed centre would likely sit under one of the three major service branches before eventually being upgraded into a joint space command.

“The question of whether it can acquire satellites by 2028 aside, establishing a space centre in anticipation of that capability is something that makes sense,” he said.

Brawner said the military was working with the Philippine Space Agency (PhilSA) on the initiative. “We might start with orbiting satellites. These will all be interconnected,” he said.

PhilSA has primarily operated low-Earth-orbit satellites such as Diwata and Maya, and already runs geostationary satellites, which orbit roughly 36,000km (22,400 miles) above Earth and remain fixed over a single location to provide continuous communications and surveillance.

In 2022, the Department of National Defence and PhilSA signed a memorandum of understanding to strengthen national security through cooperation on satellite applications, intelligence sharing, research and training.

The military needed a timely, accurate picture of its operating environment, particularly in the South China Sea, Parada said.

The Philippine military already has access to intelligence-sharing initiatives such as Canada’s satellite-based dark-vessel detection system, along with its own monitoring stations, but it still relies heavily on manned surveillance platforms.

“Domain awareness is a central component of any defence or deterrence strategy in the South China Sea,” Parada said. “Having dedicated satellites would significantly increase its ISR [intelligence, surveillance and reconnaissance] capabilities and, crucially, strengthen command and control over its units to ensure operational effectiveness.”

Future-proofing the military

A space command would give the Philippines a sovereign capability resilient to foreign political influence, said Justin Kyle Malarayap, a defence analyst and visiting assistant professor at Arellano University in Manila. It would also improve military communications and interoperability, including in remote areas where signals are often degraded or disrupted by terrain.

“For any meaningful military space capabilities to be developed by the Philippines, it will need the collaboration of its more technically experienced allies such as the US, Japan and Australia,” he said.

“In the long term, the Philippines can opt to network its satellite with other countries. The proposed space command may model this arrangement through Nato’s Alliance Persistent Surveillance from Space, which incorporates both allied and commercial space surveillance data.”

Chris Gardiner, CEO of the Institute for Regional Security, said the wars in Ukraine and Iran had highlighted the importance of space for command and control and operational advantage.

He said Ukraine’s experience showed the need for greater self-reliance to avoid being left at the mercy of another country, or company, switching off a system or denying access at a crucial moment.

“The reality in the short term is that the Philippines, like Australia, will need to rely on the US in the space domain. The Philippines, collaborating with Australia and Japan, however, should be exploring increased investment in independent satellite systems and in shared space-based ISR,” Gardiner said.

For Brawner, the military must prepare for future conflicts rather than wait for threats to emerge, as modern warfare is increasingly defined by drones, missiles, cyber capabilities and space technologies.

“The most important thing is really to look at the situation and plan for the future. If you start acting once the threat is there, it’s already too late,” he said.
