Japan wants 60 million tourists, but China isn’t sending them

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1074 words | 2026-07-12

Japan has found a new export industry and it doesn’t come off an assembly line. It arrives by plane, spends freely and is, increasingly, what Tokyo is banking its economic future on.

Last year, 42.7 million foreign visitors arrived in the country, a record that shattered the previous year’s high by nearly 16 per cent. Now Tokyo wants to turn that surge into something more lasting: a permanent pillar of growth to rival, or even outlast, the industries that built modern Japan.

To achieve that ambition, the government has set a target of 60 million arrivals a year by 2030, with billions being funnelled into the infrastructure needed to get there.

At Narita Airport, the main international gateway serving the Japanese capital, crews are extending the second runway to 2,500 metres (8,200 feet) and building an entirely new 3,500-metre (11,480-foot) third runway, as part of a multi-year expansion that aims to push annual capacity from 340,000 flights to half a million.

Rail planners, meanwhile, are working to nearly double train frequency between Narita and central Tokyo by the early 2030s, with new extensions set to link two lines directly to Haneda Airport to the capital’s south, aiming to make domestic transfers easier.

Private capital has followed the government’s lead. Hotels are opening across every price bracket, from backpacker hostels to luxury resorts, and a new generation of travel companies is emerging to serve them.

“Tourism is one of the fastest-growing areas of the Japanese economy,” said Martin Schulz, chief policy economist at Fujitsu’s Global Market Intelligence Unit.

“It has a strong impact on domestic services, such as hotels and restaurants, and it acts as a major help to the broader economy … Tourism may not be the single largest engine, but it is a very important one because of the impact on labour and so on.”

Tourism, domestic and inbound combined, now accounts for roughly 5.6 per cent of Japan’s gross domestic product, according to government figures, worth an estimated 34.3 trillion yen (US$211 billion) a year.

That makes it the country’s second-biggest export earner, trailing only an automobile industry that finds itself increasingly under siege from US tariff pressures and booming Chinese competition in electric vehicles, especially.

Searching for growth

Therein lies the logic of Tokyo’s tourism push. Japanese firms that once dominated global markets in electronics, heavy engineering, financial services and cars are losing ground, not least to Chinese rivals encroaching on territory long considered unassailable.

Tourism, meanwhile, offers a rare growth story at a moment when many of the old ones are losing their punch.

Schulz traces the pivot back to the 1990s and the wreckage of Japan’s “lost decades”. After the property bubble burst and manufacturers began shifting production overseas, banks were left nursing mountains of bad debt.

It soon became clear that restructuring alone would not be enough, Schulz said. Japan needed new revenue streams and it found two: digital services and tourism.

“Japan could see that other mature nations, like those in Europe, all had strong tourism sectors with investment and income flowing into those economies from overseas,” he said.

The turnaround began with visas. Rather than exclusively courting the relatively small, relatively wealthy travel markets of Europe and North America, Japan opened its doors to the vast populations of neighbouring Asian countries, wagering on volume over exclusivity.

There were, inevitably perhaps, some speed humps along the way. The Tohoku earthquake, tsunami and nuclear crisis of 2011 sent arrivals tumbling before a slow recovery eventually took hold. Then came 2020: after a record-breaking 2019, the Covid-19 pandemic brought global travel to a standstill overnight.

Each time, Japan bounced back – and last year’s numbers suggest the recovery has now overshot its pre-pandemic ambitions entirely. Visitors spent 9.5 trillion yen in 2025, another record, drawn in part by a weak currency that has turned Japan into one of the world’s best-value destinations.

Momentum has cooled only slightly this year. Arrivals in the first five months of 2026 dipped a marginal 1.1 per cent, to 17.94 million, but officials insist the 60-million target remains firmly within reach.

Strained by success

Success has brought its own complications, however. “There is a need to diversify to different parts of the country, but the information capacity is very limited,” said Kumi Kato, a tourism professor at Japan’s Wakayama University.

Nearly 70 per cent of all overnight stays by foreign visitors cluster in just five prefectures: Tokyo, Osaka, Kyoto, Hokkaido and Okinawa. Kyoto alone accounted for almost 63 million domestic and international visitors last year, leaving its historic streets and temples groaning under the strain.

The result, according to Kato, has been a kind of tourism monoculture: familiar routes, familiar sights and lines of foreign visitors trailing tour guides with flags through the same handful of postcard locations.

“Japan is affordable now and we are getting tourists that are visiting for the first time but are not going to different parts of the country,” she said.

“We see more of the stereotypical tours to the same places in Kyoto and Tokyo, with groups of foreign tourists following a guide with a flag.”

The China ‘lever’

Schulz identified another flaw in the grand tourism plan that no amount of domestic investment could fix: geopolitics.

“Theoretically, Japan is trying to distance itself from the Chinese economy at a time of strained ties,” he said. “But the Chinese government is still able to exert pressure … as it has ordered its nationals not to go to Japan.”

Relations hit a fresh nadir in November, when Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi told the country’s parliament that a Taiwan “contingency” could require a military response – remarks that infuriated Beijing, which regards the island as a part of China to be reunited by force if necessary.

Most countries, including Japan and the US, do not recognise Taiwan as an independent state, but are opposed to any attempt to take the self-governed island by force and Washington is committed to supplying it with weapons.

The fallout was immediate, in tourism terms. While arrivals from South Korea rose more than 14 per cent in May and those from Taiwan climbed 14.6 per cent, visitor numbers from mainland China plummeted 60.4 per cent to just 310,000, marking the sixth straight monthly decline.

“There are lots of tourists arriving from other parts of Asia, but China is using its travellers as another lever to pressure Japan,” Schulz said. “It’s easy for Beijing to do and it hits Japan hard.”
