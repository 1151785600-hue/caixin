What have the Americans ever done for us?

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1054 words | 2026-07-08

In Monty Python’s classic comedy Life of Brian, one of the many bickering revolutionary groups, the Campaign for a Free Galilee – or was it the People’s Front of Judea? – was plotting revenge against the Romans.

One of them broke into a song: The Romans are all b*******,/they have bled us ’till we’re white,/they’ve taken everything we’ve got/as if it was their right,/and we’ve got nothing in return/though they make so much fuss./ What have the Romans ever done for us?

For all their brutalities, the Romans did contribute substantially to human progress. Among those contributions, the rebels reluctantly acknowledged, were aqueducts, public hygiene, wines and fermentation, public baths and toilets, roads and canals, cheese, medicine, irrigation, Roman law and classical education.

I was going to add crucifixion, the method by which the titular Brian was executed. But according to archaeologists, that honour goes to the Assyrians. The Romans merely refined it in the form of a cross, thus helping to give birth to one of the world’s great religions, and the West itself.

According to a survey a few years ago, American men apparently spend a lot of time thinking about the Roman empire. Yes, mostly men, not women. Before Elon Musk briefly became Donald Trump’s cost-cutting tsar, thus contributing to America’s decline in governance standards at home and abroad, he also fretted about Rome.

“Anyone feeling late stage empire vibes?” he tweeted in 2023. “Watching the Roman Empire collapse again but with Wi-fi and memes this time.”

In her latest interview with The New York Times, the eminent British historian Mary Beard says she “looks at Trump and can’t not think of ancient Rome”. I wonder which emperor(s) she might also have in mind – Caligula, Nero, Commodus?

Personally, I was thinking more of Monty Python on this anniversary of America. Certainly, America has made many contributions to civilisation.

In technology, there is, well, the internet. As sociologist Manuel Castells wrote in The Rise of the Network Society, “The internet originated in a daring scheme imagined in the 1960s by the technological warriors of US Defence Department Advanced Research Projects Agency (the mythical Darpa) to prevent a Soviet takeover or destruction of American communications in case of nuclear war.”

He compares the tech idea to Mao Zedong’s “tactics of dispersal of guerrilla forces around a vast territory to counter an enemy’s might with versatility and knowledge of terrain … The outcome was a network architecture that, as its inventors wanted, cannot be controlled from any centre, and is made up of thousands of autonomous computer networks”.

Darpa ended up creating an alternative virtual universe! While it may feel good to sing, “War, what is it good for? Absolutely nothin’!”, war is, in fact, behind much, perhaps even most, of human progress throughout history. That’s a sad commentary on human nature.

In music, you would probably pick jazz, America’s most original contribution. Sadly I don’t know the first thing about it. I was a big fan of British rock ‘n’ roll, until Howard, a former colleague and friend, introduced me to American blues. After that, I just couldn’t listen to the Rolling Stones, The Who and Led Zeppelin without thinking: what rip-offs they were from southern American music!

Pete Townshend of The Who did pay tribute to the blues when he played a decent cover of Howlin’ Wolf’s “I Put A Spell On You” in one of his solo albums.

In literature, it’s the Great American Novel. You would probably name Melville’s Moby Dick, and something or another by William Faulkner, John Steinbeck, F. Scott Fitzgerald or Hemingway. And you would be right.

Not me, though. Philip Roth’s Portnoy’s Complaint is that one great American novel that shaped the whole spiritual journey of my teenage years. Who can possibly forget that immortal scene where Portnoy bought a piece of raw liver with which to pleasure himself and then accidentally left it in the kitchen for his poor mother to cook for the family’s dinner?

Without Portnoy’s Complaint, the whole genre of raunchy Hollywood comedies would not have been possible. Think of American Pie, the movie, in which one of the main characters satisfied himself by poking a hole in a warm apple pie – what can be more American? – and left it on the dining table while his terribly liberal father pretended not to notice to avoid embarrassing his teenage son. That was right out of Roth’s novel.

Or There’s Something About Mary in which Cameron Diaz used something of Ben Stiller, her lifelong secret admirer in the movie, to gel her hair.

In philosophy, as a lifelong student of it, I suspect America produced the only school of thought that I think is true: pragmatism, such as that of William James, among others.

I find many philosophies and philosophers more exciting, and I don’t find James especially interesting. But I have to admit that pragmatism, as America’s unique contribution to world philosophy, happens to be true, or closest to the way things really are.

For pragmatists, words are just tools, and their meanings are in the way they are used in intercourse. There are no Platonic ideals, innate ideas or eternal thoughts in the all-knowing mind of God.

Truth is what a group of people or community ends up agreeing on and believing in, and may well change when they move on or die out. You want to find meaning in life? It’s the same with words. It all depends on how you live/use your life.

In entertainment, I have to say 1980s America created the best action movies that never get old for me. I mean Aliens (the second one by James Cameron, a Canadian), Lethal Weapon (the first one, though Mel Gibson is technically Australian), and Die Hard (the first one), from which many a Cantopop movie ripped off through the decades. I have lost count of how many times I have watched them, from start to finish. Oh, there is also The Rock, with Nicolas Cage, but that was from the 1990s.

So thank you, America, for making my life less dull. And happy birthday!
