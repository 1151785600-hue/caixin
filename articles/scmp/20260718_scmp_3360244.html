If anyone deserves a red card it’s Donald Trump and Gianni Infantino

SCMP | FREE | 1112 words | 2026-07-18

The US had high hopes for success in the World Cup both as co-hosts and with the performance of its national team, promising to deliver the best tournament ever.

All appeared to be going well, especially when the home nation beat Bosnia-Herzegovina in the first knockout round to progress to the last 16.

Then President Donald Trump stepped in. It was an intervention waiting to happen. The scandal has gravely undermined the integrity of the sport, its governing body and the World Cup itself.

It all began with a red card, waved by Brazilian referee Raphael Claus at US star striker and top goalscorer Folarin Balogun. After viewing video replays, the ref sent the player off, deeming his treading on a Bosnian opponent’s ankle to be serious foul play. This did not prevent the US team from winning 2-0.

The red card, according to well-established and widely understood rules, meant that Balogun would automatically be suspended for the next game, a quarter-final clash with Belgium. A longer ban would not have been surprising.

This is the usual practice, no matter how controversial the issuing of the red card might be. Anyone who follows football knows this. But that, it seems, does not include the US government.

Officials professed to be astonished and outraged that a key US player would miss the big game. Andrew Giuliani, executive director of the White House World Cup task force and Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick discussed appealing to the sport’s governing body, Fifa, to lift the ban. Lawyers were made available.

But Trump preferred the direct approach. He picked up the phone and called his good friend Gianni Infantino, Fifa’s president, asking for the suspension to be reviewed – even though the rules do not allow an appeal.

This highly improper personal, political intervention should have been instantly rebuffed. But on the eve of the match, Fifa stunned the global football community by announcing that the implementation of Balogun’s ban would be suspended for a year, clearing him to play.

It was an outrageous decision but one that will not surprise anyone aware of the close relationship between Trump and Infantino. The football chief’s fawning even went as far as awarding the president a hastily created “peace prize” last year.

Trump spoke openly about his flagrant and apparently successful bid to influence the tournament. He was characteristically shameless.

The president admitted he had not known “what the hell a red card was”. But this did not stop him forming the opinion, as someone who “understands sports really well” that Balogun’s challenge “wasn’t a foul.”

He branded the suspension unfair and said: “We have to have the best players.”

Trump even sought to unfairly tarnish the reputation of the referee, describing him as “a little bit suspect.”

Infantino admitted receiving a call from Trump but insisted he had nothing to do with the decision to suspend the player’s ban, stating it was made by an independent disciplinary committee.

A statement issued by that committee’s chairman was at pains to point out that the referee’s decision and the consequent ban had not been overturned. Viewed in legal terms, it had given him a suspended sentence.

The chairman said the decision was made under Article 27 of the disciplinary code which gives the committee the discretion to suspend the implementation of any disciplinary measure. This was done, the statement added, considering “all of the specific circumstances surrounding the incident and evidence available”.

More telling, however, is what it did not say. No explanation was provided. Fifa must, for the good of the game, make it clear precisely what circumstances and evidence persuaded the committee to take this extraordinary step.

Until it does, the decision appears arbitrary and the perception is that the only “specific circumstance” that mattered was the pressure applied by Trump. It will continue to be regarded as the bestowing of preferential treatment on the US team at the behest of the nation’s leader. That is a dangerous precedent.

Article 27 has been used before. Fifa invoked it, also controversially, to allow one of the giants of the game, Cristiano Ronaldo, to play in Portugal’s opening two matches of the World Cup even though he was supposed to be serving a ban.

But the decision to allow Balogun to play is the first time the provision has been used to suspend an automatic ban mid-tournament.

Balogun’s red card was a little harsh. His contact with the opposing player was accidental. But it is not unusual for such challenges to result in a sending off. There were no reasonable grounds to revoke it or suspend the match ban that followed. Some commentators in the US did not seem to understand that.

US officials have suggested slow motion replays should not have been used in the video review that led to the red card. That is incorrect. The protocol for video assistant referees expressly permits slow motion footage to be viewed to establish facts such as the “point of contact for physical offences.”

Fifa’s decision, unsurprisingly and rightly, prompted a furious backlash from prominent figures and football authorities around the world.

Europe’s governing body for football, Uefa, which has a history of conflicts with Fifa, slammed the move as “unprecedented, incomprehensible and unjustifiable”, declaring that it had crossed a red line.

The decision raises serious questions. Are other team’s now able to challenge red cards? Can their nation’s leaders make representations to Fifa? Why were suspensions for the other 12 World Cup red cards not also suspended under Article 27?

Belgium, facing a US team with Balogun restored to the line-up, was understandably furious. They suddenly and unexpectedly had to prepare for his presence.

Justice was ultimately done, but on the field rather than thanks to Fifa. The US put in a woeful performance, seemingly unsettled by the controversy, and Balogun was a shadow of his former self. Belgium won 4-1, with some of their players apparently mimicking the famous Trump dance when they scored. They went on to lose to Spain in the quarter-final.

The World Cup, which reaches its conclusion next Sunday, has been a success on many levels. There have been exciting matches, less-fancied teams from small nations have exceeded expectations and fans from different parts of the world have enjoyed the action together in good spirits. Football has shown, once again, that it can be a force for good.

But if this World Cup is to be remembered as the best ever, it will be in spite of the best efforts of Trump and Infantino, not because of them.
