How Hong Kong is fighting to redefine its future with an institutional moat, wealth surge

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 2164 words | 2026-07-15

For decades, warnings of Hong Kong’s terminal decline have arrived in periodic waves.

When Stephen Roach, the former chairman of Morgan Stanley Asia, ignited a firestorm with his February 2024 assertion that “Hong Kong is over”, he pointed to a toxic cocktail of domestic and external pressures.

Roach, who doubled down on his “wake-up call” a year ago, argued that the city’s economic glory was being extinguished by a loss of political autonomy following 2020’s national security law, the spillover from mainland China’s protracted malaise, and the city’s precarious position in the crossfire of Sino-US tensions.

Despite the prominent American economist’s perceived gloom, the “Hong Kong is back” camp – led by a defiant local government – is wielding evidence to the contrary. The city claimed the IPO fundraising crown last year and ranked behind only New York and London in the latest Z/Yen Global Financial Centres Index.

Behind the heated debate lies a fundamental question: in an era defined by shifting capital flows, geopolitical fragmentation and evolving regulatory frameworks, how can Hong Kong keep shining as a globally leading financial centre?

“Hong Kong must adapt to a changing world order,” said Anthony Cheung, chair professor in public administration at the Education University of Hong Kong. As the city’s traditional intermediary role between China and global markets erodes, he argued, it must rely more explicitly on its institutional strengths to recalibrate its competitive position.

A shrinking moat in the wake of China’s rise

Historically, Hong Kong’s competitive “moat” – the structural barriers protecting its status – was built on its role as the exclusive gateway for global capital entering a mainland China that was only beginning to open its arms to investment.

In the 1980s, the height of this moat was measured by the city’s relative economic might. Its gross domestic product was equivalent to around 20 per cent of mainland China’s total output.

Today, that landscape has transformed following the outpaced economic take-off of the mainland – now the world’s second-largest economy and widely seen as being on a path to eventually surpassing the United States.

As the mainland has been developing its own infrastructure, Hong Kong’s traditional pillars have come under pressure. Its dominance in regional shipping has long since given way to Shanghai, which is now looking for a bigger slice of the offshore-finance pie. Meanwhile, Hong Kong’s property sector has experienced significant volatility, particularly since the pandemic, but some analysts suspect it “has become the first major city in China whose property market has bottomed”.

And in terms of GDP, Hong Kong was surpassed in 2018 by neighbouring Shenzhen – the southern metropolis home to telecoms giant Huawei Technologies, internet services firm Tencent, drone giant DJI and electric vehicle leader BYD.

Its important competitors might share one or a few advantages, but it is hard to collect all of them like Hong Kong

Le Xia, BBVA Research

But despite these challenges, Hong Kong retains a rare collection of institutional advantages that remain difficult for mainland rivals to replicate. Le Xia, chief economist for Asia at BBVA Research, identified the city’s enduring moat as a combination that includes its British-inherited common law system, first-class financial infrastructure and appealing geographic location.

“Its important competitors might share one or a few advantages, but it is hard to collect all of them like Hong Kong,” he said, also noting the city’s deep talent pool and long-held reputation as a global financial centre.

Hong Hao, chief investment officer at Hong Kong-based Lotus Asset Management, put it simply: “There is no place like Hong Kong in China.”

He noted that Hong Kong remains an international capital market with free capital flows, making it the only truly global financial centre within China’s sovereign framework.

“Mainland China relies on Hong Kong more than ever as a bridge between its economy and the global market,” Hong said.

From ‘superconnector’ to ‘super value-adder’

From the launch of the “Brand Hong Kong” platform a quarter-century ago to promote the city to a global audience, to the ambitious “Hello Hong Kong” and “Hong Kong is back” campaigns of the post-pandemic era, authorities have deployed a multipronged strategy to revive the city’s status – with manoeuvres ranging from hosting high-stakes investment summits to implementing sweeping tax overhauls.

The 2025 policy address by Hong Kong Chief Executive John Lee in September pinned hopes on leveraging China’s national development strategies – including five-year development plans, the Greater Bay Area blueprint and the Belt and Road Initiative, along with a push to internationalise the yuan. Lee also unveiled the government’s GoGlobal Task Force aimed at encouraging firms across the border to use Hong Kong as a platform for expanding overseas.

Just last month, he announced plans to create Hong Kong’s first five-year development blueprint, calling the plan’s formulation a “historic step”. And on the official News.gov.hk platform, he discussed how the city government was helping both local and mainland businesses expand their global reach.

“When we talk about being a ‘superconnector’,” Lee said, “we can, of course, introduce and connect. But it is more than connect – we do value-adding.”

This strategy, aiming to not only link China and global markets, but also generate higher value across capital flows, has included outreach to the Middle East and Central Asia to woo businesses back to Hong Kong.

This ambition was on full display at the Hong Kong FIC and Bond Connect Summit on Tuesday, as officials announced the trial operation of a gold clearing and settlement system. The move, supported by expanded physical vaults, marks a “milestone” in the city’s bid to evolve into an international hub for trading precious metals, and into a future global price-setter.

Efforts have already yielded high-profile wins, including last year when Hong Kong claimed the crown as the world’s top IPO-fundraising venue, raising US$37.4 billion across 119 listings, including two of the top five globally.

And while the Hong Kong stock exchange surrendered that crown during the first half of this year to Nasdaq following SpaceX’s blockbuster listing last month, Hong Kong saw its IPO proceeds register a year-on-year rise of about 84 per cent from January to June – consolidating the region’s position as a primary offshore financial hub for Chinese issuers.

Meanwhile, assets and wealth managed in the city rose 20 per cent, year on year, to a record HK$42.2 trillion (US$5.38 trillion) in 2025, underscoring the city’s edge over Switzerland as a wealth hub, according to the Securities and Futures Commission. The city also issued two stablecoin licences in April, drawing international attention to its ambition of becoming a global crypto hub.

The battle for talent and capital

Hong Kong has pivoted towards more aggressive fiscal incentives to bolster its competitiveness against regional rivals. This includes long-time “frenemy” city Singapore, which competes with Hong Kong across several ranking lists and has lured some high-profile financial industry executives and Chinese billionaires in recent years.

In June, Hong Kong authorities proposed tax incentives targeting fund managers, including preferential treatment for performance-based bonuses and carried interest, as part of a broader effort to attract global asset-management talent.

Under the proposed framework, qualifying private equity funds approved by the Hong Kong Monetary Authority could see eligible carried interest taxed at 0 per cent – a stark contrast to the 24 per cent in Singapore and significantly higher rates in the US and Britain.

This follows the 2023 introduction of a preferential tax regime for family-owned investment holding vehicles, offering qualifying family offices a 0 per cent profits tax rate on eligible income to lower entry barriers for global wealth.

‘Beijing’s discretion’ and the fragile moat

While Beijing’s support is vital for the city, some have expressed concerns about it being a double-edged sword.

Christopher Marquis, a Chinese-management professor at Cambridge Judge Business School, argued that some of Hong Kong’s core strengths – such as currency convertibility – are not fully autonomous. “The privileges, of course, exist at Beijing’s discretion,” he said.

The city’s dominance is currently anchored in having the “world’s deepest renminbi liquidity pool outside the Chinese mainland”, with deposits of around 1 trillion yuan (US$147.4 billion), according to the Hong Kong Monetary Authority. The hub also handles more than 70 per cent of global offshore yuan payments and settlements.

If your moat is not fully under your control, can it still really be considered yours

Christopher Marquis, Cambridge Judge Business School

Although Beijing has reaffirmed its pledge to support Hong Kong’s role, analysts warn that these advantages could be under pressure. The city’s long-term pricing power in the offshore yuan market could be diluted as mainland China expands its offshore financial functions in Shanghai as part of a broader push to internationalise the currency.

Marquis argued that this made it all the more important for Hong Kong to strengthen the institutional foundations that underpin investor confidence, rather than relying solely on inherited structural advantages.

“If your moat is not fully under your control, can it still really be considered yours,” he asked.

Internationally, the rivalry between Beijing and Washington has served as a catalyst for Hong Kong’s IPO market, with analysts saying it offers an advantage over New York as a listing destination. However, appearing on the US radar is often accompanied with uncertainties.

Investor sentiment was further unsettled by comments in April 2025 from US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent that “everything is on the table”, regarding the possibility of Washington delisting Chinese companies from American stock exchanges as part of its trade negotiations, adding that it was up to US President Donald Trump to decide.

This prompted a number of firms, including Alibaba and Baidu, to reassess cross-border listing strategies. Alibaba owns the South China Morning Post.

Jacky Tang, chief investment officer for emerging markets at Deutsche Bank in Hong Kong, said the proximity to mainland China’s corporate ecosystem and investor base had become increasingly valuable, with global capital markets becoming more fragmented and geopolitical considerations playing a larger role in listing decisions.

“Hong Kong is shifting from ‘backup venue’ to ‘default international venue’ for a growing group of Chinese issuers,” Tang said.

A moat fed by more sources

However, the persistent reliance on mainland issuers has raised questions over whether Hong Kong can preserve its status as a truly global financial centre. Market participants suggest that this concentration is far from sufficient, and that long-term resilience requires a more diversified issuer and investor base.

“The dominance of mainland Chinese companies in Hong Kong’s IPO market is likely to continue in the medium term,” said Tommy Ong, managing director of T.O. & Associates Consultancy, describing it as “a natural result” of Beijing’s 2024 capital market guidelines.

Mainland Chinese companies accounted for more than 90 per cent of both total funds raised and deal volume in the first half of 2026, according to a Deloitte research report published in June.

Ong recommended that Hong Kong diversify both the geographical mix of its issuers and the range of products available in its capital markets, attracting more companies from Asia, particularly the Asean bloc.

Beyond equities, Hong Kong should leverage its strengths in bond and foreign-exchange markets by expanding products such as tokenised bonds to broaden the investor base, while developing offshore renminbi-denominated commodity futures to meet rising demand for risk-hedging from Chinese companies, Ong added.

Lee Siu-po, economist

The predictability premium

Since the implementation of the National Security Law in 2020, debates over Hong Kong’s institutional autonomy have intensified, with some investors reassessing long-term exposure to the city’s markets.

For many market participants, the central concern is predictability, with regulatory consistency increasingly seen as a core determinant of investor confidence.

Economist Simon Lee Siu-po said Hong Kong’s strength has long rested on its stable and rules-based environment, which allows global capital to be deployed with clarity.

But he warned that Beijing’s recent regulatory interventions, when viewed alongside broader geopolitical uncertainties, have made that predictability less certain.

“International investors, fearing their capital could be trapped or affected by sudden policy interventions, are losing the confidence required to hold Hong Kong assets over the long term,” Lee Siu-po said.

More recently, tighter oversight of cross-border capital flows and enforcement actions targeting certain forms of offshore trading have reinforced a more cautious stance among parts of the global investment community.

The economist argued that maintaining Hong Kong’s status would depend on reinforcing a transparent and stable regulatory framework that reduces uncertainty for long-term investors, rather than relying on short-term market sentiment.

“If policies are unpredictable and volatile, investors who commit significant capital will never feel secure,” he said.
