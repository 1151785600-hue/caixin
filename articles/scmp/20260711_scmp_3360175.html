Anwar, Anutin hit diplomatic high notes to mask Malaysia-Thailand tensions

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 840 words | 2026-07-11

After a rendition of My Way and a saxophone accompaniment that felt closer to crooning uncles at a wedding party than high-stakes diplomacy, Malaysian Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim and his Thai counterpart Anutin Charnvirakul announced the serious business of squashing a damaging trade row and bringing greater security to the porous border between the nations.

The pair, long-standing friends, also share a love for public spectacle, with Anutin playing the classic song popularised by Frank Sinatra on the saxophone while Anwar gripped the microphone and reached for the chorus high notes at a lunch on Thursday.

The Thai leader then took his turn to sing the Elvis Presley ballad Can’t Help Falling in Love as the leaders exchanged effusive praise of their friendship.

Calling Anutin a “dear friend” – and a swift responder to his WhatsApp messages – the Malaysian host said the two days of summitry were “meetings among old friends”.

“Is there a problem? Yeah. We’re going to solve it … When? Next week,” he said as an example of the neighbours’ ability to resolve issues quickly.

But behind the bonhomie, a weeks-long row over seafood and fish imports between the nations has vexed relations and severely damaged the Thai shrimp industry.

It has raised trade protectionism inside Southeast Asia just as the outside world is roiled by tariffs, war and fuel spikes.

The neighbours have not always seen eye to eye on issues of trade and security. Thailand’s southernmost provinces are wracked by a festering 20-year insurgency by Malay-Muslim rebels, who operate along the porous border between the countries. The border is also plagued by smuggling – both ways – with oil, wildlife, guns, drugs and people being trafficked.

But both governments agreed on an MOU to drop restrictions on each other’s fishery imports from next week, reducing an initial six-week tapered return to business as usual.

“We have resolved this outstanding issue of fisheries, we agreed that the memorandum should be effective in one week,” Anwar told reporters.

Thailand and Malaysia share a long southern border – kinship within the Asean bloc of nations – as well as a raft of challenges posed by the unsettled global outlook.

Both leaders agreed to cooperate more closely on border security issues and build new infrastructure – a second bridge at Sungai Golok to Rantau Panjang – and to revive a worn railway between the countries as part of what Thailand’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs said was a commitment to boosting bilateral trade to US$30 billion by next year.

Experts say the ease of relations hangs to a small degree on the leaders’ personal relationship.

“The My Way moment, and that kind of chemistry should not be dismissed,” said Andrea Passeri, a senior lecturer of international relations at Taylor’s University in Selangor.

“But I would not overstate it either. Personal chemistry can help create political goodwill and speed up problem-solving, as we saw with the seafood issue. Still, the deeper drivers are structural: trade, border connectivity, tourism, southern Thailand, food security and supply chains make Malaysia and Thailand important to each other regardless of who is in office.”

The two nations are also wrestling for foreign investment as the US-China trade war calcifies.

Malaysia had the edge with its domestic semiconductor production, Passeri said, but “Thailand has a deeper manufacturing base, a strong automotive ecosystem and better mainland Southeast Asian connectivity”.

“There is competition, but it is not necessarily toxic. In some areas, the two economies can still be complementary, especially if border connectivity improves. The real contest is over who captures the higher-value segments of regional supply chains,” he added.

For Anutin, who became prime minister barely six months ago, his now signature musical moments in Southeast Asian diplomacy have won warm praise at home.

“It has had the effect of buttressing Anutin’s image at home among his base,” said Ken Lohatepanont, a Thai politics expert.

“Anutin certainly looks like a much more loosened up prime minister compared to a conservative predecessor such as Prayuth Chan-ocha,” he added, referencing the junta leader turned prime minister who anchored Thai politics for a decade.

The seafood row between the neighbours – that saw tit-for-tat import freezes allegedly over safety standards – showed the “frictions” embedded within the relationship, Passeri added.

“The seafood row is a good example: at first it looked technical, but it quickly became politically sensitive because it touched farmers, market access and food safety,” he said.

With the deal to end the row still a week from taking effect, Thai shrimp farmers say the damage has already been done by what they describe as a sudden import ban issued by Malaysia.

“The only people benefiting from this ban are those who own warehouses and cold storage facilities,” said Preecha Sookasem, 60, owner of Sri Songkhla Shrimp Farm in the Thai border province of Songkhla.

“They’ve been able to buy shrimp at rock-bottom prices because we can no longer export, and now they’re selling them at much higher prices.”
