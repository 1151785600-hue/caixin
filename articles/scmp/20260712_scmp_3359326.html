Why Citigroup CEO Jane Fraser left the US-China summit feeling optimistic

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1851 words | 2026-07-12

For Citigroup’s global CEO Jane Fraser, her time in Beijing last month meant far more than a handshake and photograph with heads of state.

Fraser was among 17 business leaders US President Donald Trump invited to China as part of his three-day visit to Beijing from May 13 to May 15. Standing on the western steps outside the Great Hall of the People – an enormous building near Tiananmen Square that is the primary venue for China’s legislative activities, diplomatic receptions and state banquets – Fraser took in one of China’s most iconic panoramas.

Amid analyst caution about the structural tensions shaping the China-US relationship, some saw the meeting as little more than good optics. To Fraser, such takes missed the big picture, one that was broader and more encouraging.

“Two important powers in the world were engaging with each other, making sure there was an understanding of each other’s points of view,” she said in an exclusive interview.

As China courted foreign investment and its companies pursued global expansions, the summit reaffirmed stabilisation and collaboration as shared priorities, Fraser said. That meant China would remain a strategic imperative for Citi and many other firms.

“In the room, it felt much more like a meeting of equals,” Fraser said during an Asia trip late last month, before flying to Beijing again to meet clients and senior government leaders. There was recognition of China’s importance as a leader in many industries, and therefore “the discussion was very much about stabilisation”, she said, adding that there was “real investment” on both sides towards improving the economic relationship.

The number of US leaders at the summit, the support of the Trump administration for US companies engaging with China and the support of the Chinese government were all strong positive signs, she added.

The summit marked the seventh face-to-face meeting between Trump and President Xi Jinping. Focused on the US-Israel war on Iran, trade and the status of Taiwan, among other issues, it was also the first trip by a US leader to China since 2017. Following the meetings, both nations committed to establishing a “constructive relationship of strategic stability”, and the two leaders agreed to meet in the US in September.

While Fraser did not deny tensions between the two countries, she said she was encouraged to see their commitment to healthy ties.

“There are areas where there is a decoupling that has occurred, but instead of the word ‘de-risking’, we heard the word ‘collaboration’ all the time on both sides,” Fraser said. “I think that’s good for the world.”

Fraser, 58, is often referred to as the most powerful woman on Wall Street. Born in St Andrews, Scotland, she studied economics at Cambridge University. Before joining Citi, she was a partner at McKinsey & Company. She was named chair of Citi’s board in October. She is married with two children.

Last year, her pay climbed to US$42 million, elevating her into the ranks of the best-paid bosses on Wall Street as investors cheered her efforts to restructure the group’s businesses and streamline its management. Citi’s stock has gained 101 per cent since she took the helm in March 2021, and the company’s value is now around US$238.7 billion. In the latest recognition of her services to the global financial sector, she was awarded a damehood during King Charles III’s birthday honours last month.

Fraser’s presence at the Beijing summit highlighted the strategic importance of the Chinese market for the global bank, as well as its active pursuit of a wholly owned securities licence in China. The licence would permit Citi to directly operate an onshore brokerage and investment banking business in mainland China, allowing it to compete against already licensed peers like JP Morgan, Goldman Sachs and UBS.

Beijing reportedly approved the licence during the summit, after a four-year process, but it has not yet been issued.

“I’m very hopeful we do get it, because the role we play in connecting China and the world, the world and China, it requires more of the breadth and depth of the local capital market and capabilities,” Fraser said. “It would be beneficial for our Chinese clients.”

Fraser said the banking group was focused on serving Chinese companies looking to expand overseas as well as clients with cross-border needs, adding that many of her bank’s international clients, including those from the US, had been showing growing interest in China and its world-leading companies in many industries.

In the first four months of this year, 20,113 new foreign-invested enterprises were established in China, representing a 6.8 per cent year-on-year increase, according to data from the Ministry of Commerce. By source, investment flows into China grew significantly from Luxembourg (up 110 per cent), Switzerland (61 per cent), France (58 per cent) and the US (25 per cent).

“We see a lot of the American companies recognising that if you don’t know what’s happening in China and don’t understand where that is … then you are going to be irrelevant,” she said. “It doesn’t necessarily mean to say that they’ll be as competitive as the Chinese companies, but they want to understand.”

Citi is known as the “flower flag” bank in China, a nickname that originated in 1902 when locals thought the stars on the American flag flown above the first Shanghai branch of Citi predecessor International Banking Corporation looked like a representation of flowers.

The bank has shifted its strategy to primarily focus on institutional banking, capital markets and corporate services. It has largely completed an exit from 14 international consumer markets, including mainland China, India and Taiwan. Two years ago, it completed the sale of its mainland consumer wealth portfolio to HSBC.

Citi has advised on a number of big cross-border transactions in the last 12 months, including acting as an exclusive financial adviser to US-based General Mills in the sale of its Haagen-Dazs shops in mainland China to an investor group including China-based lemon tea chain Ningji.

Today, Citi serves 70 per cent of the Fortune 500 companies operating in China, as well as over 300 local enterprises. In the last five years, the bank helped raise more than US$300 billion for Chinese clients from global capital markets, according to data from analytics platform Dealogic.

China stepped up policy support for cross-border financing in May by raising the leverage ratios for overseas lending by foreign banks in the country. The move aimed to promote trade and investment as well as accelerate the global use of the yuan amid rising demand for cross-border financial services as Chinese companies quicken their pace of overseas expansion.

The People’s Bank of China, the National Financial Regulatory Administration, the China Securities Regulatory Commission and the State Administration of Foreign Exchange announced major financial measures separately at the Lujiazui Financial Forum last month.

“Obviously, for Chinese clients – as China goes more global – it is very useful to have the renminbi internationalised and to make sure they have the asset base behind the needs and ability to hedge,” Fraser said. “Given the size of China and the globalisation of Chinese companies, the internationalisation of the renminbi is something clients want and need, and we’ll be there to support them.”

While at the moment the US dollar was “in debt” it would remain the reserve currency “for a long time”, she said.

Looking ahead, Fraser said the increase in cross-border trade and Chinese companies going global was “a major trend that will not change”. Changes in money flows would create more opportunity in Asia, including mainland China and Hong Kong, she added.

The world was seeing increasing flows between China and other regions – including the Middle East and Latin America – mediated through Hong Kong, as well as important flows to the United States and Europe, according to Fraser.

“China has enormous scale, but it’s still young in developing that scale,” she said, adding that clients wanted to use Hong Kong as a springboard to mainland China, ensuring the city would “play an absolutely critical role” in the new phase of globalisation.

Fraser emphasised the bank’s long commitment to the city, which she said had a special place in her heart. “We’re unique in having a 124-year history in Hong Kong,” she said.

Hong Kong had a “long and deep history of being one of the big global hubs and [financial] centres,” Fraser said, highlighting the city’s talent base, infrastructure and regulatory framework as a solid ecosystem.

The city was “not just a gateway to China” but a “China plus”, providing access to where “all the new innovations are coming from and how they are being applied”, she said.

Last year, Citi took part as a primary liquidity provider in the enhanced offshore yuan bond repo scheme introduced by the Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA) and the PBOC, acting as the only US bank among the 11 dealers in the programme.

As Citi continues to develop digital asset solutions to meet the market’s needs, Fraser praised Hong Kong’s technological innovations and regulatory framework to support such developments. With Beijing’s support, she said the HKMA was doing well in establishing frameworks for many innovations that would shape the future financial system, citing the successful tokenised bond issuances.

“What I really appreciate here is the regulation is forward-looking,” she said.

The city made history by launching the world’s first tokenised government green bond in 2023. This was followed by the issuance of the world’s first multicurrency digital bond in 2024, as well as the largest digital bond issuance at the time of issuance and the first digital bond to integrate tokenised central bank money in the form of e-CNY and e-HKD in 2025.

A lot of Chinese wealth is flowing through Hong Kong, as the city’s market offers sophistication, breadth and depth, as well as investment access to different global opportunities in the region and beyond, Fraser said.

She noted that family offices from China and around the world were setting up in Hong Kong, and said Citi planned to expand its wealth-management business to serve them.

During her trip to Hong Kong in late June, Fraser met Victor Fung Kwok-king, group chairman of the Li & Fung group of companies, which Citi has served for 117 years. They discussed the next phase of reinvention, including how the bank could support the group on topics including blockchain, logistics, AI and digitisation.

In the first quarter of this year, Citi’s net income jumped 42 per cent year on year to US$5.8 billion on US$24.6 billion in revenue, the highest quarterly trading haul in a decade.

“What we have proven in the last four years is not only could we do all of that restructuring of the organisation, but we grew,” she said, adding that there was a lot of growth and upside ahead, which should make investors “very happy”.

“Our competitors should be fearful,” Fraser said, adding she planned to celebrate her damehood with her family following her packed Asia trip.
