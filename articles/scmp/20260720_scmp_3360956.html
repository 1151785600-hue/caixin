China’s tech giants fuel the rise of AI tokens as a new corporate currency

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1811 words | 2026-07-20

Every month, a Beijing-based ByteDance employee says he burns through close to a billion units of a new corporate currency – one that cannot buy a coffee or pay rent.

Yet, as he noted, that staggering volume “doesn’t even put me near the top of the consumption rankings in my department”.

That currency is not yuan, but artificial intelligence tokens.

“Using AI has become part of almost every task now,” said the employee, who requested anonymity as he was not authorised by the company to speak to the media.

A token – dubbed ciyuan in mainland China – is the basic unit through which AI services are measured and billed. Every prompt submitted to a model and every response generated consumes tokens, much like electricity is measured in kilowatt-hours.

As Chinese tech giants from ByteDance to Alibaba Group Holding and Tencent Holdings rush to encourage staff to integrate AI into daily workflows, tokens have become a new kind of corporate allowance. Employees are no longer just budgeting their time; they are also expected to budget their token use as proof of their AI literacy.

This surge in AI use in the corporate sector is being fuelled by a brutal price war. When Hangzhou-based AI unicorn DeepSeek slashed the price of its flagship V4-Pro model by 75 per cent in May, it highlighted the defining feature of China’s AI industry: an accelerating race to make intelligence cheap.

The overarching logic is that as token costs plummet, a broader economy built around ubiquitous AI adoption can finally take shape.

The token surge

For decades, Chinese companies bought software through fixed, predictable subscriptions – often called “per-seat” licensing. If a company bought 500 licences for an office software suite, the monthly bill remained the same whether an employee typed one sentence or 1,000 pages.

AI billing, however, scales proportionally with usage. While a short question might cost a regular user a few dozen tokens, a software engineer scanning an extensive corporate spreadsheet or building a computer program can burn through millions at a time.

The exact price of a token varies by the model. DeepSeek, for instance, later reversed course and raised its prices, charging 12 yuan (US$1.77) for every million output tokens during peak hours and 6 yuan during non-peak time for API access to its latest V4 models.

To adapt, Chinese tech companies are experimenting with new allocation methods.

In May, cybersecurity firm 360 Security Technology launched a companywide initiative to directly distribute a total of 100 million AI tokens across employee accounts, entirely eliminating managerial approval and reimbursement procedures.

Founder Zhou Hongyi has repeatedly argued publicly that AI tools must be integrated into ordinary workflows, rather than restricted mostly to specialised engineering teams.

In March, Tencent, developer of the Hunyuan model, handed out free tokens to employees through products such as QClaw, an internal AI tool. During testing, some individual quotas reportedly reached as high as 40 million tokens per day.

Alibaba, the Hangzhou-based e-commerce giant, launched a similar internal incentive scheme the same month, granting token allowances for in-house tools like its agentic platform Wukong and coding assistant Qoder.

Internal adoption is expected to climb further after Alibaba’s security team banned the use of Anthropic’s Claude Code earlier this month, citing data security risks.

Alibaba owns the South China Morning Post.

Rise of the token economy

This major corporate push has contributed to a massive explosion in token usage across the country.

According to the National Data Administration (NDA), China’s annual AI token consumption surpassed 140 trillion in March, up from 100 billion two years earlier. That is equivalent to roughly 100,000 tokens per person based on the country’s population of about 1.4 billion.

The [Chinese] digital economy has gone through the stages of the data economy and the computing economy. Today, the token economy is emerging

Yin Hao, Chinese Academy of Sciences

In May, Premier Li Qiang said average daily token consumption in the country had reached hundreds of trillions by the end of the month.

Top Chinese policymakers have openly touted the emergence of a “token economy”.

When NDA head Liu Liehong convened executives from top domestic companies including Alibaba, Tencent and China International Capital Corporation at a token-economy symposium in May, he described tokens as the new accounting and settlement units for AI services.

At the China Internet Conference in Beijing last week, Yin Hao, an academician at the Chinese Academy of Sciences, said, “The [Chinese] digital economy has gone through the stages of the data economy and the computing economy. Today, the token economy is emerging.”

Tokens were more than just a technical metric, they had become a new production factor connecting AI model capability, computing resources and commercial value, he said.

China Unicom viewed “token operation” as an important driver of efficiency across its businesses, company vice-president Qiu Baohua said in a speech at the conference.

Qiu said Unicom’s growing AI token consumption mirrored the expanding use of AI across its business.

AI agents had improved document drafting and contract retrieval efficiency by 70 per cent, he said, while AI now handled more than 85 per cent of calls to Unicom’s customer service hotline, reducing annual operating costs by more than 100 million yuan (US$14.8 million).

Other industry leaders also expressed optimism about the future of the token economy, with Huawei Technologies vice-president and head of data communication products Wang Lei saying the company believed it was “the greatest opportunity we can be certain of in an otherwise uncertain era”.

The developments reflect a growing belief among policymakers and technology companies that AI output is becoming an economic resource in its own right. As model usage spreads across coding, research, customer service and content creation, tokens are increasingly being used to measure, allocate and price access to that resource.

“Tokens are effectively the energy unit of the AI era,” said Zhou Zhengang, research vice-president at IDC China.

“Just as companies in the industrial era budgeted for electricity, companies in the AI era will have to budget for tokens,” added Lu Yanxia, research director at the market intelligence firm. “They are becoming a financial metric rather than simply a technical one.”

IDC’s latest market tracking report, published in May, projected that China’s model-as-a-service (MaaS) market would process 40,000 trillion tokens in 2026, an increase of about 20 times versus 2025.

The surveillance shift

The rapid expansion of token allowances, however, has also created a new flashpoint for corporate strife: unprecedented managerial surveillance. As AI usage surges across entire companies, bosses have gained unprecedented visibility into how employees work.

Some companies are now linking token consumption directly to employee performance evaluations.

Beijing-based internet company Kunlun Tech announced in February it would provide engineers access to top US coding AI agents, including OpenAI’s Codex and Anthropic’s Claude Code, while evaluating employees based on how many tokens they consumed.

This mirrors controversial productivity tracking methods seen in the US.

According to internal reports from late last year, Meta Platforms, the parent of Facebook and Instagram, briefly used an internal dashboard known as the “Claudeonomics” leaderboard to track AI token consumption across more than 85,000 employees before shutting it down after internal statistics were leaked.

Amazon similarly retired “KiroRank”, an internal leaderboard that ranked developers by AI activity. Some employees had been inflating token usage through unnecessary agent tasks to fake productivity, spiking compute costs without delivering real output, the Financial Times reported in late May.

Similar tensions are beginning to emerge in China.

Since June, Tencent has tightened internal AI allowances, requiring employees to seek fresh approval every two weeks for additional tokens. Quotas have been slashed to less than half of previous levels and are now increasingly tied to specific job functions and workloads, according to an employee working on AI agents.

Tencent did not respond to a request for comment.

For tech enterprises in both the US and China, the primary drivers are the same: intense financial pressure to increase engineering efficiency, speed up product releases and drive down development costs.

While corporate executives often pitch the token economy as a way to liberate workers from boring, repetitive tasks, some employees describe a more complicated reality. Part of the reason is that AI tools are not yet reliable enough to fully automate entire workflows.

Employees from various Chinese companies have reported a distinct gap between corporate optimisation targets and reality. AI tools remain prone to hallucinations – instances where models generate incorrect or fabricated data – meaning heavy token usage often translates to more oversight work for humans, not less.

“Cursor and Codex are basically used by everyone,” said a software engineer in Nvidia’s IT Software Engineering group in Shanghai, referring to popular AI-assisted coding tools. “But that does not mean the workload is lighter. I spend more time reviewing AI-generated output now.”

China’s state-owned telecoms giants are looking to monetise the AI boom by introducing tokens as a new unit of measurement in monthly bills, after having billed users by the gigabyte of data for years.

In May, China Telecom unveiled nationwide, token-based pricing packages on its official app, targeting everyone from enterprise developers to casual smartphone users.

Business packages, which support coding and AI agent deployment, range from 39.9 yuan to 299.9 yuan per month, with token allowances from 15 million to 250 million. Consumer packages, tailored for everyday tasks, start at 9.9 yuan per month for 10 million tokens and increase in price and volume to 49.9 yuan for 80 million tokens.

Beyond ‘big tech’

As the cost of running AI models continues to drop, the token economy is reshaping ecosystems beyond corporate cubicles – namely, venture capital and higher education.

In the start-up world, the traditional practice of exchanging cash for corporate equity is being supplemented by a more immediate need: access to raw compute.

In March, prominent Chinese venture capital firm ZhenFund, in partnership with start-up developer community Crossing, launched an initiative dubbed “Token Grant”.

Instead of offering standard cash financing, the programme awards early-stage AI developers 50,000 yuan worth of direct model-access credits. The initiative specifically targets solo founders and prototype-stage teams who are building AI agents or multimodal products before they approach institutional investors for cash.

“For early-stage developers, token credits often matter more immediately than cash itself,” said an angel investor, who requested anonymity because they were not authorised to speak to the media. “A small team with sufficient compute access can now prototype agents, generate code, process video and test products without hiring large engineering teams.”

The replacement of cash with tokens has even reached the halls of higher education. In May, during a public panel discussion with Moonshot AI president Zhang Yutong, Tian Xuan, the dean of Peking University’s Guanghua School of Management, suggested that universities might eventually “stop giving scholarships and distribute more tokens instead”.
