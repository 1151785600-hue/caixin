Iran war is no longer a contest over just the Strait of Hormuz

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 905 words | 2026-07-13

Early on July 12, after Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz closed until further notice and struck a Cyprus-flagged container ship, the United States launched its third round of strikes in a week, hitting some 140 targets. Most were along Iran’s southern coast overlooking the strait – including Bandar Abbas, Sirik, Jask and Qeshm – but one strike days earlier pointed to a different logic.

On July 9, US cruise missiles hit the Aq Taqeh Khan bridge in Aq Qala, in the northern province of Golestan, an attack confirmed by the Revolutionary Guard’s provincial command. It was not a coastal military site but a node on the overland corridor linking Iran to Central Asia, Russia and China.

During the naval blockade that followed the war’s outbreak in February, it was one of the routes through which Iranian trade kept moving when the sea lanes closed. Striking the bridge signalled that Washington is no longer only contesting Iran’s control of the strait, it is starting to target the infrastructure that lets Iran function when the strait is contested.

The confrontation is expanding from sea lanes to supply chains. To see why, it helps to separate the layers of leverage. The first is maritime. Under the US-Iran June memorandum of understanding, Iran was to arrange safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz. Tehran has read that as a mandate to manage the waterway itself, requiring vessels to obtain authorisation from the Revolutionary Guard navy and use routes it designates. Ships attempting the southern lane, closer to Oman, have reportedly been fired on.

For Washington, this is precisely the outcome the memorandum was meant to prevent and why it cannot let it stand. A strait managed by Iran is one through which Tehran can extract concessions or impose costs in future crises, blunting the sanctions instrument that has defined US pressure for years.

The second layer is logistical. When the sea is closed, trade moves overland. Iran’s rail links north and east are the arteries of that alternative, and they connect directly to the Chinese and Russian networks that have grown more active as maritime access narrowed. This is why the Golestan strike matters beyond Iran.

The corridor it serves is a strand of the Eurasian connectivity architecture Beijing has spent years building, and on which Moscow has leaned since 2022. To strike it is to send a message that extends past Tehran: the routes designed to bypass Western-controlled shipping are not beyond reach.

The third layer is resilience. Iranian authorities said the Golestan line was back in service in about a day. The claim is hard to verify independently, but its significance lies in what it represents rather than the exact timeline. In a prolonged confrontation, destroying infrastructure is rarely decisive; what matters is the speed of reconstitution.

A network repaired faster than it is degraded imposes a different cost calculus on the attacker. The capacity to absorb and recover from disruption is itself a component of national power, measured in supply chains as much as in arsenals.

Together, these layers mark a shift in the geography of coercion. For years, US pressure on Iran centred on keeping its oil off the market. If the recent pattern of targets is any guide, that pressure is migrating from energy to the connectivity domain, from what Iran sells to how it moves what it sells and how it moves everything else when the usual channels are blocked. This is a broader, more ambitious target – and a more exposed one.

That exposure is what Beijing will watch most closely. China is the largest stakeholder in the stability of both the Persian Gulf and the overland corridors, with more to lose from their disruption than the US. Roughly a fifth of global seaborne oil passes through the strait, with much of it heading east.

If maritime access becomes chronically unreliable and the overland alternatives are drawn in as well, Beijing faces a squeeze on both fronts. The likely response is accelerated investment in redundancy, alternative routes and the strategic reserves that cushion against precisely this shock, for a state that has watched Washington move from Venezuela’s oil to Iran’s sea lanes to its railways in a matter of months.

It also exposes the limits of Washington’s strategy. A campaign confined to coastal military sites is sustainable. One that expands to the logistical spine of a country of 90 million people is not. It carries regional costs, as this weekend’s exchanges across the Persian Gulf showed, reaching bases in neighbouring states.

It carries economic costs as insurers reprice risk and shipping thins. It carries strategic costs because a prolonged effort to sever an adversary’s connective tissue, without a decisive result, tests the credibility of American power before the very audience that is measuring it.

Diplomacy has not vanished. Omani and Qatari mediators continue to shuttle proposals, but the sticking point is now clearly defined: the management of the strait and whether passage runs on Iran’s terms or around them. That is a narrow dispute with wide consequences, and nothing in the past week suggests it is near resolution.

It is becoming clear that this is no longer only a contest over the Strait of Hormuz but over the infrastructure that lets countries keep functioning when Hormuz itself is in play. The war is moving inland, and that is where its outcome could ultimately be decided.
