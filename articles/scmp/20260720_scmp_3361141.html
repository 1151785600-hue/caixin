Hong Kong restaurants score extra HK$500 million thanks to World Cup

SCMP | FREE | 1041 words | 2026-07-20

The World Cup brought in an extra HK$500 million (US$63.8 million) for Hong Kong’s catering sector this year, an industry leader has said, after restaurants and shopping centres hosted overnight viewings of the final with dim sum, snacks and milk tea.

Businesses were also urged on Monday to devise creative strategies and set competitive prices to maintain the momentum from the city’s World Cup fever.

The final between Spain and Argentina drew crowds of football fans, including children and the elderly, who flocked to shopping centres at 3am – packing every floor of Tuen Mun Town Plaza, Olympian City, MCP in Tseung Kwan O and Kai Tak Mall.

Simon Wong Ka-wo, president of the Hong Kong Federation of Restaurants and Related Trades, estimated that the catering industry had pocketed an additional HK$500 million between June 30 and Monday, representing an uptick of about 6 per cent from its initial estimate in July.

In Tuen Mun Town Plaza, cheers erupted as Lionel Messi and Lamine Yamal appeared on a 540-inch screen, building to a crescendo when Spain clinched victory 1-0 after extra time.

Stalls and restaurants sought to capitalise on the crowds, setting up booths nearby to offer bubble tea or delivering dim sum directly to the viewing area.

Supermarket chain ParknShop stayed open overnight, selling drinks and fruit, while some spectators ordered food deliveries to the venue.

Many vendors offered promotions such as “buy three, get one free” to attract customers. Taxis also lined up at the mall’s entrance, ready to pick up passengers after the match.

Nini Man, manager of Nap Tea’s Tuen Mun branch, said the World Cup pop-up stand had only started turning a profit in the last two matches after the spot opened for a trial run on July 3.

She said she was inspired by the strong turnout at the mall for the Brazil-Japan match earlier in the tournament. As more vendors joined, the screenings evolved into a plaza-wide event.

“It was worth continuing [during the World Cup],” she said.

Man added that the exposure had helped introduce the shop to nearby residents who had never visited before.

“We have become like old neighbourhood friends,” she said, referring to the rare opportunity to interact with customers face-to-face.

Terence Chan Tak-ming, general manager of the portfolio leasing department at Henderson Land, said MCP recorded double-digit growth in both foot traffic and sales turnover throughout the World Cup.

He noted that tenants offering sports, lifestyle retail goods, or food and drinks had performed particularly well, creating business opportunities outside traditional peak hours.

In addition to vendors offering snacks and drinks ranging from coffee to craft beer, MCP also arranged free shuttle buses to Central and Mong Kok to help fans travel directly to work on Monday morning after watching the final.

Catering sector leader Wong said that while the significant time difference between Hong Kong and North America had dampened the usual business boost over the match period, many fans still visited restaurants and shopping centres to watch the final.

Venues raking in extra business included traditional Chinese dim sum restaurants.

“Restaurants need new directions in operations to attract new clientele. For important matches in the future, now that large screens have been installed, restaurants can roll out different activities and discounts to attract customers,” Wong said.

Secretary for Culture, Sports and Tourism Rosanna Law Shuk-pui, who watched the final at Kai Tak Mall, said that even with mega-events, businesses need to think creatively about strategies to attract customers.

She highlighted the emerging trend of traditional dim sum restaurants live-streaming World Cup matches as a successful example.

“I used to think watching a match in a dim sum restaurant might seem out of place, but it is actually feasible,” Law said. “That is why everyone has to rack their brains and examine their own strengths to discover more business opportunities.”

She added that a proactive approach from these businesses would help boost the economy.

Economist Simon Lee Siu-po said he believed business among restaurants and retailers would return to baseline levels after the World Cup, given lukewarm nighttime spending sentiment.

He urged the catering industry to be transparent and creative to attract patrons, citing the example of Lin Heung Lau’s Sheung Wan branch.

The restaurant rolled out an HK$3,888 eight-person VIP package and a HK$368 set for singles for the final match, with tea and service charges included.

Lee said the operator addressed the two pain points of Chinese restaurants by setting out their prices clearly and catering to the needs of solo diners.

“Hong Kong has too many options. With a competitive price and quality food, patrons will be interested,” he said, adding that restaurants could offer smaller meal portions to attract those dining alone.

Among those drawn to the city’s shopping malls for the final match’s “vibrant” atmosphere was Tuen Mun resident Shannon Dias, 42, who came to support Spain.

“A lot of people are here for both teams. It feels like a stadium – it’s better to be here,” he said.

Although he had eaten before the match, Dias said services by vendors at the shopping centre were “pretty good”.

“They’re doing their best to set up shop, especially for an event like this,” he said. “It definitely helps boost the economy.”

Another fan, Kit Ching, attended with his family after seeing promotions on social media.

Despite the crowds, he said he enjoyed the atmosphere and spent about HK$50 on drinks and snacks from ParknShop before kick-off.

“It was good to have something to eat while watching,” he said.

But some Argentina supporters left disappointed after the final whistle.

McQueen Zeng, who watched the match with friends, said it was a “pity” Argentina could not defend their title.

“I applaud Spain, and I also applaud our Argentinian players because they kept making efforts and trying to score,” he said. “I’m grateful to them for that.”

Zeng added that he would continue to support the team and hoped to watch a future World Cup in person, noting that Argentina, Paraguay and Uruguay would co-host the opening three centenary matches for the 2030 Fifa tournament.
