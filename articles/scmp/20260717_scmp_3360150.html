Southeast Asian scammers’ new disguise: your leader’s face

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1758 words | 2026-07-17

There is a bogus version of Singapore’s prime minister that exists only to steal money. It connected to a Zoom call, thanked a stranger for his time and helped relieve him of US$3.8 million – with the real Lawrence Wong, for a time, none the wiser about what had happened.

The victim had received a WhatsApp message purporting to be from the secretary to the cabinet, inviting him to a private meeting.

What followed was not some doctored photo or a 15-second clip designed to fool a distracted doomscroll. It was an entire fabricated summit, complete with assembled officials including Wong, President Tharman Shanmugaratnam, cabinet minister Indranee Rajah and foreign dignitaries, all rendered convincingly enough to believe they were discussing funding tied to the Strait of Hormuz.

The synthetic Wong closed the meeting by thanking the victim for attending. Then a fake lawyer took the call and S$4.9 million (US$3.8 million) vanished into an account that no longer exists.

Southeast Asia’s scam industry has found a powerful new weapon, and it isn’t malware, cunning or even greed – it’s borrowed faces.

Criminals are increasingly cloning the region’s most trusted leaders using generative AI, wrapping fraud in the unimpeachable authority of a president’s voice or a prime minister’s visage and cashing in on the thing no security system can patch: human trust.

In Indonesia, a similar con surfaced within days of Prabowo Subianto’s 2024 election win, when an Instagram clip began circulating in which he appeared to ask viewers: “Who hasn’t received aid from me? What are your needs right now?”

The incoming president had said no such thing. But the voice was his, the face was his and for many, that was enough. Across 20 provinces, an unknown number of Indonesians each wired scammers “administrative fees” of up to 1 million rupiah (US$55), chasing government aid that was never coming and never had been.

In the Philippines, the fraud wore Ferdinand Marcos Jnr’s face, superimposed onto footage endorsing an online trading scheme with a polish good enough to convince one dentist to hand over 93 million pesos (US$1.5 million) before the illusion broke.

Three countries. Three cons. One unsettling realisation: artificial intelligence has stopped merely helping scammers write better emails. It has started manufacturing their credibility from scratch.

“If you’re not trained to look at it and you don’t have the discernment to know that it’s impossible for the president to be endorsing investment schemes, you will believe it,” said Art Samaniego, a Manila-based tech journalist and co-founder of ScamWatch Pilipinas. “Because you can clearly see the president’s face and he appears to be speaking.”

Old con, new face

An Interpol report released in June identified AI-driven crime as one of Southeast Asia’s most urgent emerging threats, warning of criminal networks that are growing leaner and more organised as technology races ahead of regulation.

From January 2024 to March 2025, it found “phishing” attacks – where scammers send fake emails, messages, or links to trick victims into sharing sensitive data – and related swindles were the costliest form of cybercrime across Asia and the South Pacific, with a third of countries logging more than 10,000 cases apiece.

“These include the use of deepfake videos to impersonate public figures, celebrities, or business executives,” it said. “Such schemes have resulted in significant financial losses and have targeted both individuals and institutions.”

On criminal forums and Telegram channels, chatter about deepfakes surged 600 per cent in just one five-month period in 2024, the report found.

But strip away the technology and the con itself is nothing new. All the AI does, experts say, is play on the same old human weaknesses – fear, hope, desperation, deference to power – but with a more believable face.

“Many people are raised to respect authority,” said Jun Angelo Suglao, a clinical psychologist who chairs the Psychological Association of the Philippines’ mental health support group.

“So if someone calls and can impersonate someone we inherently perceive as good or trustworthy, our default instinct is to comply, not to doubt them.”

The elderly made for some of the easiest targets, Samaniego said, as many had been conditioned over long lifetimes to trust a phone call at face value.

But no one is immune from scammers, especially as the technology becomes more advanced. Voice cloning adds a crueller dimension still, mimicking the reassuring tones – or panicked cries – of a loved one.

“Hearing someone you care for cry for help triggers an immediate fight-or-flight response, completely shutting down your rational thinking,” Suglao said. “You act on pure emotion.”

Nobody understands that better than a Filipino domestic worker identified only as Maria, who reportedly believed she had found love with a Dubai prince on a dating app.

He wrote constantly. He placed video calls, warm and attentive, present in every way that mattered. But he was never real.

By the time Maria saw through the facade, she had sent 100,000 pesos – a year’s wages – for a “marriage certificate” and a fictitious “royal membership card” meant to secure her a job in Dubai, Agence France-Presse reported earlier this month.

“They prey on isolated individuals,” Suglao said of the scammers. “Slowly building a deep emotional connection with the goal of scamming them out of money.”

AI has not changed the criminals’ cruelty. It has simply given it a face worth falling for.

Tricking the experts

Jeremy Douglas, deputy director of operations at the UN Office on Drugs and Crime, confirmed to This Week in Asia that AI and other tech tools were increasingly being integrated into scam operations across Southeast Asia and further afield.

“It’s classic organised crime behaviour, innovating to accelerate business and stay ahead of law enforcement,” he said. “The use of deepfake images and videos, and mimicking or copying voice intonation and patterns of partners and contacts are on the rise and we can expect more sophisticated use going forward.”

Neal Jetton, who leads Interpol’s cybercrime directorate, pointed to estimates that global cybercrime losses hit US$10.5 trillion in 2025, with a 142 per cent rise forecast by the end of this decade.

“Cybercrime exists wherever anyone is using a smartphone or laptop … it is a global problem,” Jetton said, adding that AI had become “one of the main drivers”.

What makes the scams so effective, according to Interpol, is their reliance on social engineering techniques, exploiting human trust rather than technical vulnerabilities.

This has transformed so-called romance baiting into a booming subsector all of its own, part of a cyberfraud economy the UN Office on Drugs and Crime estimates has cost the world up to US$37 billion and counting.

The method rarely changes: weeks or months of patient, tender contact, before the big ask arrives.

Analysts are careful not to overstate the novelty, however. AI has not built a new criminal underworld, it has retooled an old one – at speed.

“These capabilities are enabling organised crime syndicates to radically change scam and associated money-laundering businesses,” Douglas said, adding that such operations were “professionalising, using fewer people to run or perpetuate scams” and sometimes switching jurisdictions or shifting from the notorious large scam compounds “into a model of networked locations”.

Samaniego has watched and reported on the retooling happening in real time.

“Before, if an email had wrong grammar, you could automatically tell it was a scam,” he said. “Now you can no longer identify it that way, because their grammar has become so good, because they’re using AI to create fake links and fake websites.”

“AI is not replacing cybercriminals, it’s making them more efficient … Their efficiency has doubled or tripled.”

Even seasoned professionals occasionally fall foul of the scammers’ tactics.

Jetton recalled hearing of a cybercrime expert who clicked on a fraudulent email disguised as an Uber receipt, simply because he had recently used the e-hailing app.

“Awareness campaigns are helping, but we’re still seeing the human element – desperation, need, naivety. And the phishing emails are so good now,” he said, citing a Microsoft report that found a human was 4½ times more likely to click on a phishing email created by AI.

John Wojcik, senior threat researcher for Asia at cybersecurity firm Infoblox, said there had been “rapid acceleration” in the kinds of AI tools that scammers were using.

“From deepfakes, lifelike chatbots and complex, nuanced machine translation, to automated scam outreach, domain registration and phishing attacks, it’s clear this technology is being rolled out at scale within this criminal ecosystem,” he said, fed by a surge in “malware-as-a-service” kits sold on the dark web.

Doubting what you see

There is no silver bullet solution, Jetton concedes – only what he calls a “whole-of-society effort”.

And as AI-generated content edges towards the indistinguishable, old methods of detecting fakes, such as listening for a stilted phrase or watching for an unnatural blink, are fast losing relevance.

“As AI reaches human-level output, traditional visual or audio cues are becoming unreliable,” Samaniego said.

The only defence that still held, he said, was verification through a separate channel entirely.

“If you receive an urgent or high-stakes request, even if it looks or sounds exactly like someone you know, do not respond to the message or caller directly,” Samaniego said.

“If someone claiming to be your friend asks for money on social media, call him. If he texts, use other channels. Best if you can verify in person.”

His best advice for countering the scammers? Forcing them “off script”.

“AI models and scam scripts are excellent at handling expected flows but often falter when faced with unpredictable, human-centric interruptions,” he said. “Ask a question that other people won’t know the answer to.”

Suglao’s solution, meanwhile, was more community-focused.

“If a suspicious investment opportunity or a sudden emergency request comes up, run it by a trusted peer first,” he said. “Talking it out disrupts the high-pressure environment the scammer relies on.”

For ScamWatch Pilipinas’ Samaniego, pre-emptive measures such as strong cyber literacy are the best defence.

“Hearing or seeing someone talk online is no longer sufficient proof that what you’re seeing is genuine or real,” he said. “Because of AI, we all need new ways to verify identity.”

What his cybersecurity advocacy movement wanted, Samaniego said, was for people to already be educated “before the scam occurs” – so they never had the misfortune to become victims in the first place.
