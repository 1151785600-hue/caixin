Nurses baffled by closed medicine tube to premature baby, inquest hears

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 654 words | 2026-07-12

An infusion tube delivering medicine to a premature baby at a Hong Kong public hospital was closed for unknown reasons shortly before her death three years ago, an inquest has heard.

Two nurses testified at the Coroner’s Court on Friday about the circumstances of what Prince of Wales Hospital in Sha Tin acknowledged as a “serious untoward event” on June 13, 2023.

Both witnesses said they had no idea why a three-way valve controlling the flow of medicine to the baby was closed, even though it had been working properly and no alarm had gone off.

One of them, who was primarily responsible for caring for the newborn, also said she resigned two days later as she came under immense pressure amid scathing media criticism.

The deceased, Angel Yu Pui-fei, was one of a pair of twins admitted to the hospital’s neonatal intensive care unit after they were born two months prematurely on June 12 that year.

The court heard on the first day of the inquest on Thursday that Angel suffered from a congenital heart defect that restricted blood flow to her lungs, whereas her sibling had exhibited poor growth in the womb.

Their mother underwent an urgent caesarean section in her 27th week of pregnancy after doctors found Angel’s twin could be in danger.

Medical staff placed Angel on assisted ventilation and began administering adrenaline, one of two medications used to support heart contractions, at 9.45pm on June 12, the court heard on Friday.

Cheung Mei-lin, a nurse with 32 years of experience, said she was asked to inject a more concentrated dose of adrenaline to raise the infant’s blood pressure at around 3am the following day.

She said the tube’s three-way valve was properly adjusted when she inspected it three times between 3.10am and 3.27am, but the baby’s hypotension persisted.

Dr Jaime Chiu Chi-ying, who was on duty in the unit that night, expressed concerns that the infusion system might not be functioning normally, even though the device did not give a warning signal.

When Cheung was asked to increase the drug’s concentration again at 3.50am, she realised the medicine could not reach the baby because the valve was closed.

She immediately opened the valve and notified Dr Yennie Pang Lap-lan, a paediatrics specialist at the hospital.

“Dr Pang asked why the alarm had not gone off if the valve was closed. The moment she finished speaking, the alarm strangely went off,” the witness said.

Angel’s blood pressure returned to normal levels but dropped gradually after half an hour. She died at 4pm on June 13.

Cheung said she was certain the valve was in the correct position when she inspected it at 3.27am, and denied touching the device before discovering it closed 23 minutes later.

The twins’ mother, Law Ka-sin, questioned whether Cheung was physically fit on the day in question, noting that she resigned on June 15 for personal reasons.

Cheung said she had followed her family’s advice to leave the post in light of the “very negative” media portrayal of the incident.

“I was under immense pressure,” she said. “My family was very worried about my mental health. I spoke with them and believed I should leave this environment as soon as possible.”

She added: “I was overwhelmed by pain and sadness because I am also a mother.”

So Lap-kwan, the other nurse on duty in the unit that night, said she and Cheung had twice conducted independent inspections of the medical device and found it to be working properly.

She acknowledged that the paediatrics department’s guidelines at the time did not require healthcare staff to check the valves in infusion tubes, nor was it necessary to adjust detectors when only a small amount of medicine was used.

The inquest before Coroner Raymund Chow Chi-wei and a five-member jury is scheduled to run for seven days.
