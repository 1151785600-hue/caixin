Why the Philippines is losing out in Southeast Asia’s investment boom

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 871 words | 2026-07-14

The Philippines still lags behind its neighbours in attracting foreign direct investment, with inflows stagnating in 2025 even as a financing influx swept the region.

This shortfall has cast a spotlight on a corruption scandal that has roiled the country and dented investor confidence, analysts say, compounding long-standing systemic issues.

Manila ranked sixth in Southeast Asia for FDI last year, capturing just US$9 billion of the region’s US$244 billion investment haul, according to the UN Conference on Trade and Development’s 2026 World Investment Report.

Southeast Asia overtook East Asia as the largest recipient subregion within “Developing Asia”, the report said, as total FDI inflows rose 10 per cent.

Investment was concentrated in high-value sectors such as semiconductors, electronics, communications and renewable energy, lifting the region’s total inflows to US$244.17 billion from US$222.50 billion the previous year.

Singapore remained the region’s top destination for FDI with US$150.90 billion, up 11 per cent from 2024, while Indonesia held onto second place with US$21.44 billion, despite a 13 per cent drop from the previous year.

Vietnam followed with US$20.35 billion, ahead of Thailand and Malaysia with US$19.10 billion and US$15.39 billion, respectively.

The Philippines spent much of 2025 embroiled in a billion-peso scandal over anomalous flood-control infrastructure projects, triggering public outrage, investigations and agency shake-ups.

“Political noise since 2025 amid the anomalous flood-control projects … kept some investors on the sidelines or in a wait-and-see mode until more priority measures, especially those that further improve governance standards, are passed to help improve investor confidence,” said Michael Ricafort, chief economist at Rizal Commercial Banking Corporation.

But the corruption saga is merely the latest symptom of deeper structural constraints that dampen the country’s competitiveness, according to Ruben Carlo Asuncion, chief economist at Union Bank of the Philippines.

“These include relatively high power costs, infrastructure and logistics gaps, regulatory complexity and the slow pace of project implementation,” Asuncion said. “While recent investment liberalisation measures are steps in the right direction, investors typically require time and policy consistency before deploying significant capital.”

Ricafort agreed, arguing that Manila should implement reforms that strengthen the economy, overhaul governance and convert “investment commitments to actual FDI”. He added that expanding STEM and technical-vocational education to match talent requirements could also help draw more investment into hi-tech sectors.

Losing out

Despite several promising opportunities, the Philippines continues to lose out to its neighbours, according to analysts.

“Many Asean economies offer either greater scale or stronger integration into global supply chains,” Asuncion said. “Indonesia benefits from its large domestic market and strategic mineral resources, while Vietnam has successfully positioned itself as a manufacturing hub amid supply chain diversification.”

Despite the Philippines’ strengths in services and consumption-driven growth, “it remains less integrated into regional manufacturing and export ecosystems than some of its peers”, he added.

The country’s overall investment slump comes even as major hub projects take shape, including the Luzon Economic Corridor – a trilateral initiative of the United States, the Philippines and Japan – and the 1,619-hectare Pax Silica hub at New Clark City in Tarlac province, designed for AI and other critical supply chains.

Ricafort believes these projects could draw fresh inflows from the US and allied nations over the next two years, including new frontier investments in AI, as well as rare earths, electric vehicles and semiconductors.

“[We can expect] FDIs from other like-minded or allied countries, as well as for competitors and suppliers for these hi-tech industries that would also locate in the Luzon Economic Corridor,” Ricafort said.

“This would create more employment and other business or economic opportunities, especially in nearby localities during the construction and operating phases.”

On Tuesday, Philippine Finance Secretary Frederick Go announced that Taiwan’s Foxconn – known for manufacturing Apple iPhones – would anchor the US-backed Pax Silica hub.

Asuncion said the Philippines’ close ties with the US and other partners could open opportunities in electronics, semiconductors, digital infrastructure and renewable energy.

“Ultimately, competitiveness matters more than geopolitical alignment,” he said, noting that while geopolitical concerns might be “increasingly part of the investment equation, [they are] not the main driver of FDI decisions”.

Ricafort is optimistic that Pax Silica will “open more doors of opportunities for the Philippines” to play a bigger part in global supply chains, attracting “suppliers and even competitors” to New Clark City and the wider Luzon Economic Corridor, creating jobs and other economic spillovers.

The hub could also help the Philippines carve out a niche at the higher end of global supply chains, such as in industries “that require highly skilled professionals,” Ricafort said, allowing the country to differentiate itself from “low-cost and low-skilled” labour markets “such as Vietnam”.

In that vein, analysts say the Philippines should continue improving its ease of doing business by cutting bureaucratic red tape, lowering costs and upgrading infrastructure.

“The priority should be improving overall competitiveness by lowering the cost of doing business, reducing regulatory friction, accelerating infrastructure development, and ensuring reliable and affordable energy,” Asuncion said.

“Sector-wise, the country should deepen its position in electronics and semiconductors, digital services, data centres, renewable energy, logistics, healthcare and higher-value manufacturing. Sustained reforms and consistent policy execution will be critical to attracting long-term investments.”
