China’s missile test reveals fragile state of world nuclear governance

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 949 words | 2026-07-16

On July 6, a Chinese strategic nuclear submarine fired a missile carrying a training dummy warhead into a designated area of the Pacific. Beijing described the launch as routine, said relevant countries had been notified and insisted that it targeted no state.

The regional reaction was immediate, with Australia, Japan, the United States and Pacific nations raising concerns around insufficient notification and the politics of nuclear-free zones. Those reactions matter because they show how far nuclear politics has moved beyond the arsenals of large powers.

The test was read through several lenses: evidence of Beijing’s maturing second-strike capability, a challenge to US allies and partners, a problem for Pacific nuclear-free norms, and a signal to states already reassessing nuclear weapons policy. The test revealed a nuclear order in motion.

That order is increasingly shaped by three tracks. The first is Sino-Russian strategic alignment. The second is a US-led system of extended deterrence. The third is a diverse middle layer of secondary nuclear powers, nuclear-threshold states and “umbrella-anxious” allies. The first two tracks are hardening. The third remains fluid.

The Sino-Russian track has become clearer in recent years. Even though their nuclear doctrines differ, Beijing and Moscow have built a common vocabulary around “strategic stability” since 2016. Joint statements frame instability around US missile defence, long-range conventional strike capabilities, Aukus, space militarisation and the pursuit of “absolute security”.

For China and Russia, arms control cannot be reduced to counting deployed warheads while leaving US alliances, precision-strike networks, missile defences and foreign bases outside the discussion.

China’s missile test strengthens the credibility of China’s sea-based retaliatory capability while supporting Beijing’s claim that its nuclear modernisation is defensive.

In this framework, Russia benefits from having a partner that broadens criticism of US power beyond Europe, while China avoids constraints on its capabilities before it reaches a sufficient level of deterrence. Their strategic narratives are aligned: both seek to redefine stability in the context of American power.

The second track is the US-led deterrence system. As Washington modernises its nuclear triad, its more urgent task is reassuring allies that they do not need their own atomic weapons. Nuclear weapons sharing within Nato remains the most institutionalised form of shared deterrence. The US and South Korea have created a “nuclear consultative group” to deepen information sharing, nuclear-crisis procedures and conventional-nuclear integration.

Tokyo has intensified extended deterrence dialogue with Washington. Australia is entering a deeper technology relationship through Aukus. Poland, Germany and Finland are reassessing how much visible nuclear reassurance they require under Russian pressure.

This system is designed to prevent proliferation among US allies. At the same time, it also fuels China and Russia’s concerns. Missile defence, long-range conventional capacity, strategic deployments, nuclear consultation and allied basing reassure US partners. But to Beijing and Moscow, they represent integrated military architecture that could weaken deterrents, compress decision time and create incentives for pre-emption in a crisis.

The third track is the nuclear middle. It includes existing secondary nuclear powers such as Britain, France, India, Pakistan, Israel and North Korea. It also includes nuclear-threshold states – referring to states with the means to acquire at least one nuclear device within months – and states that harbour doubts about the nuclear umbrella. These countries include South Korea, Japan, Saudi Arabia, Poland, Turkey, Iran and Australia. These actors are often treated as secondary to great-power nuclear politics. On the contrary, they represent a crucial swing layer.

Britain and France are Western nuclear pillars. India is an autonomous nuclear-armed power balancing China and Pakistan while presenting itself as a responsible actor. Pakistan’s arsenal is driven by security competition with India. Israel relies on nuclear opacity. North Korea has shown how a proliferator can force regional powers to adjust around it.

Umbrella-anxious states have more of a consequential role. South Korean public support for a nuclear option reflects doubts about external guarantees. Japan’s non-nuclear posture remains strong, yet its technological latency, counterstrike debate and dependence on US reassurance make its choices central to Northeast Asia.

Saudi Arabia’s nuclear calculations are similarly tied to regional dynamics. Poland’s interest in nuclear sharing reflects Russia’s nuclear posturing. Turkey’s nuclear rhetoric, Australia’s nuclear-propulsion pathway and Iran’s threshold capabilities show the tension between restraint and the impulse to hedge against it.

These states can reinforce the non-proliferation order or accelerate its erosion. They may abandon non-proliferation when restraint looks like vulnerability. That happens when security guarantees lose credibility, regional rivals cross nuclear thresholds, nuclear-armed states expand their arsenals while preaching restraint to others, peaceful nuclear technology becomes politicised or proliferation appears to bring bargaining power. Hedging becomes rational; proliferation becomes more normalised.

States become stewards when restraint brings security, technological development and inclusion. That requires credible reassurance without reckless deployment, fair access to peaceful nuclear energy, accountability to multilateral agencies, regional launch notifications, nuclear-crisis hotlines, and a real role for middle powers in shaping risk-reduction rules.

Great powers set the temperature for the nuclear system. Washington must reassure allies without normalising endless nuclear sharing. Beijing must make its no-first-use and nuclear-free-zone rhetoric credible through greater transparency and restraint. Moscow must stop using nuclear threats in conventional war. Yet the survival of non-proliferation may depend on whether the nuclear middle believes the system still works for them.

China’s missile test was a great-power signal. Its deeper warning lies in the reactions it produced. The nuclear order will only work if the states closest to the threshold have practical reasons to stay below it.
