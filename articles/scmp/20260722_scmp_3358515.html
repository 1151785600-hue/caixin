Malaysia has never had so many Chinese tourists. It wants more

SCMP | FREE | 1447 words | 2026-07-22

Jane Lyu flew to Malaysia on Tuesday to visit a city that she had never heard of until recently. The 32-year-old engineer from Guangxi, southern China, first spotted it on Weibo.

Now, standing outside the pink-domed Putra Mosque in Putrajaya, she explains through a translation app how the country’s administrative capital, a planned city barely three decades old, ended up as the first stop on her Malaysian itinerary.

Lyu, who arrived as part of a company trip that began in Singapore, is one of millions of Chinese tourists poised to visit Malaysia this year – and Malaysia is counting on all of them.

Her group decided to look beyond the buzz of Kuala Lumpur in favour of Putrajaya’s staid grandeur, long, empty roads flanked by government offices and the Putra Mosque’s lakefront arches opening onto a wide ceremonial square that is perfect for photographs.

“Before this, we never heard of the Putra Mosque but we saw it on [microblogging site] Weibo and Douyin [the mainland Chinese version of TikTok]. We had to go. it looked so beautiful,” Lyu said

“On social media, we saw durian stalls as well. We can’t wait to eat the fresh durian.”

Travellers like Lyu are exactly the type Malaysia is targeting with its Visit Malaysia 2026 campaign. The country aims to welcome 7 million Chinese tourists this year, banking on visa-free travel, expanded air links to third-tier Chinese cities and a digital campaign built around mainland mega-apps such as RedNote, Douyin and Weibo.

Tourism Malaysia, the government-run tourism promotion board, said Chinese arrivals rose 25 per cent to 4.7 million last year, outpacing overall tourist growth of 11 per cent to 42.2 million. In the first quarter of this year alone, Chinese arrivals climbed a further 25 per cent year on year to 1.41 million.

It comes as Thailand – long the No 1 Southeast Asian destination for Chinese travellers – has lost some of its allure. Its travel agents’ association has cut its 2026 Chinese arrivals forecast from 9 million to 7 million, citing safety fears over scam-related crimes and higher travel costs.

Vietnam, which drew a record 21.2 million international visitors in 2025 with China as its biggest market, claimed the top spot regionally for Chinese visitors last year.

Even so, Malaysia holds its own as a Southeast Asian tourism powerhouse, not just in arrivals but also in revenue. Foreign tourist receipts rose to 110.6 billion ringgit (US$26.8 billion) in 2025 – up from 95.3 billion ringgit the year before – ranking among the top three regionally.

Familiar yet foreign

Malaysia’s pitch to Chinese visitors rests on numerous advantages, including its Muslim-majority yet multicultural society, ease of communication and a reputation as a safer destination than regional rivals.

Asia travel market analyst Gary Bowerman said Chinese visitors were often intrigued by Malaysia’s “tri-ethnic population” and the way Chinese heritage had become woven into a broader tapestry of Malay and Indian cultures.

“This is comparatively rare in Asia and young Chinese tourists are eager to learn more about a country that has strong ethnic Chinese history and traditions, but which are integrated with Malay and Indian cultures and sensibilities,” he said.

With 22 per cent of its population ethnic Chinese and predominantly Mandarin-speaking, Malaysia offers a cultural affinity not easily found elsewhere in the region, according to Sam Huang, a tourism professor at Edith Cowan University in Australia.

Social proof matters too: Bowerman said Chinese outbound travellers relied heavily on peer insights and word of mouth, with Malaysia currently “scoring well on Chinese social media for its diversity and sense of difference”.

As of February, 871 weekly flights connected Malaysia to 30 Chinese cities, providing the connectivity to convert online interest into arrivals.

Makan makan

Food is another major draw. Angie Ng, president of the Malaysia Inbound Chinese Association, said motivations for visiting diverged along generational lines, with older travellers seeking history and culture, while younger visitors gravitated towards Instagrammable islands, diving and cuisine.

But a love of “makan-makan”, the playful Malay phrase for eating, unites most Chinese visitors.

Durian has become the clearest emblem of this voracious appetite. A protocol signed by Malaysia and China in 2024 opened the Chinese market to fresh Malaysian durian – previously limited to pulp, paste and frozen whole fruit – unlocking a higher-value trade tied to orchards, agritourism and social media storytelling.

“Different tourists have different demands,” Ng said. “A lot of tourists go to Penang because of durian tours … A lot of people go to Terengganu because of island tours.”

The economics of Chinese tourism are not always straightforward, however. A typical five-day, four-night trip can cost as little as 5,000 ringgit (US$1,200) per person, according to Ng, including roughly 2,000 ringgit for flights.

Questions persist about how much of that spending stays in Malaysia given the dominance of Chinese booking platforms such as Ctrip. But Ng cautions against oversimplification.

“The local operators still benefit and the hotels also benefit,” she said.

Nigel Wong, president of the Malaysian Association of Tour and Travel Agents (Matta), agreed that the benefits trickled down. “And not just the tour operators,” he said. “Everything from restaurants to retail outlets and hotels as well.”

Language barriers, local customs and transport logistics still leave plenty of business for Malaysian travel agents and local fixers, observers say, even as Chinese platforms continue to grow their footprint.

“While there is definitely competition, we are still seeing that these platforms can be part of the Malaysian ecosystem in dealing with Chinese tourists,” Wong said.

Tour groups are a sticking point, however. As in Thailand, Chinese group itineraries are often controlled by China-based operators who choose all the hotels, restaurants and tour stops in a closed economic loop that irks local traders.

But independent travellers are another matter altogether, according to Hannah Pearson, director of Southeast Asia-focused tourism consultancy Pear Anderson.

“For independent travellers, they are free to choose what and where they visit and that means they can visit those locally owned Malaysian businesses,” she said.

The real challenge, according to Pearson, is getting independent Chinese travellers to look beyond heavily promoted hubs such as Kuala Lumpur and Penang to places the algorithms have not yet made famous.

Search, save, go

Malaysia has been welcoming Chinese tourists for decades. What has changed in recent years is how rapidly interest in a destination can be built online – and one platform now dominates the decision-making of millions of young travellers.

RedNote, the wildly successful Chinese lifestyle and travel app, was cited by 53 per cent of respondents to a survey last spring by Dragon Trail International as their top source of outbound destination information, ahead of Douyin at 48 per cent and online travel agencies such as Ctrip at 42 per cent.

Among travellers aged 18 to 35, around 60 per cent used RedNote for outbound travel research, compared with 26 per cent of those aged 45 to 65.

Sienna Parulis-Cook, director of marketing and communications at Dragon Trail International, a China-focused travel marketing agency, said the app consistently ranked as the top source of destination information in consumer surveys.

The social media space for the Chinese market is a very strategic tool

Nigel Wong, Malaysian Association of Tour and Travel Agents

“The online space, social media space for the Chinese market is a very strategic tool,” said Wong of Matta. “It’s very effective, and it will continue to be effective.”

Indeed, the spike in Chinese visitor numbers has brought a lot of spending and attention to Malaysia, but it has also had its downsides.

Chinese-only menus and signage are multiplying, heritage streets are growing crowded and poor etiquette at religious sites has also drawn concern. In 2018, two Chinese tourists caused an online furore after being filmed performing an inappropriate dance outside the Kota Kinabalu City Mosque, leading to a temporary visitor ban.

“Tensions will rise when the number of Chinese visitors is too overwhelming for a destination,” tourism professor Huang said. “We need to watch for potential overtourism in any tourist destination targeting Chinese visitors.”

Still, Malaysia has largely avoided the negative sentiment currently affecting Thailand, where worries over scams and safety concerns have kept some Chinese visitors away.

Huang also warned about the risk of social media-driven hype shaping tourists’ expectations before a trip had even begun.

“If tourists visit a place where the real experience cannot match their expectation as driven by the algorithms, they are more dissatisfied and may spread negative word of mouth,” he said.

Back in Putrajaya, Lyu and her friend were already scrolling through the app for their next destination, with one item firmly at the top of the list.

“I really want to try durian in Malaysia,” she said. “Everyone says you have to eat it here.”
