How overlapping US and China sanctions are complicating business in Africa

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 824 words | 2026-07-11

US-imposed sanctions targeting other countries and a growing list of Chinese laws aimed at countering the measures are creating a compliance minefield for African businesses dealing with the world’s top two economies.

At risk is a broad range of industries including mining, banking, telecommunications, technology, energy, infrastructure and others in which African businesses are simultaneously exposed to US and Chinese counterparties, according to analysts.

Compliance consultants covering the continent have reported a surge in inquiries by companies and investors gauging their exposure.

“China’s new regulations present a complex challenge for the continent as it is not a simple binary choice but a navigation of overlapping pressures,” said Cheta Nwanze, a partner at SBM Intelligence, a risk consultancy based in Lagos, Nigeria.

Investors coming into Africa were increasingly devoting significant effort and resources to ensuring their operations did not violate the sanctions and export controls, Nwanze added.

At present, the US has at least 37 active sanctions programmes with diverse targets ranging from countries to companies and individuals, according to the US Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC), a unit of the Treasury Department that manages them.

China is a target of many, as are Afghanistan, Cuba, Iran and North Korea. Some are related to African countries, including the Democratic Republic of Congo, Ethiopia, Mali, Somalia, South Sudan and Sudan.

The OFAC website lists the diverse nature of its sanctions, which are wide-ranging in scope and targeted in their application, covering geographical units, entities and individuals. These also include counternarcotics and anti-terrorism measures.

As the sanctions have expanded over the years, China has responded with its own countermeasures.

China’s 2020 Export Control Law established a unified legal framework that allowed Beijing to take reciprocal action against countries whose trade policies threatened its interests.

Building on this statutory foundation, China has systematically tightened controls over strategic materials, implementing comprehensive export licensing and extraterritorial tracking systems for vital resources such as rare earths and their processing technologies.

The 2021 Anti-Foreign Sanctions Law forms the bedrock of China’s legal framework to counter unilateral, restrictive foreign measures targeting Chinese businesses and individuals.

Reinforced by subsequent state regulations, the law codifies a robust countermeasure toolkit that includes freezing domestic assets, prohibiting local transactions, and denying or cancelling visas and entry.

China in 2020 created the Unreliable Entities List, aimed at countries and bodies engaged in discriminatory actions against Chinese ventures. Foreign companies and entities on the list can be banned or face other restrictions in the Chinese market.

Beijing has also stepped up actions in response to Washington’s sanctions and restrictions. New decrees issued in April expanded the scope of China’s response, including the creation of a Malicious Entities List.

In May, China forbade companies based in the country from recognising or applying US OFAC measures against five Chinese refiners, and also rebuffed the European Union’s foreign subsidy investigations.

Apart from US sanctions, African businesses and investors must contend with those imposed by EU countries and Britain.

This is especially the case for businesses that use their financial systems or currencies, according to analysts, or if American, British or EU entities or people are involved.

Dentons, a global law firm that provides consultancy on compliance, said in a June 1 note to clients: “In practice, sanctions risk can arise even where the relevant African jurisdiction does not itself impose sanctions.

“We often see exposure through counterparties, joint venture partners or supply chains linked to sanctioned jurisdictions or designated individuals.”

It warned that an American company whose African subsidiary was engaged in business with a local company with some Chinese ownership risked violating US sanctions.

This was despite the entire transaction occurring in Africa without violating any local laws.

China’s greater deployment of its own retaliatory responses has added yet another layer of complexity that businesses and investors must navigate.

For many African enterprises, the sanctions significantly disrupt supply chains and market access.

According to Nwanze of SBM Intelligence: “African banks and businesses operating with both US and Chinese partners face conflicting legal obligations and the risk of penalties from either or both sides.”

With US-Africa trade hovering at just 5 per cent compared with China’s booming 20 per cent share, Nwanze argued that Beijing could shield the continent from the harshest impacts of Western economic sanctions.

Recently, Standard Bank of South Africa, the continent’s biggest lender by assets, was appointed a clearing bank for renminbi transactions in Africa by the People’s Bank of China.

The move offered a clear example of financial institutions straddling China and the West amid competing sanctions.

For many executives across Africa, what quickly comes to mind is the experience of South African telecommunications company MTN in Iran in 2018.

Due to US sanctions on Iran, the company’s 49 per cent stake in mobile network operator Irancell became stranded, leaving MTN unable to withdraw dividends or dispose of the assets.

Most businesses want to avoid a similar fate.
