Long March-10B: China’s net-capture rocket joins space race with edge on SpaceX

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 954 words | 2026-07-14

China on Friday became only the second nation in history to achieve the controlled recovery of an orbital-class rocket booster, after successfully retrieving part of its Long March-10B reusable rocket during its maiden launch.

Unlike the methods pioneered by Elon Musk’s US-based SpaceX, the Chinese rocket achieved the first-ever use of a unique sea-based net-capture system for a more adaptable recovery.

The Chinese rocket also offers a new reusable path to space flight, as Beijing doubles down on strengthening its commercial space sector amid an intensifying global race to the moon.

The 63-metre (207-foot) tall, 5-metre wide medium-lift rocket lifted off from the Wenchang space launch site in southern China at 12.15pm on Friday, sending its satellite payload into a predetermined orbit, according to state news agency Xinhua.

About six minutes after the first and second stages of the rocket separated, the first stage returned vertically and was caught by the Linghangzhe recovery platform, Xinhua said.

State broadcaster CCTV said the maiden flight validated the rocket’s design for first-stage reusability and was expected to help cut launch costs.

“Reusable rockets are a crucial pathway for large-scale, free access to space in the future, supporting the trend towards lower-cost, higher-frequency space launches,” Jiang Zhou, a structural systems expert at the China Aerospace Science and Technology Corporation (CASC), told China Space News on Friday.

The two-stage Long March-10B’s first stage is powered by seven YF-100K engines that burn kerosene and liquid oxygen. The rocket can deliver up to 16 tonnes to low Earth orbit and is mainly designed for cargo missions.

Chen Muye, a technical expert from the rocket’s designer, the China Academy of Launch Vehicle Technology, told Xinhua that the Long March-10B was primarily aimed at China’s commercial market and could be used to deploy satellite internet constellations and large commercial satellites.

In February, China used part of a Long March-10A – another variant in the family – for a successful in-flight abort test for the Mengzhou crewed moon ship. The test stage completed a controlled descent and splashed down next to a recovery vessel, raising expectations for the flight on Friday.

The mission was also China’s first attempt at mastering reusable rocket recovery, a key step towards cutting costs, increasing launch capacity and boosting the international competitiveness of the country’s space sector.

In December, two other Chinese rockets attempted SpaceX-style vertical landings using grid fins and landing legs, but both failed. Zhuque-3, a privately developed methane-liquid oxygen rocket from LandSpace, had its first stage crash near the target recovery area.

The state-owned Long March-12A’s first stage crashed mid-air after an engine failed to relight for the final landing burn.

Unlike the Falcon 9 approach of landing a rocket on its own legs, Long March-10B uses a sea-based net-and-cable system.

During re-entry, the first stage slows by using its engines and aerodynamic control surfaces before descending into the recovery system. In the final landing phase, the net-and-cable device absorbs the rocket’s remaining energy and slows it further for a soft landing.

“Net-based recovery simplifies the rocket’s structure, reduces its weight and increases its carrying capacity; it also has a strong ability to adapt to landing-point deviations and can ‘amplify’ the capture window through the coordinated action of the net system,” Chen said.

Zhu Pingping, head engineer of CASC, told China Space News that the re-entry process was called the “Gates of Hell” – because an engine failing to start or stop at the right time could cause recovery failure.

The recovery process required balancing recovery and payload capacity, navigating and locking in on moving targets, and real-time flight monitoring using thousands of sensors, experts from CASC told China Space News.

The idea of catching spacecraft with nets or cables dates back to the Cold War, when the US military recovered film capsules from Corona spy satellites using mid-air capture.

More recently, companies including SpaceX and Rocket Lab, tested methods to catch returning rocket components using nets or helicopters, but largely abandoned those approaches because of operational challenges, such as high winds at sea.

During a hearing before the US-China Economic and Security Review Commission in April last year, General B. Chance Saltzman, chief of space operations at the US Space Force, said the United States remained the world leader in launches but warned that rivals could rapidly close the gap.

“As soon as those reusability capabilities start to be used in other countries, they will start to catch up and be able to use space for different missions and in different ways, in more resilient ways,” he said.

“In the near term, China’s investments in reusable launch vehicles and on-orbit refuelling represent an inflection point in space access that may result in China overtaking US leadership in the domain.”

As the first orbital test of the Long March-10 family, the flight on Friday aimed to validate key technologies for the more powerful crewed lunar variant, Long March-10, which would use 21 YF-100K engines at lift-off to launch Chinese astronauts and a lunar lander towards the moon before 2030.

Nasa also aims to send US astronauts back to the moon by 2028 for the first time since the Apollo missions ended in 1972.

Threads on Chinese social media suggest a second Zhuque-3 rocket could make another attempt at vertical landing in the coming weeks, while a full Long March-10A flight is expected later this year.

According to CCTV, another test flight using the reusable Long March-10B booster recovered today is expected later this year.
