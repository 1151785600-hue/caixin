Johor election: BN streaks ahead of Anwar’s PH, unofficial tally shows

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1259 words | 2026-07-11

Malaysia’s Johor state election closed on Saturday with unofficial tallies showing the dominant coalition Barisan Nasional (BN) firmly ahead of Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s Pakatan Harapan (PH).

The final results will determine the stability of Anwar’s coalition government, with Johor turned into a battleground between the federal partners. Both coalitions fielded candidates in every constituency.

As at 9pm, BN was leading in 23 seats to Pakatan’s 1, according to an unofficial count by the Election Commission.

BN’s own unofficial tallies showed the party leading in 49 state seats, with PH ahead in seven, although the figures had yet to be confirmed by the Election Commission.

Ahmad Zahid Hamidi, deputy prime minister and president of BN, said official and unofficial results so far showed BN crossing the 40-seat mark in the Johor state assembly.

“In the last state election, we won 40 seats. God willing, this time we will win more than that,” he told reporters at BN’s command centre in Johor.

Ahmad Zahid thanked Johoreans for choosing BN to govern the state, stressing that the party “will not be arrogant in victory” and would work hard to fulfil its campaign pledges.

“We also hope this victory will trigger a blue wave in the elections to come.”

The Election Commission said turnout stood at 67.44 per cent as of 5pm, with 172 candidates vying for all 56 seats. The figure was significantly higher than the 54.9 per cent in 2022, with this round driven by high public interest and intense campaigning.

Full results are expected as early as 10pm.

In this election, 14 seats had straight fights, 27 featured three-cornered contests, 12 were four-way battles and three had five candidates.

BN entered polling day defending the 40 seats it held before the state assembly was dissolved on June 1, while PH had 12, opposition Perikatan Nasional three and youth-centric Muda one.

A simple majority requires 29 seats and a two-thirds majority needs 38.

Analysts say the result would not change Anwar’s parliamentary majority, but a sweeping BN victory would strengthen Umno’s – the dominant Malay party in BN – hand inside his unwieldy national coalition while exposing PH’s inability to match its federal influence on state ground.

Bersatu president and former prime minister Muhyiddin Yassin, who cast his vote in Muar, earlier expressed confidence that the turnout would reach at least 70 per cent. He urged voters to support the candidates of their choice.

Bersatu, a Malay-first party, was formed in 2016 after Muhyiddin and other senior leaders were expelled from Umno for calling out corruption and kleptocracy within the political vehicle.

Bersatu contested 16 seats in Johor.

Munir Ratna said she woke up at 5.30am and arrived at a polling centre in Puteri Wangsa two hours later.

“There were not many people. It was all right,” the 67-year-old said after voting.

In Johor Jaya, where Chinese voters form the largest electoral bloc, a woman who wanted to be known only as Melanie said she and her sister completed the process in about five minutes.

“We have done our part as citizens,” the 50-year-old told This Week in Asia. “As Malaysians, we have a responsibility to come and vote. It matters for the country, the economy, everything.”

Speaking after casting her ballot in Kulai, Deputy Communications Minister Teo Nie Ching said the Malaysian Communications and Multimedia Commission had received 29 complaints during the election period from June 1 to July 9. They involved false information, hate speech touching on race, religion and royalty, and one fraud case. The commission has asked social media platforms to take action.

Afya Ghazali, 26, who works in Singapore and commutes across the Causeway every day, said she wanted the next government to improve infrastructure and create more jobs in the state.

“We need better infrastructure and more job opportunities in Johor so that more of us can serve our own state,” she said. “There is still a shortage of jobs in the city, especially for us.”

Matthew Sim, a central Johor-based e-commerce entrepreneur, said his voting experience in Paloh was smooth.

“The crowds are not too large yet. Maybe people are still at work and have not come back to vote,” the 36-year-old said.

Punitha Ananthan, 37, who works in quality assurance for an oil palm plantation, said turnout had been steady at her voting station in Bekok.

“The crowd was OK and I saw many people home from outstation to vote,” she said, voicing confidence that her party would “definitely win”.

Pollster Ilham Centre on Friday projected BN ahead in 39 seats, enough for a two-thirds majority, with PH leading in nine and eight constituencies too close to call. It attributed BN’s advantage to its stronger political machinery, familiar candidates and support in Malay-majority zones.

PH, meanwhile, was relying on Chinese-majority urban and semi-urban seats, where Ilham said turnout of at least 65 per cent would be needed for the coalition to hold its ground.

Johor, Malaysia’s southern gateway to Singapore, has attracted vast investment promises, yet the campaign repeatedly returned to wages, housing and living costs, along with the distrust between Umno and Chinese-majority Democratic Action Party (DAP) that their alliance in Putrajaya has never fully buried.

The state contributed about 14 billion ringgit (US$3.7 billion) to federal coffers between 2023 and 2025, according to Finance Ministry figures cited by Anwar, who said Putrajaya returned 16 billion ringgit to Johor over the same period.

In a fishing village in Iskandar Puteri, Sabri Abdullah had little patience for the intrigue in Kuala Lumpur. The 58-year-old measured governments by the roads, street lights and running water that had reached his community during his lifetime.

“Put simply, this place started from zero. There was nothing,” Sabri told This Week in Asia on Friday. “Now, alhamdulillah, people in the village can see the development.”

That record, he said, was why his vote remained with BN.

Izuddin Zulhaidat, who runs a food business, was coy about his vote, but pointed to the range of developments in Bekok, in the northern Johor district of Segamat.

These include the completion of a new railway station as part of the Electric Train Service that runs along the west coast of Peninsular Malaysia from the Thai border to Johor Bahru, and new franchises and consumer brands opening up in Bekok.

“Overall, I am satisfied with the state government’s performance, because we also get to enjoy the benefits of development from southern Johor,” the 29-year-old said.

Former DAP state legislator Gan Peck Cheng urged the state government to develop other Johor towns to boost overall growth, even as she lauded initiatives such as the Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone.

“We are not asking [the state government] to slow down Johor Bahru’s development, but we are asking that the rest of Johor be brought along so that they don’t miss out,” she said.

In the lead-up to polling day, caretaker chief minister Onn Hafiz Ghazi and BN sold continuity, political stability and Johor’s investment record, while promising that the state would remain aligned with Putrajaya even without DAP in its government.

Anwar and PH pressed wages, affordable housing and the cost of living, while DAP chief Anthony Loke warned against “one-party dominance” earlier this week.

In an odd turn, PN component Islamist party PAS instructed its supporters to vote for BN candidates in the 23 seats the opposition coalition did not contest, saying the move was intended to stop DAP from gaining more ground.

Among the newer forces, Parti Bersama Malaysia leader Rafizi Ramli urged Johoreans to punish the country’s “dinosaur parties”.
