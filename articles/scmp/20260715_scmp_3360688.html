Hong Kong police arrest 5 over national security after raiding 2 bookstores

SCMP | FREE | 481 words | 2026-07-15

Hong Kong police have arrested five people and seized books during a raid on two independent bookshops, the South China Morning Post has learned.

A source told the SCMP that national security police searched Have a Nice Stay bookshop in Prince Edward and Greenfield Book Store in Mong Kok on Wednesday.

Officers seized books and made multiple arrests during the operation.

A force spokesman said the Customs and Excise Department had referred a case of an overseas shipment containing books deemed seditious to the national security department.

Upon investigation, national security police had arrested three women aged between 30 and 59 and two men aged 37 and 57 on suspicion of doing an act with seditious intent.

“Police investigation showed that the five arrestees had allegedly displayed seditious items within the stores, and sold seditious publications, including content that incited hatred against the Hong Kong government, the judiciary and law enforcement agencies,” the spokesman said.

The five suspects remained in police custody as of Wednesday evening.

Under the Safeguarding National Security Ordinance, the maximum punishment of doing an act with seditious intent is seven years’ imprisonment.

Both shops are independently owned and focus on books related to the humanities. They have not taken part in the city’s annual book fair at the Hong Kong Convention and Exhibition Centre, which opened its doors on Wednesday.

Have a Nice Stay was founded in 2022 by a few former reporters. The shop, located in a walk-up building in Prince Edward, sells books related to journalism studies as well as the broader humanities and social sciences.

The bookstore announced its impending closure on Tuesday, with owners writing on Instagram that the shop would cease operations on August 30, citing a variety of reasons including financial losses and unclear red lines over what books would be considered “problematic”.

“Recent developments were perhaps the last straw that broke the camel’s back. Before that, the camel had already been shouldering way too much of a burden,” the owners wrote.

A separate source cited books written by the late opposition lawmaker Bottle Shiu Ka-chun and former Democratic Party lawmaker Emily Lau Wai-hing’s memoir Stay, which was published last year, as titles that were suspected of promoting anti-government sentiment.

Hong Kong’s independent bookstores, many of which organise community events such as author sessions, have faced legal and business blows in recent weeks.

Leticia Wong Man-huen and her husband, the owners of Hunter’s Bookstore in Sham Shui Po, were arrested on suspicion of sedition and money laundering last month. Both have been released on bail, with their bookshop resuming operations on July 8 after a 13-day hiatus.

Two other independent bookstores – Elmbook and Luck Win Bookstore – were disqualified from taking part in the annual book fair, despite their previous participation.

A week after the disqualification, Elmbook announced on social media that it would close in April next year.
