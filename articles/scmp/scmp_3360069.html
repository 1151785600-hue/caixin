China recovers reusable rocket used in the maiden launch of Long March 10B

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 585 words | 2026-07-10

China succeeded in recovering part of its Long March 10B reusable rocket on its maiden launch on Friday, marking the country’s first controlled orbital-class booster recovery and making it only the second nation in the world to achieve the feat.

Unlike the methods pioneered by Elon Musk’s US-based SpaceX, the Chinese rocket achieved the first-ever use of a unique sea-based, net-capture system.

The 70-metre (230-foot) tall, 5-metre wide medium-lift rocket lifted off from southern China’s Wenchang space launch site at 12.15pm.

About six minutes after the first and second stages of the rocket separated, the first stage returned vertically and was recovered via a sea platform, according to state broadcaster CCTV.

The maiden flight validated the rocket’s design for first-stage reusability and was expected to help cut launch costs, CCTV said.

The two-stage Long March 10B’s first stage is powered by seven YF-100K engines that burn kerosene and liquid oxygen. The rocket can deliver up to 16 tonnes to low Earth orbit and is mainly designed for cargo missions.

As the first orbital test of the Long March 10 family, the flight on Friday aimed to validate key technologies for the more powerful crewed lunar variant, Long March 10, which would use 21 YF-100K engines at lift-off to launch Chinese astronauts and a lunar lander towards the moon before 2030.

Nasa also aims to send US astronauts back to the moon by 2028 for the first time since the end of the Apollo missions.

In February, China used part of a Long March 10A – another variant in the family – for a successful in-flight abort test for the Mengzhou crewed moon ship. The test stage completed a controlled descent and splashed down right next to a recovery vessel, raising expectations for the flight on Friday.

The mission was also China’s first attempt at mastering reusable rocket recovery, a key step towards cutting costs, increasing launch capacity and boosting the international competitiveness of the country’s space sector.

In December, two other Chinese rockets attempted SpaceX-style vertical landings using grid fins and landing legs, but both failed. Zhuque-3, a privately developed methane-liquid oxygen rocket from Landspace, saw its first stage crash near the target recovery area.

The state-owned Long March 12A’s first stage crashed mid-air after an engine failed to relight for the final landing burn.

Unlike Falcon 9’s approach of landing a rocket on its own legs, Long March 10B uses a sea-based net-and-cable system.

During re-entry, the first stage slows using its engines and aerodynamic control surfaces before descending into the recovery system. In the final landing phase, the net-and-cable device absorbs the rocket’s remaining energy and slows it further for a soft landing.

The idea of catching spacecraft with nets or cables dates back to the Cold War, when the US military recovered film capsules from Corona spy satellites using mid-air capture.

More recently, companies including SpaceX and Rocket Lab tested methods to catch returning rocket components using nets or helicopters, but largely abandoned those approaches because of operational challenges, such as high winds at sea.

According to Chinese social media, a second Zhuque-3 rocket could make another attempt at vertical landing in the coming weeks, while the full Long March 10A flight is expected later this year. Another launch of the Long March 10B reusable rocket is also expected later this year, according to CCTV.
