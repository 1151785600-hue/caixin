Tuscan, Korean and Cantonese: 3 chefs reimagine classic beef dishes in Hong Kong

SCMP | FREE | 1378 words | 2026-07-23

It’s the same animal. In a Tuscan kitchen, it’s carved tableside as a ritual. In a Korean kitchen, it’s sliced thin and gone in seconds. And in a Cantonese kitchen, it’s been braising since morning.

In Hong Kong, three chefs are bringing their love of beef from home, each cooking through the lens of a childhood tradition that shaped them long before they ever stepped into a professional kitchen. Here is how to eat beef well, because whether in Tuscany, Seoul, or Hong Kong, the rule is simple: when it comes to beef, do as the locals do.

Tuscan at Carna by Dario Cecchini

Dario Cecchini is a proud Tuscan – and when an eighth-generation butcher from Panzano in Chianti tells you how to eat beef, you listen.

For Cecchini, the Bistecca alla Fiorentina is the centrepiece of Italian beef. The steak arrives whole, close to 2kg, still shimmering from the grill. It is 4cm thick, which allows the surface to develop a deep, mahogany crust while the inside stays rare and cool. What you get in a single bite is a series of contrasts – char and blood, fat and lean, smoke and minerality. Cecchini calls this “a conversation of textures”.

He brings that conversation to Mondrian Hong Kong, where his eponymous Carna by Dario Cecchini serves the same Bistecca by the same rule: never season the steak before it touches fire. Salt and Tuscan olive oil are applied only after cooking. Salt draws moisture to the surface, which then steams rather than sears, robbing the crust of its depth. But the rule runs deeper than food science. “You are saying to the steak, you are already complete,” Cecchini says. “I am only here to finish your essence.”

If you want a deeper cut, Cecchini recommends the spider steak. Known in Italian as ragnolino, it is a small, humble cut from the inner thigh. A lean and muscular cut, it is often overlooked in favour of more famous names. Grilled simply on the bone and dressed with extra virgin Tuscan olive oil and Cecchini’s own herb salt, Profumo del Chianti, it proves his philosophy better than any sermon could: no cut is tough if treated with care. It is only ever waiting for the right hands.

That philosophy traces back to cucina povera – the cooking of the poor – that has shaped Tuscan tradition for centuries. Cecchini’s grandmother made one of his favourite dishes from boiled beef knees – Tenerumi in Insalata. “Those memories shaped me,” he says. “It is knowledge passed down through generations, and what we are doing is keeping that conversation alive.”

On Carna’s menu, each muscle is named: the scamone, the costata, the cappello del prete. Diners can look at an illustration of the cow marked with numbers and pick the part they want to try. “It is an act of respect,” Cecchini says. “To the animal that gave its life, to the farmer who raised it, and to the guest, whom we trust to care about where their food comes from.”

Korean at Moo-Lah by Born and Bred

The grill is already hot. Thin slices of beef hit the metal and curl at the edges within seconds, smoke rising as chopsticks dart across the table. The meat goes from raw to perfect in the time it takes to grab a spoonful of rice. If Tuscany celebrates beef with stately reverence, this is the Korean rhythm, where speed matters.

For Jung Sang-won, co-founder of Born & Bred in Seoul and Moo-Lah in Hong Kong, the etiquette of the grill is one he learned young. He remembers eating tteok-galbi patties made from rib finger meat as a child: the meat’s aroma slowly grilling, the texture tender yet structured, and the way the fat caught the edge of the fire.

“For me, this memory is not just about food,” Jung says. “It was my first deep experience of understanding texture and flavour in beef.”

That memory now travels to Hong Kong, where Jung is developing a wonton inspired by tteok-galbi, using hand-chopped Hanwoo finger meat to intentionally preserve structure and bite.

Hanwoo, Korea’s native cattle, is at the centre of Jung’s kitchen. But it is more than just a breed of cow; it is “the result of farming environments, the philosophy of producers, and long-developed rearing practices”, the chef says.

Historically, cattle in Korea were not just livestock – they were partners in farming. Because of this, Korean beef culture developed around the idea of using the whole animal rather than focusing only on the premium cuts. The Korean saying, Il-dubaekmi, literally translates to “one cow, one hundred flavours”. While Western butchery divides a cow into about 10 primary cuts, Korean butchers can break a cow down into over 120 distinct parts.

Ask Jung to name one cut most non-Koreans may have never heard of, and his answer is immediate: jebichuri. Jebichuri (neck chain) comes from the area between the neck and chest, a heavily used muscle that develops a deep flavour and a distinctive chew. “Many people assume that highly marbled cuts define quality,” Jung says. “But once they experience jebichuri, they realise that the beef’s flavour is not defined only by fat content.”

Because it is lean, jebichuri demands a fast hand and tastes best when thinly sliced and quickly grilled over high heat, allowing the surface to caramelise fast while the inside stays juicy. It is the perfect expression of the Korean preference for quick cooking, tied to communal dining. Meat is shared at the table, cooked together and never eaten in isolation. “Korean food culture is deeply tied to rice,” Jung explains. “Thin slicing allows for the ideal balance of temperature and texture when eaten with rice.”

Cantonese at Duddell’s

The pot starts cooking long before service. Inside are beef brisket tendon and white radish, submerged and simmering; the liquid barely moving as the hours pass. You’ll know it’s ready when the meat yields and absorbs the sweetness of the radish. As much as the brisket is the star, time is the ingredient that cannot be rushed. And for chef Chan Yau-leung of Duddell’s, this patience is the foundation of his cooking.

Chan grew up in Hong Kong in the 1960s, in modest circumstances. Beef brisket and tendon (ngau gan lam) noodles were a rare luxury reserved for festivals or birthdays. He still remembers the weight of those bowls. “It left an indelible impression,” he says.

He holds ngau gan lam to an exceptionally high standard because he knows what it meant to him and others like him even before he stepped into a professional kitchen. The tendons must be thick and unctuous. The brisket must have a robust beef flavour and be soft and aromatic – elevated into what he calls the signature “radish brisket” profile that defines comfort in Cantonese cuisine.

The preparation demands patience, and the cut must be subjected to slow, gentle heat. This is where Chan sees home cooks go wrong. Cuts that require time, like brisket, are rushed, while delicate cuts that need a flash of heat, like rib-eye or Wagyu, are overcooked. “The key is to match the cooking time and method precisely to the cut’s characteristics,” he says.

Chan has honed this precision in the kitchen for more than four decades. At Duddell’s, his Spicy Mala Beef Shank is marinated, boiled for two to three hours, then infused again with Sichuan peppercorns and chilli for depth. He takes a different approach to the tender rib-eye, choosing to mince and steam it with 20-year-old tangerine peel for his steamed minced beef patty.

His own favourite cut is one that rarely appears in the spotlight: ngau lei sam (ox tongue heart). It is also the most challenging to cook properly, according to the chef. “The texture varies significantly throughout the muscle,” Chan says. “Some parts are soft while others are tough. It requires immense precision, care, and experience to handle correctly.” To honour it, he slowly simmers the tongue to infuse it with flavour, then isolates only the tenderest core, preparing it with port wine and vine tomatoes. Time does the rest.
