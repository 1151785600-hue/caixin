Bridging the divide: how AI scientists unite despite growing geopolitical rifts

SCMP | FREE | 1247 words | 2026-07-21

For policymakers in Beijing and Washington, artificial intelligence (AI) has become a high-stakes arena in their strategic rivalry, closely tied to national interests and security.

From export controls on high-end semiconductors to heavy scrutiny of foreign investment, the future of AI would appear to be binary: one country’s technological leap is seen as a direct threat to the other.

Yet, far from the halls of power, some developers and scientists see that the true potential of AI is in expanding global capabilities.

This view united a diverse group of AI innovators and entrepreneurs who gathered in Hong Kong this month to explore how global collaboration and sharing knowledge could forge an entrepreneurial ecosystem that transcends deepening geopolitical rifts.

For Pak-Sun Ting, co-founder and CEO of Votee AI – developer of the first open-source Cantonese large language model (LLM) – scientific progress and collaboration in AI are like a “small undercurrent to help go against geopolitics”.

“Geopolitical winds change from time to time,” Ting said. “Government to government, there could be some disagreements. But from civilian to civilian, technology to technology, as long as they allow us and it is doable, we will take it and work with it.”

Votee AI has built partnerships with universities in Hong Kong and the United States, and with mainland Chinese tech giants. It has also set up an AI research lab in Toronto, as well as regional hubs in Kuala Lumpur and Ho Chi Minh City.

The company is on a mission to bring AI to underserved languages and replicate its foundry model to preserve languages in Southeast Asia, Africa and elsewhere.

“These collaborations validate the fact that geopolitics – if you take a big enough ruler, that is not going to matter much,” Ting said.

The former banking executive’s pragmatism is echoed by Votee AI’s technology adviser, John Whaley, who is also an adjunct lecturer in computer science at Stanford University and runs Inception Studio, an accelerator for AI founders.

Whaley said the commercial and productivity incentives for AI adoption were so immense that they pulled the global industry towards integration.

“The size of the opportunity dwarfs even the governments and geopolitics. The amount of productivity boost and positive impact is much bigger than any individual country.”

“This is why everyone is trying to vie for who is going to be the leader in the AI world,” Whaley added, highlighting the focus on sovereign AI and the strategic importance of access to AI, data, graphics processing units and computing power.

Global AI growth should not be a zero-sum game, he said, calling the creation of new AI technologies an expansion of the overall “pie” of global economic growth and productivity.

“A world where people have access, knowledge and capability – that is the world that we want to live in. It is going to help so much. There are a lot of things that humans should not be doing manually any more.”

Whaley also highlighted changes in AI regulation, saying the US government had been shifting from an unregulated position to a “new era” in which certain models were flagged as national security risks or of strategic importance.

When it comes to oversight, some leading AI developers in the US have suggested an even more cautious approach.

For instance, Dario Amodei, CEO of American AI research company Anthropic, wrote last month that AI’s exponential growth necessitated government oversight.

Amodei called for legal frameworks that would grant governments the authority to “block or deter deployment of the model if it is determined … to present unacceptable risks”.

“US export controls on frontier chips and semiconductor manufacturing equipment to China have been a major contributor to the US’ overall lead in AI, and these policies need to be expanded, tightened and coordinated with other like-minded states,” he added.

Meanwhile, as innovators around the world work on AI, Whaley described the future of AI as global and “not going to be necessarily just centred in Silicon Valley”, where he said there was “a hyper concentration of capital and talent”.

The next great AI company could very well emerge from Hong Kong or the Greater Bay Area, he added.

Whaley voiced an eagerness to bring the founder and builder energy to the region, as his accelerator organises intense retreats for select AI founders to workshop their business ideas and pitch to potential investors.

“Silicon Valley is Silicon Valley because it is a place where [failure] is very socially accepted. People happily, proudly tell you about all the things that they tried and failed. Culturally, it may be a little bit harder to do that here in Hong Kong.”

He said the focus should be on fostering an environment with freedom to experiment and build, rather than replicating the Silicon Valley model and overlooking the robust local university system and available capital.

Chinat Yu, an AI researcher and strategist from Hong Kong, is working to bridge that gap.

By using Hong Kong’s status as a regional “human capital magnet”, the city could synthesise local research with an entrepreneurial spirit that encouraged STEM students to pursue moonshot innovations, said Yu, who helped organise this month’s Hong Kong AI Summit.

“Too many people ask if it is the US or China [that will win the AI race],” said Yu, who graduated from Stanford’s master’s programme in learning, design and technology in 2024. “The right question is to ask how you do it [with both] the US and China.”

“By building this bridge between the two bay areas and connecting those innovation hubs, we are able to hopefully transform the culture here such that this then becomes the future innovation hub globally.”

“A lot of companies from China that are thinking about going global are already naturally going through Hong Kong,” he added, pointing to mainland Chinese AI start-ups such as Zhipu and MiniMax that were being listed in the city.

Yu said that by bringing in mentors who had been in the start-up trenches for decades, local founders and aspiring entrepreneurs could learn to define customer needs and problem-solve as innovators.

“We want to build local but dream global.”

And as AI collapses the barriers to building software, more individual creators can take part in developing new technologies.

Quincy Larson, founder of online learning platform freeCodeCamp.org, said the focus for acquiring programming skills should not be rote syntax, but rather domain-specific expertise fused with technical literacy.

“I would encourage people to learn the fundamentals of programming, but you do not need to spend thousands of hours getting good at programming any more, unless you really want to focus on some area that is beyond what LLMs are capable of.”

Larson envisioned a future in which the next wave of builders would be industry experts – lawyers, accountants and managers of different fields.

“Everybody is going to need to become a technical product manager,” he said. “They need to understand the fundamentals. They also need to understand some domain that they have a lot of insight into.

“They understand that world really well and now they can potentially build solutions within their existing organisation or create a company that builds those solutions.”

Larson said his platform provided children in California and western China access to the same learning resources for free, and that the future was “more about one person who holds all the moving parts in their head and shared memories in the computer”.
