Hong Kong and Shenzhen the perfect end points for modern Silk Road: leading economist

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1085 words | 2026-07-10

Prominent economist Jeffrey Sachs says he chose Hong Kong and Shenzhen as the final stop on an overland journey retracing the footsteps of Marco Polo because of their significant role in powering the world’s green and digital age.

The Columbia University professor set off from Rome on June 13 for a 43-day, 15,000km (9,300-mile) journey, the Marco Polo Drive of Peace, Culture and Sustainable Development.

The former adviser to the UN secretary general said the journey was designed to send a message of peace, connectivity and sustainable development through green technology, as well as to highlight the importance of the modern equivalent of the Silk Road.

While the eastern end of the ancient Silk Road was Xian in northwest China, Sachs said Hong Kong and Shenzhen now played that role.

“Shenzhen and Hong Kong are our final stops because together they form the world’s greatest innovation cluster – the one that will power the world’s green and digital age,” he said in an email.

“If Venice was the western gateway from which Marco Polo set out, Hong Kong and Shenzhen are the eastern gateway of our own time: the great innovators and interconnectors of East and West, North and South.”

He said the Hong Kong also served as a connecting point between the overland Silk Road and the maritime Silk Road.

“There is a deep historical resonance as well. In the era of Marco Polo and after, the Yuan and Ming dynasties were the world’s greatest seafarers of their age – Zheng He’s astounding voyages reached the coasts of Arabia and East Africa decades before the Europeans rounded the Cape of Good Hope,” he said.

“The maritime Silk Road began on these shores. So ending in Hong Kong brings us from the overland Silk Road to the ancient and modern maritime one, and from the age of caravans to the age of green and digital technology.”

The expedition commemorates the 750th anniversary of the Venetian merchant’s arrival at the court of Kublai Khan. Polo then spent the next 17 years at court and travelling around China before returning to Venice.

His account of his travels gave Western audiences their first in-depth insight into the country and has become a symbol for centuries of cultural and commercial exchanges between East and West.

Sachs is travelling with his wife Sonia, Patrick Zhong, founding managing partner of private equity firm M31 Capital, and Zhong’s son Jimmy.

They are travelling in a vehicle provided by the Chinese electric car giant BYD. Sachs said green infrastructure was improving far more rapidly than commonly understood, with charging stations available along the entire route.

“The route is very comfortable and welcoming. It is only needless wars and bloc politics that threaten to sever these ancient connections. Our journey demonstrates that the corridor is open, safe, and full of promise,” he said.

In a post on his website last month, Sachs wrote that he had set off from Rome, rather than Polo’s native city of Venice, because “like the Polos before us, we are carrying a message from the Pope, in our case Pope Leo XIV’s new encyclical [official proclamation], Magnifica Humanitas, a plea for peace and for the wise and humane use of our powerful new technologies”.

“The journey seeks to carry forward a mission of friendship, mutual understanding, and peaceful coexistence at a time when conflict, mistrust, and division continue to shape many parts of the world,” a brochure about the trip said.

Sachs travelled through Croatia, Serbia, Bulgaria, Turkey, Georgia, Azerbaijan and Kazakhstan before reaching Uzbekistan last week. He will cross into China through the Khorgos land port in Xinjiang around July 15.

The travellers will then pass through several major mainland Chinese cities, including Urumqi, Xian, Suzhou, Shanghai and Shenzhen, before reaching Hong Kong around July 26.

The brochure said several governments along the route had extended a warm welcome, including meetings with the presidents of Croatia, Slovenia and Serbia and the prime minister of Georgia. Sachs is also expected to meet a mainland Chinese government minister and the chief executive of Hong Kong, John Lee Ka-chiu.

Upon reaching Uzbekistan, Sachs described the journey as “a wonderful, fascinating trip”.

Sachs is also using the journey to hit out at US foreign policy and the belief that “the civilisations of East and West are fated to clash rather than cooperate”.

“Marco Polo knew better. At the far end of the longest road to the East, he found not enemies but a brilliant, sophisticated civilisation eager to trade ideas as readily as goods,” he wrote on his website on June 19.

In a second “postcard” posted on Tuesday from Khiva, Uzbekistan, Sachs wrote that as the United States marked its 250th anniversary, it continued to antagonise ancient civilisations such as Iran – a nation whose “statecraft and poetry and science reach back some 10 times longer than America’s own short national life.”

He added: “There is a wisdom in the old city walls and ancient madrasas of Khiva that Washington would do well to relearn: that great and ancient nations are not to be bullied into submission but should be met in trade as equals.”

He also described the overland journey as a testament to human unity, saying it was designed to celebrate “the long, patient, borderless collaboration of human minds across every frontier of nations, faiths and languages”.

The comments echoed a pre-departure interview Sachs gave to the Italian newspaper Il Fatto Quotidiano, where he called for a multipolar world.

“No one should be viewed intrinsically as an enemy,” Sachs said. “For every other power, there are issues that are real. Security issues come first. Economic issues come next.”

He urged world powers to adopt a standard of “indivisible security” where states accommodate rather than dismiss the core concerns of rivals.

“Multipolarity means not only the reality of multiple power centres, but a different kind of geopolitics, where every power centre relates in a prudential way with every other power centre,” he said. “We respect your red lines. You respect our red lines. We discuss. We make what used to be called indivisible security.”

He also restated his long-standing view that Europe should engage with Russia independently rather than following the US lead.

“Europe, as Europe, should be having diplomacy with Russia as Russia,” Sachs told the publication. “In other words, not hugging [Ukrainian President Volodymyr] Zelensky for the 10,000th time, but sitting down with [Russian] President [Vladimir] Putin for the first time in years.”
