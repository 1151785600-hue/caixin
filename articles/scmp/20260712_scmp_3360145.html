China to head off AI-fuelled inequality by lifting salaries, narrowing pay gap

SCMP | FREE | 490 words | 2026-07-12

China has made income growth and the reform of its wage-distribution system a priority within broader efforts to cushion the domestic job market against the impact of artificial intelligence over the next five years.

A new blueprint released by the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security on Thursday laid out a detailed road map for the strategy, vowing to promote collective wage bargaining within the private sector, ensure steady wage growth, and tilt pay towards frontline workers.

Within state enterprises, the plan urges managing “excessively high earnings” while raising pay incentives for critical talent and linking compensation directly to technical expertise. To protect the bottom tier of the workforce, Beijing called for refining its mechanism for adjusting minimum wage standards and initiating legislative research on minimum wage laws.

Authorities aim to “adopt a comprehensive approach to manage how shifting external environments and AI advancements affect the workforce, prioritising job-expanding initiatives”, according to the blueprint.

The document comes as a wide income gap, especially between rural and urban areas, remains a major concern in the world’s second-largest economy. And some economists warn that the rapid rise of AI could make matters worse.

Beijing has also pledged to “combine investment in physical assets with investment in people” – a new slogan introduced in its 15th five-year plan – to tap into the spending power of lower-income groups while grappling with weak domestic demand.

Even if AI enhances worker efficiency, compensation hasn’t grown in tandem

Huang Yiping, Peking University

The importance of policy guidance in stabilising employment has become more pronounced amid the swift advancement of AI technology, which could exacerbate income inequality, according to Huang Yiping, dean of Peking University’s National School of Development.

“Capital captures a larger share of the profits from enhanced productivity, as it stems from technology rather than individual skills of the labours,” Huang said in a June interview with the Shanghai-based Jiemian News, an online platform for financial and business news. “That’s why, even if AI enhances worker efficiency, compensation hasn’t grown in tandem.”

Unless those AI-driven gains translate into new jobs and higher household income, consumer demand will fail to match expanding supply, prolonging the current supply-demand imbalance in China’s economy, he added.

In the blueprint, China pledged to steadily expand its middle-income population over the next five years, with a specific focus on lifting skilled labourers and rural migrant workers into that bracket.

Furthermore, China will launch “large-scale” vocational-training programmes for the unemployed. The plan said these initiatives would focus on areas tied to “new quality productive forces” – such as AI, advanced manufacturing, the digital economy, and the low-altitude economy – as well as services such as childcare and senior care.

The plan also called for strengthening the protection of labour rights and improving the paid-annual-leave system, alongside advancing feasibility studies for legislation on fundamental labour standards.
