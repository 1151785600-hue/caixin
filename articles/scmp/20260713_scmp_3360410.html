How Philippines is reshaping South China Sea defence, 10 years after Hague ruling

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1013 words | 2026-07-13

A decade after a tribunal in The Hague ruled on July 12, 2016, that China’s sweeping South China Sea claims had no legal basis, Philippine officials and analysts say Manila remains heavily outmatched at sea but has begun turning its landmark legal victory into a more credible form of deterrence.

The Philippines brought the case in 2013 under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (Unclos), challenging Beijing’s nine-dash line and other claims.

The tribunal ruled that China’s nine-dash line had no legal basis and that it had violated Manila’s sovereign rights in its exclusive economic zone, though it did not decide which country owned the disputed reefs and features.

China rejected the proceedings and the ruling, saying the tribunal had no jurisdiction and insisting it had sovereignty over the disputed features. It has since built and militarised artificial islands on reefs also claimed by the Philippines, a development critics cite as proof that the award did little to restrain Beijing.

Speakers at a Manila forum on Friday said the past decade had seen the Philippines strengthen its ability to raise the cost of what they described as Beijing’s “creeping invasion” in the West Philippine Sea – Manila’s term for waters it claims in the South China Sea.

Realistically, the country could not really “take on China because we will never have parity with China in terms of military, but at least [we will have the capability] to be able to inflict enough pain to deter, to prevent, and for China to at least hesitate”, former military chief General Emmanuel Bautista told the forum.

The forum, entitled “A Decade of the Arbitral Award: Credible Deterrence in Defence of the West Philippine Sea”, was sponsored by Stratbase, a local think tank established by the late Philippine foreign secretary Albert del Rosario, who led Manila’s arbitration case.

“I think when it comes to building deterrence, clearly there has been a lot of improvement, especially in the last couple of years and the air force is in a much, much better place now than it ever was before, even though there’s still a lot that needs to be done,” said Professor Jay Batongbacal, director of the University of the Philippines Institute for Maritime Affairs and Law of the Sea.

“We know the odds are not clearly in our favour, but yet we stay the course,” Defence Secretary Gilberto Teodoro Jnr said in his keynote address.

Teodoro pointed to several ways forward, saying the military arsenal should no longer concentrate on storing small-calibre ammunition and “should not be outsourced”.

He said the scope of military operations was being reoriented to active defence covering an additional 2 million km of maritime domain – a result of a partial shift under the Comprehensive Archipelagic Defence Concept, which treated the Philippines’ maritime zones, islands and surrounding waters as a single defence space.

General Romeo Brawner Jnr, the current Armed Forces chief of staff, said “one of the most important lessons” was maintaining a sustained and constant presence in the maritime domain area by the armed forces, the coastguard and the Bureau of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources.

For both Brawner and Teodoro, the armed forces have been able to leverage the arbitral victory by getting like-minded nations to support the award, engage in multilateral maritime activities and assist the military in upgrading its war assets.

On Friday, 14 countries sent their envoys to the forum and jointly signed a statement affirming that the arbitral award remained “final, legally binding and definitive”.

They also rejected China’s nine- and 10-dash lines and opposed its use of coercion to harass Manila’s lawful operations in the South China Sea.

China reiterated on Sunday that the ruling was “null and void and has no binding force” and Beijing “neither accepts nor recognises it”, Associated Press reported.

Deterrence also extended into cyberspace, said retired navy Rear Admiral Roy Vincent Trinidad, spokesman for Philippine operations in the South China Sea.

Trinidad said online messaging, which he attributed to Chinese influence operations, sought to “destroy the will to fight of the Filipino people, to create divisions internally, politically, economically, everything, to cause us to fight among ourselves”.

He added that such online operations constantly promoted the message that the arbitral victory was but “a mere scrap of paper” – a quote by former president Rodrigo Duterte as an excuse to temporarily shelve the dispute in favour of billions of dollars’ worth of concessionary loans from China – and that other Southeast Asian countries viewed the arbitral victory with disfavour.

Critics have long argued that the “scrap of paper” description appeared to be borne out by events on the water.

After Manila filed the arbitration case in 2013, Beijing built six more military islands on Philippine-claimed Fiery Cross Reef, Subi Reef, Johnson Reef, Gaven Reef, Hughes Reef and Cuarteron Reef, in addition to its structures on Mischief Reef in the mid-1990s.

“I was in the office of the [foreign] secretary at the time. It took us completely by surprise,” said Evan Garcia, former Permanent Representative of the Philippines to the United Nations Office at Geneva and ambassador to the United Kingdom, who spoke on the issue for the first time.

“After we protested, the Chinese said, don’t worry, this is a fisherman’s shelter. Especially because they said they’d never militarise Mischief Reef. And now we can see what has happened and we can see the full extent of Beijing’s ambitions,” Garcia said.

“So you have to understand what the Philippines had faced since 1995, since all the attempts to talk bilaterally and multilaterally had failed.”

As for online posts saying that no other Asean state backed the arbitration and its ruling, Garcia stressed of the regional bloc: “Asean has never supported the Chinese [nine or 10-dash] line as a group. Just because you’re silent doesn’t mean you support it.”

Garcia said the 2016 ruling made clear that “nothing on the nine-dash line is legitimate. So it’s all Philippine EEZ as far as we’re concerned”.
