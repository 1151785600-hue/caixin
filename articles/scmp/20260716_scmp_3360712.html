Hongkongers’ new pet peeve over dogs at diners sparks calls for tougher vetting

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 982 words | 2026-07-16

Hong Kong’s introduction of pet-friendly dining in designated restaurants has drawn significant public criticism, leading some lawmakers to call for tighter vetting of licences.

Since the scheme began on July 9, the Food and Environmental Hygiene Department has received about 20 complaints.

The department earlier said most complaints involved minor issues such as leash length and seating arrangements.

However, viral clips showing dogs licking plates and sitting on tables and chairs have drawn widespread criticism.

On Monday, a Chinese restaurant in Kai Tak that holds a pet-friendly permit received a warning letter after the department found that food was repeatedly cooked or heated on the dining table over three days.

In response, catering sector lawmaker Jonathan Leung Chun said the initial “chaos” would only happen once as the industry settled in.

“This is a learning phase for the whole society; if the restaurants find dog-friendliness is not their thing, they should return the permits, but those who are good at it will thrive,” he said.

“Some operators tell us their business has doubled; that is a surge you do not even see during holidays.”

Leung said businesses should not be banned from becoming dog-friendly based on the type of food they offered, because that would be unfair to those that perform well.

However, Leung admitted many rushed to apply purely out of a fear of missing out without assessing whether their customers owned pets or if their building’s deed of mutual covenant legally allowed dogs.

He believed that by the next round, businesses would be better prepared, having familiarised themselves with what is required to operate as dog-friendly.

According to the department list, the number of dog-friendly restaurants fell from over 940 on Thursday to 937 on Saturday, before climbing to 947 by Wednesday as more eateries received permits.

A check by the South China Morning Post found that several restaurants were removed from the list.

Three were located inside public housing estates: a McDonald’s branch in Yau Tong’s Yau Lai shopping centre, an Itamomo Cafe in Kwun Tong’s Tsui Ping North Shopping Circuit and a Cafe 100% branch in Tseung Kwan O’s Ming Tak shopping centre.

Another three were hotel restaurants. Alva House and Yat Heen in Alva Hotel in Sha Tin, both of which shut down in June, were removed, along with Nan Fong inside Le Méridien Hong Kong in Cyberport.

The remaining removed venues were Nodi in M+ Museum in the West Kowloon Cultural District, Poach by 3rd Space in Mong Kok, and a Super Super Congee and Noodle branch at the Manhattan Mid-Town shopping arcade in Sham Shui Po.

The SCMP reached out to five restaurants – Itamomo Cafe, McDonald’s, Nam Fong, Super Super and Poach by 3rd Space – but none could be contacted for comments.

Nodi withdrew because M+ bans pets, while Cafe 100%’s parent company withdrew over concerns over the impact the scheme might have on its other patrons.

Dog-owner Damon Chan, in his thirties, told the SCMP that he has not visited any dog-friendly restaurants in the past week because of a wave of online hostility towards pets at dining venues.

“I originally thought dining out with my dog would be a happy thing, but I don’t want to feel pressured by the feeling that we are constantly being monitored [by other customers],” Chan said, adding he would feel safer to visit once those comments die down.

He said dog owners must exercise self-discipline, and urged non-dog owners to be more understanding. “Only then can everyone dine together comfortably and happily,” he added.

Lawmaker Adrian Pedro Ho King-hong, who is also a pet owner, described the initial friction as an expected “adjustment period,” saying there was clear room for improvement.

He argued that certain venues – such as cha chaan teng, fast-food outlets and traditional Chinese teahouses – were fundamentally unsuited for animals due to their cramped layouts and high foot traffic.

However, he stressed that the onus was on operators to adapt, saying that restaurants should either redesign their space to accommodate pets, or simply opt out of the scheme. “The policy itself isn’t wrong; the issue is the unsuitable hardware.”

He criticised the current lottery system, arguing that a blind draw could unfairly hand quotas to big-name chains with multiple branches, even when their layouts were not appropriate for animals.

But some smaller independent shops that had successfully operated as pet-friendly venues for years were left empty-handed in the lottery, he said.

The government previously received over 2,100 applications for the scheme, more than double the initial quota of 1,000 places. After a ballot, over 940 restaurants received permits to begin operations on July 9.

The government said it would launch a second round of applications after six months, offering an additional 1,000 quotas.

“The government should assess each applicant individually during the screening process,” Ho said.

He added that authorities should also mandate designated pet zones to give diners clearer seating choices, including those who do not own dogs.

Currently, the government has only said operators may decide whether to designate dog-friendly zones based on their circumstances, and advised physical barriers in the guidelines on good practices.

“There needs to be that physical distance, even if it’s just setting aside two out of 10 tables in a designated corner.”

John Burns, an emeritus professor at the University of Hong Kong’s department of politics and public administration, noted that the city lacks experience in implementing pet-friendly dining compared with neighbouring jurisdictions like Taiwan, leaving local operators with a limited understanding of the practical consequences.

Nevertheless, Burns said he believed that any friction over the policy would remain contained because it did not affect every member of society, but a citywide survey would be helpful to gauge public sentiment and guide future fine-tuning.
