China boosts North Korea partnership with rare praise during high-profile visit

SCMP | FREE | 495 words | 2026-07-10

North Korean Premier Pak Thae-song has begun his three-day trip to China, as Beijing offers rare praise for its socialist neighbour’s economic achievements.

Pak, a member of the Politburo Presidium of the ruling Workers’ Party of Korea, landed at Beijing Capital International Airport on Friday morning accompanied by a joint party and government delegation.

He is expected to attend an event marking the 65th anniversary of the signing of Beijing’s only formal defence pact, the China-North Korea Treaty of Friendship, Cooperation and Mutual Assistance.

In a rare move early Friday, People’s Daily, a Communist Party newspaper, published an article praising important North Korean construction projects for “infusing socialist construction with robust vitality”.

The piece highlighted Pyongyang’s progress in tackling housing issues for both urban and rural residents, noting the completion of 50,000 housing units in the capital over the past five years and some 32,000 newly built homes in rural areas in 2025.

It also commended North Korea’s progress in rural food production and large-scale construction of industrial factories, hospitals and service facilities across the country.

Pyongyang has struggled to revive its economy in recent years following strict border closures during the coronavirus pandemic, but it is now seeing unexpected boosts driven by arms sales and troop deployments to Russia, alongside supplies and financing from China.

Its economic downturn began to reverse following Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in February 2022, with growth expanding to 3.7 per cent in 2024, according to estimates from central bank observers. Pyongyang has openly supported Russia in the Ukraine war.

Beijing has moved to strengthen its own strategic partnership with Pyongyang after North Korea and Russia signed a mutual defence pact in 2024, and expanded ties in trade, science and technology.

Pak’s trip came just weeks after Chinese President Xi Jinping’s landmark visit to North Korea last month. It marked Xi’s first overseas trip this year and his first to the country in seven years.

During the summit, Xi described Beijing’s commitment to North Korea and its leader, Kim Jong-un, as “unwavering”. The Chinese president called for stronger exchanges at all levels in areas such as foreign policy, law enforcement and the military.

The two heads of state laid a floral tribute at the China-North Korea Friendship Tower, a monument dedicated to the Chinese troops who fought in the Korean war.

They later visited the new campus of the Workers’ Party’s Central Cadres Training School, Pyongyang’s top academy for officials.

Last year, Kim travelled to Beijing for commemorations for the 80th anniversary of the end of World War II.

The ceremony saw Xi, Kim and their Russian counterpart, Vladimir Putin, standing side by side in Tiananmen Square, a rare display of solidarity among the three countries amid mounting pressure from the United States and its allies.

In October last year, Chinese Premier Li Qiang visited North Korea, joining celebrations for the 80th anniversary of North Korea’s ruling party.
