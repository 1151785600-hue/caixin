EU should think twice before weaponising its market against China

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 917 words | 2026-07-11

For much of the post-Cold-War era, the European Union understood economic interdependence as a source of mutual prosperity. Open markets, free trade and multilateral rules were regarded not merely as economic principles but as the foundations of European normative influence. That assumption is rapidly giving way to a different logic.

In an era of intensifying geopolitical rivalry, Brussels increasingly views trade, investment and technology as the power instruments of strategic competition.

The shift is unmistakable. Export controls, foreign investment screening, the Anti-Coercion Instrument and growing discussions of an economic deterrence doctrine all reflect an ambition to transform Europe’s vast single market into a geopolitical asset.

Some European analysts have gone further, arguing that access to the EU market should become a strategic weapon against economic coercion, particularly from China. With a population of 450 million and the world’s second-largest economy, the EU appears to possess formidable leverage.

Yet this calculation rests on a questionable assumption: that market size automatically translates into geopolitical power.

Market size and coercive leverage are not the same thing. Large markets undoubtedly attract investment, shape global standards and generate trade influence. But coercive power also depends on whether one can impose costs that the counterpart cannot easily avoid while remaining resilient to countermeasures. Market size alone does not guarantee that leverage.

Three conditions determine whether economic leverage is likely to succeed: dependence must be asymmetric, substitution must be costly and the assets being restricted must be genuinely irreplaceable. Across much of the EU-China economic relationship, these conditions are either absent or weaker than many policymakers assume.

Europe remains an important destination for Chinese exports, but it is no longer China’s indispensable market. The EU’s share of Chinese exports has declined from 17.1 per cent in 2019 to 14.4 per cent in 2024 as Chinese companies diversify procurement, relocate production and accelerate indigenous technological capabilities.

By the first quarter of this year, trade with Belt and Road Initiative partner countries accounted for 51.2 per cent of China’s trade. As Beijing’s capacity for trade substitution improves, the coercive leverage of Europe’s market diminishes. China would incur substantial costs if access to the European market were restricted, but they would be significantly lower than a decade ago.

Even if asymmetric dependence existed, successful coercion would still require a second condition: low import substitutability. Here, Europe’s position is considerably weaker than often acknowledged. China remains by far the EU’s largest source of imports and, importantly, Europe’s dependence is concentrated precisely in sectors that underpin its industrial competitiveness.

By 2024, imports of electrical machinery and components had reached €96.8 billion (US$110.42 billion), telecommunications equipment €60.9 billion, and office and data-processing equipment €45.9 billion. Together, they represented nearly 40 per cent of all EU imports from China. These products constitute critical inputs for European manufacturing, digital infrastructure and the green transition. Replacing them would require lengthy supply chain adjustments, new production capacity and substantial investment.

The final condition is irreplaceability. This is where Europe possesses genuine strategic leverage, but it is double-edged. The clearest example is the Dutch company ASML, whose monopoly over extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography and advanced deep ultraviolet (DUV) systems has become a critical chokepoint for China’s semiconductor ambitions.

China remains highly dependent on foreign suppliers for leading-edge semiconductor manufacturing equipment, particularly lithography. It remains the world’s biggest buyer of semiconductor manufacturing equipment and its share of domestically produced wafer fabrication equipment has risen dramatically after years of intensified state investment.

Rather than halting China’s technological progress, Western restrictions have accelerated indigenous innovation across much of its semiconductor equipment industry, even as advanced lithography remains a significant technological bottleneck.

Huawei Technologies has reportedly made a breakthrough in reducing its dependence on EUV lithography. It is bypassing export restrictions on EUV lithography by shifting its semiconductor strategy from brute-force transistor shrinking to system-level efficiency. If this breakthrough is confirmed, then Europe’s most monopolised sources of strategic leverage would become less irreplaceable, weakening the coercive value of export controls.

Market size generates centripetal impact because it attracts investment, talent and innovation, shaping commercial incentives and encouraging integration. Strategic leverage, by contrast, derives from control over indispensable assets, critical technologies and irreplaceable capabilities. Conflating these two forms of power risks encouraging policies that overestimate Europe’s ability to coerce while underestimating its vulnerabilities.

Protecting economic security and critical technologies, diversifying supply chains and responding to trade imbalances are entirely legitimate in a fragmented world economy. The size of the EU market remains its greatest structural advantage. But it is not leverage when there is no alternative supply of its needed materials, components, equipment and technology.

China supplies 100 per cent of the EU’s heavy rare earth elements, 85 per cent of the light rare earth elements and 97 per cent of the magnesium imports required for the digital and green transitions. China holds dominant positions in specific manufacturing machinery, green technology supply chains and consumer electronics components. The European industrial base remains deeply reliant on Chinese-sourced intermediate goods and battery components.

In conclusion, the EU’s market remains a source of considerable economic influence, but its coercive leverage should not be overstated. As China diversifies its markets, advances its technological capabilities and deepens economic ties with the Global South, Europe’s leverage inevitably declines.

In an increasingly diversified world economy, strategic influence will derive less from market size than from technological competitiveness and innovation, industrial capacity and resilience, and the ability to build mutually beneficial economic partnerships.
