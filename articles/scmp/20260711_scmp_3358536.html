Why Hong Kong firms looking to integrate AI must consider fresh grads

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 792 words | 2026-07-11

Hong Kong is trapped in a structural economic paradox that threatens both its long-term competitiveness and social stability. Organisations are paralysed by the urgent need for artificial intelligence (AI) transformation, complaining day in and day out that they lack technological talent.

An International Data Corporation (IDC) study last year revealed that 55 per cent of Hong Kong companies face significant challenges in implementing AI – higher than the global average of 48 per cent. Shockingly, 43 per cent admit completely lacking AI expertise or skills – despite being willing to pay premium rates to attract talent.

The irony would be amusing if it were not so damaging. While executives bemoan a talent drought, an incredibly bright, AI-native generation is practically starving for employment opportunities. Entry-level job vacancies for graduates have plummeted by 60 per cent over the past three years as AI, compounded by the city’s economic restructuring, reshapes labour demand.

Let’s be clear: this decline is not a temporary, cyclical blip. The 2026 PwC Global AI Jobs Barometer found that standard entry-level roles have shrunk by 10 per cent globally since 2019. Roles traditionally considered the stepping stones to a professional career, such as junior administrative and basic IT positions, are being swiftly automated out of existence.

This trend has triggered an existential threat to our long-term talent pipeline. In the IDC study, 74 per cent of Hong Kong companies say shrinking entry-level positions have made it increasingly difficult to hire and train future leaders. But whose fault is that?

Organisations can only achieve real productivity gains when AI is deeply integrated into their daily workflows. But they are stuck because senior executives frequently lack the basic tech fluency required to safely integrate AI into daily corporate operations. Instead of adapting, management relies on a legacy hiring mindset that blindly filters out fresh graduates for lacking “relevant prior experience”.

We can’t change the deeply entrenched mindset of employers overnight. But reverse mentoring could serve as a strategic eye-opener, providing employers with proof that there is value in a multi-generational workforce. Senior executives possess business acumen, deep institutional memory and vital strategic intuition, but they often lag behind in technology.

When employers see how tech-savvy youth safely automate away a chronic operational bottleneck, it shatters the institutional bias that fresh graduates bring nothing to the table. It forces management to appreciate the unique, transformative value of Gen Z, overhaul their long-term employment strategy and open up newly reimagined, permanent entry-level positions.

This cross-generational framework is far more than an idealistic theory; it is an actionable business model. This October, Esperanza is launching an AI-Powered Workflow Automation Lab in partnership with the University of Hong Kong, Hong Kong General Chamber of Commerce and Jobsdb to provide a concrete blueprint for this exact paradigm.

Under the guidance of an expert AI practitioner, participants will bring one real workflow from their organisations – such as human resource onboarding, client triage or project management. They will be paired with AI majors from HKU as their coaches. Together, these multi-generational teams will build, test, improve and prepare an automated workflow for internal roll-out. The executives provide the business context, while the students support the technical execution.

Scaling this model across the city requires a synchronised, systemic stakeholder reset across our educational, business, governmental and philanthropic organisations. Universities can no longer graduate students into a vacuum. By working alongside executives, students can acquire commercial domain knowledge, workplace etiquette and durable life skills.

We need more robust programmes like HKU’s Co-Operative Education Programme, a credit-earning course where students work full-time in a professional setting for at least six months.

But it takes two to tango. Businesses should provide more real-world project-based learning opportunities to students. To encourage more academic and business partnerships, the government and higher education institutes need to review the funding model for placement programmes. The scope of the STEM Internship Scheme, which subsidises tech companies and start-ups to engage interns for up to 90 days, should be broadened to cover all companies.

AI transformation is a universal business survival requirement not a niche for tech firms. Instead of asking interns to perform routine grunt work, companies should capitalise on their creativity, digital savviness and market insights. Foundations could also fund NGOs to run similar schemes for the rising NEET generation: young people not in education, employment or training.

Hong Kong cannot afford to let its youth languish while its businesses fall behind in the global digital economy. By formalising reverse mentoring, we can shift employer mindsets, close our technical talent gap and build a genuinely resilient economy. Talent is on our doorstep; we simply need the foresight to let them guide us forward.
