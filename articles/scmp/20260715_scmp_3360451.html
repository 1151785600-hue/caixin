China’s trade surges in first half of 2026, maintaining growth amid global tensions

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 830 words | 2026-07-15

China’s exports continued to grow robustly in June, supported by a boom in technology exports – including artificial intelligence products and robotics – extending the positive momentum seen in previous months and shrugging off geopolitical headwinds.

The country’s exports rose by 27 per cent year on year to US$412.39 billion last month, according to Chinese customs data released on Tuesday, beating the 18.5 per cent growth forecast by economists in a poll from financial data provider Wind.

Imports grew by 36 per cent last month to US$286.76 billion, higher than the Wind poll’s projections of 24.25 per cent growth, leading to a trade surplus of US$125.62 billion, up from US$105.43 billion in May.

In the first half of the year, exports jumped 17.6 per cent while imports rose 26.6 per cent.

“China’s export growth exceeded market expectations again in June. The export boom has been a key driver of China’s economy so far this year,” said Zhang Zhiwei, president and chief economist at Pinpoint Asset Management.

Zhang said that strong exports were also expected in the second half of the year, which would demonstrate the competitiveness and resilience of China’s manufacturing sector, while also potentially fuelling trade tensions, particularly with Europe.

China’s June exports surged to a 53-month high, while imports also reached their highest level since June 2021, according to Lynn Song, chief economist for Greater China at the Dutch bank ING.

The trade surplus was the highest since January 2025, he added.

“Exports have been one of China’s primary growth engines in recent years, and this year’s acceleration continues to beat expectations ... the base case is for solid export growth to continue,” Song said in a research note on Tuesday.

Officials noted that an increase in global inflationary pressures and geopolitical conflicts may put pressure on China’s trade in the latter half of 2026.

“But ... we are confident and capable of maintaining the positive momentum in the development of foreign trade,” said Wang Jun, vice-minister of the General Administration of Customs.

The latest export figures extended China’s strong momentum this year, with demand driven by the global AI boom, data centre expansion and energy security concerns, which supported shipments across sectors ranging from batteries to semiconductors.

The data also came ahead of a closely watched July 24 deadline, when temporary US tariffs imposed under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 are due to expire, and when Washington could replace them with new measures under Section 301.

China’s exports to the United States rose by 13.9 per cent year on year in June, set against a low baseline in the same month last year.

Exports to members of Asean rose by 34.6 per cent year on year, while shipments to members of the European Union increased by 18.5 per cent.

Xu Tianchen, senior economist at the Economist Intelligence Unit (EIU), said June’s export performance reflected substantial front-loading, both directly to the US and via the Association of Southeast Asian Nations, with shipments to the US reaching 95.5 per cent of their “normal” level from two years earlier.

“Front-loading will gradually ease after new US tariffs take effect in late July. The good news for China is that it’s not singled out by US tariffs, which seem to be an indiscriminate hike against its trade partners,” he said.

Last month, China’s exports of integrated circuits rose by 121.9 per cent year on year in value terms, while microchip import value grew by 72.3 per cent.

Gary Ng, senior economist for the Asia-Pacific region at Natixis, said China’s external trade continues to outperform expectations due to the AI boom.

“The momentum comes from strong domestic demand for AI infrastructure and adoption. It also plays a core role in importing components and exporting ICT [Information and Communications Technology] final products to the world,” he said.

“While the tech narrative will remain supportive, it is too early to say that demand in other sectors has picked up, which could continue to drag on overall economic growth,” Ng added.

Overall, technology-related exports drove growth across multiple sectors in June, with China’s global shipments of hi-tech products rising 52.2 per cent year on year and exports of mechanical and electrical products up 34.2 per cent in value terms.

Chinese officials also highlighted the rapid expansion of the country’s robotics industry during a press conference on Tuesday. Exports of Chinese-made surgical robots more than tripled in the first half of the year, and the country’s cleaning robots are now used in thousands of households worldwide.

China’s rare earth exports fell by 34.1 per cent year on year by volume, as Beijing continued to tighten controls on shipments of minerals crucial to a range of hi-tech industries.

Elsewhere, exports to countries taking part in the Belt and Road Initiative climbed 27.5 per cent year on year last month.

China’s exports to South Korea, a key technology manufacturing hub, surged 42.6 per cent year on year last month.
