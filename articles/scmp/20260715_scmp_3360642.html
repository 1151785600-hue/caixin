Malaysia’s Anwar deals with another split with BN in Melaka after Johor poll rout

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 819 words | 2026-07-15

Malaysia’s unity government is holding in Putrajaya, but its uneasy alliance is fraying in the states after Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s Pakatan Harapan (PH) moved into opposition in Melaka even as it is still reeling from a rout by its federal partner Barisan Nasional (BN) in Johor.

The split followed a constitutional amendment allowing Melaka’s government to appoint up to seven unelected assembly members with voting rights. PH’s five lawmakers opposed the bill, which passed 23-5 on Tuesday.

Four lawmakers from the Democratic Action Party (DAP), the predominantly ethnic Chinese and largest component in PH, resigned from their government posts in protest against the amendment and joined Amanah’s Adly Zahari on the opposition bench.

The walkout would not bring down Chief Minister Ab Rauf Yusoh as BN holds 21 of the 28 seats and can govern alone, according to analysts. But it showed how quickly Anwar’s alliance of former enemies was splintering across Malaysia’s states, they said.

“What has come apart is not a formal agreement, but the spirit of power-sharing created after the unity government was formed in Putrajaya,” said Awang Azman Awang Pawi, a political analyst at Universiti Malaya.

BN never needed PH’s numbers in Melaka, but it handed its coalition partner government posts in the state as a gesture after Malaysia’s inconclusive 2022 general election brought the former rivals together in Putrajaya, Awang said.

According to BN, nominated lawmakers would widen representation for professionals, women, young people and minorities. PH said allowing unelected representatives to vote breached a basic democratic principle.

Melaka DAP chairman Khoo Poay Tiong said: “Once they are appointed as assemblymen, they can sit in the state assembly and vote. That goes against the principles of our electoral system, where representatives should be elected by the people.”

One pact, two fronts

Anwar urged Melaka DAP on Tuesday to delay its exit until the state election, saying he had spoken to the party’s secretary general Anthony Loke and Ab Rauf. The state election is expected to be held later this year.

“I’ve already raised the matter and spoken to Loke, Melaka DAP and the chief minister, asking them to negotiate first,” Anwar told reporters on Tuesday. “Postpone it for the time being so that we can continue working with the state government until the election.”

Ab Rauf said PH was never formally incorporated into the Melaka administration, with its posts granted only to mirror the unity pact in Putrajaya.

“Their presence in this government is like a marriage without a kadi, a divorce without a talak,” he said, invoking Islamic terms for a marriage solemniser and repudiation, respectively.

Ab Rauf has said Melaka’s government term ends in December and the election can be held before then. He previously said he would weigh the Johor result and the August 1 Negeri Sembilan election before choosing a date.

The rupture comes with BN on the ascendancy after it won 48 of Johor’s 56 seats on Saturday. PH took the remaining eight, down from 12, while every other bloc was wiped out.

BN chairman Ahmad Zahid Hamidi, who is also Anwar’s deputy prime minister, said the result should unleash a “blue wave” across other states.

Shazwan Mustafa Kamal, a director at government affairs consultancy Vriens & Partners, said the landslide in Johor had “emboldened” the Malay nationalist United Malays National Organisation (Umno), BN’s dominant party.

“This weakens [Anwar’s] hand precisely when BN is riding high,” Shazwan said. “We can expect more pressure from Umno following Johor, as seen in Melaka.”

The split also sharpens questions over an early national election. Anwar said in May he would consider a snap poll if tensions within his governing alliance were to worsen. But in parliament on Tuesday, he brushed aside calls to dissolve the legislature.

“Give us the time under the mandate that we have until the election. Then we can fight it out,” Anwar said.

The next general election is not due until February 2028 at the latest.

Syaza Shukri, an associate professor of political science at the International Islamic University Malaysia, said the Melaka exit allowed PH, particularly DAP, to recover ground with supporters without threatening the state government.

“By going back to opposition, they’re signalling that they are no longer willing to compromise,” she said.

Working with BN had made PH appear to retreat from promises on reform, corruption and communal harmony, Syaza said.

“It was a marriage of convenience with little ideological convergence,” she said. “The experiment has been shown to have failed for both BN and PH.”

Awang Azman described the arrangement as “competitive coalition federalism”, with the two blocs cooperating nationally while pursuing separate mandates in the states.

“The real danger is not the immediate fall of the federal government, but the slow erosion of trust,” he said. “The BN-PH pact may remain formally intact, but become increasingly fragile politically and at the grass roots.”
