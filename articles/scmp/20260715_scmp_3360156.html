China’s developers eagerly line up to offer commercial-property Reits amid recovery signs

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 645 words | 2026-07-15

China’s first four exchange-traded real estate investment trusts (Reits) backed by commercial properties have opened the floodgates for fundraising by office developers, shopping centre builders and hotel owners amid investors’ heightened hopes for a market recovery.

According to data provider Wind Information, as of June 24 the pipelines of the Shanghai and Shenzhen stock exchanges had 19 listing applications for investment trusts backed by commercial property assets, with six having already secured regulatory approvals.

“Lucrative projects with potentially high returns will fuel the growth of Reits in China and eventually attract more investors,” said Ivy Lu, senior director of CBRE China Research. “Signs are encouraging that more developers will gain access to the financing platform.”

Reits allow asset owners to raise cash from valuable but ­illiquid assets without losing control of them. The investment vehicles also give investors the opportunity to collect regular dividends with income generated by the underlying properties.

The first four Reits backed by commercial properties were all heavily oversubscribed and gained in value on their first day of trading in Shanghai on June 18, after raising a combined 20.3 billion yuan (US$3 billion).

Pacific Securities predicted that they could deliver annual returns of 4.4 to 5.65 per cent, compared with one-year deposit rates of around 1 per cent offered by commercial lenders.

The six trusts in the second batch are in the bookbuilding process now, after receiving the green light in late June.

“In the long run, the country’s commercial real estate Reits are entering a new stage of normalised issuance, becoming an important fundraising tool for serving the real economy and building a new model for real estate development,” the brokerage said in a report.

“Some companies are actively preparing for the issuance of Reits. We expect more companies to resort to the real estate investment trusts to revitalise their property assets.”

Beijing began considering Reits in 2012 with the aim of diversifying risks for developers while enabling institutional and retail investors to profit from the property boom that was then under way.

But regulators took a go-slow approach, spending time educating would-be issuers and investors.

Recent brisk transactions of residential units in major cities like Shanghai triggered speculation that home prices were bottoming out following the six-year property slump in the world’s second-largest economy, according to brokers and analysts.

China’s property sector and related industries, such as home appliances and construction materials, account for about a quarter of mainland China’s economic output.

“Prices of commercial property assets like office buildings and shopping malls have plunged as much as 50 per cent [during the slump], and some institutions are now hunting bargains,” said Yin Ran, a property and angel investor in Shanghai. “More investors, including individuals, are willing to participate in Reits to chase long-term stable returns.”

CBRE data showed that 27.3 billion yuan worth of commercial properties in Shanghai were transacted between January and June, up 18 per cent from a year earlier.

Economists from international banks such as Citigroup have been calling on Beijing to roll out stronger incentives, including interest-rate reductions, tax cuts, further relaxation of home purchase restrictions and buy-backs of idle land, to buoy the slowing economy over the past year.

Falling home prices could deter mainland consumers – particularly the country’s 400 million middle-income residents – from freely spending on goods such as clothing and home appliances. Consumption has also long been a major growth driver for the Chinese economy.

China’s first nine exchange-traded Reits made their trading debuts in May 2021, receiving an enthusiastic response from investors.

The tranches offered to the general public were all oversubscribed on the first day of the offering, but at that time, only infrastructure companies were allowed to raise capital by resorting to the new financing source.

At present, about 90 Reits backed by infrastructure assets are traded on the Shanghai and Shenzhen stock exchanges.
