DAP’s election loss in Malaysia’s Johor prompts soul-searching ahead of next key poll

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1036 words | 2026-07-13

As Malaysian political coalition Barisan Nasional (BN) basks in the afterglow of its landslide win in the Johor state election, the soul-searching within Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s defeated Pakatan Harapan (PH) bloc could run deepest within its largest group – the Chinese-majority Democratic Action Party (DAP).

BN improved its grip on the country’s southernmost state, securing 48 out of 56 seats. PH managed to win only eight seats, and within the coalition, DAP lost 11 of the 17 seats it contested, including four previously held spots in Johor Jaya, Tangkak, Jementah and Perling. It had won 10 seats in the 2022 election.

The Johor election was the second state poll loss for DAP in the last eight months.

In November last year, DAP suffered a crushing defeat in the 17th Sabah state election, losing all eight seats it contested under the PH banner – the first time since 2004 that it had no representation in the Sabah State Legislative Assembly.

The losses have turned the Negeri Sembilan election on August 1 into a critical test of whether DAP’s setbacks in Sabah and Johor were an aberration or a sign of a deeper erosion in voter support, with the party’s national congress on August 16 likely to see demands for changes in strategy, messaging and grass-roots engagement.

“DAP is seen as being overly cautious and has not sufficiently explained to its supporters the compromises it has made within the unity government,” said Awang Azman Awang Pawi, a sociopolitical analyst from Malaysian-based Universiti Malaya.

He said DAP was facing the consequences of promised reforms moving slowly and persistent cost-of-living pressures, as the party appeared less vocal in defending its principles.

“Some supporters chose not to vote or cast protest ballots,” Awang added.

The coming election in Negeri Sembilan could see DAP hold on to its previous gains, with experts saying that it still possesses a strong base in the state run by a PH minority government.

“The greatest threat may not be the loss of many seats, but a decline in vote share and majorities because supporters do not turn out to vote,” Awang warned.

Azmi Hassan, a senior fellow at the Nusantara Academy for Strategic Research, said the negative perception of DAP had become apparent in Sabah and resurfaced in Johor, but the party had not adequately addressed it.

“With so little time before the Negeri Sembilan election, it will be difficult for DAP to change that perception, and the same trend may recur,” he said.

The minimum benchmark for DAP would be to retain all 11 seats in the 36-member state assembly it won in 2023, most of which were secured with healthy majorities of more than 2,000 votes, Azmi said.

“Retaining the 11 seats with larger majorities will be an uphill task, but DAP has to defend all of them to show that it has responded to what happened in Sabah and Johor,” he said.

For DAP Secretary General Anthony Loke, the party’s objective was to defend its seats in Negeri Sembilan, and he was confident it could still form a PH state government following the election.

“The main objective is to win a majority, and that is achievable. We just need to win at least a few more [seats] to form a stable government,” Loke told This Week in Asia last week.

“The election is still very much about the future of the state, bread-and-butter issues, development [and] progress.”

Analysts say if DAP loses just two or three more seats, pressure would mount on the party to reassess its strategy, candidates, voter outreach and position within the unity government.

“If it becomes extremely marginal or DAP loses four or more seats, the result could trigger a crisis of confidence in the leadership,” Awang added.

Kalimulah Hassan, a Malaysian political observer, said the blow for the DAP came as “promises made during the polls kept being broken” in Johor.

“The largest in Anwar’s coalition and for 60 years the loudest in criticising and preaching against corruption, power abuse and good governance – what did they do? Kept silent and revelled, enjoying the perks of power and fame,” he said in a social media post.

DAP’s purported Chinese-first strategies to gain voters’ support in Johor did not work out well, according to analysts.

Columnist Joceline Tan, in an opinion piece published by The Star, said PH had focused heavily on winning Chinese voters as it had largely accepted that Malay support was out of reach.

“DAP’s campaign was more realistic than that of PKR. It made no pretence of wooing the Chinese vote to survive,” Tan wrote, referring to the People’s Justice Party.

Multiple factors contributed to DAP’s losses, including its choice of candidates and party machinery, BN’s dominance, the Malaysian Chinese Association’s resurgence and multi-cornered contests, according to analysts.

“It would therefore be inaccurate to conclude that all the losses were solely caused by weaknesses in DAP’s central leadership,” Awang said.

DAP has long been branded as Chinese-first by Malay-led parties such as the United Malay National Organisation and the opposition party Bersatu.

Analysts say its national congress next month would be a platform for demands for a firmer strategy.

The convention could steer DAP in a different direction as it looked to erase negative perceptions of DAP among the Chinese voters, Azmi said.

“From Sabah to Johor, nothing has been done [to improve the perception], so during the convention, I believe something will be proposed to counter the negativity,” he said.

Ahmad Mohsein Azman, a senior analyst at BowerGroupAsia, pointed to DAP’s weaknesses in urban constituencies, saying that voters there were more aware of current criticisms of DAP and PH, a factor that had dented the coalition’s support in Johor.

While DAP’s losses in Sabah alone were not an accurate gauge of public sentiment towards the party, the losses in Johor should serve as a warning that its current campaign approach might be “too removed from reality”.

“By staying largely silent on such issues, the party has become an antithesis of its own manifestos,” Ahmad said, adding that DAP’s traditional Chinese and urban seats could no longer be treated as safe havens.
