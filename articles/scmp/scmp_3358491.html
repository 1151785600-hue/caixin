Hong Kong’s 5-year plan will hinge on how it measures success

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 917 words | 2026-07-08

Hong Kong’s first five-year plan will undoubtedly mark a significant milestone for the city. The public consultation launched last month covers a range of issues, such as economic development, innovation and technology, housing, healthcare, education, infrastructure and regional cooperation.

The exercise offers a chance not only to discuss what Hong Kong hopes to achieve by 2030, but also how its success should be measured. That second question is perhaps the more important one.

Hong Kong has never lacked ambition. Over the years, the city has set out clear aspirations for strengthening its position as an international financial centre, developing into an innovation and technology centre, attracting global talent and improving the quality of life for residents. The challenge has rarely been defining the destination. It has been more about measuring progress along the journey.

No successful company would invest billions of dollars without establishing objectives, setting milestones and regularly reviewing performance. Cities should be no different.

If Hong Kong’s first five-year plan is to make a lasting contribution, it should focus on a small number of priorities and establish clear indicators with which progress can be assessed. Among the many important issues facing the city, three stand out.

The first is the Northern Metropolis. Every successful city eventually creates a new engine of growth. Shanghai had Pudong. Shenzhen transformed vast areas of land into innovation and technology hubs that now attract talent and investment from around the world. Each project expanded the city’s economic capacity and reshaped perceptions of its future. For Hong Kong, the Northern Metropolis has the potential to play a similar role.

The project is Hong Kong’s chance to reshape its economic geography, create new industries, deepen Greater Bay Area collaboration and provide space for future growth.

The question is not whether the Northern Metropolis is important; it is whether progress can be measured transparently. By 2030, Hong Kong should be able to point to measurable outcomes such as land released for development, transport links completed, businesses established, jobs created and residents housed. The Northern Metropolis’ success should not be assessed by the scale of its vision, but by the extent to which that vision materialises.

The second priority is innovation-led growth. Hong Kong’s strengths are clear: finance, professional services, logistics and international connectivity. Yet, future competitiveness will increasingly depend on the city’s ability to capture a stake in emerging industries.

Artificial intelligence (AI), life sciences, advanced manufacturing, green technology and digital innovation are reshaping economies around the world. Hong Kong does not need to compete on scale, but rather leverage its strengths in finance, research, international connectivity and role within the Greater Bay Area development zone. The challenge is turning those advantages into globally competitive industries.

The five-year plan presents an opportunity to establish practical goals. Success will not be measured by the number of policy papers or initiatives. Real success will be based on attracting talent and investment, creating high-value jobs, commercialising research, establishing companies and successfully bringing technologies to market.

The third priority is housing and liveability. For many residents, no issue is more immediate. Housing affects everything from family and retirement planning to social mobility and overall quality of life.

Efforts to increase housing supply have begun to show progress, with the composite waiting time for subsidised rental housing falling to 4.7 years, the lowest in more than eight years. However, the long-term objective should be broader than simply building more units. Hong Kong should aspire to become a city where people can reasonably expect access to affordable housing, efficient transport networks, quality public services and attractive community spaces.

This is where measurable outcomes matter most. Residents should be able to see whether promised supply is being delivered, whether major projects remain on schedule and whether living conditions are improving over time through shorter waiting times, increased housing completions and improvements in connectivity and community amenities.

These three priorities are closely connected. A successful Northern Metropolis can support housing supply and create economic opportunities. A stronger innovation ecosystem can generate higher-value jobs and attract global talent. Improved liveability can strengthen Hong Kong’s ability to retain residents and compete internationally.

The challenge is not unique to Hong Kong. Around the world, cities that have transformed their economies and infrastructure have combined long-term vision with consistent execution. Singapore, for example, is recognised as one of the world’s most competitive and business-friendly economies. Many of its major national projects were guided by plans that extended beyond annual budgets and political cycles.

Hong Kong need not simply replicate Singapore’s model. Every city has its own circumstances and strengths. Rather, the common thread is setting clear priorities, measuring progress and maintaining focus on delivery. Strategic planning is most effective when it is matched by consistent execution and accountability.

Regular public updates can also help maintain momentum and allow for necessary adjustments when circumstances change. The most effective plans are not static documents but living frameworks that evolve while remaining focused on clearly defined outcomes.

The value of Hong Kong’s first five-year plan will not be determined by the number of initiatives or the ambition of its aspirations, but by whether residents can see tangible improvements in housing, economic opportunity and quality of life.

Hong Kong has the right vision. The challenge is to translate that vision into measurable results and sustained progress. The real test begins not when the plan is published, but when its promises become reality.
