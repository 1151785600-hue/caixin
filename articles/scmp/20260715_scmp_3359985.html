Xiaomi preps for fierce EV race with new SUV line, long-range battery tech

SCMP | FREE | 499 words | 2026-07-15

Xiaomi, the smartphone vendor that has become one of Tesla’s biggest challengers in mainland China after entering the electric vehicle (EV) market, has doubled down on research and development to combat a slowdown in domestic sales.

The Beijing-based tech giant announced on Thursday it would assemble and sell models under a new sport-utility vehicle (SUV) line known as Sky Nomad, a product range analysts said would be likely to include extended-range battery technology to lure consumers away from domestic rivals such as Li Auto and Xpeng.

“Sky Nomad will be synonymous with smart, adaptable, large-space SUVs,” Xiaomi EV said in a statement posted on social media. “This represents Xiaomi’s efforts in car manufacturing over the past five years, catering to diverse user needs.”

The company, whose SU7 sedan became China's first “Tesla killer” after outselling the US firm’s Model 3 in 2025, did not say which battery technology it would use to build Sky Nomad-branded vehicles.

But analysts and dealers said they expected the first batch of production models to feature extended-range technology, including small internal combustion engines that generate additional power to charge the ­battery when needed.

In June, China’s Ministry of Industry and Information Technology published a new catalogue of EV models, which included extended-range battery vehicles built by Xiaomi. Models on the list are allowed to begin production, and are partially exempted from vehicle purchase taxes until the end of the year.

Extended-range EVs are classified as cars that can travel more than 1,000 kilometres (621 miles) on a single charge.

“Xiaomi, whose smartphones and EVs have attracted a large base of loyal fans, has already become a strong contender in the automotive market,” said Phate Zhang, founder of Shanghai-based industry data provider CnEVPost. “An expanded product line will help consolidate its leading position as competition gets fiercer.”

Stephen Dyer, head of the Asia automotive and industrial consulting practice at AlixPartners, said last week that the gap between the industry’s leaders and the rest of the field would widen in the second half of 2026, as top brands attracted more new car buyers.

Sales of Xiaomi’s fully electric SU7 – its first production model that hit the mainland market in early 2024 – hit 258,164 last year, nearly 30 per cent higher than the Model 3’s 200,361 deliveries, according to data from the China Passenger Car Association.

At present, Xiaomi builds two pure electric models, the SU7 sedan and the YU7 SUV.

It handed a total 185,055 units to customers in the first half of 2026, up 17.2 per cent year on year and falling short of the company’s full-year delivery growth target of 34 per cent. Xiaomi said in January that it aimed to sell 550,000 vehicles this year, compared to 410,000 deliveries in 2025.

According to an HSBC report released in June, a record 156 new models would hit the mainland’s vehicle market in the second half of 2026, intensifying concerns about the sector’s profitability as intense competition could trigger a new price war.
