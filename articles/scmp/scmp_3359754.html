The missile funding dispute endangering Taiwan’s ‘T-Dome’ air defence plan

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 760 words | 2026-07-08

Taiwan’s flagship “T-Dome” air defence programme is facing delays, after budget disputes left one of its critical indigenous missile systems without a clear funding source.

The island’s opposition-controlled legislature approved a reduced NT$780 billion (over US$24 billion) special defence budget in May, rejecting a larger NT$1.25 trillion package proposed by the government.

Among the excluded projects was funding for the indigenous Chiang-Kong, or “Strong Bow”, anti-ballistic missile system, a vital component of Taiwanese leader William Lai Ching-te’s proposed T-Dome missile shield to counter aerial threats from Beijing.

After the programme was dropped, the military sought to revive it either through a supplementary budget this year or by incorporating it into the annual defence budget next year.

But differences between the cabinet and the defence ministry had prevented agreement on an alternative funding mechanism, Taiwan’s Liberty Times quoted unnamed senior government officials as saying on Monday.

As a result, the Strong Bow system could not be included in the cabinet’s recently proposed special bill on uncrewed systems, while the chances of securing supplementary funding this year had also diminished, the officials said.

They added that finding room for the programme in next year’s defence budget was becoming increasingly difficult because of competing procurement priorities, raising the possibility that funding could be postponed to 2028, thereby delaying a core element of Lai’s T-Dome initiative.

The integrated air defence programme combines US arms purchases, commercial acquisitions and indigenous weapons development. It includes Patriot PAC-3 MSE (Missile Segment Enhancement) missiles, additional National Advanced Surface-to-Air Missile Systems (Nasams), the Strong Bow missile system and the Sharp Kite II uncrewed aerial vehicle.

Military analysts said the systems were designed to function as an integrated layered network rather than standalone capabilities, warning that delays to individual programmes would create capability gaps and disrupt deployment schedules.

The funding uncertainty comes amid three days of live-fire air defence exercises at the Jiupeng test range in southern Pingtung county from Monday, including tests of the US-made Avenger air defence vehicle, twin-mounted Stinger launchers and shoulder-fired Stinger missiles.

The drills are the first live-fire launch of Taiwan’s newly introduced man-portable Stinger missile system, according to the defence ministry.

Troops practised target acquisition, identification friend-or-foe procedures and missile engagements against aerial targets, while Avenger and twin-mounted Stinger units intercepted high-speed target drones in simulated combat scenarios.

The exercise also featured the first nighttime live-firing of the vehicle-mounted Avenger system, with crews relying on infrared sensors and target acquisition equipment to engage targets in darkness as the military expanded its all-weather air defence capabilities, ministry officials said.

On Tuesday, the military incorporated several new uncrewed aerial systems into simulated anti-landing operations, underscoring Taiwan’s growing emphasis on drones as part of its asymmetric defence strategy.

A new military-grade commercial surveillance drone made its first appearance in a field exercise, providing long-range reconnaissance and target designation for concealed anti-ship missile units, ministry officials said.

Working alongside smaller surveillance drones, it relayed targeting data through jam-resistant communications links to mobile anti-ship missile launchers, which then carried out simulated strikes against an approaching amphibious assault force, they said.

According to defence ministry budget documents, the drone’s main mission is to provide targeting intelligence for mobile coastal missile batteries, enabling Taiwanese forces to detect and engage invasion forces farther from the island’s coastline.

The exercises reflected the operational concept underpinning T-Dome, which aims to integrate air defence systems, drones, sensors and artificial intelligence-assisted command networks into a layered architecture capable of countering aircraft, ballistic missiles, cruise missiles and other aerial threats.

“T-Dome would integrate indigenous missile systems with US-made Patriot and Nasams systems to build a layered air defence network covering low, medium and high altitudes,” said Su Tsu-yun, a senior analyst at the government-funded Institute for National Defence and Security Research,

“As long as this defensive network remains intact, Beijing would be unable to achieve its strategic objective of rapidly seizing Taiwan and presenting the world with a fait accompli, thereby helping preserve long-term peace and stability across the Taiwan Strait.”

Beijing sees Taiwan as part of China to be reunited by force if necessary. Most countries, including the United States, do not recognise Taiwan as an independent state, but Washington is opposed to any attempt to take the self-ruled island by force and is committed to supplying it with weapons.
