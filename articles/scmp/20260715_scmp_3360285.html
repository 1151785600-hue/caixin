Meet CXMT’s Zhu Yiming: the engineer building China’s answer to global memory-chip giants

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1042 words | 2026-07-15

When ChangXin Memory Technologies (CXMT) – China’s attempt to build a home-grown rival to the world’s top makers of memory chips – kicked off one of the country’s most anticipated initial public offerings (IPOs) of 2026 last week, the spotlight fell on its founder Zhu Yiming.

The Shanghai offering, set to start taking subscriptions on Thursday, aims to raise 29.5 billion yuan (US$4.4 billion). And some analysts predict that CXMT could eventually command a market value of 3 trillion yuan, placing it among China’s most valuable listed technology companies.

The listing will be a milestone for Zhu, who reportedly pledged in 2018 not to draw a salary from CXMT until the memory-chip project became profitable – which it did last year.

Yet Zhu’s wager on Chinese memory chips began long before CXMT. More than two decades ago, when he was a Silicon Valley engineer with a chip design but little money, Zhu predicted that the centre of the global memory industry would eventually shift towards mainland China.

In emails retained by Li Jun, one of his earliest investors, and later reproduced in a Tsinghua University alumni publication, Zhu observed that the industry had migrated from the US to Japan, and then to South Korea.

“It is time for China to play [a] role in this industry,” he wrote.

His long-term ambition was even bolder: “to be the biggest memory designer and maker in China”.

Zhu’s interest in technology had already paid off. While studying physics at Tsinghua, he took programming jobs rather than the tutoring work common among students, making 300,000 yuan a year by the late 1990s – when, according to Tsinghua’s alumni magazine, the average annual income was about 10,000 yuan. As the software he wrote moved closer to the underlying hardware, he became fascinated by the integrated circuits that China largely relied on foreign companies to supply.

He later studied electronic engineering at Stony Brook University in New York and worked for technology companies in the United States, including memory-chip developer Monolithic System Technologies.

By the summer of 2004, Zhu had decided to start his own company.

At a Starbucks in Silicon Valley, he made a second pitch to Li, a fellow Tsinghua alumnus and technology investor. Their first meeting had produced little interest. This time, Zhu had quit his job, registered GigaDevice Semiconductor and prepared a design for a memory chip that promised lower costs and power consumption.

The pitch persuaded Li to introduce Zhu to another investor, who provided US$100,000 and rented his garage to the fledgling company for cheap. Zhu and fellow engineer Shu Qingming turned their design into a working sample there and applied for a patent.

While the garage origins followed a familiar Silicon Valley script, what came next was more characteristically Zhu: move quickly, even when the money was not quite enough.

In 2005, a group of investors, many of them Tsinghua alumni, assembled US$920,000 for GigaDevice’s first major fundraising round. The target had been US$1 million, but Zhu decided it was close enough and returned to Beijing to start the company.

The firm’s first technology struggled to win over large electronics makers, which were reluctant to rely on an unknown Chinese supplier. GigaDevice changed course and secured its first domestic order from chip designer Rockchip. The contract, worth only 100,000 yuan, put its memory technology into chips for MP3 players.

GigaDevice eventually grew into one of China’s leading chip-design companies and listed in Shanghai in August 2016.

For many founders, that would have been the capstone of a successful entrepreneurial career. For Zhu, it coincided with the start of a more ambitious second act.

That year, he joined forces with the Hefei government to launch the project that became CXMT. While GigaDevice could design chips and outsource their production, CXMT would have to build multibillion-dollar factories, master manufacturing processes and compete with companies that had decades of expertise.

In 2018, Zhu stepped back from GigaDevice to focus on CXMT – and made his no-salary pledge.

The most important thing in entrepreneurship is to stay alive

Zhu Yiming

CXMT began producing its first self-designed mainstream memory chip in 2019, giving mainland China a foothold in a market that was almost entirely supplied by foreign manufacturers.

One of Zhu’s important early moves was to secure licences to patents originally developed by Qimonda, a failed German memory maker spun out of Infineon Technologies. This gave CXMT an established intellectual-property base and reduced the risk of disputes with overseas rivals.

CXMT nevertheless remained loss-making for years as it built factories, recruited engineers and strived to improve its production yield.

Its fortunes changed dramatically as artificial intelligence data centres drove demand for memory to record levels. The boom also attracted Apple, which has reportedly tested CXMT chips for use in devices sold in China.

The IPO has also cast an earlier investment by Alibaba Group Holding in a new light. Its Hong Kong-listed shares jumped as much as 17.1 per cent last week amid a broader rally in Chinese AI stocks, just as its exposure to CXMT drew renewed attention.

Subsidiaries of Alibaba own just under 5 per cent of the chipmaker after investing about 7.6 billion yuan since 2021, making Alibaba its largest industry investor. Alibaba is the owner of the South China Morning Post.

Despite CXMT’s success, Zhu has not yet won his original wager. South Korea’s Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix, along with US firm Micron Technology, still control more than 90 per cent of the global DRAM market and remain ahead in scale and advanced products. CXMT must continue spending heavily on manufacturing technology while navigating cyclical prices and tightening restrictions on China’s access to semiconductor equipment.

The engineer who once handled technology, fundraising and early customer development now chairs a manufacturer that employed nearly 19,300 people at the end of 2025.

In an interview published by Tsinghua’s alumni magazine after GigaDevice’s listing, Zhu attributed the company’s success to choosing the right market and the hard work of his employees. He also expressed a dogged determinism that is likely to serve him well as he continues working on his prediction about the industry’s migration to China.

“The most important thing in entrepreneurship is to stay alive,” he said.
