Everything you never wanted to know about hosting the World Cup

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 739 words | 2026-07-16

Every four years, billions of football fans spend a month arguing about offside decisions, whether Ronaldo or Messi is a grass-grazing horned mammal, and whether football is finally “coming home”.

For nations, however, the Fifa World Cup is far more than football. Beyond the national pride that comes with simply qualifying, the tournament offers an unparalleled opportunity to capture the world’s attention.

Cabo Verde’s fairy-tale run to the round of 32 transformed the little-known island nation into a global household name – an exposure economists forecast could generate billions of US dollars in long-term tourism revenue.

It is hardly surprising, then, that the race to host the Fifa World Cup is among the most fiercely contested bidding processes in international sport.

The rewards are dazzling: the 2018 Fifa World Cup in Russia cost over US$14 billion, while the 2022 tournament attracted more than 1 million international visitors to Qatar.

Beyond tourism and jobs, hosting offers global prestige irresistible to governments – for one glorious month, the world’s cameras focus not on inflation, elections or traffic jams, but on gleaming stadiums, vibrant cities and jubilant fans.

Behind the glamour, however, lies an avalanche of legal commitments for lawyers to navigate.

Hosting requires navigating through Fifa regulations, commercial contracts, domestic public law, immigration, policing, intellectual property, broadcasting rights, merchandising, taxation, insurance, human rights and climate commitments – all before anyone has even argued about VAR.

Take Fifa’s commercial regulations. Official sponsors pay billions for exclusivity, meaning host nations must establish “clean zones” around stadiums where unauthorised advertising and street trading are prohibited.

A local cafe displaying a home-made “World Cup breakfast special” may discover that enthusiasm, while admirable, is no defence in court. Counterfeit jerseys, ambush marketing and pirate broadcasts become the legal equivalent of pitch invaders – they must be removed before they spoil the spectacle.

On the other hand, stadium construction invites antitrust disputes, construction claims, and labour law issues. Millions of tickets create a hotbed for fraud and privacy breaches.

Security planning demands unprecedented coordination between police and emergency services.

The legal drama rarely ends with the final whistle. Brazil 2014 became entangled in the infamous Odebrecht scandal, in which construction contracts were secured through bribery and bid-rigging. Thousands of residents challenged forced relocations to make way for infrastructure.

South Africa 2010 saw its national Competition Tribunal finding cases of collusive tendering among major construction companies responsible for World Cup projects.

No tournament, however, has generated more legal scrutiny than Qatar 2022.

International attention centred on migrant workers who built the stadiums and infrastructure, with allegations of unsafe working conditions, excessive hours, unpaid wages, restrictions on worker mobility, limitations on freedom of expression and concerns surrounding LGBTQ+ rights.

Qatar introduced significant labour reforms in response, though debate over their effectiveness continues. Whatever one’s view, Qatar fundamentally changed the conversation: future hosts are now judged not only by the quality of their football stadiums, but also by the legal and ethical standards underpinning the tournament.

This brings us to the 2026 tournament, jointly hosted by the United States, Canada, and Mexico.

Organising one World Cup is difficult enough; organising one across three sovereign nations requires a quantity of Zoom calls previously thought impossible.

Lawyers must reconcile three immigration systems, three tax regimes, three labour law frameworks and multiple approaches to privacy, security, and border control – all while ensuring millions of fans can cross borders more smoothly than midfielders complete a five-yard pass.

Within days of the tournament, authorities were already investigating allegations of widespread ticket fraud on secondary sales platforms. As it turned out, the final whistle merely signalled the kick-off for the lawyers.

To supporters, a successful World Cup is remembered for spectacular goals, dramatic upsets and unforgettable celebrations.

To lawyers, success is rather less glamorous – no constitutional crises, no procurement scandals, no sponsor litigation and no international headlines beginning with the words “breaking news”.

The beautiful game may last only 90 minutes, but the legal planning begins years before the opening whistle and continues long after the confetti has been swept away.

Behind every unforgettable World Cup lies a simple truth: while the players chase the trophy, it is often the law that keeps the tournament on the pitch.

Victor Dawes SC is a former chairman of the Hong Kong Bar Association and a commercial litigator.
