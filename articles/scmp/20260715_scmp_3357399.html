Why the AI future won’t be decided by algorithms and chatbots

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 910 words | 2026-07-15

When people talk about the race for artificial intelligence, they usually focus on software. Headlines revolve around ChatGPT, Gemini, DeepSeek or the latest breakthrough model.

Governments announce AI strategies and investors pour billions into start-ups promising to transform everything from medicine to education. Nonetheless, the most consequential battle in the AI age may not be over algorithms at all. It may be over the machines.

Behind every chatbot response and AI-generated image lies a vast physical infrastructure of semiconductor factories, data centres, cloud networks, transmission lines and power plants. These are the factories of the 21st century. Increasingly, the divide may be between countries that own the infrastructure behind AI and those that only access the applications built on top of it.

History offers a familiar lesson. During the Industrial Revolution, economic power flowed to countries that controlled manufacturing capacity. In the 20th century, geopolitical influence often rested on oil, pipelines and energy markets. Today, computing power is emerging as the new strategic asset.

The United States currently hosts more than 4,200 data centres, accounting for nearly half of global capacity. Germany, Europe’s second-largest hub, has slightly over 500. China has fewer than 500, while India, despite being the world’s most populous country, has nearly 300. This gap matters because AI runs on infrastructure. Training and deploying advanced AI models require enormous processing power. Countries with stronger computing ecosystems are better positioned to attract investment, host digital industries and capture more value from the AI economy.

The US’ advantage extends beyond numbers. Microsoft, Amazon, Google and Meta operate some of the world’s largest cloud networks and collectively spend tens of billions of dollars each year expanding AI infrastructure. In the last financial year, Microsoft said it was investing around US$80 billion in AI data centres.

American dominance, however, tells only part of the story. Taiwan, with a population of just 23 million, has become indispensable to the global technology ecosystem. The island produces more than 90 per cent of the world’s most advanced semiconductors, while Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) dominates the global foundry market. Without Taiwan’s chips, there is no modern AI industry.

This concentration also creates vulnerabilities. Any disruption in the Taiwan Strait would ripple across global supply chains, affecting cloud providers in the US, automobile manufacturers in Japan, industrial firms in Europe and technology companies across the developing world. Semiconductors have become as much a geopolitical issue as an economic one.

At its core, the competition is more than just AI leadership. It is about computational sovereignty.

Beijing illustrates this shift clearly. Despite lagging behind the US in advanced semiconductors and cloud infrastructure, China has invested heavily in expanding domestic computing capacity. Through initiatives such as “Eastern Data, Western Computing”, it is moving computing workloads to inland regions where land and energy are cheaper.

Beijing’s goal is now fairly clear: China does not want its AI future to depend on foreign chips, foreign cloud providers or foreign technological standards. And the implications extend far beyond Washington and Beijing. Across Asia, governments increasingly view digital infrastructure the same way earlier generations viewed ports, highways and industrial estates.

India is investing in AI infrastructure and semiconductor capabilities. Japan is strengthening sovereign cloud systems and supply-chain resilience. South Korea continues to expand support for advanced chip production. Singapore has emerged as one of Asia’s leading digital hubs despite severe constraints on land and energy.

At the same time, the infrastructure behind AI is not weightless. It comes with a heavy demand for power, water, land and capital. AI requires enormous amounts of electricity. According to the International Energy Agency, global electricity demand from data centres could more than double by 2030. A single large AI-focused facility can consume as much electricity as a medium-sized city.

Water is becoming another strategic constraint. Semiconductor manufacturing depends on massive quantities of ultra-pure water. Taiwan’s recurring droughts have highlighted how environmental pressures can threaten even the most advanced industries.

As AI expands, the concern is whether countries can sustain the infrastructure those machines require. This challenge is particularly important for the developing world. Low- and middle-income countries are embracing AI applications in education, healthcare, agriculture and public services.

Yet most possess little of the underlying infrastructure. Africa accounts for less than 2 per cent of global data centre capacity. South Asia is home to nearly one-quarter of humanity but only a small share of global computing resources.

Consequently, many countries risk becoming consumers rather than producers in the AI economy. The first digital divide was about internet access. The next will be about infrastructure ownership: who controls the chips, cloud networks and computing platforms where profits, talent and rule-making power increasingly concentrate.

This does not mean every country must build its own semiconductor industry. Such ambitions are prohibitively expensive. But governments do need to think more strategically about digital sovereignty. Regional cloud infrastructure, diversified technology partnerships, domestic research ecosystems and resilient energy systems are becoming essential investments.

AI is often described as a software revolution. That description is incomplete; deeper transformation is infrastructural. The countries that structure the next century may not necessarily be the ones that build the smartest algorithms. They may be the ones that own the factories, chips, power grids, data centres and networks that make those algorithms possible. In the end, the defining question of the AI age may not be who created the intelligence, but who owned the machines.
