Why Chinese wealth is still powering Hong Kong and Singapore property

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 889 words | 2026-07-13

Hong Kong’s residential real estate market is roaring back to life. Prices for second-hand properties have risen 18 per cent since March last year, while average rents continue to hit new highs. The number of units sold in the primary market in the first half of this year reached a 22-year high.

According to Knight Frank, which tracks sales of “super-prime” properties above US$10 million in 12 leading residential markets, Hong Kong was the second-most widely traded market in the first quarter of this year as “global private capital remain[ed] highly active where liquidity, lifestyle and long-term confidence align”.

Yet confidence was shaken by Beijing’s measures to stem capital outflows from the mainland, which reached a record high of US$807 billion last year, data from the Institute of International Finance shows. The crackdown, which included penalties imposed on three prominent brokerages often used by ultra-high-net-worth Chinese to invest offshore, has put the surge in mainland capital inflows into Hong Kong under scrutiny.

The city’s residential property market is particularly exposed given the critical role of mainland buyers as a source of demand, especially for high-end properties. According to Midland Realty, in the two years following the removal of cooling measures in February 2024, mainland buyers accounted for 72 per cent of transactions in the primary market above HK$50 million (US$6.4 million) and 55 per cent of sales in the HK$10-20 million bracket.

In a report on July 3, Citigroup noted that transactions had slowed in recent weeks. However, in an earlier report on June 2, the bank put its finger on a key source of resilience of mainland demand: the permanent resident status of many buyers. Mainland buyers without a Hong Kong identity card accounted for just 5.5 per cent of primary and secondary transaction volumes in the financial year ending March 2025.

A gauge of mainland demand based on the use of Chinese pinyin names in Land Registry data for 2025 as a whole put the share at 26 per cent, rising to 41 per cent in the primary market. This cohort of mainland buyers includes those who obtained permanent residency and “are less impacted [by the restrictions] as they earn HK$ salaries and have [their] own offshore bank and securities accounts”, Citigroup said.

“Most mainland buyers’ funding has been sourced in Hong Kong for many years. It’s not demand that’s the problem, it’s the lack of supply in sought-after locations,” said Alan Li, executive director at Habitat Property.

CBRE pointed to the scarcity of luxury supply in prime areas in The Peak and Island South. “This ‘limited edition’ attribute makes existing luxury housing assets increasingly valuable, [enhancing their appeal] as safe haven assets,” it said.

In fact, Citigroup said one of the likely consequences of Beijing’s clampdown on cross-border capital flows was that it would encourage more mainland Chinese to apply for permanent residency in Hong Kong.

A similar trend is playing out in Singapore. In April 2023, the government imposed one of the most punitive taxes on property purchases by doubling the additional stamp duty rate for foreigners buying any private property to a staggering 60 per cent. This was aimed at non-resident buyers, especially mainland Chinese buyers, in the core central region, the area most favoured by overseas investors.

Yet the tax increase, part of a package of cooling measures designed to “pre-emptively manage investment demand”, ended up accentuating the importance of domestic demand in Singapore’s private housing market.

An analysis of data on mainland Chinese buying activity by property portal Mogul.sg shows those with the right of permanent residence accounted for nearly 72 per cent of private property sales in 2019. Fast forward to the first half of this year, this group constituted 97 per cent of purchases. Moreover, the total number of units bought by Chinese individual buyers – the largest group of foreign buyers – was only marginally below the 558 acquired in the first half of 2019.

In fact, the proportion of properties in the core central region bought by Chinese buyers who are permanent residents rose to almost 20 per cent, up from 13 per cent in the first half of 2019.

Residency-based differentiation in property taxes – the extra stamp duty rate for permanent residents buying their first property is only 5 per cent, rising to 30 per cent for second homes – benefits both the government and mainland Chinese buyers. “The government wants them to eventually become Singaporean citizens but it doesn’t want the property market to overheat,” said Nicholas Mak, chief research officer at Mgogul.sg.

Singaporean property editorial platform Stacked Homes noted that “Singaporeans and [permanent residents] are no longer just visitors or investors in [the core central region] – they’re moving in, raising families, and reshaping what it means to live in the city centre”.

Even in Australia, which has taken a tougher approach to foreign purchases of residential real estate because of the severity of the country’s housing crisis, Pauline Hanson’s far-right One Nation party – which is leading in some opinion polls – was forced to clarify that its plan to compel foreign owners to sell their properties within two years excluded permanent residents.

Policy risks in Asian housing markets are intensifying. But in Hong Kong and Singapore, many mainland Chinese buyers are a key source of domestic demand, underscoring the resilience of Chinese purchasing activity.
