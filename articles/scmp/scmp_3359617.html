China’s asteroid hunter closes in on target after 400-day trip, though size is a surprise

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 810 words | 2026-07-08

China’s Tianwen-2 spacecraft has captured its first close-up image of a near-Earth asteroid, revealing that the target is even smaller than anticipated – a factor scientists say will make the sample-return task “far more difficult” than previous Japanese and American missions.

The probe captured images about 20km (12.4 miles) from asteroid 2016 HO3 during its approach phase, the China National Space Administration (CNSA) announced on Monday.

It was close enough for the spacecraft to begin scientific exploration of the asteroid, the agency said on its website.

Formally designated 2016 HO3, or asteroid 469219 Kamo‘oalewa, the target is a very small Apollo-type near-Earth asteroid previously estimated to measure about 40 to 100 metres (130 to 330 feet) in diameter. However, the image captured by Tianwen-2 and its accompanying scale bar suggests that the asteroid is likely to be less than 40 metres in diameter.

This makes it far smaller than Ryugu and Bennu – the targets previously explored by Japan and the United States – which are about 900 metres and 500 metres across, respectively.

“Based on the image released so far, this asteroid appears to be somewhat smaller than previously predicted – it seems to be only about 20 to 30 metres across, whereas the earlier estimate from our paper was around 57 metres,” said Zhang Pengfei, a researcher from the Institute of Geochemistry at the Chinese Academy of Sciences.

Compounding the challenge, the asteroid is an elongated body that spins rapidly, completing one full rotation every 28 minutes.

According to an article published in China National Astronomy magazine on Monday, 2016 HO3 is an irregular rubble-pile object spanning about 30 to 40 metres in diameter. Its gravitational field is extremely uneven and its surface terrain highly complex, leaving few flat areas suitable for stable sampling.

Furthermore, its rapid spin, combined with its loosely bound structure, means the spacecraft could easily bounce off the surface or lose the sample during contact operations.

“This greatly increases the complexity of the sampling process and the risk of the mission, making it much more difficult than targeting the slow-rotating asteroids Ryugu and Bennu,” the article said.

The CNSA said the mission team had used optical navigation data gathered during the close approach to refine the asteroid’s predicted orbital path, or ephemeris.

This helped reduce its positional uncertainty – previously determined solely through ground-based observations – from hundreds of kilometres down to a single-kilometre scale.

It took Tianwen-2 nearly 400 days and about 1 billion kilometres through deep space to reach the target.

Launched in May last year as part of China’s broader efforts to explore the solar system and advance planetary science, Tianwen-2 is the country’s first mission tasked with returning an asteroid sample.

The probe, which lifted off aboard a Long March 3B rocket from the Xichang Satellite Launch Centre in southwestern China, has two core objectives: collecting samples from 2016 HO3 and then performing a fly-along study of the main-belt comet 311P.

During its voyage, the probe executed a series of deep-space manoeuvres and trajectory correction operations, the CNSA said.

On June 6, the spacecraft achieved its first optical detection of the asteroid, and the following day, at a range of 30,000km, it entered a coplanar trajectory with the target. By June 19, it had closed the distance to within 2,000km.

“Next, the probe will progressively conduct more detailed scientific exploration to acquire data on the asteroid’s morphology, material composition and internal structure, laying the groundwork for subsequent sample collection operations,” the agency said.

First spotted in April 2016 by the Pan-STARRS 1 asteroid survey telescope on Haleakala, Hawaii, 2016 HO3 is a small quasi-satellite of Earth that scientists say may be a fragment of the moon.

Tianwen-2 is expected to collect samples from its fast-spinning surface and return them to Earth late next year via a re-entry capsule.

Unlike the brief touch-and-go sampling method used by Japanese and American asteroid probes, Tianwen-2 is also equipped with hovering and anchoring sampling modes to better adapt to the uncertainties of 2016 HO3’s terrain.

“Tianwen-2’s mission is far more complex than previous deep-space explorations, and represents a completely new path for humanity’s exploration of the universe,” the China National Astronomy article said.

After delivering the capsule, the main spacecraft will use Earth’s gravity for a catapult manoeuvre to begin a seven-year journey to comet 311P/PanSTARRS – an object in the solar system’s main asteroid belt known for shedding dust in multiple tails.

Tianwen-2 is the second mission in China’s Tianwen, or “Questions to Heaven”, series – the CNSA’s deep-space and planetary exploration programme focusing on the solar system.

The first, Tianwen-1, placed an orbiter around Mars and landed a rover on its surface in 2021.

Tianwen-3, aims to retrieve the first Martian rock samples by 2031, while Tianwen-4 is scheduled to launch around 2030 to explore the Jupiter system and perform a fly-by of Uranus.
