Dollar risks rising but Plaza Accord-style coordination not feasible now: ex-IMF adviser

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 586 words | 2026-07-14

With central banks prioritising domestic objectives, Plaza Accord-style exchange-rate coordination is now out of reach despite growing concerns over the dollar-centred monetary system, complicating the smooth transition to a more multipolar international monetary system, according to a prominent economic historian.

Barry Eichengreen, a former senior policy adviser at the International Monetary Fund, said that if erratic US policies and weakening institutional guard rails were to persist for another decade, the probability of an abrupt crisis of confidence in the US dollar would exceed 50 per cent.

“I don’t think something like the Plaza Accord of 1985 … is possible in the 21st century,” Eichengreen said, speaking during an online economic lecture on Friday. The accord, widely regarded as a landmark in international monetary coordination, brought major economies together to weaken the dollar through coordinated exchange-rate action.

Eichengreen, now a professor of economics and political science at the University of California, Berkeley, said he favoured a more multipolar international monetary system that “better matches the multipolar nature” of the current global economy. Still, he argued that such a transition would be far more difficult to coordinate than in the past. Eichengreen’s 1996 book, Globalizing Capital, is widely considered the definitive history of the international monetary system.

Central banks, with “very different views of what appropriate policy is”, were primarily mandated to pursue domestic objectives such as price stability and employment, leaving little scope for large-scale accord-style coordination, he noted.

Underlying his warning was a growing list of risks that, in his view, were eroding confidence in the dollar-centred international monetary system, including mounting US fiscal debt, threats to the Federal Reserve’s independence, financial deregulation, tariffs and weakening geopolitical alliances.

Stability … and a very gradual transition are in everyone’s interest

Barry Eichengreen, economist

Eichengreen sketched two possible paths ahead: a gradual shift towards a more multipolar monetary order as the dollar slowly loses ground, or a disorderly loss of confidence in the dollar before credible alternatives are in place.

“What we’ve learned is that the probability of the second scenario, where the confidence in the dollar is lost abruptly … is higher now than we would have imagined a few years ago,” he said during a virtual appearance at the lecture, which was co-hosted by the Society for the Analysis of Government and Economics and by Tsinghua University’s Academic Centre for Chinese Economic Practice and Thinking.

He cautioned that the risk would rise above 50 per cent if today’s “erratic policies” continued under President Donald Trump and his successors for another decade.

Talk of “de-dollarisation” has grown louder in recent years as concerns over sanctions, the “weaponisation” of finance, and Washington’s fiscal trajectory have prompted countries to seek greater use of alternative currencies, with the euro and the yuan often cited as potential alternatives to the US dollar’s dominance.

While Eichengreen said that neither the euro nor the yuan was ready to replace the US dollar, both Europe and China were incentivised to expand the international role of their currencies. However, he noted, nobody wanted to see global financial markets destabilised by a disorderly transition.

He also dismissed the idea that Beijing could use its massive holdings of US Treasuries as leverage by selling them en masse.

“Chinese authorities realise that if they do that, it could upend the global financial system,” he said, adding that this would hurt China as well as the US. “Stability … and a very gradual transition are in everyone’s interest.”
