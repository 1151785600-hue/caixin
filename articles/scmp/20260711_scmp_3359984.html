China aims to revive physical stores, make them ‘immersive’, as shield against e-commerce

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 529 words | 2026-07-11

With retail sales growth cooling in May to the slowest pace since December 2022 and persistent weakness in big-ticket items extending through last month, Beijing has stepped in to reinvigorate bricks-and-mortar operators facing intense price competition from e-commerce platforms.

The Ministry of Commerce, together with eight other relevant authorities, on Thursday released guidelines to accelerate the innovative development of the retail sector. The document charts out a path for differentiated competition between online and offline retailers, featuring plans to build a more rational pricing system over the next few years.

The road map arrives against a backdrop of “trading down”, a trend that emerged during the Covid-19 pandemic and has persisted through 2026. The behaviour sees shoppers switch from premium brands to lower-priced alternatives, or simply purchase less, due to economic uncertainty or less disposable income.

“Backed by highly efficient logistics networks and e-commerce platforms squeezing sellers’ profit margins to the extreme, online retail has maintained faster growth over recent years,” said Zhou Changqing, general manager at the Beijing-based Retail China consultancy.

To shore up consumption growth and upgrade the sector, the guidelines – which run through 2030 – stipulate that China will strive to transform physical retail spaces into destinations for shopping, immersive experiences and social entertainment.

Meanwhile, the government aims to ensure that online platforms maintain extensive product ranges alongside compliant business operations.

“Young consumers display a strong appetite for self-reward consumption and immersive experiences,” Zhou said. “More offline stores featuring those would undoubtedly lift average transaction values, but the revival of offline retail delivers far more benefits.

“Boosting in-store consumption is also vital to regional development within a city and supports the real estate sector at the same time. E-commerce platforms simply cannot match offline retail on these fronts.”

The new road map also encourages merchants to refine pricing strategies to narrow price disparities between online and offline channels, promising targeted crackdowns on unfair pricing practices. By 2030, a “well-structured”, “competitively orderly” modern retail system will take shape, while a batch of “globally competitive” retail firms will emerge, the document said.

Yet, some analysts argue that steady job and income prospects are essential for a sustained uptick in consumption, and for the time being, many Chinese consumers remain relatively wary about purchases.

Average household spending on fast-moving consumer goods during the first quarter of this year “was driven primarily by softer purchase volumes and downtrading within and across brands”, according to a mid-June study by global consultancy Bain & Co and market research firm Kantar Worldpanel.

The study further noted a shift to “a more cautious consumption pattern”, with households managing spending through lower volumes and “value-oriented choices”.

“Parts of the workforce hold muted expectations over future job prospects and wage growth,” said a commentary published last month in the Qiushi Journal, the most influential theoretical journal of the Communist Party. “Coupled with inadequate access to high-standard social security and public services, this has driven stronger precautionary saving and dampened consumer appetite.”

The piece also highlighted that “a sluggish property market has left many households feeling as though their financial buffers have eroded, prompting more cautious spending habits”.
