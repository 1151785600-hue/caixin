‘Disbelief, shock’: Singapore PM on Faishal’s inappropriate messages with woman

SCMP | FREE | 1110 words | 2026-07-22

A married Singapore minister’s sudden resignation amid revelations he had exchanged inappropriate messages with a woman had rocked the city state’s establishment circles, with a groundswell of sentiment calling for him to be retained.

Amid online petitions and calls to keep former minister-in-charge of Muslim affairs Faishal Ibrahim following his resignation on Monday, Prime Minister Lawrence Wong shed further light on the controversy, suggesting the matter was more serious than previously believed.

Faishal resigned from the cabinet, parliament and the People’s Action Party after accepting he had fallen short of standards expected of him in his online interactions with a woman, which resulted in harassment accusations by both parties.

Wong learned of the interactions after the woman emailed him with details of their exchanges.

Speaking to local media on Wednesday, Wong said he initially thought the contents in the “detailed” email were fabricated.

“My initial reaction was disbelief and shock.”

Police were called in to investigate the accusations but no criminal action was taken.

Faishal, 58, who was also senior minister of state for home affairs, said in his resignation letter that while there was no physical relationship between him and the woman, and he did not intend for the interactions to develop into one, there was a “lapse of judgment” in how he handled the matter.

Wong noted that he had attempted to see if there was a way for Faishal to continue in office despite the circumstances, but the complaint against Faishal had made it a public matter.

Several key colleagues on his team independently reviewed the evidence and reached the same conclusion, Wong added.

The prime minister stressed that if any other MP from the ruling People’s Action Party had been in a similar situation, the same standard would have applied.

He said he recognised the temptation to make an exception for Faishal, but “doing so will only cause more damage and harm to Singapore”.

“We have to do the right thing, and we have to continue to uphold the trust that Singaporeans have in our political system and in our public institutions,” Wong said.

“He accepts that he made a very serious mistake in engaging her, even when the nature of the interactions crossed the line, and he also accepts that his responses, his interactions with her, objectively speaking, were questionable and inappropriate,” Wong added.

On Tuesday evening, Wong, the new acting minister in charge of Muslim affairs Zaqy Mohamed and Home Affairs Minister K. Shanmugam met members of the Malay-Muslim community.

It was understood that the issue of retaining Faishal was raised during the meeting.

Earlier on Wednesday, Shanmugam revealed on social media that there was a series of online interactions between Faishal and the woman, and Faishal expressed concern that the woman might react negatively if he abruptly cut her off.

“Nevertheless, his responses were, objectively, inappropriate and questionable. They come across as engaging with her. Indeed, that was how the lady interpreted his responses, and said she was encouraged to continue,” Shanmugam said.

Shanmugam explained that once the woman emailed the prime minister, the matter was no longer a private, consensual matter between two people and was “semi-public”. Since she lodged a complaint against a public office holder, the Prime Minister’s Office had to “consider the appropriateness of Faishal’s interactions”.

Shanmugam said Faishal received a fair hearing, and noted that regardless of his intentions, one had to look at the evidence and facts.

“Faishal himself reflected on the matter and acknowledged that he should not have allowed his exchanges with the lady to carry on, and he should have established clear boundaries from the start,” Shanmugam said.

Independent political observer Felix Tan said Wong and Shanmugam’s comments reinforced the point that the interactions were egregious enough to warrant Faishal’s resignation.

He noted that the comments and petition in support of Faishal appeared to be gaining traction among Singaporeans, suggesting that segments of the public questioned whether the response was proportionate to the circumstances.

“Having to deal with such an issue early in his tenure is far from ideal, so the government is signalling that the decision was not made lightly or on a whim, but followed careful consideration. By addressing the matter decisively, PM Wong is also seeking to prevent the issue from escalating into a prolonged distraction for the government,” Felix Tan said.

Political observer and law professor Eugene Tan said he believed the minister’s comments on Wednesday had to do with the public clamour over whether Faishal’s lapse of judgment warranted his resignation.

“I think the preference was not to go into more detail, but there was probably pressure to demonstrate that Faishal’s resignation was warranted,” Eugene Tan said.

“[The] government had to show that it was not a case of nothing much and to use that to demonstrate the high standards it demands of its MPs and political office holders and that it is prepared to enforce those standards.”

An online petition urging Wong to reject Faishal’s resignation has since garnered 1,340 signatures.

The petition’s creator, Ridjal Noor, wrote that Singapore needed “experienced, steady public leaders who understand the realities people face and can keep serving without disruption”.

“We therefore appeal to the Prime Minister of Singapore to reject the resignation and confirm that Assoc Prof Faishal Ibrahim should remain in his position,” Ridjal said.

“This decision would reflect respect for public service, confidence in proven leadership, and a commitment to keeping capable ministers where they can continue to do the most good.”

Some political observers also spoke against Faishal’s resignation.

On Tuesday, Walid Jumblatt Abdullah, an associate professor of public policy at Nanyang Technological University, said on social media that while the public did not know much about the interactions, what was known was that they were not criminal and were unrelated to his public work.

“I therefore do not think he should have resigned. And I wonder whether we are setting impossible standards to meet for (aspiring) politicians, and whether younger people would be deterred from stepping forward to join politics because of that,” Walid said.

Former nominated member of parliament Calvin Cheng said on Facebook on Monday: “A good man should not have his whole career and life destroyed for something like this.”

Cheng said Faishal should be allowed to apologise and be transparent, while community leaders could decide whether he should keep his Muslim affairs position. A by-election could also be called in his ward to see if he should retain his MP position.

“We don’t live in 17th century Puritan England. The people will understand,” Cheng said.
