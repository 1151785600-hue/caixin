Philippines’ Pax Silica AI hub plan slammed for mineral ‘plunder’

SCMP | FREE | 1098 words | 2026-07-17

On a 1,620-hectare (4,000 acres) site north of Manila, a hi-tech hub is pitched by Philippine and US officials as a global gateway to AI, semiconductors and critical minerals.

For critics, however, the proposed development – part of the US-backed global Pax Silica project – is concrete proof of another infrastructure push over contested land at the expense of communities, threatening their livelihood and scarce water supply.

The project is a further example of ambitious investment pitches moving ahead of accountability on environmental safeguards and proper cost-benefit analysis, they say.

Manila is seeking to finalise a framework agreement with Washington for the hub within two to three months, according to Joshua Bingcang, president and chief executive of the state-run Bases Conversion and Development Authority (BCDA).

The BCDA is tasked with turning former US military bases into centres of economic growth, including in New Clark City in Capas, Tarlac province, where the hub is set to be located, about 100km (62 miles) north of Manila.

Pax Silica aims to secure critical supply chains for artificial intelligence, semiconductors and other strategic technologies, with the Philippines and Singapore the only Southeast Asian countries in the 13-member coalition.

Bingcang said that the hub would likely start operations in two years, attract an initial investment of US$10 billion and generate “hundreds of thousands of jobs”.

The hub is part of the Luzon Economic Corridor, a US-Japan-Philippines project linking four cities on Luzon Island, the latter of which is projected to generate US$100 billion for the local economy.

Bingcang confirmed that Manila and Washington were exchanging draft agreements and denied earlier reports that the hub would operate under a US legal framework despite being on Philippine soil.

In a television interview on Wednesday, Bingcang said the US was aiming to conclude the deal before President Donald Trump’s potential visit to the Philippines to attend the 49th Association of Southeast Asian Nations summit and mark 80 years of ties between Manila and Washington in November.

The US State Department has described Pax Silica as an effort to build “a secure, resilient” technology supply chain, as Washington looks to reduce its reliance on Chinese suppliers, especially for critical technologies.

Opposition to the planned hub has grown, with critics expressing concerns about the continued marginalisation and displacement of indigenous communities and farmers, environmental degradation and resource exploitation.

IBON Foundation challenged claims that the hub would accelerate growth of industries in the Philippines, saying in a social media post in May that the country “doesn’t have a strategy for national industrialisation”.

“Pax Silica isn’t about industrialising the Philippines but about deeper integration into US-controlled semiconductor and AI supply chains,” the non-profit think tank said. Industrialisation required building “national productive power” through Filipino industries, labour, science and technology, it added.

On Tuesday, around 3,000 members of the indigenous Aeta community in Capas marched in protest against what they said were restrictions imposed by authorities on their lands, including limits on access and basic supplies, which they claimed were partly related to the hub.

“The single most dangerous thing about Pax Silica is what it adds to a crisis that was already exploding before the announcement was made,” said Cathleen De Guzman, national coordinator for the Kalikasan People’s Network for the Environment, an environmental campaign centre in the Philippines.

De Guzman said the Aeta community had been fighting for its ancestral domains for decades, with the government declining to issue its members the relevant titles.

“The absence of a paper document was used to erase the existence of a community that has lived there since before the Spanish arrived,” De Guzman said, adding that an estimated 20,000 indigenous people and 15,000 farmers were at risk of displacement from New Clark City.

She also raised concerns about the Sacobia watershed, saying the risks of the primary water source for areas across New Clark City drying up had been flagged as far back as 2020.

This Week in Asia has contacted the BCDA for comment.

Middle ground?

The opposition has exposed gaps in transparency, timing and legal process, according to environmental analysts.

Paulo Burro, an environmental policy and government affairs consultant and managing partner of Climate Balanced Business Advisors, said land conversion and the displacement of farmers, fisherfolk and indigenous people remained the biggest concern arising from the development of the Pax Silica hub.

“With the drive to consolidate the supply chain, there may be increased mineral demand ... questions remain around its actual economic impact on displaced farmers and fisherfolk,” Burro said.

Jonas Marie Dumdum, an energy and sustainability researcher and consultant in the Philippines, said such concerns were understandable given the scale of the project, noting the potential demand for minerals such as nickel, cobalt and copper to feed the hub.

Aside from drawing 5,000 megawatts from a coal-dependent grid in Luzon, companies at the hub would be located near identified watersheds and northeast of Mount Pinatubo, he added, referring to the active volcano that caused hundreds of deaths following its eruption in 1991.

“The real pressure points in Capas come down to three things: carbon emissions, water, and waste,” Dumdum said.

The industries would draw on the same water systems used to irrigate farms in central Tarlac, with mineral processing producing heavy-metal waste in an area vulnerable to flooding and volcanic eruption, he said.

“These points raise a question not just about our climate commitments but about whether there’s enough power, fresh water, and enough space and capacity to treat e-wastes in the area.”

Burro said, however, that there was potential for a compromise to ensure that the interests of all parties, from businesses, environmental groups, to local communities, would be protected. The question was “how the project gets built and who gets a say, not necessarily over whether it should exist at all”, he added.

Dumdum said the public should be informed about the sources of the hub’s water and power supply, and the waste treatment process for mineral extraction.

Apart from a proper assessment of the potential environmental impact, there should be “real consent” from farming and indigenous communities before the finalisation of any deal, he added.

De Guzman said she “outrightly rejects Pax Silica”, calling it an attack on national sovereignty and patrimony, as well as a “plunder” of the country’s critical minerals and energy sources.

She also questioned the authorities over their commitment in engaging communities to address various concerns, calling the process a “pattern of consultation theatre”.

“This unequal agreement that the Philippines has joined opens our lands and vulnerable sectors to further exploitation and displacement,” she said.
