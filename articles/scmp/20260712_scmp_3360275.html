Beijing renews attacks on landmark South China Sea tribunal on ruling’s 10th anniversary

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 794 words | 2026-07-12

Beijing has denounced a “thoroughly illegal” international tribunal that ruled against its expansive claims in the South China Sea, with a senior diplomat describing it as a “thorn” in the side of its relationship with the Philippines on the 10th anniversary of the decision.

In an article published in People’s Daily on Sunday, Jing Quan, the ambassador to the Philippines, said clearing its “lingering toxic legacy” was a matter of the utmost urgency.

“The most important task for Manila today is to … abandon illusions and remove this stumbling block to allow bilateral relations to return to the right track,” Jing wrote in the Communist Party mouthpiece.

Beijing’s sweeping claims of sovereignty over nearly all islands, rock features and the rights to the adjacent waters in the South China Sea have locked it into long-standing disputes with rival claimants, including the Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia and Brunei.

In 2013, Manila took the unprecedented step of launching an arbitration case under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS).

The tribunal in The Hague ultimately ruled that although it could not rule on questions of sovereignty, Beijing’s claims to historical and economic rights over most of the features in the South China Sea were invalid.

Beijing refused to recognise the tribunal’s authority to hear the case and has rejected the ruling. It has also said it would not implement its findings.

Jing wrote: “The South China Sea Arbitration was a thoroughly illegal affair from start to finish.”

In the run-up to the anniversary, China has stepped up its campaign against the ruling with a series of articles in People’s Daily, while a Ministry of Natural Resources think tank published a report about UNCLOS that warned of “abusive practices”.

Jing said that the 2016 decision created immense hurdles for improving relations with the Philippines and managing disputes.

“It has become a painful ‘thorn’ embedded deeply between China and the Philippines, and a convenient pretext for extra-regional forces to interfere in regional affairs,” he wrote.

Jing argued that the dispute was fundamentally about territorial sovereignty over maritime features – something that lies outside the scope of UNCLOS.

He also said Beijing had excluded itself from the convention’s compulsory dispute resolution mechanisms via a formal declaration in 2006, something allowed under Article 298.

However, the convention also stipulates that if two nations fail to resolve a maritime dispute through negotiation, one party can unilaterally take the other before an international judicial body or tribunal.

Jing warned that further “infringements and provocations” by Manila would meet a firm response from China to protect its rights.

A primary point of contention was the tribunal’s decision to categorise Taiping Island, the largest natural feature in the Spratly Islands, as a “rock” rather than a habitable feature that can support economic activity – making it ineligible for a 200-nautical mile exclusive economic zone.

“There are countless such absurdities in the ruling, laying bare the true motive of this farce: to deny China its territorial sovereignty and maritime rights in the South China Sea,” Jing wrote.

“China’s decision not to accept, participate in or recognise the arbitration and its award is a stance not only to defend our own sovereignty and interests, but also to uphold the authority and dignity of international law.”

Jing also criticised Manila for introducing domestic legislation aimed at unilaterally implementing the award, while “repeatedly provoking incidents” around features such as Sabina Shoal and Sandy Cay.

Sunday’s anniversary came after three years of repeated tensions between China and the Philippines following a string of confrontations between their ships in the disputed waters over the past three years.

Since becoming president in 2022, Ferdinand Marcos Jnr has reversed his predecessor Rodrigo Duterte’s policy of downplaying the South China Sea dispute in pursuit of better relations with Beijing.

Instead, his administration has an “assertive transparency” policy of publishing footage of confrontations, including collisions between ships and China’s use of water cannons, to expose what it describes as aggressive tactics from Beijing.

On Sunday, 14 countries – led by the United States and the Philippines, and including Britain, Japan and Australia – issued a joint statement reaffirming their “unwavering commitment” to a rules-based, free and open Indo-Pacific.

The statement said the 2016 award decision was a “significant milestone” that is “final, legally binding, and definitive”.

It added there was no legal basis for Beijing’s sweeping maritime claims, including those based on historic rights.

“We reaffirm our strong opposition to the use of coastguard, military and maritime militia forces to harass, obstruct, or intimidate lawful operations by other states at sea or in the air, and in so doing endanger the safety of personnel and fishermen and seriously degrade regional peace and security,” the statement said.
