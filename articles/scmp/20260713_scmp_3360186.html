BN cruises to victory in Malaysia’s Johor, in blow to Anwar and PH

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1128 words | 2026-07-13

The coalition of Malaysian Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim was dealt a blow on Saturday after state rival Barisan Nasional (BN) trounced Pakatan Harapan (PH) in the Johor election.

BN cruised to victory with 48 seats out of 56 up for grabs, while Anwar’s PH managed only eight. BN’s result was better than the 40 seats it secured in the 2022 race. The two-thirds majority it retains gives it absolute power to pass constitutional amendments and redraw electoral boundaries in Johor.

The state was turned into a battleground between the federal partners, with both coalitions fielding candidates in every constituency and a voter turnout nearing the expected 70 per cent mark for an electorate of more than 2.7 million.

The result now casts doubt on Anwar’s unwieldy coalition government and could have implications for whether a snap national election is called.

Ahmad Zahid Hamidi, deputy prime minister and BN president, earlier thanked Johoreans for once again choosing BN to govern the state, stressing that the party “will not be arrogant in victory” and would work hard to fulfil its campaign pledges.

“We also hope this victory will trigger a blue wave in the elections to come,” he said.

The election saw 172 candidates vying for all 56 seats, with the turnout significantly higher than the 54.9 per cent in 2022, driven by high public interest and intense campaigning.

Fourteen seats had straight fights, 27 featured three-cornered contests, 12 were four-way battles and three had five candidates.

BN entered polling day defending the 40 seats it held before the state assembly was dissolved on June 1, while PH had 12, opposition Perikatan Nasional three and youth-centric Muda one.

The result is not expected to change Anwar’s parliamentary majority, but a sweeping BN victory strengthens Umno’s – the dominant Malay party in BN – hand inside his national coalition while exposing PH’s inability to match its federal influence on state ground.

Anwar had earlier said he would consider a snap election if tensions within his ruling coalition worsened.

BN had ruled Malaysia for six decades before losing power in 2018 to PH. After the 2022 national election ended in a hung parliament, BN joined Anwar’s bloc to form the present unity government.

Wong Chin Huat, a political scientist at Sunway University, said the Johor election underscored PH’s “under-competitiveness against BN”.

He said BN, with clear aims of leading the next federal coalition government, must win 70 of the 165 federal seats, exceeding its current 23 seats by a large margin.

“If PH is strong, BN would train its gun at the opposition, but now PH is weak, it might enter a deal with PAS to divide the Malay heartland and focus on winning most of PH’s seats,” he said.

In Bekok, a town in the northern Johor district of Segamat, BN’s victory was warmly received by locals such as Kumutha Ramasamy, who paid tribute to incumbent state legislator Tan Chong’s hands-on approach to constituency work.

The BN candidate won by 64 per cent, or 10,230 votes, against his Democratic Action Party (DAP) opponent, according to official results from the Election Commission.

“Whatever help is needed, Tan Chong is always the first to arrive on the spot to help the people of Bekok,” Kumutha, 41, said.

She hoped the new state government would build a road linking her small town to Kluang to boost employment access beyond Bekok’s limited job market.

Earlier in the day in Johor Jaya, where Chinese voters form the largest electoral bloc, a woman who wanted to be known only as Melanie, said she and her sister completed the voting process in about five minutes.

“We have done our part as citizens,” the 50-year-old told This Week in Asia. “As Malaysians, we have a responsibility to come and vote. It matters for the country, the economy, everything.”

Voter Afya Ghazali, 26, who works in Singapore and commutes across the Causeway every day, said she wanted the next government to improve infrastructure and create more jobs in the state.

“We need better infrastructure and more job opportunities in Johor so that more of us can serve our own state,” she said. “There is still a shortage of jobs in the city, especially for us.”

Pollster Ilham Centre on Friday before the race attributed BN’s advantage to its stronger political machinery, familiar candidates and support in Malay-majority zones.

PH, meanwhile, was relying on Chinese-majority urban and semi-urban seats, where Ilham said turnout of at least 65 per cent would be needed for the coalition to hold its ground.

Johor, Malaysia’s southern gateway to Singapore, has attracted vast investment promises, yet the campaign repeatedly returned to wages, housing and living costs, along with the distrust between Umno and Chinese-majority DAP that their alliance in Putrajaya has never fully buried.

The state contributed about 14 billion ringgit (US$3.7 billion) to federal coffers between 2023 and 2025, according to finance ministry figures cited by Anwar, who said Putrajaya returned 16 billion ringgit to Johor over the same period.

In a fishing village in Iskandar Puteri, Sabri Abdullah had little patience for the intrigue in Kuala Lumpur. The 58-year-old measured governments by the roads, street lights and running water that had reached his community during his lifetime.

“Put simply, this place started from zero. There was nothing,” Sabri told This Week in Asia on Friday. “Now, alhamdulillah, people in the village can see the development.”

That record, he said, was why his vote remained with BN.

Izuddin Zulhaidat, who runs a food business, was coy about his vote, but pointed to the range of developments in Bekok.

These include the completion of a new railway station as part of the Electric Train Service that runs along the west coast of Peninsular Malaysia from the Thai border to Johor Bahru, and new franchises and consumer brands opening up in Bekok.

“Overall, I am satisfied with the state government’s performance, because we also get to enjoy the benefits of development from southern Johor,” the 29-year-old said.

Former DAP state legislator Gan Peck Cheng urged the local government to develop other Johor towns to boost overall growth, even as she lauded initiatives such as the Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone.

“We are not asking [the state government] to slow down Johor Bahru’s development, but we are asking that the rest of Johor be brought along so that they don’t miss out,” she said.

In the lead-up to polling day, caretaker chief minister Onn Hafiz Ghazi and BN sold continuity, political stability and Johor’s investment record, while promising that the state would remain aligned with Putrajaya even without DAP in its government.

Anwar and PH pressed wages, affordable housing and the cost of living, while DAP chief Anthony Loke warned against “one-party dominance” earlier this week.
