As Laos’ methanol poisoning case begins, victims’ families, friends slam ‘cover-up’

SCMP | FREE | 1177 words | 2026-07-24

Almost two years after six travellers died and one survivor was left blind from suspected methanol-tainted drinks in Laos, the criminal case over the incident is finally proceeding with charges filed against a distillery owner as families of the victims continue to demand answers and stern punishment.

Last Friday, the owner of the distillery where the drinks linked to the deaths were thought to have originated was given one charge of running an illegal business and one charge of selling harmful food products, and could face a jail term of between three months and four years if convicted.

The families described the potential punishment as inadequate to reflect the scale of the tragedy, having earlier called for a charge of manslaughter against the owner and bar manager of the Nana Backpackers Hostel in the backpacker town of Vang Vieng.

Two Danish nationals, 20-year-old Anne-Sofie Orkild Coyman and 21-year-old Freja Vennervald Sørensen, died after drinking suspected methanol-tainted alcohol at the hostel in November 2024.

Bianca Jones and Holly Morton-Bowles, both 19-year-old Australians, British lawyer Simone White, 28, and American national James Louis Hutson, 57, also died in the incident.

Foreign media were barred from attending a press conference on July 17, where Laotian authorities announced the charges.

On the same day, Danish Foreign Minister Lars Lokke Rasmussen expressed dissatisfaction with the case.

“I am … deeply disappointed that the charges do not reflect the seriousness and scale of the tragedy that has affected so many families. We will now discuss next steps in the case together with the bereaved families and the other affected countries,” he said.

British podiatrist Bethany Clarke, who now lives in Australia and survived the methanol poisoning incident that killed White, her childhood friend, told This Week in Asia that she was frustrated with the investigation.

Clarke and White had spent the day of the incident tubing in Vang Vieng before attending a nightly happy hour at the hostel, where they were given six free shots of locally produced whisky and vodka.

“It is just a cover-up, and the charges are not even for the right person. The owner of the hostel and the person managing the buying of the alcohol are responsible,” Clarke said.

According to Clarke, she and White consumed vodka that appeared to be from locally made Tiger brand bottles.

“No other bar was affected, so how could it be a distillery issue?” she said.

Not long after the reported deaths, the foreign affairs department in Canberra warned Australians not to drink the Tiger Vodka and Tiger Whisky made in Laos “due to serious safety concerns”.

Laotian state media has reported that police said they could not determine responsibility for some of the deaths due to difficulties in establishing the evidence. Methanol was detected in blood samples from the two Australian victims and excessive methanol was found in the Tiger Vodka that they had consumed, according to local reports.

Warning on travel to Laos

Poisoning from consuming methanol, a toxic form of alcohol that is colourless and odourless, can occur when it is not properly removed from an alcoholic product. Methanol poisoning is common across Southeast Asia, where illicit alcohol is often sold. A small shot glass of methanol, or about 25ml, can cause death in a person if consumed.

Around 40,000 people have died globally of suspected or confirmed methanol poisoning in recent years between February 2019 and last month, according to data from Medecins Sans Frontieres.

Authorities in Laos have said the case would remain open for 15 years, and more charges could be filed if fresh evidence appears, according to the Danish foreign ministry.

However, Vientiane’s statement offered little comfort to Clarke.

“It is just dragging it on, and for what? No one is going to come forward. It is not good enough. There is no transparency and accountability,” she said.

Following news of the charges against the distillery owner, the Australian government updated its advisory for travel to Laos.

“We now advise exercising a high degree of caution due to crime, the risk of methanol poisoning and ongoing concerns around lack of transparency in the justice system,” it said last Friday.

Australian Foreign Minister Penny Wong said in a statement on the same day that she was “deeply frustrated and bitterly disappointed” by the charges.

Canberra had sent an envoy to Laos “to convey the Australian government’s objections and reinforce our expectations for an investigation that delivers justice for Holly, Bianca and the other victims of the methanol poisoning”, she added.

Laos’ ambassador to Canberra was also summoned for a meeting, Wong said.

Phil Robertson, director of Asia Human Rights and Labour Advocates, said he was not surprised by the charges, which he said appeared aimed at securing a quick conviction while limiting further scrutiny.

The charges also raised questions about whether authorities were “cutting a deal with the distillery owner” and reflected Vientiane’s desire to protect its tourism industry, Robertson said.

Local authorities are likely to be more worried about the new Australian travel advisory than the legal proceedings, according to Robertson.

“From the Lao government perspective, this case has always been less about those who died and more about trying to repair the serious public relations damage,” he said.

Moving on

British national Calum Macdonald, 24, survived the methanol poisoning incident but was left permanently blind after the methanol in the drinks he consumed attacked his optic nerves.

He had been drinking free shots at the hostel with Coyman and Sørensen, a day before White and Clarke did the same.

Macdonald told This Week in Asia that he had only found out some months after he returned to the UK that Coyman and Sørensen had died.

“It was a very eerie feeling as their faces were some of the last I ever saw before I lost my sight. I feel lucky. If I had had one more drink, I might not be alive.”

Regarding the ongoing case, Macdonald said he did not wish for anyone to face criminal charges.

“I don’t think anyone did anything on purpose to hurt anyone. The hostel had also been operating for a long time and this had never happened before. It is difficult, but I don’t feel the need to see anyone punished or any particular charges,” he said.

Macdonald acknowledged, however, that his views were different from the families who had lost loved ones in the incident.

“I didn’t lose a child, or a brother or a sister. I understand the level of distress caused to the families and I support whatever justice they feel is necessary,” said Macdonald, who is studying to be a financial analyst while working in the investment sector.

“I am not angry or upset about it any more. I have accepted what happened to me and adapted in my own life.”

This Week in Asia has reached out to three law firms in Laos for comment on the case and the country’s legal system. One declined to comment, while the others did not reply.
