Hong Kong man pleads guilty to subversive promotion of pro-Taiwan party

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 573 words | 2026-07-11

A Hong Kong man has pleaded guilty to inciting subversion under the national security law for promoting a pro-Taiwan party and disseminating anti-Communist messages between 2024 and last year.

The District Court heard on Thursday that Chan Ho-hin, a 23-year-old waiter, had proclaimed that the Taiwanese government was the only legitimate regime in the country and called on Hongkongers to support and join the Revive the Republic of China Freedom Party in Taiwan through social media, leaflets and letters.

The defendant also advocated an uprising similar to the 1911 Xinhai Revolution to overthrow the Communist Party of China, saying the Taiwanese government could “liberate” Hong Kong if it returned to power in mainland China.

A prosecution summary said Chan published 118 subversive messages on his social media accounts between June 27, 2024 and April 24, 2025.

He described the party as a murderous regime and contended that only Taipei had lawful sovereignty over Hong Kong.

Self-ruled Taiwan is viewed by Beijing as a breakway province that must be reunited with the mainland, even by force if necessary.

Chan also shared videos featuring “Glory to Hong Kong”, a popular protest song banned by authorities, and depicted the tune as the city’s “regional anthem under the rule of the Republic of China”.

On April 7 and 8 last year, the waiter staged what he called a “paper revolution” by affixing offensive posters across Kowloon to advocate his political stance.

In the same month, Chan attempted to spread anti-Communist messages through letters to people with similar views.

A letter he attempted to send to Thailand on April 23 contained 12 leaflets promoting the Taiwan-based party. The same leaflets were also found in 28 envelopes seized from the defendant’s public rental flat at Choi Wan (I) Estate.

National security police arrested Chan on April 28 and found he had frequent exchanges with a person claiming to be Cheng Kai-li, the party’s secretary general.

In a subsequent interview, the defendant said he was not a member of the Taiwan-based group, but had echoed its manifesto and supported China’s unification by the island’s regime.

He said he had intended to send the party’s application forms and advertising materials to anyone he had previously contacted on social media.

Defence counsel Yvonne Leung Lai-kwok on Thursday urged the court to sentence Chan to no more than five years behind bars, saying the accused was not a fervent supporter of the Taiwanese government and posed a low risk of reoffending.

She said her client, who was diagnosed with epilepsy and an intellectual disability, had been ostracised by peers at school and had undertaken such actions in the hope of being praised and recognised by others.

Leung also highlighted the lack of evidence that Chan received financial assistance from Cheng to promote the pro-Taiwan party.

Judge Stanley Chan Kwong-chi adjourned sentencing until July 20. The defendant, who has been denied bail since April 30 last year, remains in custody.

Inciting subversion carries a maximum jail sentence of 10 years under the national security law, but is capped at seven years when the case is heard by a district judge. A minimum sentence of five years applies to an offence “of a serious nature”.

More than 400 people have been arrested for allegedly committing offences endangering national security since the legislation took effect on June 30, 2020, according to official statistics.

Police had laid charges against 212 people and five companies as of June 1 this year.
