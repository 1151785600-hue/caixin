Hong Kong gazettes bill to enable joint immigration checks at Shenzhen crossing

SCMP | FREE | 491 words | 2026-07-14

Hong Kong authorities have gazetted a new law regulating an upgraded border crossing between the city and Shenzhen, marking another major step towards introducing a joint clearance model that is expected to cut clearance times from 30 minutes to five.

The Huanggang Port Hong Kong Port Area Bill was gazetted on Tuesday, with a Security Bureau spokesman saying the official opening date of the upgraded port had yet to be finalised with mainland Chinese authorities.

“The official opening date is to be agreed upon by the Guangdong and Hong Kong governments,” the spokesman said.

The bill provides the legal grounds for the city to implement a co-location arrangement at the border crossing, which would be the first to use a “joint inspection” model for clearance.

Under the arrangement, passengers will undergo departure and arrival clearance at a single checkpoint by presenting only the travel document required for their destination, instead of passing through separate counters.

The bill will be tabled at the Legislative Council on Wednesday for the first and second readings.

Ahead of the local law, the National People’s Congress Standing Committee, China’s top legislative body, passed a bill to authorise the city’s jurisdiction over part of the port area on June 26.

The State Council, China’s cabinet, confirmed the specific boundaries on July 8 and approved commissioning the Hong Kong port area at midnight on July 31, with the city exercising jurisdiction.

A government spokesman said the new “joint inspection” model would reduce clearance times from about 30 minutes at the existing Lok Ma Chau and Huanggang crossings to around five minutes.

“The design flow of the redeveloped Huanggang Port will increase to 200,000 passenger trips per day, and could be further increased to about 300,000 upon the future commissioning of the Northern Link Spur Line of the MTR Corporation,” the spokesman said.

The bureau spokesman added that the city would still need to introduce more subsidiary laws to meet the revamped border crossing’s operational needs.

These include the administration of the Hong Kong side of the crossing as a closed area, the designation of detention facilities, transport and traffic support, and the provision of public mobile radio communication services.

He said the revamped port building had been “basically completed”, with contractors continuing interior works and installing required facilities, adding that the passage of the bill regulating the Hong Kong side of the crossing would help further preparatory work between the city and Shenzhen authorities.

These would include joint tests, trials and drills on facilities and operational management.

Lawmakers previously expressed concern over the lack of a car park at the revamped crossing, saying a shuttle bus service for tour groups or drivers to reach the checkpoint building would be inconvenient.

Secretary for Security Chris Tang Ping-keung told the Legislative Council in March that space limitations at the rebuilt crossing would limit the provision of a public car park or bays for coaches, but authorities were exploring nearby options.
