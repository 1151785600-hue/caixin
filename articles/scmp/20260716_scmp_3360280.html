65 arrested as Hong Kong police raid unlicensed nightclubs, gambling dens

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 626 words | 2026-07-16

Hong Kong police have arrested 65 people in a crackdown on a criminal syndicate accused of running unlicensed nightclubs and gambling dens, where suspects allegedly supplied illicit drugs and hired illegal workers as hostesses.

Superintendent Billy Ching Chi-yan said on Sunday that the force’s intelligence indicated a criminal syndicate was running illegal nightclubs inside industrial buildings in Hung Hom, as well as gambling dens in other areas of Kowloon West, prompting the operation.

“Over the past two days, the Kowloon West regional crime headquarters launched a large-scale operation, code-named Thunderbolt and Roaring Tiger, to disrupt this syndicate’s sources of income,” Ching said.

From Friday to Saturday, police and the Immigration Department deployed more than 170 officers to raid 10 venues across Hung Hom, Yau Ma Tei, Tsim Sha Tsui, Mong Kok and Sham Shui Po.

Of those arrested, 40 were men and 25 women, aged 25 to 63. They face a range of charges, including drug trafficking, illegal gambling, money laundering and breach of conditions of stay in Hong Kong.

The first raid targeted suspected illegal nightclubs operating inside industrial buildings in Hung Hom, where syndicate members were also suspected of supplying illegal drugs to patrons and hiring mainland Chinese women who had entered the city on two-way permits to work as hostesses.

Chief Inspector Yim Wan-ha said police and immigration officers had raided three industrial units in Hung Hom that were suspected to house illegal entertainment venues of the syndicate, arresting 19 men and seven women, aged 25 to 45.

The 26 suspects were charged with offences such as trafficking in a dangerous drug, possession of dangerous drugs, providing alcoholic beverages without a licence, possessing alcoholic beverages for sale without a licence, breaching conditions of stay and failing to display an identity card.

Yim said officers found loudspeakers, condoms, mouthwash, lubricant and large quantities of alcohol inside the unit. They also seized 360 grams of illegal drugs, including a mixture known as “happy powder”, ketamine, methamphetamine and cocaine, with an estimated street value of HK$190,000 (US$17,856).

Officers subsequently raided two venues in Tsim Sha Tsui suspected to be nightclubs run by the same syndicate. Ten women, aged 25 to 40, holding two-way permits were arrested for breaching their conditions of stay in Hong Kong. All were suspected of working as hostesses at the nightclubs.

In addition to the nightclubs, police raided three suspected gambling dens and a suspected staff dormitory used by the syndicate, which doubled as a storage facility.

Chief Inspector Alex Yau Yu-sing of Kowloon West’s regional anti-triad unit said investigations showed the syndicate had been operating three gambling dens offering fishing arcade games.

“Syndicate members would also purchase access to customers’ bank accounts to use as stooge accounts to handle the gambling den’s proceeds,” Yau said.

He added that profits from these suspected gambling dens were transferred to a personal bank account belonging to a core member of the syndicate. Financial investigations revealed that more than HK$13 million in proceeds had been laundered through that account over the past few years.

During the raids, officers arrested 21 men and eight women, aged 27 to 63, on suspicion of money laundering, conspiracy to operate an illegal gambling establishment, running an illegal gambling establishment and gambling in an illegal gambling establishment.

Officers also confiscated 20 game console motherboards, card readers, gambling reward cards and funds worth HK$47,000.

Under Hong Kong law, selling alcohol without a licence carries a maximum penalty of HK$1 million and two years’ imprisonment. Consuming alcohol on unlicensed premises is also an offence, punishable by a maximum fine of HK$2,000.

Running a gambling establishment illegally carries a maximum of seven years’ imprisonment and a HK$5 million fine, while gambling inside an illegal gambling establishment is punishable by up to nine months in jail and a HK$50,000 fine.
