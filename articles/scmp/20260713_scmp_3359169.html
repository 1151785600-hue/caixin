Hong Kong’s Northern Metropolis project needn’t neglect agriculture

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 761 words | 2026-07-13

Hong Kong’s Northern Metropolis is positioned as the city’s next engine of growth – a long-term solution to land supply, economic transformation and cross-boundary integration. Just as importantly, the government has stressed that this massive undertaking will embed nature conservation and urban-rural integration at its core. Yet, a growing contradiction lies at the heart of this vision.

The very landscapes that could support a green, sustainable and liveable Northern Metropolis – its remaining farmland – now face growing pressure under proposed legislation aimed at streamlining development. Prioritising speed, in this context, would eventually compromise the vision of a green economy.

Food security and economic development were Hong Kong’s biggest challenges for most of the last century. A large portion of the workforce was engaged in agriculture, shaping the territory’s landscape and laying the foundation for further economic development. Today, our economy depends on services, with fewer than 2,500 farms – many run by part-timers – occupying just 0.6 per cent of the territory.

While economic development remains a priority, climate change has been added to this challenge. The weather is getting hotter, more humid and more volatile, increasingly threatening the liveability of the city. This is why farmland is more critical than ever. Traditionally, farmland in Hong Kong has been viewed as a source of food production, but it plays a far broader and more important role.

These rural landscapes sustain some of the city’s last functioning lowland ecological networks, supporting biodiversity, regulating water flows (the “sponge city” effect) and buffering the climate impact. They also carry immense cultural and social value, preserving rural heritage, sustaining livelihoods and providing much-needed green relief within an increasingly dense urban environment.

Farmland is central to the government’s policy commitments. The Blueprint for the Sustainable Development of Agriculture and Fisheries recognises the need to preserve high-quality agricultural land. Agricultural priority areas (APAs) were proposed as a key mechanism to safeguard these spaces and support urban-rural integration. However, the fundamental challenge lies in timing.

The APA framework remains under review. Meanwhile, the government proposes to facilitate development by introducing dedicated legislation to streamline rezoning processes and extend the duration of temporary land uses within the Northern Metropolis. This creates a clear risk: invaluable farmland may be lost before the policy intended to protect it is put in place.

Equally concerning is the fate of high-quality farmland within or near proposed new development areas (NDAs) under the Northern Metropolis, including Sandy Ridge, Tai Po Tin, Shan Kai Wat, Lei Uk, Ma Tso Lung and Chow Tin. These sites are not covered by the APA framework.

As a result, some of Hong Kong’s most valuable and intact agricultural land falls into a policy gap – lacking long-term protection while facing increasing development pressure. Temporary land use must be tightly managed. Retaining a shorter approval period would help ensure such uses remain genuinely transitional, reducing the risk of permanent land degradation.

The city must also differentiate between agricultural methods. Some types of so-called hi-tech agriculture may cause irreversible damage, such as soil degradation and hydrological disruption, and should be excluded from areas suitable for soil-based farming. These hi-tech farms can be operated much more cost-efficiently just across the border.

Safeguarding farmland is not separate from development – it is fundamental to achieving the Northern Metropolis vision. What is needed is not a rejection of development, but better sequencing and integration. Farmland protection must come before planning acceleration. The government promotes nature-based solutions and the “sponge city” concept. Simply integrating existing farmland and greenways would make a meaningful contribution to these approaches.

The Northern Metropolis represents a rare opportunity to redefine how Hong Kong grows and shapes its future, showing development and conservation can be mutually reinforcing. But this balance will not happen automatically; it requires clear priorities, proper sequencing and firm commitment. If Hong Kong is serious about building a resilient, sustainable and liveable future, it must ensure its green foundations are secured. Once farmland is gone, it is lost.

Farmland in the Northern Metropolis supports several objectives: green open spaces, community activities, recreation, food education, wildlife habitats, carbon sinks, alternative employment opportunities, and more. It must be integrated into the Northern Metropolis and, to map this out, we propose an integrated greenway network.

This would serve as a blueprint combining agriculture, nature and heritage, outlining a vision for ecological resilience that addresses both economic development and climate change, a vision in which agriculture continues to play an important role in the future of Hong Kong.
