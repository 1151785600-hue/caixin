China targets 190 million inbound travellers, more global influence in 2030 plan

SCMP | FREE | 493 words | 2026-07-12

China aims to attract 190 million international visitors by 2030 – generating US$150 billion in spending – under a new five-year tourism plan that also seeks to increase Beijing’s influence over global industry rules.

The State Council on Tuesday announced its 15th five-year plan for the fast-growing tourism industry, which included an annual target of 8.3 billion domestic trips worth 7.7 trillion yuan (US$1.13 trillion) by the end of the 2026-2030 period.

Tourism would further play a part in “showcasing China’s image” while continuing to improve its “ability and influence in participating in the formulation of international rules”, according to the plan.

Inbound tourism has grown since late 2023, when China began expanding its visa-free entry scheme to cover an additional 50 countries. Their spending helps support a domestic economy that has been soft since the Covid-19 pandemic.

More than 154 million inbound visitors, including those from Hong Kong, Macau and Taiwan, arrived in mainland China last year, a 17.1 per cent increase over 2024, according to the Ministry of Culture and Tourism.

“The inbound angle is especially important,” said Subramania Bhatt, CEO of the marketing and technology firm China Trading Desk.

“China wants tourism to support domestic consumption, local economic development and the country’s international image,” he said. Those goals should encourage more investment in multilingual services, “foreigner-friendly” payments, and air-rail connectivity, he added.

“For foreign tourists, the visible improvement should be a more seamless trip,” Bhatt said.

Despite worries about employment and property values, Chinese citizens remain keen to travel on long weekends as well as take holidays of a week or more, often in search of new experiences.

The push to showcase China’s image through tourism stands out in this plan, said Charles Chang, a professor of finance at Fudan University in Shanghai.

“We hear a lot about how Hollywood transmits US culture implicitly, but this is really explicit, both for domestic travel and for international visitors,” Chang said.

China is also taking a stronger leadership role in rule-making for the industry, hosting a technical committee meeting of the International Organisation for Standardisation related to tourism in May after proposing national standards for shopping venues, travel for the elderly and wheelchair-accessible hotels.

The 2026-2030 plan further called for improving tourism service quality and strengthening inter-agency coordination. It said tourism’s role would grow in terms of boosting domestic demand and employment.

Because tourism spans transport, payments, visas, public security, culture and retail among other components, “many of the sector’s bottlenecks are therefore cross-departmental, not purely commercial”, Bhatt said.

“By asking different ministries and local governments to coordinate around tourism development, the plan gives businesses a clearer sense of where policy support, infrastructure spending and regulatory attention are likely to go,” he said. “For tourists, the likely impact is broader and higher-quality choice.”

China’s previous five-year plans for tourism emphasised the growth of cultural tourism, technological advances and reduction of risks such as the Covid-19 pandemic.
