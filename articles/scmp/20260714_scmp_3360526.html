Singapore-Malaysia relations enter ‘mature’ era with Tharman’s state visit

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 856 words | 2026-07-14

Singapore President Tharman Shanmugaratnam’s first state visit to Malaysia this week comes amid strengthening ties between the neighbours and a greater willingness by both sides to let go of historical “baggage”, analysts say.

Observers add that the new generation of Southeast Asian leaders also possesses a heightened awareness of mutual needs in an increasingly fragmented world order.

Tharman, who was elected president in 2023 after stepping down as senior minister, is on a state trip from Sunday to Wednesday to Kuala Lumpur at the invitation of Malaysia’s king, Sultan Ibrahim Iskandar, who visited Singapore in May 2024.

In an interview published by Malaysian media outlet Bernama on Sunday, Tharman stressed that the trip showed the importance both countries placed on building understanding at all levels.

“It has never been, and must never be, a purely transactional relationship. In an increasingly troubled world, the trust that we have accumulated and kept renewing over the past 60 years is critical,” Tharman said.

That trust was the bedrock upon which both neighbours built cooperation and symbiotic development in areas such as economic integration, renewable energy and talent development, he added.

Singapore and Malaysia have had disagreements predating the 61 years since the city state separated and became independent.

Sore points have included a water agreement allowing Singapore to buy and import water from Malaysia, and territorial disputes involving the Pedra Branca islets, which were escalated to the International Court of Justice.

Yet, the countries share deeply integrated supply chains, infrastructure projects and a joint economic zone. Singapore is Malaysia’s second largest trading partner with total trade reaching 402.35 billion ringgit (US$98 billion) in 2025, an increase of 1.5 per cent compared with 2024.

According to local media, the most recent estimate indicates that about 120,000 Singaporeans live and work in Malaysia.

Mutual needs

There has always been a recognition of the multilayered relationship between the two countries, according to Francis Hutchinson, principal fellow and coordinator of the Malaysia Studies Programme with ISEAS – Yusof Ishak Institute.

“But over the past 10 to 15 years, you have seen a new generation of leaders on both sides of the causeway taking over, and this has enabled more initiatives to be explored with less ‘baggage’,” Hutchinson said.

He cited the negotiations underpinning the Johor-Singapore Special Economic Zone (JS-SEZ) as an example of discussions being more positive and less couched in zero-sum terms than might have been emblematic of the past.

Beyond generational change, Hutchinson noted that both sides were also deeply aware of their mutual needs regarding supply chains for crucial goods such as electronics, oil and gas.

“It is likely that we will see the two countries forge ahead in key areas both to harvest low-hanging fruit and demonstrate the potential for more ambitious groupings to follow,” he said.

Shashi Jayakumar, executive director of SJK Geostrategic Advisory, said underpinning the incremental changes in the relationship was the recognition that “even as thorny issues may occasionally arise, both sides will increasingly need each other in this fragmenting world”.

There was clear impetus to manage issues – including those that could cause friction – through channels such as the leaders’ retreat, he said. Projects such as the JS-SEZ and Johor Bahru–Singapore Rapid Transit System Link also showed how both sides would benefit from cooperation.

“Does all this mean that disagreements will never arise? I do not think so – but the cementing of processes at the highest level, as well as standing mechanisms at the senior and technical officials’ level, might help minimise the possibility that issues could suddenly flare up in an antagonistic way,” he said.

On whether the close relationship would last even if leaders changed, Jayakumar said while ties might have become “somewhat more tricky” when Mahathir Mohamad returned as prime minister in May 2018, negotiations still occurred then.

Both sides mutually suspended port claims in March 2019 and agreed to an international third-party dispute settlement procedure if bilateral talks failed. The annual leaders’ retreat also continued, including Mahathir and Lee Hsien Loong’s first together in 2019.

“I would suggest that even if there is a major political shift, there is still a relatively durable institutional scaffolding to manage differences that from time to time might arise,” Jayakumar said.

While positive personal relationships between leaders were good for ties, such rapport was not always possible nor was it sufficient on its own, said Ariel Tan, senior fellow and coordinator of the Malaysia Programme at the S. Rajaratnam School of International Studies.

“What is essential is that both sides see that cooperation and dispute settlement are in their respective national interests, otherwise, the friendliest partners would not be able to sell it to their own people,” Tan said.

Jayakumar, however, cited Tharman’s Bernama interview as showing signs of a “maturing” bilateral relationship.

The president drew on memories of his time in a paddy-farming village in Kedah nearly 50 years ago as an early impression of Malaysian hospitality, and closed the interview with the Malay saying “jiran sepakat membawa berkat” (neighbours in agreement bring blessings).

“The seemingly imperceptible markers of the maturing relationship are going to add up and count for something over the longer term,” Jayakumar said.
