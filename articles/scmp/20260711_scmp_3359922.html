Philippines gets economic upgrade, yet many Filipinos feel it’s no ‘big deal’

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 952 words | 2026-07-11

The World Bank has upgraded the Philippines to an upper-middle-income country, but the classification means little to many Filipinos grappling with rising cost of living, mounting debt and stagnant wages.

Manila resident Ann Michelle Federez-Abato, 35, said her entire salary went towards her parents’ mortgage, while she and her two-year-old daughter subsisted on a monthly remittance of 20,000 pesos (US$325) sent home by her husband, who works as a production officer at a factory abroad.

Much of the public high-school teacher’s money is spent on bills, utilities, groceries and transport, a situation she described as “not really financially comforting, but more of surviving” in Metro Manila – especially with electricity costs up 10 per cent and national inflation at 6.4 per cent.

“I laughed actually. Like, for real? Where did the data come from?” Federez-Abato said of the July 1 announcement that her country’s gross national income (GNI) per capita reached US$4,850 in 2025, surpassing the US$4,636 threshold for upper-middle-income economies.

“Filipinos are already at a huge loss with inflation, coupled with their daily expenses and with many sinking further into debt just to get by,” she said, reflecting on a milestone that took nearly 40 years to reach but did not match the nation’s economic reality.

For Hans Bautista, executive director of Inklusibo, a non-profit research centre advocating for the country’s poor, the World Bank’s upgrade “should not be considered a big deal” when millions of Filipinos are still struggling with “meagre wages, attacks on livelihoods and inaccessible basic necessities”.

“If we look at the bigger picture, the Philippines ranks 130 out of 201 countries, and is far from the global GNI average,” he said, adding that the World Bank used the classification to determine countries’ eligibility for funding.

Bautista said the country’s inequality gap remained wide, with wealth distribution “highly skewed”.

Citing the IBON Foundation, Bautista said 44.1 per cent of Filipinos fell within the middle-income bracket, while middle- and low-income earners together made up 41.4 per cent of the population. Just 0.2 per cent hold the majority of the country’s wealth.

He called on the government to rethink how it measured income, including how it calculated food and non-food expenses, urging officials to rely on actual data rather than “crude estimates and minimum values”. Food variety and “dignified nutritional standards” should factor into any assessment of consumption patterns, he added.

Progress, not the destination

The disconnect between the World Bank’s upgrade and the strain felt by ordinary Filipinos comes down to what the classification actually measures: average national income, not whether households feel richer, more secure or closer to the middle class.

“It indicates that the country’s average income level has risen enough to meet the World Bank’s threshold for upper-middle-income economies,” said Ruben Carlo Asuncion, chief economist of the Union Bank of the Philippines.

The new label reflected years of sustained growth, increased productive capacity and improved economic resilience, he said, signalling that the Philippines was “gradually moving up the development ladder and is better positioned to attract investment and create more economic opportunities”.

But the country-level measure “does not mean that most Filipinos are now middle class, nor does it guarantee” that growth had benefited households, regions and sectors evenly, Asuncion said.

“Upper-middle-income status is not our destination. It marks another stage in our development journey,” Arsenio Balisacan, secretary of the Department of Economy, Planning and Development, told reporters on Monday.

“Development is not measured by average income alone. It’s measured by whether growth creates quality jobs, increases productivity, expands opportunities, reduces poverty and improves the quality of life of our people.”

John Paolo Rivera, a senior research fellow at the Philippine Institute for Development Studies, called the new status “an important milestone that reflects the country’s long-term economic progress”.

“It recognises that the Philippine economy has grown over time and that average incomes have improved,” Rivera said, adding that the next challenge was ensuring the gains of that growth were shared more widely through better jobs, higher productivity and improved public services.

Rivera argued the World Bank classification and the lived experience of many Filipinos were not necessarily at odds, and that the “milestone should be viewed as an opportunity to accelerate reforms that make growth more inclusive”.

“In fact, macroeconomic stability and household welfare are complementary perspectives in understanding the Philippine economy. The classification is based on average national income, while household welfare depends on purchasing power, employment and the cost of living,” he said.

Yet even middle-class households that keep the economy humming “are only a few shocks away from financial stress”, Rivera warned, given the burden of housing, education, transport, health and debt obligations.

“High inflation and weak purchasing power mean that many families are still vulnerable to falling back into poverty after a major shock,” he said. “They may not be poor, but they are exposed.”

Rivera urged the government to strengthen such households’ purchasing power and resilience through quality jobs and affordable public services, to help the country hold on to its new status.

Asuncion said the milestone reflected gains built up over many years, but warned that inflation, governance concerns and softer investor sentiment could weigh on growth and change how households and businesses viewed the economy in the near term.

“These challenges remind us that income classification upgrades and current economic conditions are not always the same thing,” he said.

While the upgrade marked “an achievement worth acknowledging”, he said it should be seen as “a milestone rather than a destination”.

“The real challenge is ensuring that stronger economic performance translates into tangible improvements in the lives of ordinary Filipinos.”
