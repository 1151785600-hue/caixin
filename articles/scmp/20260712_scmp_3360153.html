Philippine defence chief says China talks ‘not possible’ as anti-spy laws loom

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 835 words | 2026-07-12

For a man sanctioned by Beijing, barred from visiting China and accused of damaging bilateral ties, Gilberto Teodoro Jnr sounded remarkably composed.

Composed, that is, until the conversation turned to the South China Sea – the very subject that landed him in hot water in the first place.

In an interview this week, the Philippines’ defence chief said that Manila would keep only limited military communication channels open with Beijing, while ruling out broader engagement unless China changed its conduct around Scarborough Shoal and other contested areas.

“Carefully controlled communications are necessary, but negotiations and other activities are not possible, unfortunately, because of the lack of good faith on their part and a lack of respect for our territorial integrity,” Teodoro told reporters after he and military chiefs had met President Ferdinand Marcos Jnr for a command conference late on Tuesday.

His remarks followed earlier reports that the Philippine defence and armed forces attache in Beijing, Navy Captain Arnold Fortin, and his staff had been recalled to Manila for “consultations”. Teodoro told local news site Rappler the move was part of a “process of re-establishing and readjusting” postings under a “new engagement framework”.

Pressed on what could soften his South China Sea position, Teodoro told This Week in Asia that China should stop “whatever they’re doing right now” and “get out of where they are”.

He referred to Bajo de Masinloc, Manila’s name for Scarborough Shoal, noting it lies barely “100 or so nautical miles” (185km) from Philippine shores but roughly 900km from Hainan, China’s southernmost island province.

“What is it to them to get out of our EEZ?” he asked, referring to the Philippines’ exclusive economic zone, which extends about 200 nautical miles (370km) from a country’s coast under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea.

China’s presence in the zone served no purpose, he argued, beyond strategic appetite: “They need it for expansionism … it’s a creeping strategy.”

Blacklisted by Beijing

Beijing sanctioned Teodoro and his family on June 11, accusing him of making “erroneous remarks” that undermined its interests and damaged bilateral ties.

He, his wife and child are now barred from entering mainland China, Hong Kong or Macau and Chinese entities are prohibited from having any dealings with them.

It followed remarks the Philippine defence secretary made in May on the sidelines of Singapore’s Shangri-La Dialogue security summit, where he told Reuters that his country was “under severe threat territory-wise and politically too by China”, further accusing Beijing of failing to show genuine good faith on a sustained basis.

The sanctions have injected an awkward personal complication into Teodoro’s, and China’s, diplomatic calendar. Later this year, the Philippine defence chief is set to host the 13th Asean Defence Ministers’ Meeting-Plus in Manila, gathering counterparts from Australia, India, Japan, New Zealand, South Korea, Russia, the US – and China.

Asked by This Week in Asia whether he would still invite Beijing’s Defence Minister Dong Jun, Teodoro demurred.

“I don’t know the answer to that. That is something that I need to discuss with my staff and the rest of the government. I cannot answer that,” he said, before adding: “I could [invite him], but it would be very awkward for me to do so. Why? They sanctioned me and then I invite them.”

Manila’s foreign ministry has called the sanctions “an unfriendly act”, and Marcos, during an official visit to Canada earlier this month, labelled Beijing’s move “unhelpful” and said it raised tensions “rather than lessen” them – even as he signalled his openness to eventually resetting ties.

The diplomatic friction now appears to be bleeding into domestic security policy.

Teodoro revealed on Tuesday that he and military officials had asked Marcos to fast-track legislation that would update the Philippines’ 85-year-old Anti-Espionage Act and introduce new laws against “foreign interference and malign influence”.

“Many are escaping punishment because foreign spies are not covered specifically by our criminal law” during peacetime, he said, claiming that this loophole had allowed “disruptions and illegal operations” to continue unchecked.

Asked whether Marcos had committed to urgently certifying the legislation, Teodoro said: “I believe that he mentioned it, so we can take it as that he agreed.”

The legislative push follows a string of Philippine investigations into suspected espionage, from surveillance near sensitive military sites to monitoring of US Navy movements at Subic Bay and alleged attempts to recruit Filipino defence workers to leak details of Manila’s South China Sea resupply missions.

China has rejected the allegations made against it as politically motivated smears.

Though none of the proposed legislation specifically names China, Teodoro said he had told Marcos that officials were examining what he characterised as China-linked interference, including activities associated with Beijing’s United Front Work Department, the body responsible for coordinating domestic and overseas influence operations.
