Philippines’ impeachment trial: court ruling heightens stakes for VP Sara

SCMP | FREE | 1153 words | 2026-07-23

Philippine Vice-President Sara Duterte-Carpio suffered a serious setback in her impeachment trial this week after the Senate allowed prosecutors to examine key financial records in an unexplained wealth case against her.

Analysts said the Senate impeachment court’s ruling could give prosecutors a clearer route to show she amassed ill-gotten wealth and pull down her popularity ratings in time for the 2028 presidential election, thus raising the stakes for Duterte-Carpio as she fights not only to keep her office but also to avoid being barred from the race.

Senate impeachment court presiding officer Francis “Chiz” Escudero ruled on Monday that the records could be opened in connection with one of the four articles of impeachment against Duterte-Carpio, whose trial has become a test of both her political survival and the limits of accountability for one of the Philippines’ most powerful families.

She was impeached two months ago, on May 11, for the second time by the lower chamber on four counts: unexplained wealth, bribery, misuse of confidential funds and grave threats.

Wednesday marked the ninth day of her trial and the last day for the prosecution to present evidence that Duterte-Carpio had issued grave threats against President Ferdinand Marcos Jnr, his wife Liza, and cousin Martin Romualdez, the former House speaker.

Duterte-Carpio said during a Facebook live stream on November 23, 2024, that she had already hired a hitman to assassinate the three of them in the event she was killed.

According to Escudero, a conviction on any one of the four counts would be enough to remove Duterte-Carpio from office, but voting on all four would take place only after the court had heard each one.

On Wednesday, six senator-judges allied with her submitted written objections to the subpoena of her records.

Among those who objected was Senator Imee Marcos, the president’s sister, who said she was “not against” opening the financial records but that “these should be strictly limited to her current term as vice-president”, which began in 2022.

Escudero had ruled that previous legal precedents showed that all of Duterte-Carpio’s records, starting from when she was vice-mayor of Davao City in 2007 and later mayor, could be subject to scrutiny by the House of Representatives prosecution team.

However, he said this would only be “for purposes of determining his [or her] capacity for future property acquisitions during his [or her] tenure as a public officer”.

He said the financial records would be compared with the Statements of Assets, Liabilities and Net Worth (SALNs) that Duterte-Carpio signed and filed each year, and used as a baseline to determine her wealth at the start of her term as vice-president.

But the records “will not be used to allege additional independent impeachable offences”, Escudero noted.

He also ruled that the dollar accounts jointly owned by Duterte-Carpio and her husband could not be scrutinised, nor could the financial records of two corporations the prosecution wanted to examine, but said prosecutors could appeal against the decision if they could show enough evidence to examine them.

Litigation lawyer and human rights advocate Dino Singson de Leon called this “a very important development” given the allegation that Duterte-Carpio had accumulated ill-gotten wealth.

“Since we will presumably now see her bank transactions, it will expose the amount of money that went into her personal accounts, and [this] should be reconciled with her legitimate income, her income tax returns and SALNs,” he told This Week in Asia.

Duterte-Carpio’s lawyers have not indicated whether they would appeal against the ruling.

De Leon said the vice-president “may resort to a petition for certiorari before the Supreme Court and allege grave abuse of discretion on the part of the impeachment court”, but was unlikely to succeed as the ruling was “anchored upon established laws and jurisprudence as applied in the facts of the case”.

“I do not believe that any attempt to go to the Supreme Court to question the order […] will succeed.”

In the last impeachment trial conducted by the Senate, financial records were key to convicting then Supreme Court chief justice Renato Corona in 2012.

Political risk analyst Ronald Llamas earlier told This Week in Asia that outright conviction was not necessarily the goal in her impeachment trial.

Rather, it was to pull down her numbers in the same manner that then vice-president Jejomar Binay’s ratings were affected in the 2016 presidential election by evidence he had amassed unexplained wealth, he said.

Binay was never impeached but a Senate Blue Ribbon Committee probe examined his financial records.

On Wednesday, House prosecutors wrapped up their case on the charge of grave threats with the testimony of National Bureau of Investigation (NBI) chief Melvin Matibag.

Matibag said he was 100 per cent sure Duterte-Carpio “already hired someone to kill President Bongbong Marcos” and claimed the NBI had identified “persons of interest” as her hired killer but refused to name the suspect.

The NBI chief also surprised the defence by playing a video clip showing Duterte-Carpio punching a sheriff in 2011 while she was vice-mayor.

“I think the intent of showing this is to demonstrate another reason why this is not a joke and was not and cannot be treated lightly by the NBI: it is because of the fact that Sara Duterte has the propensity and the track record of resorting to violence,” De Leon said.

But Duterte-Carpio’s lawyers insisted that her remarks about a hired killer were merely part of free speech.

Matibag also read portions of an unsigned affidavit from self-confessed Davao Death Squad hitman Arturo Lascanas that claimed the vice-president was “one of the architects of Oplan Tokhang”, the name of former president Rodrigo Duterte’s bloody anti-drug war.

Matibag said he presented the affidavit to establish Duterte-Carpio’s “patterns of violence”.

The defence objected, saying it was “highly improper” for the impeachment court to allow Matibag to read an affidavit he did not write.

Instead of striking this portion from the court records, Escudero merely ruled that Matibag should stop reading from the Lascanas affidavit.

Duterte-Carpio’s lawyers told local media they were unsure whether they would ask her to testify in court.

She was seen entering the Senate premises only once, on July 7, the second day of the trial, but did not appear in court.

Instead, she gave a press conference where she said she was “bloodied but unbowed”.

She is currently on a private trip to Qatar, the United Kingdom and the Netherlands, where her father is being held while awaiting his own trial before the International Criminal Court (ICC) on charges of crimes against humanity over his bloody war on drugs.

Lascanas is a key witness against the elder Duterte and has been granted a “limited immunity” deal by the ICC, meaning his testimony will not be used to prosecute him.
