China’s Xi Jinping arrives in a North Korea armed with advanced warships and AI drones

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1045 words | 2026-07-12

When Chinese President Xi Jinping arrived in Pyongyang on Monday, he was entering a country undergoing notable development in both its nuclear programme and conventional combat capacity.

North Korea has been aided in these developments by Russian military technology and economic aid, received in exchange for sending troops and equipment to support Moscow’s war in Ukraine. These advances could help Pyongyang threaten the balance of power against South Korea and the United States.

Surface and underwater fleet

The construction of Pyongyang’s Choe Hyon-class guided-missile destroyer, which has a displacement of up to 5,000 tonnes, was first unveiled in December 2024. It is North Korea’s largest-ever surface combatant.

Its first ship in the class, the Choe Hyon, was launched in April last year, while the second, the Kang Kon, was relaunched the following June after it capsized during its launch ceremony a month earlier. The two vessels are undergoing sea trials before being delivered to the North Korean navy.

Despite wider scepticism about the extent of damage to the Kang Kon during the incident, North Korean leader Kim Jong-un attended the sea trial of the vessel, state media reported last week.

It said the most important task in the new five-year national defence development policy was the “rapid strengthening of naval forces to play a part in nuclear war deterrence”.

Kim earlier called for two additional Choe Hyon-class destroyers to be deployed every year. The third vessel is reportedly under construction at the Nampo Shipyard near Pyongyang and scheduled to be launched by October.

The Choe Hyon-class destroyers are North Korea’s first naval vessels equipped with phased-array radar and vertical launch systems or VLS, possibly with technical support from Russia.

North Korean state media said the destroyer could launch various types of ballistic and cruise missiles for anti-aircraft, anti-ship and anti-submarine missions, and that some had hypersonic capabilities and potential nuclear armament.

North Korea is also ramping up its underwater fleet, regarded as one of the largest in the world in terms of numbers, and building its first nuclear-powered submarine, following Kim’s announcement in 2021 that the project had moved beyond the design and research phase.

Despite doubts around Pyongyang’s technological capabilities, the plan was realised in December when Kim visited the shipyard that was reportedly constructing a “nuclear-powered strategic guided-missile submarine” with a displacement of 8,700 tonnes.

Nuclear-powered submarines utilise on-board reactors, which allow a submarine to operate underwater at higher speeds for longer periods than conventional submarines, possibly complicating regional missile defence for the US and its allies.

Drones take flight

As North Korean troops continue to engage in the Ukraine war, its military has gained significant combat experience in unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) warfare, as it builds its own military drone fleet.

Going beyond its older reconnaissance models, North Korea’s new drone fleet incorporates new features for strategic surveillance UAVs, AI-integrated suicide drones and loitering munitions.

In 2023, North Korea unveiled the Saetbyol-4 and Saetbyol-9 strategic surveillance drones, modelled after the US RQ-4 Global Hawk and MQ-9 Reaper, respectively, and designed for deep-penetration intelligence gathering.

North Korea has also tested and showcased its Kumsong-series tactical attack drones, which debuted at the country’s 2024 weaponry exhibition. These low-cost loitering munitions are built for mass production and are produced in one of two shapes: a cross-shaped structure with X-shaped wings at the front and back; and a manta ray-shaped structure.

These shapes suggested possible imitation of foreign models, such as Russia’s Lancet-3 and Israel’s Harop suicide drone, respectively.

According to North Korean media and some external analysis, the drones have artificial intelligence (AI) technology capable of autonomous target detection and flight operations, with likely technical support from Russia. Reports indicate Pyongyang has sent workers and experts to Russian drone production facilities, such as those building Iranian Shahed drones, to rapidly scale their own capacities.

Missiles and nuclear programme

North Korea often makes announcements about its nuclear weapons programme around major diplomatic engagements with China.

Only a day before his trip to Beijing and summit with Xi in September, Kim visited a factory producing nuclear missile materials, including a new solid-fuel engine to be used for the Hwasong-19 and the new “next-generation” Hwasong-20 intercontinental ballistic missile.

Shortly after returning from that trip, Kim oversaw a test of a solid-fuel missile engine, which is more difficult to detect and more survivable than liquid-fuel weapons.

Last week, just a day before Xi’s visit to North Korea was announced, North Korean state media revealed that Kim had inspected a new plant that produces weapons-grade nuclear material.

Will China or Russia help?

Analysts said Xi’s visit could indirectly advance North Korea’s military build-up as Pyongyang strengthened diplomatic ties with Beijing and Moscow.

Kang Jun-young, a professor of Chinese studies at Hankuk University of Foreign Studies in Seoul, said Pyongyang’s attempt to strengthen relations with China was akin to “managed dependency”, as North Korea sought to “maintain its autonomy through close ties with Russia and the pursuit of dialogue with the US”.

He said restored North Korea-China relations could serve as a “pillar” for North Korea’s development of surface ships and nuclear submarines, with Russia as the technological supporter and China a “diplomatic and economic safety net”.

“These changes are highly likely to accelerate North Korea’s weapons development indirectly rather than through direct technology transfer,” Kang said.

“The recovery of trade between North Korea and China has improved conditions for procuring parts, materials and funding, which could enhance the sustainability of naval modernisation, though it is difficult to guarantee a qualitative leap.”

Lim Eul-chul, a professor at the Institute for Far Eastern Studies at Kyungnam University, said China now had to deal with a North Korea whose military capabilities had been “further strengthened”, potentially pushing Beijing to use Pyongyang to contain both US and Japanese military advancements.

“For China, effectively and properly countering the militarisation of not only the US but also Japan is of paramount importance,” Lim said.

“In this regard, it seems they have concluded that strategically utilising North Korea’s military capabilities would be more beneficial.”
