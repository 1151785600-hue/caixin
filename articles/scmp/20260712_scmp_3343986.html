How the next China shock is shaping hearts and minds

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 919 words | 2026-07-12

For decades, China’s role in the global economy was easy to define. It made things cheaply and at astonishing scale. “Made in China” became shorthand for industrial capacity. It was often contentious, sometimes admired, sometimes feared.

In the years after China joined the World Trade Organization, its firms were deeply embedded in global supply chains, mostly at the lower end of the value chain. They produced for others. Western and Japanese companies controlled the premium segments and brand recognition, while Chinese companies handled the unglamorous manufacturing work.

That division is now eroding. In smartphones, electric vehicles (EVs) and digital services, Chinese firms are climbing the value chain, competing with Western companies and, in many sectors, leading globally. But the next China shock may take a different form.

Call it China Shock 2.0 or 3.0, depending on how you count them, but China is no longer just manufacturing the world’s products. It is increasingly manufacturing the world’s preferences, too. Especially among younger consumers, Chinese brands are shaping tastes, aesthetics and the global image of China.

The “Chinamaxxing” trend, in which influencers showcase Chinese wellness and lifestyle as symbols of modern cool, is one visible sign. The global enthusiasm around the video game Black Myth: Wukong, the popularity of Labubu, the rise of micro dramas and the global spread of Chinese products such as boba tea chains all reflect a broader trend. The rise of “Created in China” across consumer products and cultural exports may, in the long term, prove more consequential than China’s advances in green tech or artificial intelligence.

Geopolitically, this shift matters. Competition between China and the West is no longer confined to trade and technology. It is increasingly playing out in the realm of cultural imagination. If tariffs and export controls are tools of hard power rivalry, brands are tools of soft power. The question is how to compete with China in the latter arena.

Brands, after all, are stories. China is finally learning how to tell them well. This is no overnight miracle, but the product of structural forces decades in the making.

First, it boils down to a familiar story of Chinese efficiency. China’s strength is no longer primarily about low labour costs. Wages have risen substantially. Its efficiency advantage today lies in supply chain depth and industrial coordination. No other economy combines manufacturing density, logistics efficiency, a deep engineering and skilled labour force and vast digital platform distribution at a comparable scale.

What began as an assembly hub has evolved into an innovation ecosystem built for rapid iteration and commercialisation. Products are designed, prototyped, launched, scaled, refined and relaunched at remarkable speed. The same dynamic now drives tech products and consumer brands.

If supply chains are the skeleton of Created in China, digital commerce is its nervous system. China’s consumer economy grew through online platforms rather than traditional storefronts. The dominance of e-commerce giants, widely used digital payments and tightly integrated logistics allowed China to leapfrog the retail evolution. In China’s deeply digital retail culture, brand building runs through live streaming, algorithms, influencer ecosystems and constant engagement.

The integration of data and manufacturing further compresses the brand cycle. Real-time consumer feedback goes directly into design and production decisions. A product can launch, go viral, flop, relaunch and scale nationally within weeks. Chinese firms have mastered this hyper-responsive form of capitalism. It helps explain how platforms like Shein and Temu price aggressively while adapting quickly to shifting demand.

Created in China is also supercharged by generational shifts among entrepreneurs and consumers. A new generation of Chinese start-up founders has emerged that is digitally native, globally aware and attuned to cultural nuance. Many have studied abroad or worked in multinational firms before launching their own businesses.

On the demand side, younger Chinese consumers approach brands differently from their parents. For earlier generations, foreign brands symbolised quality and status. That hierarchy is no longer apparent. Many domestic brands are more digitally integrated, more innovative in design and more responsive to local tastes. The deeper shift is psychological confidence. A society that once consumed foreign modernity now feels capable of producing its own.

This is where Created in China intersects with soft power. China’s official efforts to enhance soft power have produced mixed results. Consumer brands, however, are intimate carriers of national image. When global consumers download Chinese apps, watch Chinese micro dramas or drive Chinese EVs, they engage with contemporary Chinese creativity.

For global firms, this presents a different kind of challenge. Manufacturing can be reshored. Supply chains can be diversified. Cultural resonance cannot be export controlled.

Still, the rise of Created in China is not guaranteed. Brands require trust. Geopolitical suspicion, regulatory barriers and security concerns can constrain Chinese firms abroad. Some reactions may be knee-jerk, but others reflect legitimate concerns about data governance, privacy, intellectual property protection and regulatory transparency.

At home, intense competition breeds fragility. China’s price wars are relentless and often destructive. Many brands rise quickly and fade just as fast. Sustaining global prestige demands long-term investment in quality, differentiation and innovation.

Brands are also judged by sustainability, corporate governance practices and social responsibility. Chinese firms aspiring to global recognition may face hard questions regarding environmental and labour standards.

Still, the next China shock matters because it is not just an economic story. It may signal a new phase in China’s global positioning, defined as much by soft power as by hard power. That story is still being written.
