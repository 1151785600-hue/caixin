Why the Philippines is losing out in Southeast Asia’s investment boom

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 941 words | 2026-07-10

The Philippines still lags behind its neighbours in attracting foreign direct investments (FDI) after its inflows remained stagnant in 2025, even as a financing influx swept the region.

This has cast the spotlight on a corruption scandal that has roiled the country and dented investor confidence to some extent, on top of long-standing systemic issues, analysts say.

Manila ranked sixth in Southeast Asia after capturing just US$9 billion of the US$244 billion capital boom last year, according to the UN Conference on Trade and Development’s (UNCTAD) 2026 World Investment Report.

Southeast Asia overtook East Asia as the largest recipient subregion in “Developing Asia”, the report said, after total FDI inflows grew by 10 per cent.

Investment in Southeast Asia centred on high-value sectors such as semiconductors, electronics, communications and renewable energy. The region’s total inflows grew to US$244.17 billion in 2025 from US$222.50 billion the previous year.

Singapore continued to receive the largest amount of FDI at US$150.90 billion, increasing its inflow by 11 per cent in 2024, while Indonesia retained its position as the second-largest recipient in the region at US$21.44 billion, despite a 13 per cent drop from the previous year.

Vietnam followed at US$20.35 billion, while Thailand and Malaysia received US$19.10 billion and US$15.39 billion respectively.

In 2025, the Philippines was embroiled in a billion-peso saga involving anomalous flood-control infrastructure projects, sparking public outrage and a series of investigations and agency shake-ups.

“Political noise since 2025 amid the anomalous flood-control projects … kept some investors on the sidelines or in a wait-and-see mode until more priority measures, especially those that further improve governance standards, are passed to help improve investor confidence,” Michael Ricafort, chief economist at Rizal Commercial Banking Corporation, told This Week in Asia.

However, the corruption issues were merely the tail end of persistent structural constraints that “dampen its competitiveness for large-scale foreign investments”, said Ruben Carlo Asuncion, chief economist of the Union Bank of the Philippines.

“These include relatively high power costs, infrastructure and logistics gaps, regulatory complexity, and the slow pace of project implementation,” Asuncion said. “While recent investment liberalisation measures are steps in the right direction, investors typically require time and policy consistency before deploying significant capital.”

Ricafort agreed. Manila, he said, needed to boost investor confidence by implementing reforms to improve the economy, revamp governance standards and raise “investment commitments to actual FDIs”. Offering STEM and other technical-vocational courses that match talent requirements could also help attract more FDI in hi-tech sectors, he noted.

Losing out

Despite several promising opportunities, the Philippines continues to lose out to its neighbours over scale and stronger integration into global supply chains.

“Many Asean economies offer either greater scale or stronger integration into global supply chains,” Asuncion said. “Indonesia benefits from its large domestic market and strategic mineral resources, while Vietnam has successfully positioned itself as a manufacturing hub amid supply chain diversification.”

Despite the Philippines’ strengths in services and consumption-driven growth, “it remains less integrated into regional manufacturing and export ecosystems than some of its peers”, he added.

The country’s overall investment slump takes place against the backdrop of major hub projects, including the Luzon Economic Corridor – a trilateral initiative of the United States, the Philippines and Japan – and the 1,619-hectare Pax Silica centre at the New Clark City in Tarlac province for AI and other critical supply chains.

Ricafort believes these initiatives could attract more inflows from the US and other like-minded countries, including new frontier investments for AI and other hi-tech industries in the next two years, as well as rare earths, electric vehicles, and semiconductors.

“[We can expect] FDIs from other like-minded or allied countries, as well as for competitors and suppliers for these hi-tech industries that would also locate in the Luzon Economic Corridor,” Ricafort said.

“This would create more employment and other business or economic opportunities, especially in nearby localities during the construction and operating phases.”

On Tuesday, Finance Secretary Frederick Go announced that Taiwan’s Foxconn – known for manufacturing Apple iPhones – would anchor the US-backed Pax Silica hub.

Asuncion said the Philippines’ close ties with the US and other partners could help create opportunities in sectors such as electronics, semiconductors, digital infrastructure and renewable energy.

“Ultimately, competitiveness matters more than geopolitical alignment,” he said, noting that while geopolitical concerns might be “increasingly part of the investment equation, [they are] not the main driver of FDI decisions”.

Ricafort is optimistic that Pax Silica will “open more doors of opportunities for the Philippines” to play a bigger part in global supply chains.

“[This] would attract more FDIs … including their respective suppliers and even competitors to locate in the country, thereby creating more jobs and other economic opportunities, especially in New Clark City and the Luzon Economic Corridor,” he said.

The hub could also boost the Philippines’ competitive advantage at the higher end of global supply chains, such as in hi-tech industries “that require highly skilled professionals, [thereby] transcending low-cost and low-skilled labour offered by other Asean countries such as Vietnam, as a source of differentiation”, he added.

In that vein, analysts say the Philippines should continue improving the ease of doing business by removing bureaucratic hurdles, lowering costs and improving infrastructure.

“The priority should be improving overall competitiveness by lowering the cost of doing business, reducing regulatory friction, accelerating infrastructure development, and ensuring reliable and affordable energy,” Asuncion said.

“Sector-wise, the country should deepen its position in electronics and semiconductors, digital services, data centres, renewable energy, logistics, healthcare, and higher-value manufacturing. Sustained reforms and consistent policy execution will be critical to attracting long-term investments.”
