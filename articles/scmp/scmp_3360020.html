How Europe’s rush for Chinese air conditioners exposes the gap in Brussels’ trade policy

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 824 words | 2026-07-09

A heatwave is driving unprecedented demand for Chinese air conditioners in Europe, just as Brussels seeks to narrow its record trade deficit with China through new restrictions.

Observers said this exposed a glaring contradiction between public demand and political rhetoric, adding that the European Union was shifting blame for the trade deficit instead of addressing its own structural shortcomings.

The record-breaking heatwave has disrupted transit infrastructure, triggered droughts and led to thousands of heat-related excess deaths across the continent, forcing countries such as France and the Netherlands to issue a run of red alerts.

Governments of European countries are struggling to cope with the extreme weather, facing sharp criticism over poor emergency management and underinvestment in public services and climate policy.

Meanwhile, Europeans are flocking to buy Chinese air conditioners, leading to a dramatic surge in demand for certain portable units, even as Brussels toughens its trade policy towards Beijing.

Ding Chun, director of the Centre for European Studies at Fudan University, highlighted the disconnect between Brussels’ leadership and ordinary citizens, noting that the public naturally focused on cost-of-living realities and daily life.

“European leadership is heavily focused on its own strategic goals and political narrative-building, and needs a narrative to foster internal cohesion and justify policies like industrial protection,” Ding said, pointing to surging air-conditioner sales as proof of the gap.

Across Europe, people are queuing up for Chinese air conditioners, with some even driving between cities just to secure a unit, calling the appliances “rarer than Pokemon cards”. There are also websites devoted to tracking inventory in real time.

Some portable units are especially popular because their minimal installation requirements bypass strict urban historic preservation codes forbidding alterations – such as drilling – to outer walls.

Amid the buying frenzy, Chinese actor Liu Ye went viral on social media after buying his wife an air conditioner for their home in Nice, France, for their 17th anniversary.

Earlier this month, the European Union introduced a new tax on small packages, as a surge in low-value imports – 90 per cent of which come from China, according to Brussels – had put local retailers at an “unfair disadvantage”, said European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen.

Cui Hongjian, a former diplomat and a research fellow at Beijing Foreign Studies University’s Country and Area Studies Academy, said Europe’s political narrative, often focused on overcapacity, unfair competition and “de-risking”, had become deeply entrenched, distorting natural market relations between Beijing and Brussels.

This could widen the gap between politicians’ agendas and the needs of ordinary citizens, Cui said, adding that this explained why far-right political forces had gained momentum and expanded rapidly in recent years.

“Traditional European elites, including politicians from mainstream parties, have severely detached themselves from the needs of ordinary people to preserve their specific political agendas and narratives,” the scholar said.

“Disappointed by mainstream politics under these circumstances, citizens are left with no choice but to start looking for new representatives.”

The record trade imbalance between Beijing and Brussels has fuelled anxieties about the bloc’s over-reliance on China to sustain its own industrial backbone.

EU leaders met last month to address concerns over “China Shock 2.0” – anxieties about China’s dominance in electric vehicles, batteries and solar power. To avoid a trade war over the EU’s €360 billion (US$411 billion) deficit with China, both sides have agreed to three months of talks.

The imbalance continues to rise. It reached €98 billion (US$112 billion) in the first quarter of this year, marking its highest level since 2022, with electrical equipment and machinery ranking among the top imports.

Ding said the underlying issue was Europe’s lagging competitiveness and innovation in manufacturing, a decline that coincided with China’s surging advantages in cost, scale and supply chains.

He said the bloc faced a multi-front squeeze: balancing green leadership against Chinese market competition, while navigating domestic cost-of-living crises and US geopolitical pressure.

Cui stressed that instead of looking inward to address its lagging competitiveness, Brussels had singled China out as a primary target and ignored its own trade surpluses with the US and other economies.

He said the growing China-EU trade deficit stemmed mainly from the diversion of Chinese goods from the US market to Europe in the wake of unilateral American sanctions and tariffs, but Brussels was scapegoating China for economic problems that were triggered by Washington.

“Essentially, grievances that should be blamed on the US are being pinned on China instead, creating the appearance of an ever-intensifying economic and trade competition between the two sides,” Cui said.

He suggested that rather than resisting, Europe should admit that China was now the leader in several industries and find ways to cooperate while showing a willingness to adopt best practices from Beijing.

“This psychological hurdle may be difficult for Europe to overcome at the moment; it is a barrier they have to clear moving forward,” Cui said.
