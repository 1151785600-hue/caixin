14-nation South China Sea statement is an expansionist overreach

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 897 words | 2026-07-16

On July 12, a coalition of 14 nations – the United States, Philippines, Australia, Canada, Britain, Japan, New Zealand, and seven European states including Germany, Italy and the Baltic nations – issued a statement marking the 10th anniversary of the South China Sea ruling at The Hague in 2016.

The document recycled familiar arguments, urging compliance with the ruling and framing it as a cornerstone of the rules-based international order. But beneath it lies a fundamental disconnect from international law, historical reality and the contemporary political environment in Southeast Asia.

To understand why the 2016 arbitral award remains a dead letter, one must return to the foundational principles of international law – specifically, the limits of the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (Unclos). The South China Sea dispute is, at its core, a matter of territorial sovereignty over maritime features and subsequent maritime delimitation. Unclos, by its text and design, does not regulate sovereignty over land territory.

Furthermore, international law respects the principle of state consent. As early as 2006, China invoked Article 298 of Unclos, explicitly filing a declaration that excluded disputes concerning territorial sovereignty and maritime delimitation from compulsory arbitration. When the Philippines unilaterally initiated arbitration in 2013, it bypassed agreed-upon bilateral mechanisms. China has argued that the resulting tribunal overreached its mandate, deliberately misinterpreting the true scope of the dispute to manufacture jurisdiction where none existed.

Beijing maintains that its sovereignty in the region is rooted in centuries of historical practice dating back to the Western Han dynasty and continues to assert a claim of “historic rights”, although the 2016 arbitral tribunal found that there was no legal basis for China to claim historic rights to resources in areas falling within its “nine-dash line”. By classifying ancient, self-sustaining geographical features as mere rocks incapable of generating economic zones, the tribunal chose to distort geographical realities.

The composition of the group of nations behind the recent statement exposes the geopolitical nature of the exercise. Aside from the Philippines, not a single signatory is a littoral state of the South China Sea. The roster is dominated by the “Five Eyes” intelligence alliance, a handful of non-littoral European nations, and Japan. What qualifies states located in the North Atlantic or Baltic Sea to dictate maritime boundaries in Southeast Asia? The reality is that this statement has little to do with upholding international law.

Instead, the 2016 award is being weaponised as a political tool by external powers seeking to maintain their historical hegemony and advance a strategy of containment against China’s development.

By amplifying a ruling that China sees as invalid, these powers attempt to drive a wedge between China and its neighbours, transforming the South China Sea into a front for great-power rivalry. This strategy seeks to disrupt negotiations for a regional code of conduct and convert Asia into a geopolitical powder keg.

This expansionist overreach is further dismantled by archival research from Chinese scholars who argue that under the 1898 Treaty of Paris, the Philippines’ legal northern boundary terminates at the 20th parallel. Consequently, Manila’s maritime delimitation talks with Tokyo over waters further north, including the Batanes islands, constitute a violation of the very colonial treaties that established its modern statehood.

The patronising approach embodied in the 14-nation statement relies on an outdated colonial mindset, assuming that regional security must be managed by external arbiters rather than the sovereign nations that live along these waters.

The most striking aspect of the 14-nation statement is what – or rather, who – was missing. Conspicuously absent were nine of Asean’s 10 members. If the 2016 award were truly the definitive solution for regional peace, one would expect widespread endorsement from the states most directly affected. Instead, the silence from most members of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations shows the region has moved past the 2016 ruling.

The priorities and sentiments of the region’s policy elites are captured in the State of Southeast Asia 2026 survey by the ISEAS-Yusof Ishak Institute in Singapore. It reveals that regional anxieties have shifted profoundly, with 51.9 per cent of respondents expressing concern over the United States under President Donald Trump’s leadership, surpassing maritime frictions as the region’s primary geopolitical fear.

Furthermore, the survey showed that if forced to choose between the two superpowers, 52 per cent preferred alignment with China over the US, a major shift driven by nations such as Indonesia (with 80.1 per cent preferring a Chinese alignment), Malaysia (68 per cent) and Singapore (66.3 per cent).

Crucially, Asean’s collective stance focuses on the peaceful resolution of disputes through dialogue, maintaining freedom of navigation and respecting international law. The bloc maintains strict neutrality regarding individual sovereignty claims, recognising that third-party legal impositions do not work. Instead, the regional consensus favours the swift conclusion of a legally binding code of conduct negotiated directly between China and Asean.

The path to long-term stability in the South China Sea does not run through Washington, London or Brussels. Ten years of friction have proved that external interference only exacerbates tensions, turning manageable bilateral disputes into dangerous geopolitical flashpoints.

As Asean and China mark the fifth anniversary of their comprehensive strategic partnership, the focus must remain on localised diplomacy. Southeast Asia’s sovereign nations possess the collective wisdom and diplomatic capacity to manage their differences. Any external attempt to use a flawed, ineffective arbitral award to alter historical fact or stall regional integration is an exercise in futility.
