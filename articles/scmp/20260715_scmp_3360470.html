How Yung Kee and other Hong Kong heritage brands innovate to thrive in challenging times

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 781 words | 2026-07-15

From franchising to diversification, Hong Kong’s heritage brands and long-running family businesses are looking for ways to navigate challenging times amid changing consumption patterns and soaring costs.

Second- and third-generation stewards of three local brands – charcoal-roasted goose restaurant Yung Kee, toy manufacturer Kader Holdings and construction group Asia Allied Infrastructure – are using different strategies to adapt to the changing times, they explained recently in an interview organised by Entrepreneurs’ Organisation Hong Kong in connection with its 30th anniversary this year.

Yvonne Kam, the third-generation owner and chief finance officer of Yung Kee, spearheaded the launch of Yung’s Bistro, a more modern take on the restaurant’s revered family recipes for its famous charcoal-roasted goose. The group’s flagship is in Central, while the two Yung’s Bistro outlets are located in K11 Musea in Tsim Sha Tsui and Taikoo Place in Taikoo Shing.

Like many food and drink operators in the city, the group has had several difficult years, Kam said, as tourists reduced their spending while residents travelled north of the border to dine and shop in Shenzhen.

“It’s tough,” she said. “We lost money, to be very honest, but then we quickly pivoted to a way that we are now breaking even.”

Now, Kam is looking into ways to spread the Yung Kee name further.

“In the past 30 to 40 years, people always asked us why Yung Kee is not elsewhere, and it was mainly because the roast goose could not be exported to other countries,” Kam said. The reasons are that only a particular breed found in Guangdong province is used, and import rules around poultry are complex and vary from country to country.

To address this “pain point”, the group is opening a pop-up store at Terminal 1 of Hong Kong International Airport, in a bid to introduce the Yung Kee name to a wider audience.

“The pop-up is going to open in November,” Kam said. “It’s a takeaway counter where we will offer our other signature products,” such as pork sausage and claypot rice.

These products would be easier to replicate in international markets, as ultimately, Kam would like to franchise Yung Kee beyond Hong Kong.

For Ivan Ting, executive director of Kader Holdings, diversification was the way forward as toy manufacturing disappeared from Hong Kong in the decades since the company’s founding in 1948, moving to places such as mainland China and Cambodia.

What we want to do is diversify, knowing that sometimes we’re not the smartest people in the room

Ivan Ting, Kader Holdings

The group has invested in diverse ventures, such as the manufacturing of model railroad toys under the Bachmann brand and the design and manufacturing of plush and educational toys under Tinco.

“We look at what resources we have, and what we try to do is to invest in other people,” he said.

For example, Kader also started a wine-storage cellar because it had space in a factory building in Hong Kong and invested in “a guy that makes bicycle helmets”.

“What we want to do is diversify, knowing that sometimes we’re not the smartest people in the room,” Ting said. “We just have to partner with smarter people, and also look at what resources we have and leverage those.”

Asia Allied, founded nearly 60 years ago as a construction business, has since entered the mainland Chinese and overseas markets, diversifying into property development and investment, security services and property services, among others.

“We always try new businesses,” said Derrick Pang, CEO of Asia Allied Infrastructure. “So we do property development and investment, and we also explored the security guard business because we need security guards in construction sites.”

Pang had also dabbled in proptech and construction tech, he added.

“Obviously, there are things that we lose money from, but you learn from them,” Pang said. “We just make sure that when we lose money, we don’t cause our main business to fail. But we continue to try new things.”

Obviously, there are things that we lose money from, but you learn from them

Derrick Pang, Asia Allied Infrastructure

Maintaining a family business was also about ensuring smooth succession, he added. This meant focusing on training the company’s own staff to be able to step in and manage the company in the future, he added.

That said, exiting a family business also remains an option for local entrepreneurs.

Pang did not rule out the possibility of selling “if the price is right”. While the previous generation might not have considered such a move, “we [already] do some things differently, and we can do some things differently”, he said.
