Reading the Sino-US rivalry: how a quiet literary relationship has shaped views on China

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1521 words | 2026-07-13

As the United States marks the 250th anniversary of its founding, it confronts a new world order dominated by its relationship with China. In this wide-ranging series, we examine the pressure points and possibilities in those ties, from hard tech to soft power. Here, Khushboo Razdan looks at how books shape public perceptions and inspire lifelong connections between the US and China even as their geopolitical rivalry intensifies.

Alexander Boyd was 14 when a book about China altered the course of his life.

Raised in a house overflowing with books in the small Massachusetts town of Acton, Boyd spent hours browsing his parents’ shelves in search of his next read.

One title in particular caught his attention – China Wakes by journalists Nicholas Kristof and Sheryl WuDunn – a book chronicling China’s transformation under former leader Deng Xiaoping’s economic reforms and opening up to the world.

Boyd said the book affected him profoundly.

“The impact of reading it was immediate and shocking,” he recalled. He read it on the school bus and between classes, absorbed by the story of a country undergoing one of the most dramatic transformations in modern history.

“It just made me hungry to know more … to figure out what the hell is going on in China,” Boyd said, observing that “the more you learn, the less you know”.

That curiosity never left Boyd.

He studied Chinese in college, travelled to Beijing as a student, and today, at 30 years old, works in New York as the associate editor at China Books Review, a digital magazine introducing English-language readers to China-related literature.

Boyd is not alone in tracing a lifelong fascination with China back to a single book.

For Canadian-American author and long-time China journalist Robert Delaney, the spark came through martial arts. After taking up kung fu as a teenager, he devoured Mark Salzman’s Iron and Silk, the memoir of an American studying martial arts in China.

“I simply wanted to follow in Salzman’s footsteps,” Delaney said.

That ambition eventually took him to China, where he lived for more than a decade. He later published The Wounded Muse, a thriller set in the run-up to the 2008 Beijing Olympics.

Delaney, 58, is now working on a second, still untitled book that returns to his early kung fu training and years in China.

Along the way, he said works such as Maxine Hong Kingston’s The Woman Warrior deepened his understanding of the lived histories behind modern China.

“I always gravitate to authors who have lived within Chinese culture, and who write from the heart and not the head.”

As the United States marks the 250th anniversary of its founding, its relationship with China is often framed through trade disputes, military competition and geopolitical rivalry. But another history has unfolded more quietly.

For generations, books have been among the first places Americans encountered China – shaping public perceptions as well as inspiring students, scholars, journalists and writers to travel there, study its language and build careers around understanding the country.

Even as the bilateral relationship has grown more contentious, that literary bridge has endured.

From The Good Earth, Pearl Buck’s bestselling 1930s novel of rural Chinese life, to Edgar Snow’s Red Star Over China, which introduced Mao Zedong and Chinese Communists to Western readers, and later Wild Swans, Jung Chang’s sweeping family memoir of 20th century China, these works have shaped perceptions, sparked curiosity, and inspired countless literal and intellectual journeys towards a country that has long fascinated the American imagination.

As US-China ties have shifted from cooperation to strategic competition, and America has turned inward under the “Make America Great Again” movement, the market for China books has widened.

American bestseller trends reveal that readers in the US are polarised yet highly engaged when it comes to books about China.

Politically charged titles such as Peter Schweizer’s Blood Money and US Senator Tom Cotton’s Seven Things You Can’t Say About China became New York Times bestsellers in 2024 and 2025, respectively, tapping into anxieties around US-China competition and national security.

“It’s quite interesting,” Boyd said of the heightened attention, adding that he sometimes joked that it was “heartening to see that American right-wingers are reading so much”.

On a more serious note, he said there “are three or four big things that Americans want to know about China”.

“There’s conspiracy theories, there’s communism, and there’s cookbooks, but there’s increasingly also fiction,” he said. “Chinese fantasy is becoming much more popular.”

This year, Chinese-inspired epic fantasy The Poet Empress topped the April bestseller list for the China Books Review. The story centres on a woman’s struggle to save her family’s village and navigate power, politics and destiny in a richly imagined imperial world.

Among the notable recent releases, Boyd highlighted Adrift in the South, a powerful work following a migrant labourer’s harsh life on factory assembly lines in southern China’s manufacturing hubs and the city of Shenzhen during the country’s economic boom.

According to Boyd, the “only consistent thing” in China-themed books is that the ones built upon “deep personal experience, expertise and of course excellent writing do the best”, even when they offer more complex or mixed views.

In 2023, conspiracy titles like Mao’s America and cookbooks such as Invitation to a Banquet led the charts.

From 2024 onwards, tech-focused books like Breakneck and Apple in China became long-running successes, while stories such as Daughters of the Bamboo Grove – a non-fiction work about identical twin sisters in China who were separated at birth to evade the one-child policy – likewise gained traction.

Yet, books have also served another purpose: helping members of the Chinese diaspora make sense of their own identities while navigating Western perceptions of China.

For Karen Ma, a New York-based writer, film scholar and author raised in Hong Kong and Japan, books were less a gateway to China than a way of finding her own place within it.

Living in Japan as a teenager, Ma was frequently reminded that she was an outsider and asked when she would “go home”.

Later, in the US, she found herself fielding simplistic questions about China, such as about feng shui, kung fu or whether all Chinese people belonged to the Communist Party.

That experience, Ma said, left her with both a curiosity and an urgency to understand her own roots more intimately.

“I was always treated as ‘the other’,” she said. “I had an unexpected, lingering burden to know my roots – and a deep shame when I couldn’t answer everyone’s silly assumptions.”

Writing became a way to find her roots and better understand her identity. Early guides included Cao Xueqin’s Dream of the Red Chamber and Jonathan Spence’s The Gate of Heavenly Peace, books that helped her connect with China’s literary and historical traditions.

Ma, who has since written the novels Excess Baggage and China’s Millennial Digital Generation, observed that another obstacle to Americans’ understanding of China lay not in the books being written but in how they were translated.

“High-quality translation is a major issue for Chinese literature,” she said.

“Many Chinese novels lack the adequate and eloquent English translations seen in Japanese literature. Chinese literature could attract a much wider readership if publishers were willing to invest more money into securing top-tier translators.”

Ma argued that the best books about China come from writers with deep immersion, regardless of nationality.

“Many Western writers risk misreading China due to language barriers and a lack of on-the-ground immersion,” she said, while some Chinese authors could also suffer from a kind of literary “tunnel vision” that German sinologist Wolfgang Kubin attributed to decades of “historical and cultural isolation”.

Delaney pointed to the increasing competition books faced in shaping perceptions from short videos that were often not well researched.

“So many of our perceptions are now shaped by sound bites on social media,” he said. “Books give us the depth and context that we need to counteract this. I just hope that novelists are not fighting a losing battle.”

Boyd also hoped books could help fill the gap and spark new interest at a time when American student numbers in China have plummeted.

According to data from the Institute of International Education’s Open Doors report, the number of American students studying in China stood at over 11,600 before the coronavirus pandemic.

Following a steep decline owing to strict policies and geopolitical tensions, US official estimates indicate that the number began a modest recovery, ticking up to about 800 during the 2023-2024 academic year.

“That was my experience,” Boyd said of his life trajectory. “I read a book about China, and I got obsessed … books can absolutely play that role. I hope so.”

“I wish books could solve US-China diplomatic problems,” he added.

“I hope that there’s room for more exchange of books between both cultures. America wants to know more about China. China should want to know more about America. I believe it does.”
