Philippines’ jeepneys sputter up costly climb to go electric

SCMP | FREE | 1012 words | 2026-07-17

For generations, jeepneys – brightly painted minibuses that are among the Philippines’ cheapest and most widely used forms of public transport – have run on diesel, but recent fuel shocks are forcing thousands of small operators and drivers to weigh volatile prices against the debt needed to go electric.

Diesel prices spiked by nearly 5 pesos this week as renewed tensions in the Gulf threatened to strain the country’s public transport sector, adding fresh urgency to the uneven shift towards “e-jeepneys”.

Since the Iran conflict began in late February, diesel prices have soared, prompting an exodus of jeepney drivers from the road. Fuel costs in Metro Manila hit a high of 114 pesos (US$1.90) per litre in March – nearly one-sixth of the minimum daily wage rate for non-agricultural workers in the capital region.

Inflation settled at 6.4 per cent in June, while diesel prices climbed to 76 pesos this week, up from 72 pesos the week before and well above the pre-conflict rate of 60 pesos per litre.

The latest increase has put the public transport sector on edge and exposed a deeper dilemma: electric jeepneys promise lower running costs, but remain out of reach for many small operators and drivers.

“We’re expecting diesel to climb again. The media has been saying that the stockpile will only last over 40 days,” said Liberty de Luna, national president of the Alliance of Concerned Transport Organisations (ACTO) in the Philippines.

“What about us? We might see another scenario where diesel climbs to 100 pesos per litre. This would be a huge blow to the transport sector.”

The country is especially exposed to price shocks from the Iran conflict, as it imports more than 95 per cent of its oil supply. Energy officials say it could take months before prices return to pre-conflict levels.

Another transport group, Pasang Masda, has urged the government to restore the 1-peso fare increase that was suspended earlier this year, citing the growing financial burden on the sector.

De Luna said more ACTO members were now considering a shift to e-jeepneys as diesel prices continued to spiral out of control.

“We’ve begun embracing e-jeepneys, especially now with the diesel crisis. Because if some of us are already on electric and others are still on diesel, at least if diesel becomes unaffordable, those on e-jeepneys can keep serving the public,” she said.

“If there’s no more affordable diesel and no electric alternatives either, how do we serve the riding public?”

In April, the Department of Trade and Industry launched a 2-billion-peso financing programme to help licensed small operators and drivers transition to electric vehicles amid the fuel crisis.

The scheme offers loans of up to 1.5 million pesos per EV, allowing operators to borrow as much as 3 million pesos, repayable over five years, with principal and interest waived for the first year.

Although only a small number of ACTO members have made the switch so far, de Luna said their experiences had been promising.

“With EV, we have lower spending. In two hours, we can fast-charge a vehicle good for 200km. If we’re spending 1,500 or 2,000 pesos for diesel, with electric, it will come around to 700 pesos. So the savings are huge,” she said.

Playing catch-up

The Philippines is fast catching up with its Southeast Asian neighbours on EV adoption as motorists look for ways to insulate themselves from rising fuel prices.

Sales of EVs, including battery and hybrid vehicles, surged by 133.5 per cent between January and May this year compared with the same period in 2025, according to data from the Chamber of Automotive Manufacturers of the Philippines and the Truck Manufacturers Association.

That trend appears to be spreading to public transport, with manufacturers keen to capitalise on rising demand.

Japan-based EV start-up Assemble Point opened a factory in Cavite province in February, just before the Gulf conflict led to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

The company, which builds 10-seater electric minibuses and aims to sell them nationwide, has seen a surge in interest, according to Japanese public broadcaster NHK, with a backlog of orders as it awaits imported components.

Not everyone, however, is keen to join the EV rush, however. Transport group Piston has pointed to gaps in the Philippines’ push to boost e-jeepney adoption, warning that the livelihoods of drivers and small operators who cannot afford the new units are at risk under the Public Transport Modernisation Programme, which forces them to consolidate franchises into cooperatives.

Sandy Mae Gaspay, an associate professor at the University of the Philippines’ College of Engineering, said the fuel crisis had raised awareness of e-jeepneys’ benefits, but added that this was only part of the picture.

“A bigger question is if jeepney operators can afford to buy new vehicles in the first place. This is why the EV discussion for jeepneys is difficult to separate from the Public Transport Modernisation Programme, which has its long list of issues,” she said, referring to the initiative to replace old vehicles with eco-friendly alternatives.

Aside from operators’ financial readiness, Gaspay pointed to a lack of government support for establishing EV infrastructure, including charging systems and electrical line adjustments.

“Those operating EVs need to set up these systems by themselves. This shifts what is usually the state’s burden to the operators,” she said.

The EV surge has also created new dependencies through battery acquisition and management systems that operators must finance privately.

“There are several who have opted to lease batteries from Chinese companies on a monthly basis,” Gaspay said. “While these arrangements are beneficial for operators with capital constraints, they create perpetual monthly expenses that operators must absorb indefinitely.”

Gaspay said the government should do more to support the public transport sector’s transition to EVs, including implementing dedicated “green routes” for electric minibuses and shared facilities such as charging or battery-swapping stations to lower the overall cost of ownership.

She also pointed to incentives such as service-contracting programmes, which could help EV drivers repay loans while allowing cooperatives to explore alternative income streams.
