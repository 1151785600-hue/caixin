Hong Kong must brace for AI bubble risk and quantum computer threat: HKMA chief

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 748 words | 2026-07-13

Hong Kong must brace for the emerging risks of an artificial intelligence (AI) bubble bursting and quantum computers capable of hacking encrypted financial systems, the head of the city’s de facto central bank has warned.

Eddie Yue Wai-man, chief executive of the Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA), also said on Sunday that the banking regulator would focus on expanding the internationalisation of the yuan in the next few years.

Yue cautioned that the current euphoria surrounding the AI sector could mask vulnerabilities, and warned about a “stacking of risks” should a stock market correction coincide with geopolitical events that triggered inflation anxieties, causing fear that interest rates would rise.

“Whether it is their capital expenditure or their financing, it has started to increase in the debt market, whether through private credit or issuing corporate bonds,” he told a radio show, adding that it was unknown if AI could be commercialised to provide a good return.

He said Hong Kong had to prepare for the possibility of an AI market downturn by ensuring local banks maintained strong capital buffers, rigorous risk management and a highly resilient financial “cushion” to absorb any sudden macroeconomic shocks.

In March, tech giant Google warned that quantum computers could potentially hack some encrypted systems by 2029, giving governments and companies less time to prepare contingencies to safeguard their data.

Yue said while the quantum computer threat was an emerging global issue, expected to arrive in five to six years, its capabilities could happen sooner and mirror AI’s rapid developments.

“We have gathered banking, academic and tech experts to proactively research and defend against that,” he said.

“What we are even more focused on is the massive scale of risk brought about by quantum computers, which is the encryption and decryption methods of the ‘post-quantum era’.”

Addressing threats where highly advanced AI models can quickly scan and detect system-wide bugs, a cross-industry task force has been created to study “multilayered defence” and rapid patching mechanisms.

Hong Kong aims to publish its first five-year blueprint in the third quarter of this year, aimed at aligning with Beijing’s plan guiding the country’s development from 2026 to 2030.

The city would play a pivotal role as a strong currency and strong international financial centre, by promoting the internationalisation of the offshore yuan, Yue said, adding that more concrete measures would be announced in coming weeks.

“People have been accustomed to using US dollars for decades. How can we encourage not only Hong Kong companies, but also overseas and Middle Eastern countries to use the yuan?” he said.

“This requires close cooperation with banks. Banks must help clients learn how to use renminbi and provide a seamless, one-stop service, making it as convenient as using US dollars.”

But challenges include several technical bottlenecks that persist in the overseas use of the yuan, such as the high cost of direct exchange between different currencies, which hinders the efficiency of cross-border transactions.

Banks must offer cheaper exchange rates, lower financing costs and improved automation, Yue said, adding that market liquidity of the offshore yuan was key, which Hong Kong could facilitate.

Separately, Financial Secretary Paul Chan Mo-po outlined three initiatives for Hong Kong to better contribute to national development in his weekly blog on Sunday, with the internationalisation of the renminbi topping the agenda.

Highlighting that more enterprises were establishing global or regional headquarters in Hong Kong and shifting to renminbi for settlements, Chan pledged to encourage greater use of offshore renminbi for financing, investment and trade.

The government would also strengthen cooperation with other central banks in the region to promote the internationalisation of the yuan, he added.

“Hong Kong is not only a beneficiary of national development, but also a contributor to advancing national strategies,” Chan said, stressing that the city had to leverage its unique edges and seize new opportunities arising from national development.

“This will enable us to make more solid, long-term and enduring contributions to the overall national development, while creating a ‘fresh growth curve’ for Hong Kong’s future.”

Chan said that Hong Kong could capitalise on its strengths in fundamental research, talent, capital, international innovation resources and market networks to support the mainland in building a modernised industrial system.

He added that the city should deepen international collaboration through its well-established professional services, such as risk management and dispute resolution, to help Global South countries address challenges including financing difficulties and lagging infrastructure.
