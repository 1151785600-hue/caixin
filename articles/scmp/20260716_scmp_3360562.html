Why scrapping Nepal’s ‘dollar fare’ could leave locals grounded

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 918 words | 2026-07-16

A plan to cut the cost of domestic flights for foreign visitors to Nepal could end up making them more expensive for citizens, according to airline operators that warn against scrapping one of the country’s most contentious tourism pricing policies.

Under Nepal’s two-tier airfare system, foreign passengers must pay in US dollars for domestic flights, with prices usually two to three times higher than for Nepalis and sometimes even more, depending on the route and season.

In April, Minister for Culture, Tourism and Civil Aviation Khadak Raj Paudel pledged to scrap the sky-high airfares to boost tourism and ease the burden on non-resident Nepalis holding foreign citizenship, who are also charged the higher rate.

He told the Kantipur newspaper that the removal of the “dollar fare” would begin with flights to the far-western Karnali and Sudurpaschim provinces – home to lesser-visited attractions such as the Rara and Shey Phoksundo lakes and Khaptad National Park – before being rolled out nationally.

The plan stalled due to the fuel crisis linked to the Iran-US conflict and the tourism ministry has said there are no ongoing discussions on the issue.

Even so, the proposal has ignited a debate over its feasibility, with tourism stakeholders warning the fallout could extend well beyond aviation.

“If you’re only looking from a tourism perspective, then lower airfares would be a good thing,” said Murali Dhar Joshi, general secretary of the Airlines Operators Association of Nepal. “But from an airline perspective, it’s not feasible. It won’t be economically sustainable.”

Nepal introduced the dollar fare in the 1970s, when the state-owned carrier – then known as Royal Nepal Airlines – began charging separately for its mountain flights offering close-up views of the Himalayas, including Mount Everest. After Nepal liberalised its aviation sector in the early 1990s, private airlines adopted the same two-tier pricing on domestic routes.

Foreign travellers have long voiced frustration at the steep pricing, however, with some calling the system “unfair and discriminatory”.

A round-trip flight from Kathmandu to Lukla – the gateway to Everest – costs foreigners more than US$250, exceeding the roughly US$200 charged for some international flights to New Delhi, while Nepalis pay about 12,000 Nepalese rupees (US$80).

Similarly, a return ticket from Kathmandu to Pokhara costs foreigners around US$225, compared with 11,500 rupees for locals.

The disparity might look stark, but Joshi said it made sense given the wider context.

He said dollar fares were essential for flights serving short take-off and landing (STOL) airports in remote mountainous areas such as Lukla, as well as major trunk routes like Pokhara.

What at first appeared to be inflated pricing for tourists reflected the real operational costs of these flights, he explained – costs that, once covered, allowed airlines to keep fares affordable for locals.

“The revenue we receive in dollars helps us subsidise the costs for Nepalis, but there is a misconception that locals are getting special privileges, which is not accurate,” Joshi said.

Mani Raj Lamichhane, a senior director at the Nepal Tourism Board, said abolishing the dollar fare would be “impractical” and noted that many tourism-dependent countries, including the Maldives, had similar pricing models.

He said he sat on the committee that advised against the same move when the then tourism minister Yogesh Bhattarai pushed for it in 2019.

In a low-income country like Nepal, where the monthly minimum wage is 19,550 rupees (US$125), observers say cheaper domestic airfares make flying possible for many, particularly in remote mountain districts where road access is limited or unreliable. Such flights often provide vital access to healthcare, education, employment and other essential services.

Madushika Lansakara, who has worked in Nepal for four years, said she had “mixed feelings” about the dollar fare, though the concept of visitors paying more was not unfamiliar to her.

In her home country of Sri Lanka, foreign tourists are routinely charged more than locals at major attractions.

She said she would not mind paying extra if it genuinely helped Nepalis. “The purchasing power of foreigners is higher than that of locals, and we’re being paid more,” she said. “So if the higher fares are allowing more Nepalis to travel efficiently, I don’t mind paying more.”

Some tourism entrepreneurs argue the dollar fare deters foreigners from exploring Nepal’s hidden gems – one even petitioned the Supreme Court this year for its removal.

Flights to Dhangadhi, for example, the starting point for the Api Base Camp trek in Sudurpaschim, are steep for foreigners at around US$450 vs 20,000 rupees for locals.

Some critics have proposed scrapping the dollar fare on non-STOL routes while retaining it for flights to mountainous regions, but airline operators say this is not a workable compromise.

Rupesh Joshi, director of marketing and sales at domestic carrier Buddha Air, said the dollar fare played a crucial role in generating foreign exchange used to finance essential imports.

With the domestic aviation market already down nearly 40 per cent as rising fuel costs push up ticket prices, he warned that scrapping the fare now could send the sector into a tailspin.

“Airlines need to pay their loans, maintenance costs and insurance all in dollars, and this won’t be sustainable. Once we introduce a uniform fare policy, it will make flying very expensive for Nepalis,” he said.

“Even if we scrap the dollar fare, what will the government do for hotels and Unesco-listed tourist sites that also have a dual pricing policy for locals and foreigners? It’s not feasible.”
