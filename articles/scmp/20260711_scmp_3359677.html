What the Iran war has revealed about Brics+

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 914 words | 2026-07-11

The US-Israeli military campaign against Iran was more than another Middle Eastern conflict. It became a defining stress test for the expanded Brics (Brics+), the grouping that is frequently portrayed as the flagship political platform of the Global South and a cornerstone of an emerging multipolar order.

The war showed that the grouping – which has grown beyond its founding members Brazil, Russia, India, China and South Africa – is neither a military alliance nor an ideological bloc. Rather, it is a flexible coalition of states whose shared dissatisfaction with Western dominance coexists with profoundly different national interests.

The conflict exposed these contradictions clearly. Iran was a target of the war, while another bloc member, the United Arab Emirates, experienced Iranian strikes on its territory. Rather than fostering solidarity, the crisis reaffirmed a familiar lesson of international politics: when sovereignty and national interests are at stake, states ultimately prioritise self-help.

This has prompted criticism that Brics+ failed its first major geopolitical test. Such criticism misunderstands the organisation’s purpose. Before asking why Brics+ failed, it is worth asking a more fundamental question: what was Brics+ designed to do?

Part of the misunderstanding stems from the analytical frameworks through which Western institutions have traditionally interpreted the international order. For much of the post-Cold War period, geopolitical influence was associated with highly institutionalised alliances characterised by common threat perceptions, formal commitments and collective responses.

Brics+ emerged in a different historical context. Its appeal derives from its ability to accommodate diversity, allowing members to cooperate selectively while preserving room for independent manoeuvre. Judging such a platform through alliance-centric assumptions risks obscuring the very logic that underpins its attractiveness.

The Iran war provided an opportunity to observe this logic in practice. Rather than producing automatic solidarity or unified positions, the crisis revealed how Brics+ members interpreted the conflict primarily through the prism of their own national interests and strategic priorities.

The first indication came from the diplomatic response. Unlike during the June 2025 confrontation involving Iran, when Brics+ issued a joint statement emphasising respect for international law and the UN Charter, no comparable collective declaration emerged this time. Instead, member states responded individually, reflecting their own strategic calculations.

While China, Russia, Brazil and South Africa condemned the attacks on Iran, India and Indonesia limited themselves to calls for restraint and de-escalation. Egypt, by contrast, adopted a more Gulf-aligned position that criticised Iran; it reaffirmed its stance by co-sponsoring UN Security Council Draft Resolution 2817 alongside Ethiopia.

The UAE found itself in an especially delicate position. As both a Brics+ member and a Gulf state directly exposed to Iranian strikes, Abu Dhabi had to balance regional security concerns with its expanding partnerships across multiple geopolitical camps.

Rather than demonstrating organisational weakness, this fragmentation reflected the distinct strategic environments in which members operate. India perhaps best illustrates this dynamic.

Long committed to strategic autonomy, New Delhi viewed the conflict less through the lens of Brics+ solidarity than through its own geopolitical priorities. Maintaining ties with Tehran while preserving relations with Washington therefore reflects India’s broader balancing strategy rather than any contradiction within Brics+ itself.

Energy security reinforces this approach. As one of the world’s largest oil importers, India has diversified suppliers and transport routes to reduce vulnerability to disruptions around the Strait of Hormuz. For New Delhi, the war was ultimately a question of national resilience rather than bloc politics.

China approached the conflict through a different set of strategic calculations, yet it arrived at a similarly pragmatic outcome. While reiterating its customary emphasis on sovereignty and negotiated settlement, Beijing displayed relatively little economic alarm. Years of supplier diversification, investment in renewables and reduced long-term dependence on imported hydrocarbons helped cushion the impact of the conflict.

Ultimately, the conflict shows that Brics+ should be understood less as a formal alliance than as an arena for strategic flexibility. Brics+ members continuously engage in what international relations scholars describe as forum shopping, leveraging multiple institutions simultaneously to maximise national interests. India exemplifies this logic through its simultaneous engagement with Brics+, the Quad and other groupings. Such diversity is a defining characteristic of contemporary multipolarity.

Too often, analysts mistakenly portray the Global South as a coherent geopolitical bloc united by common interests. It is better understood as a loose political community seeking greater strategic autonomy and broader alternatives to Western-led institutions.

From this perspective, claims that the Iran war exposed Brics+ as a failure miss the point. They evaluate Brics+ as though they expect solidarity and coordinated responses, but Brics+ was never designed to function like Nato. It is a platform for coordination, economic cooperation and institutional experimentation within an increasingly multipolar international system.

Brics+ might be less important for what it does in any single crisis than for what it represents in the evolution of international order. Whereas 20th century geopolitics was organised around competing blocs demanding political alignment, contemporary multipolarity increasingly rewards adaptability, diversified partnerships and institutional overlap.

The Iran war therefore revealed neither an identity crisis nor institutional paralysis for Brics+. Instead, it highlighted the essence of multipolarity itself: states increasingly refuse to define themselves through exclusive alliances. They seek overlapping partnerships that maximise flexibility while preserving strategic autonomy.

As global politics becomes more fragmented, analysts should resist judging emerging institutions by Cold War standards of bloc discipline. The real lesson of the Iran war is that multipolarity is built less upon rigid unity than upon the coexistence of multiple, overlapping national interests.
