Mainland Chinese tech firms find more than just deep capital pools in Hong Kong

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 662 words | 2026-07-08

Mainland Chinese technology companies newly listed on Hong Kong’s stock exchange are deepening their engagement with the city, tapping not only its capital markets but also its global connectivity to refine products, forge international partnerships and expand overseas, according to executives.

For Beijing-based service robot maker Yunji Technology, Hong Kong has become a key gateway to global markets since its listing in the city in October.

“If we use one word to describe what Hong Kong offers for us, that would be connection,” Xie Yunpeng, the company’s chief development officer, told reporters during a media tour on Tuesday in Beijing.

Xie said Hong Kong connected Yunji with business opportunities, capital, research partners and overseas markets, while exposing the company to diverse consumer habits that helped prepare its products for international deployment.

Hong Kong had become a testing ground for Yunji’s service robots before their roll-out overseas, according to Xie, as international customers tended to view successful deployments in the city as more representative of operating conditions outside mainland China.

The company plans to focus its overseas expansion on Southeast Asia, the Middle East, the US and parts of East Asia.

With its robots finding use in hotels and factories, Yunji’s overseas revenue in 2025 surged nearly 93 per cent from a year earlier, marking what Xie described as the company’s first major step in international expansion.

The company was the fourth to list under Hong Kong’s Chapter 18C rule for specialist technology companies, which was introduced in March 2023 to allow high-growth technology firms to raise capital on the city’s stock exchange without meeting traditional profitability thresholds.

The rule aims to help Hong Kong attract high-growth, deep-tech companies – in artificial intelligence, robotics, semiconductors, new energy and environmental technology – that require heavy research spending before becoming profitable.

Since the rule took effect, 21 specialist technology firms have listed in the city, raising HK$38.74 billion (US$4.9 billion), according to research by investment consultancy Zhong Shan Capital.

Yunji is not alone in capitalising on Hong Kong’s access to global capital and international markets.

Beijing-based Wenge AI, which listed in Hong Kong last month, also said the city offered a platform to build products for overseas customers, in addition to access to international investors.

“Hong Kong plays an important role in connecting [us with] global capital and [overseas] markets due to its diversified and international environment,” Wang Lei, founder and chairman, said during the tour on Tuesday.

Founded nearly a decade ago, the company – formally Beijing Zhongke WengeAI Science and Technology – develops AI-powered systems that help enterprises analyse complex business scenarios and assist in decision-making.

Listing in Hong Kong gave the company direct access to overseas markets, positioning it to develop AI products for an international customer base, Wang said.

The company established its international subsidiary at Cyberport, Hong Kong’s digital technology hub and AI accelerator, in 2023.

Since then, it has used the city as a base to engage with local and overseas customers, collaborate with partners and explore new markets.

Wang said that proceeds from its recent Hong Kong listing were going towards AI technology development and attracting talent, as well as international market expansion.

Beijing-based humanoid robot start-up Galbot also sees Hong Kong as a crucial springboard for its global ambitions. Its government-linked backer, the Hong Kong Investment Corporation, previously said that the company viewed the city as its preferred destination for a future initial public offering.

“Hong Kong is the first stop for our international expansion, so we will prioritise testing many of our new businesses in Hong Kong or Macau,” said Sun Dengke, Galbot’s chief financial officer, on Wednesday.

Sun highlighted the unique synergy between Beijing and the Greater Bay Area, adding that the company could tap into mainland China’s research and development resources while capitalising on the “internationalised talent, capital and business advantages” of Hong Kong and Macau.
