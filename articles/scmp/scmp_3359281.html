Neurobiologist and former taekwondo captain Chih-Ying Su leaves US for China

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 696 words | 2026-07-08

Celebrated neurobiologist Chih-Ying Su, who specialises in research on the sense of smell using fruit flies and mosquitoes, has left her position as faculty vice-chair at the University of California San Diego to join the Shenzhen Academy of Medical Sciences (SMART).

The Taiwan-born American scholar’s appointment as a full-time senior investigator was confirmed by SMART on July 2.

Professor Su’s lab focuses on how olfactory receptor neurons (ORNs) process odour information, as these neurons are the primary source of sensory input. Her research findings have been published in leading journals in international neuroscience and biology, including Nature, Neuron, Nature Communications and PNAS.

“I decided to join SMART at the end of last year, deeply impressed by the advanced hardware conditions and the strong academic atmosphere of the research institute,” Su said in an email interview. “The outstanding leadership and academic vision of [SMART] president Yan Ning deeply attracted me.”

The former tenured professor and vice-chair of neurobiology at UC San Diego conducts her research using fruit flies, which are a model organism – a species selected by scientists for general research on the fundamental laws of life.

They are inexpensive to breed and reproduce quickly, and their key genes and signalling pathways are similar to those in humans.

The survival of fruit flies depends on their ability to decode the meanings of the complex mix of odours around them – to determine the location of food, find potential mates and avoid predators.

But their number of neurons, or nerve cells, required for smell is much fewer than that in humans. Insects such as fruit flies have a mere 100,000 neurons to work with. Among them, about 1,300 are involved in smell.

By comparison, the total number of human neurons is about 187 billion, of which about 10 million are involved in the sense of smell.

Su’s research group has particularly focused on this question: how can a simple nervous system like that of fruit flies, which has far fewer neurons than mammals, still achieve highly efficient and flexible information processing?

Her research is not just limited to fruit flies. In 2025, Su’s latest paper investigated mosquitoes’ olfactory receptor neurons that detect carbon dioxide. This article was highlighted by the journal PNAS.

Mosquitoes detect carbon dioxide through sensory hairs known as sensilla. These hairs contain ORNs, including neurons specialised for carbon dioxide sensing. The researchers focused on these features in Aedes aegypti mosquitoes, which are known to spread yellow fever, dengue, chikungunya, and Zika viruses.

Su grew up in Taiwan and received her undergraduate and master’s degrees from National Taiwan University where – in addition to studying and conducting scientific research – she also served as captain of the taekwondo team.

In 2000, she pursued a PhD in neuroscience at Johns Hopkins University in Baltimore and graduated in 2006, before embarking on an academic career in the study of olfactory mechanisms.

In 2013, Su joined UC San Diego as a member of the Department of Biological Sciences, and two years later became one of its 12 new Hellman Fellows. The Hellman Fellows programme aims to provide early-career funding for promising teaching staff to help them advance towards tenure.

She was promoted to assistant professor of neurobiology in 2020 and became a full professor and department vice-chair in 2024.

As Su starts her new role, her lab’s official website has changed its physical address to the Shenzhen Guangming Life Science Park, where SMART is located.

“SMART boasts a world-class research environment and platform facilities. In 2025, I had the privilege of visiting it in person. I believe that in such an environment, one can gain valuable guidance and growth opportunities,” she explained, citing these as her reasons for choosing SMART as her next academic destination.

In fact, the laboratory is recruiting a variety of researchers, ranging from research assistants and postdoctoral fellows to associate/assistant researchers.

“We are always interested in hearing from motivated and talented people with an interest in sensory signalling, olfaction, neurobiology or related fields,” the lab’s website states.

SMART’s recruitment notice offers competitive salaries and welfare benefits, a strong scientific research environment and training, and help with applying for relevant talent subsidies and supporting benefits.
