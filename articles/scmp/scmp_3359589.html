China’s latest nuclear reactor goes live, first in Greater Bay Area with Hualong One tech

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 634 words | 2026-07-08

China’s domestic design is fuelling a nuclear reactor-building spree in the country’s largest provincial economy of Guangdong, with a new Hualong One reactor counting down to entering commercial operation this week ahead of peak demand for electricity this summer.

China General Nuclear Power Group (CGN) announced that the No. 2 reactor of the Taipingling Nuclear Power Plant, located less than 100km from Hong Kong in Huizhou, had been plugged into the grid on Saturday.

Many of Guangdong’s nuclear plants, including its first one built more than 30 years ago, imported foreign technologies for their superior Western design but China is increasingly building out its domestic supply chain for nuclear energy.

The nuclear reactor is the first one in the Greater Bay Area (GBA) that incorporates China’s proprietary Hualong flagship third-generation pressurised water reactor design. The GBA is China’s regional integration master plan to connect Hong Kong and Macau to nine mainland cities in Guangdong to form economic and tech synergy.

The milestone at Taipingling caps five years of construction, with its initial proposal reportedly favouring technology from Westinghouse in the United States. The project cost 120 billion yuan (US$17.58 billion) and will incorporate six reactors.

The latest nuclear buildout in Guangdong is in addition to four bases that already feed power to nearby cities, as officials hope to accelerate decarbonisation amid rapidly rising demand for electricity in the province of 128 million people, which has an annual GDP similar to both Australia and South Korea.

Beijing is hoping to cut itself off from fossil fuels, especially coal, one of the key contributors to climate change. Together with renewable energy, the world’s second-largest economy and biggest emitter of greenhouse gases aims to peak emissions by 2030 and get to carbon neutrality in 2060.

In June, the National Development and Reform Commission and the National Energy Administration (NEA) released a five-year plan for a new energy system as the country nears the 2030 goal. The plan called for maintaining a “steady pace” of nuclear plant construction with a 43 per cent rise in operational nuclear power installed capacity during the period to reach 110 million kilowatts (110 GW) by 2030.

Each reactor unit has an installed capacity of 1 million kilowatts, totalling 110 units. At the end of 2025, China’s total operational nuclear power installed capacity stood at 62.5 million kilowatts, according to NEA data.

“One reactor at Taipingling can generate 9 billion kilowatt-hours of electricity annually and help reduce carbon dioxide emissions by more than 8.3 million tons,” Zhang Guoqiang, general manager of CGN Huizhou branch, told Guangdong provincial mouthpiece the Southern Daily, adding that nuclear power was the most technologically and commercially scaleable way to decarbonise.

In April 2025, China’s State Council approved 10 new reactors across five sites, eight of them adopting the Hualong technology, with a combined investment of 200 billion yuan.

First entering commercial operation in January, 2021, the indigenous Hualong One design features active and passive safety hybrid systems and one reactor can generate output equivalent to the annual electricity needs of up to 1 million people. As China has transitioned away from importing foreign nuclear products to build its own state-of-the-art technology, the Hualong One is highly localised, with around 90 per cent of components sourced domestically.

China has also successfully built out a complete domestic supply chain for every major component – from steam generators to pressure vessels – insulating the country from Western sanctions or trade blocks, Xinhua cited CGN as saying.

Still, Beijing has not approved any nuclear power bases in inland regions due to safety considerations, even though such facilities have been in operation across the US and Europe for decades. Instead, all nuclear plants in use or under construction are along China’s coastal regions, away from seismically active areas, to ensure safety while strategically located to supply power to nearby economic hubs.
