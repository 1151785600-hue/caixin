Hong Kong’s war-risk pool testament to collaboration, insurance leader says

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 590 words | 2026-07-14

The launch of a marine war-risk pool by Hong Kong insurers is a prime example of how the city’s insurers can collaborate not just for the benefit of the local economy but for the entire nation, an industry veteran has said.

At the South China Morning Post’s China Conference, themed “Intelligence at Scale: Hong Kong’s AI-Powered Future”, panel speakers considered how the insurance industry could bolster financial resilience across the Greater Bay Area.

Speaking at the event, Selina Lau Pui-ling, chief executive of the Hong Kong Federation of Insurers, highlighted the initiative in which five local insurers came together to build a marine war-risk pool to address uncertainties over unstable conditions in the Middle East.

Lau added that the insurers provided necessary coverage for vessels in mainland China and the city to continue voyages in critical areas of the region.

“This is the kind of collaboration, the beauty of Hong Kong. We have collaborated, not only to help Hong Kong, but also our nation as well,” Lau said.

Standard cargo and marine insurance policies do not provide protection against war risks, meaning shipowners must buy separate war insurance whenever vessels sail into a war zone.

Without the city’s localised pool, mainland and local shipowners could only turn to London for cover at a higher cost.

In fact, marine insurance premiums in the city increased 33 per cent in the first nine months of 2025 compared with the same period in 2024.

Backed by five insurers in the city, the special war-risk insurance pool launched in November 2025 offers up to US$130 million in compensation for local and mainland shipowners against war and emergency risks.

Lau also noted that the recent redomiciliation of insurers to the city was “actually a fruitful result of the collaboration among Hong Kong insurers”.

“We actually set up a special task force for this. We hired a lawyer to write up all the information and all the drafts; we built the case,” said Lau.

“We are working together for a better cause, for a better future for Hong Kong, and I’m very proud of it.”

Adrian Lam, associate director of policy and legislation at the Insurance Authority, said that the use of artificial intelligence (AI) in the insurance sector must be well governed and adopted responsibly.

He added that the regulator was updating guidelines on internet services to add a specific section on AI applications.

“The Hong Kong insurance industry is very diverse. We have over 150 insurers, so inclusiveness is very important,” Lam said.

“In general terms, the regulatory environment in Hong Kong concerning the application of AI in insurance is not strong, and we’re still doing additional work.”

Lam pointed out that an official survey found 40 per cent of insurers in the city were already adopting AI two years ago, with usage expanding from internal process optimisation to customer-facing applications.

Wilton Kee, the CEO of Manulife Hong Kong and Macau, highlighted how the firm had benefited after becoming the first major global insurer to redomicile from Bermuda to the city last December.

He noted that the move gave the firm a distinct local identity with a simplified focus on domestic business, while simultaneously streamlining operational requirements and signalling the global group’s confidence in the city as a major hub for opportunities.

Kee pointed out that the insurer already had 91 AI use cases deployed and another 121 in development by the end of 2025, incorporating multilingual chatbots and 24-hour virtual assistants that enabled advisers to gain instant customer insights.
