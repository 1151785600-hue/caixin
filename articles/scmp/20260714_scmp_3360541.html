How Beijing proposes to solve South China Sea disputes 10 years after Hague ruling

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 767 words | 2026-07-14

A decade after a South China Sea ruling, analysts said Beijing should continue engaging in good-faith diplomacy and explore international mediation, arguing that the unenforced ruling had failed to curb rising regional maritime tensions.

The observations came after a weekend of fresh accusations over the 2016 decision by an international tribunal in The Hague ruling that most of China’s claims to historical and economic rights in the South China Sea were invalid. Beijing firmly rejected the decision.

On the ruling’s 10-year anniversary, a coalition of 14 countries led by the United States and the Philippines in a joint statement called it “legally binding and definitive”, while criticising Beijing for refusing to recognise or implement it.

In a strongly worded response on the same day, Beijing described US-led military deployments as the “primary threat” to regional peace in the South China Sea.

China dismissed The Hague ruling as “null and void”, calling it a politically motivated process that lacked state consent. It has also refused to recognise the tribunal’s authority to hear the case and said it would not implement its findings.

Beijing claims most of the islands and rocks in the disputed waters and rights over their adjacent waters. This has fuelled protracted territorial disputes with rival claimants, with friction escalating most intensely with Manila in recent years.

At an event on Monday about South China Sea security and co-hosted by think tanks the Asian Academy of International Law, the National Institute for South China Sea Studies (NISCSS) and the Huayang Centre for Maritime Cooperation and Ocean Governance, analysts extensively discussed what China could do to manage the disputes.

Chen Xiangmiao, a research fellow at the Chinese government-backed NISCSS, told a panel that a commitment to the principle of good faith was essential for China’s future approach to the disputed waters.

“The Philippines’ continuous attacks on China are steadily escalating bilateral tensions and fuelling nationalist sentiments on both sides,” Chen said during the panel assessing the South China Sea security situation and risk response.

“This friction will constrain and complicate decision-making for both governments. Therefore, the principle of good faith is essential. Neither side should weaponise international law.”

Chen said the 2016 ruling had undermined the practical application of international law in the South China Sea and that countries should repair the damage done to the maritime order.

Rather than remaining fixated on the arbitral ruling, both sides should redirect their efforts towards practical avenues of cooperation, such as marine conservation, fishery management and renewable energy, he added.

Earlier this year, Beijing and Manila traded accusations over escalating maritime tensions in the region.

In January, the US and the Philippines conducted joint naval and air patrols near the disputed Scarborough Shoal. Days later, Beijing responded with combat air and naval patrols.

Meanwhile, as the 2026 chair of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (Asean), Manila has pushed for finalising a code of conduct in the disputed waters within this year.

At the Shangri-La Dialogue in May, Philippine defence chief Gilberto Teodoro Jnr singled out China as the biggest obstacle to the code, triggering sanctions from Beijing that barred him and his family from entering the country.

Bao Yinan, an associate research fellow at the Huayang Centre in China’s southern Hainan province, proposed the International Organization for Mediation (IOMed) as a viable alternative to break the diplomatic deadlock.

Established last year and headquartered in Hong Kong, the China-led IOMed is the world’s first intergovernmental body focused on mediation-based dispute solutions.

The IOMed has now attracted 13 contracting states and 44 signatories, including five Asean countries such as Indonesia and Myanmar.

Beijing has criticised some existing international mechanisms that have exerted pressure on China over disputes such as competing territorial claims in the South China Sea.

In May, the mediation body settled a maritime dispute involving parties from China and Singapore.

Mediation and conciliation offer a viable middle ground between China’s preference for negotiation and the Philippines’ push for arbitration and adjudication, Bao said at a separate panel on maintaining stability in the South China Sea.

The mechanism itself was technically viable for China and rival claimants to implement in territorial and sovereignty disputes, given that Beijing has yet to file any reservations excluding its use, he added.

But the willingness to do so largely “depends on political considerations rather than legal aspects”, Bao said.

“Should the parties to the South China Sea disputes engage in good-faith interactions … making proactive use of the IOMed by taking this ‘important first step’ would offer significant benefits with virtually no corresponding drawbacks.”
