Trump scraps Hormuz toll plan but presses ahead with Iran blockade

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1011 words | 2026-07-15

US President Donald Trump on Tuesday abandoned plans to impose a 20 per cent charge on commercial cargo passing through the Strait of Hormuz, less than a day after unveiling the proposal, while pressing ahead with a naval blockade targeting Iranian trade.

Trump said the proposed fee would be replaced by trade and investment agreements with Gulf states following discussions with regional leaders.

“Based on highly productive conversations with Middle East leadership, I have decided to replace the 20% United States Reimbursement Fee with Trade and Investment Deals that the various Gulf States will be making into the United States,” Trump wrote on social media.

He said the investments would be “MASSIVE” and beneficial to the countries involved, but did not identify the participating governments, specify the value of the commitments or say whether they represented new pledges.

The reversal came after Trump declared on Monday that Washington would charge eligible vessels a fee equivalent to 20 per cent of the value of their cargo to help cover the cost of securing the strategic waterway.

Trump had described the United States as the “guardian” of the Strait of Hormuz, through which about a fifth of globally traded oil and natural gas passed before the conflict.

The proposal was widely criticised as difficult to enforce and raised questions over its compatibility with long-standing principles of freedom of navigation.

Although Washington has never ratified the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea, successive US administrations have treated many of its navigation provisions as customary international law.

The proposal also appeared to contradict recent US messaging on the issue. During a visit to the region last month, Secretary of State Marco Rubio said international law did not permit countries to impose tolls on vessels transiting waterways such as the Strait of Hormuz and stressed that freedom of navigation had to be protected.

Speaking after a meeting with Iraqi Prime Minister Mohammed Shia al-Sudani on Tuesday, Trump appeared to embrace a similar position, saying Gulf leaders had contacted him and proposed investment agreements as an alternative to transit charges.

“I was called by different people, different countries, kings and emirs,” Trump told reporters, saying they had offered to invest “billions and billions of dollars” in the United States instead of paying the fee.

“I don’t think anybody should be able to charge a fee for the strait or for any other strait,” he added, while maintaining that the US proposal had been intended as reimbursement for protecting the waterway.

China calls for resumption of normal shipping in Strait of Hormuz

China, one of the world’s largest importers of Gulf energy supplies, also renewed calls on Tuesday for the restoration of normal shipping through the strategic waterway.

“Respect for the lawful rights and interest of the coastal countries of the Strait of Hormuz and an early resumption of normal and safe passage at the strait is what the international community wants to see,” Chinese foreign ministry spokesman Lin Jian said during a regular briefing in Beijing.

Lin urged all parties to “remain calm and exercise restraint”, safeguard the ceasefire and avoid a return to wider conflict in the Gulf region.

“Relevant parties need to work in the same direction and seek a proper settlement,” he said.

The spokesman did not directly answer a question about whether Beijing would take concrete steps to offset the impact of US measures against Iran or deepen economic and military cooperation with Tehran.

Instead, he said China would continue working to de-escalate the conflict in line with proposals previously put forward by President Xi Jinping.

US to carry out ‘full blockade’ of vessels trading with Iran

Despite dropping the toll, Trump said Washington would proceed with what he called a “FULL Blockade” targeting vessels travelling to or from Iranian ports or carrying Iranian cargo.

The US military has said the blockade will resume at midnight on Wednesday in Dubai, restoring a measure Washington suspended under an interim agreement with Tehran.

Under that deal, the United States ended a blockade imposed in April, while Iran agreed to permit commercial vessels to pass safely through the strait during a 60-day negotiating period.

The agreement was intended to halt the fighting, reopen the waterway and allow negotiators to work towards a broader settlement addressing the conflict and Iran’s nuclear programme.

Renewed attacks by the United States and Iran have placed the arrangement in jeopardy, however, with both sides accusing the other of violating its terms.

US Central Command said American forces struck Iranian coastal defence systems, missile and drone sites and maritime capabilities on Tuesday.

“These strikes will continue imposing a heavy cost on Iranian forces and degrade their ability to attack innocent civilians and commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz,” the US military said.

Iran strikes vessels, US partners in response to American attacks on its bases

Iran responded with attacks targeting several US partners in the region and commercial vessels that had passed through the strait, according to regional governments and shipping companies.

Two vessels linked to the United Arab Emirates were set ablaze, killing one mariner and wounding eight others, according to the Emirati Defence Ministry.

A tanker operated by Dutch shipping company Stolt Tankers was also attacked off Oman, sparking a fire in its engine room. The company said the crew was safe.

The renewed fighting has intensified concerns that the interim deal could collapse altogether and that the conflict could again disrupt one of the world’s most important energy corridors.

Beijing has sought to maintain close ties with Iran while also preserving relations with Gulf Arab states, whose energy exports are important to the Chinese economy.

Trump’s decision to withdraw the toll may ease concerns among Gulf governments and commercial shippers that the United States was preparing to charge for passage through an international waterway.

But the expected resumption of the blockade underscores that Washington is continuing to escalate pressure on Tehran, even as regional mediators try to prevent the collapse of the interim agreement and a return to full-scale war.
