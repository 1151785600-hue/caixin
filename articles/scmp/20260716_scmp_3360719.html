Philippines’ iconic jeepneys face rocky road to go electric

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1049 words | 2026-07-16

For generations, jeepneys – brightly painted minibuses that are among the Philippines’ cheapest and most used forms of public transport – have run on diesel, but recent fuel shocks are forcing thousands of small operators and drivers to weigh volatile prices against the debt needed to go electric.

Diesel prices spiked anew by nearly 5 pesos this week, as renewed tensions in the Gulf threatened to strain the country’s public transport sector, giving new urgency to the uneven shift towards e-jeepneys.

Since the Iran conflict began in late February, diesel prices have soared, leading to an exodus of jeepney drivers from the road. Fuel costs in Metro Manila hit a high of 114 pesos (US$1.90) per litre in March – nearly one-sixth of the minimum daily wage rate for non-agricultural workers in the Philippine capital region.

Inflation settled at 6.4 per cent in June, while diesel prices rose to 76 pesos this week from 72 pesos a week ago – well above pre-conflict figures of 60 pesos per litre.

The latest increase has put the country’s public transport sector on edge while also highlighting a deeper dilemma: electric jeepneys promise lower running costs, but remain difficult for many small operators and drivers to afford.

“We’re expecting diesel to climb again. The media has been saying that the stockpile will only last over 40 days … what about us? We might see another scenario where diesel climbs to 100 pesos per litre. This would be a huge blow to the transport sector,” said Liberty de Luna, national president of the Alliance of Concerned Transport Organisations (ACTO) in the Philippines.

The country remains particularly vulnerable to continued price shocks from the Iran conflict because it imports more than 95 per cent of its oil supply. Energy officials say it could take months before the Philippines sees a return to pre-conflict prices.

Another transport group, Pasang Masda, urged the government to restore the 1-peso fare increase that was suspended earlier this year, citing the growing financial burden on the public transport sector.

De Luna said more ACTO members were now considering shifting to e-jeepneys as diesel prices continued to spiral out of control.

“We’ve begun embracing e-jeepneys, especially now with the diesel crisis. Because if some of us are already on electric and others are still on diesel, at least if diesel becomes unaffordable, those on e-jeepneys can keep serving the public,” she said.

“If there’s no more affordable diesel and no electric alternatives either, how do we serve the riding public?”

In April, the Department of Trade and Industry (DTI) launched a 2 billion-peso financing programme to help licensed small public transport operators and drivers transition to electric vehicles amid the fuel crisis.

The DTI began offering loans of up to 1.5 million pesos for an EV, allowing operators and drivers to borrow as much as 3 million pesos, and payable within five years. The repayment of principal and interest will be waived for the first year.

While only a small number of ACTO members had begun the EV shift, de Luna said their experiences were so far promising.

“With EV, we have lower spending. In two hours, we can fast-charge a vehicle good for 200km. If we’re spending 1,500 or 2,000 pesos for diesel, with electric, it will come around to 700 pesos. So the savings are huge,” she said.

Playing catch-up

The Philippines is now fast catching up to its Southeast Asian neighbours in EV adoption as motorists find ways to insulate themselves from rising fuel prices.

Sales for EVs, including battery and hybrid vehicles, surged by 133.5 per cent between January and May this year compared with the same period in 2025, according to data from the Chamber of Automotive Manufacturers of the Philippines and the Truck Manufacturers Association.

The trend appears to be crossing over to the public transport sector, with certain manufacturers keen to capitalise on the growing demand.

Japan-based EV start-up ASSEMBLEPOINT opened a manufacturing plant in Cavite province in February, just before the Gulf conflict led to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

The company, which builds 10-seater electric minibuses and aims to sell them nationwide, is dealing with a surge in interest, leading to a backlog in orders as it waits for imported components to arrive, according to an NHK report.

However, not everyone is keen to jump on the EV hype.

Transport group Piston has pointed out gaps in the Philippines’ bid to boost adoption of e-jeepneys, noting that the livelihoods of drivers and small operators who cannot afford the units are at risk due to the Public Transport Modernisation Programme that forces them to consolidate franchises into cooperatives.

Sandy Mae Gaspay, an associate professor at the University of the Philippines College of Engineering, acknowledged that the fuel crisis boosted awareness of e-jeepney benefits, but said that was only one aspect of the issue.

“A bigger question is if jeepney operators can afford to buy new vehicles in the first place. This is why the EV discussion for jeepneys is difficult to separate from the Public Transport Modernisation Programme, which has its long list of issues,” Gaspay told This Week in Asia, referencing the initiative to replace old vehicles with eco-friendly alternatives.

Aside from operators’ financial readiness, Gaspay pointed to gaps such as the lack of government support in establishing EV systems including charging and electric line adjustments.

“Those operating EVs need to set up these systems by themselves. This shifts what is usually the state’s burden to the operators,” she said.

The EV surge also created new technological dependency through battery acquisition and management systems that operators must finance privately, she pointed out.

“There are several who have opted to lease batteries from Chinese companies on a monthly basis. While these arrangements are beneficial for operators with capital constraints, they create perpetual monthly expenses that operators must absorb indefinitely.”

Gaspay said the government should boost support for the public transport sector’s transition to EVs, including implementing dedicated “green routes” for electric minibuses, and establishing facilities such as shared charging or battery-swapping stations to reduce the overall cost of owning e-jeepneys.

She also pointed to incentives such as service-contracting programmes to help EV drivers pay back their loans while allowing collectives to explore alternative income streams.
