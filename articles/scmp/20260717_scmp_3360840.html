‘Gateway to Asean’: South Korea bets on Vietnam hub for food expansion

SCMP | FREE | 1028 words | 2026-07-17

South Korean food exporters are no longer treating Vietnam as just another market for kimchi, instant noodles and premium fruit.

As Southeast Asia emerges as one of South Korea’s most important trading regions, Seoul is positioning Vietnam as a logistics and distribution base for a wider push to turn the popularity of Korean culture into sustained demand for Korean food across the region.

But analysts said the strategy would depend on more than Hallyu-driven curiosity, with exporters needing to pair cultural appeal with cold-chain reliability, local adaptation and a clearer premium proposition for increasingly selective Southeast Asian consumers.

South Korea’s Ministry of Agriculture, Food and Rural Affairs said on July 7 that it had selected Vietnam as a regional hub for expanding the logistics, distribution and supply chains of Korean food exports to Southeast Asia, following the Asean K-Food Fair 2026 in Hanoi.

The plan includes a new multimodal logistics hub in Hanoi, built jointly by the ministry and the Korea Agro-Fisheries and Food Trade Corp – its distribution agency – to help Korean food exporters cut transport costs, preserve product quality and serve growing regional demand linked to the Hallyu wave.

The Hanoi hub will feature advanced systems to store and maintain the quality of fresh agricultural and livestock products, including rapid cooling and freezing rooms and cold-chain packaging.

The facility will allow Korean exporters to bring “not only high-value fresh Korean agricultural products like strawberries, grapes and pears but also premium livestock products to local consumers in Vietnam”.

The fair also gave Seoul a platform to deepen commercial links: 45 Korean food exporters met 107 Asean importers and signed 46 memorandums of understanding worth US$21 million.

Food is still a smaller part of South Korea’s export machine, but it is growing quickly: K-Food Plus exports reached a record US$13.62 billion in 2025, including US$10.41 billion in agri-food products, and Seoul has set a US$16 billion target for 2026.

Southeast Asia is one of South Korea’s biggest trading partners and its fourth-largest consumer goods export market, accounting for 15 per cent of total exports.

Korean exports of agri-food, cosmetics, household goods, fashion and pharmaceuticals to the region reached US$6.9 billion last year.

‘Hub and spokes’ network

Seoul’s partnership with Hanoi reflects Southeast Asia’s importance as a strategic growth region for Korean food companies, according to Brian JY Yoo, CEO of CareerLab and director of trade analysis firm Dallant Consulting Group.

“From my consulting experience, Korean seafood companies are no longer viewing Vietnam solely as an end market. Increasingly, they see Vietnam as their operational gateway into Asean,” Yoo said, drawing on his work with seafood exporters expanding into Vietnam and the wider region.

Vietnam’s growing middle class, modern retail channels, strong tourism industry and proximity to neighbouring markets made it especially important, he said.

The growing Vietnamese community in South Korea also strengthened cultural exchange, creating demand for authentic products after people returned home, while K-pop, K-dramas, K-beauty and social media continued to support long-term demand among younger consumers, Yoo said.

Hyun-je Woo, CEO of export company Mirako Trade, described Southeast Asia as a “rapidly evolving” region for South Korean businesses.

Woo said while demand had once been driven largely by the cultural spillover of the Hallyu wave, Southeast Asian consumers increasingly viewed K-food “as a premium segment that guarantees rigorous quality control”.

The real opportunity, he said, lay in a structural shift across the region that pushed consumers from price-sensitive buying towards value-based consumption, particularly in health and wellness.

“This makes the region not just a large market, but a highly scalable one for those of us who focus on quality and efficient logistics,” said Woo, who focuses on exports to emerging markets such as Southeast Asia.

Woo said the partnership with Vietnam would help South Korean exporters move away from costly direct shipping to individual Southeast Asian markets, and towards a “hub and spokes” network that could consolidate goods before regional distribution.

For temperature-sensitive products, he said, direct less-than-container load shipments were often too expensive, but a Vietnam hub would allow exporters to achieve “full-container-load economies of scale”.

Vietnam’s improving cold-chain infrastructure would also help shorten lead times, reduce costs and extend shelf life, Woo said.

Yoo cited Vietnam’s improving logistics network, with distribution centres around Ho Chi Minh City and the country’s north, that would allow South Korean exporters to consolidate shipments to regional destinations including Thailand, Cambodia, Malaysia and Singapore.

Increasing premium

Korean exporters are also looking beyond familiar staples such as instant noodles, snacks and kimchi.

As Korean food marts and convenience stores grow in popularity across Southeast Asia, the region could see stronger demand for Korean-style meal kits, according to Kyung-yeon Koh, a trade consultant and adviser for How2Go Korea.

“With meal kits being increasingly popular in Korea, I think there are also opportunities for these kits to reach Southeast Asia, where there is demand for not only K-food but the also K-experience of enjoying a Korean meal,” Koh said.

While consumers valued authentic flavours, especially when Korean food was linked to the culture they encountered through entertainment, companies also aimed to broaden appeal by adapting products to local markets, including through halal certification, she added.

Beyond ready-to-eat meals, Yoo said premium seafood, seaweed, laver products, sauces and healthy beverages showed strong growth potential in Southeast Asia.

Korean exporters, he said, “increasingly differentiate themselves with processing technology, strict food safety systems, premium packaging and sustainability rather than competing only on price”.

Woo predicted the strongest growth would come from a broader health and wellness category, including functional foods, dietary supplements and inner-beauty items.

Such products had a high value-to-volume ratio, he said, which helped absorb logistics costs and deliver higher margins for exporters and local distributors.

Yoo was confident that Korean products would continue to command premium positioning because consumers associated them with food safety, high standards and innovation.

“Consumers purchasing Korean food are often buying trust and authenticity as much as the product itself,” he said.
