Why China’s technical schools offer both hope and despair for jobless college grads

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 1092 words | 2026-07-08

China’s technical schools offer hope to desperate university graduates who cannot find a job – but for many, the reality falls short of the promise.

As China’s university graduates face mounting pressure in the job market, a growing number are turning to an unconventional lifeline: vocational training schools. The phenomenon, known as “returning to the furnace”, involves degree-holders enrolling in technical programmes to acquire practical, employable skills.

The trend is gaining measurable momentum. According to a survey and field research by the Chinese Society for Technical and Vocational Education, which covers 105 technical schools, the number of institutions offering formal programmes for university graduates rose from 26 in 2023 to 45 in 2025.

And in 2024, Zhaopin, a major Chinese recruitment platform, reported that 52.2 per cent of graduates believed that returning to technical school would improve their job prospects.

To meet that demand, vocational colleges have rolled out full-time technical classes and short-term skills courses, many with “guaranteed employment” and “state-owned enterprise placements” as key selling points. Some institutions even waive entrance exams, requiring only registration and tuition payment for admission.

The graduates taking this path do so for various reasons, according to a report by the Henan Association for Science and Technology in May.

With little experience and facing a slump in the property sector, Zhou Jingbo, a civil engineering graduate, repeatedly failed in his attempts to secure an interior design role. So, he enrolled at Zhengzhou Technician College to study electrical automation, hoping a practical trade would turn his fortunes around, according to the article.

Fu Linpo, a business English major, watched his field lose ground to AI almost as soon as he started university. After brief, unrewarding jobs in customs translation and electronics repair, he abandoned his degree and, like Zhou, went into the same electrical automation programme, drawn by his interest in electronics.

Cao Hengyuan, a traditional Chinese pharmacy student, reportedly took a different path. During his internship year, he spotted a talent gap in the refractory materials industry, signed up for a course at Zhengzhou City Vocational College, and is now focusing on both theory and practice to break into that sector.

“Instructors here find that these ‘returned’ university graduates have clearer learning objectives, stronger cognitive abilities and pick up skills much faster,” said Luo Yuqi, a Zhengzhou Technician College instructor.

“Our technician class for graduates can complete three projects in a week, whereas other classes manage only one.”

The decision to embrace vocational education is an unusual one. China’s secondary-school tracking system funnels some students into academic tracks and others directly into technical schools. Traditional social attitudes have long favoured the university path, with many families investing heavily in tutoring to keep their children out of vocational education.

Yet data suggests that the realities of the job market are challenging this long-standing bias towards academic credentials.

According to a People’s Daily report on May 9, more than 70 per cent of new, highly skilled talent for modern industries over the past five years has come from vocational education. The report said the employment rate for higher vocational graduates had stayed around 90 per cent – and above 95 per cent at top schools.

But not every “return to the furnace” proves worthwhile, and many have found the vocational track falling short of its promises. An article published on June 10 in the Economic Observer offered three cautionary tales.

Wang Hui, an environmental design graduate, reportedly cycled through three short-lived jobs in the collapsing real estate sector – unpaid, unfairly dismissed, and eventually working 15-hour warehouse shifts – before spotting an advertisement from a Qingdao vocational college that promised: “Upon graduation, recommended for placement in local state-owned enterprises.” He enrolled.

According to the report, Yuan Bing, a private university graduate, left his unstable campus counselling post and saw a stint at a major carmaker as a ticket abroad. He signed up for a car-repair technician class, drawn by promises of direct entry into work for a well-known car company.

Du Song, a computer science major, found his human resources internship dull and wanted a skills certificate to bolster his chances in the civil service exams. In 2024, he found a school in Chongqing offering a convenient path to an advanced skills certificate – and the first cohort was tuition-free.

All three made the same calculation: that a technical school diploma could be a springboard to something better – a major firm, overseas work or a stable government job.

But the gap between promise and reality became clear the moment they stepped into the classroom.

Wang Hui and Yuan Bing had the same letdown, the Economic Observer reported. Their classes, originally meant for bachelor’s degree holders, were merged with younger students because of low enrolment.

Teaching was painfully slow and basic; instructors often resorted to blackboard lectures, and hands-on practice was scarce – Yuan’s workshop had just one car shared among four classes, allowing only half-hour sessions per group.

Wang’s promised state-firm jobs turned out to be low-paying service posts, such as canteen work.

Du Song fared slightly better. His computer class was also mixed with younger students, but the instructors had won skills competitions, the article said. He obtained a Level 3 advanced worker certificate in animation production, which he valued. Still, he had seen through the school’s “guaranteed employment” pledge as mere rhetoric before he joined.

When it came time to job-hunt once more, these students found themselves relying on their own resources.

Wang Hui found a job at a foreign-owned factory on his own and dropped out; five of his six classmates also quit, unhappy with the school’s job referrals. The one who stayed did not take the recommended post either.

Yuan Bing turned down the school’s labour offers and now works as a spray-painting apprentice – a role he calls “life-shortening”. He plans to gain experience and move abroad in four to five years, according to the Economic Observer.

Du Song remained an instructor, but reportedly regretted what he called a wasted year and was still preparing to take civil service exams.

Still, the tide of graduates turning to technical schools shows no sign of receding.

In April, Beijing announced it would launch six full-time technician classes and 21 skills training courses for university graduates, with internships at partner firms – part of a broader national push as the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Security unveiled a “million-youth upskilling initiative” and urged local authorities to offer similar programmes to meet young people’s skills needs.
