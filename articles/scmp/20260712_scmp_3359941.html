Shares soar as recently-listed Zhipu AI and Iluvatar CoreX launch secondary placements

SCMP | FREE | 438 words | 2026-07-12

Shares of Chinese artificial intelligence model developer Zhipu AI and chipmaker Iluvatar CoreX Semiconductor surged on Thursday after the tech companies launched separate secondary share placements to raise billions of Hong Kong dollars for hardware and research.

Zhipu AI, which trades as Knowledge Atlas Technology, saw its stock surge 11.3 per cent to HK$2,032 (US$259.23) on Thursday after launching an accelerated bookbuild to raise HK$31.4 billion. The company is offering 19.8 million primary shares at HK$1,588 each, representing a 13 per cent discount to Wednesday’s close.

Shanghai Iluvatar CoreX Semiconductor gained 5.9 per cent to HK$593 as the graphics processing unit (GPU) maker prepares to raise HK$7.1 billion by offering 14.9 million shares at HK$476 each, a 15 per cent discount to Wednesday’s closing price.

Investor appetite remained robust despite the recent expiration of lock-up periods following the companies’ initial public offerings – which freed up a significant tranche of cornerstone investor shares.

Surging valuations and favourable market sentiment have provided a window for the two mainland Chinese firms to lock in heavy funding to pursue their chip ambitions.

Their share sales also exemplify an increasingly common playbook for newly listed Chinese tech companies to quickly return to Hong Kong’s equity markets shortly after their market debuts.

In a stock exchange filing on Thursday, two days after its lock-up period expired, Zhipu said rapidly growing customer demand and expanding application scenarios required more substantial capital reserves to support research and development, computing infrastructure, commercial expansion and global ecosystem building.

Zhipu, the developer of the high-performing GLM-5.2 model, is in early talks with domestic chip design companies to develop tailored AI processors as its daily token usage spikes, according to a report on Tuesday by The Information, a tech-focused media outlet based in the United States.

That would mirror a recent move by domestic rival DeepSeek, which is also reportedly looking to custom silicon to drive down the costs of running large language models. However, analysts warn that such efforts will require heavy upfront investment and could take years to yield positive financial returns.

Meanwhile, Iluvatar said in its filing that it “identified post-listing funding requirements that were not fully contemplated at the time of the global offering”. The firm said it intended to use the proceeds to strengthen supply-chain resilience, accelerate next-generation GPU iteration and secure strategic procurement.

Minimax, a major AI model developer in China whose lock-up period ended on Wednesday, saw its share price drop 18 per cent on Thursday, dragging its market cap below HK$100 billion. Its lock-up expiry freed up 150 million shares for trading – just under half of those issued.
