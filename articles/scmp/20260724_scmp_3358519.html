As China’s tech firms adapt to AI era, workers worry they’ll be ‘optimised’ out of a job

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 2082 words | 2026-07-24

When a friend checked in on a Meituan employee late last month to see if he had survived the latest round of corporate culling at the food delivery giant, he responded drily: “I don’t know whether it will be me next.”

Anxieties were running high at the company then, after chat screenshots circulated on Chinese social media claiming Meituan planned to slash up to half of its product roles by the end of June, coupled with deep cuts to other departments.

While Meituan quickly denied the rumours, the viral speculation struck a sensitive nerve across China’s tech industry. Behind the online claims, employees whisper that a quieter, more insidious form of retrenchment has been under way for months.

For years, tech workers in China have harboured a deep-seated dread of the word youhua – “optimisation”. The term serves as corporate shorthand for painful lay-offs, often masked by euphemisms like “organisational restructuring”.

This year, however, the term carries a chilling new undertone. The increasingly urgent question echoing through office corridors is no longer whether a worker is performing, but whether their job can be done by artificial intelligence.

Meituan is far from an isolated case. From Baidu to Xiaomi, China’s technology giants have been trimming their teams, according to internal sources and tech recruiters.

The agents arrive

The shift follows prophetic warnings from global tech leaders. Last year, Dario Amodei, CEO of US generative AI pioneer Anthropic, cautioned that the technology’s rapid evolution could wipe out half of all entry-level white-collar jobs within five years.

Today, the tools driving that shift are capable of far more than conversations and data retrieval. The industry has rapidly pivoted from basic chatbots to autonomous “AI agents” – systems capable of writing code, conducting market research, analysing legal documents and executing multi-step business tasks.

Anthropic said that as of last month, more than 80 per cent of the code merged into its codebase had been written by its Claude AI model.

Software engineers, long viewed as possessing one of the most secure and sophisticated white-collar skill sets, have found themselves on the front lines of this automation wave. Coding, it turns out, is a task where AI models excel.

We now require engineers to be full-stack ... even if they are doing it side-by-side with AI

Rose Cao, talent acquisition specialist

“I have barely written a line of code by hand for almost a year,” said a veteran AI developer known to the public as Tuxi, who has worked for several of China’s major technology companies.

At a live-streamed industry event this month, senior Tencent Holdings executive Dowson Tong said most of the tech giant’s code written this year was generated by AI systems.

Tong said human engineers were becoming supervisors, spending more time focused on architecture and system design. They delegated the coding work to AI and only stepped in periodically to guide and correct its output.

Such changes have significantly raised the baseline requirements for landing or keeping a tech job.

“We now require engineers to be full-stack,” said Rose Cao, a talent acquisition specialist at a Chinese AI firm, adding that “even if they are doing it side-by-side with AI”, programmers were now expected to handle everything from front-end to back-end tasks.

The automation contagion

AI automation is expanding beyond the engineering department. Specialised tools originally built for programmers – such as OpenAI’s Codex or Anthropic’s Claude Code – are evolving to support broader corporate workflows.

A parallel ecosystem of enterprise AI assistants has sprung up across China: Moonshot AI has pushed out Kimi Work; Tencent has rolled out WorkBuddy; ByteDance has launched Coze; and Alibaba Group Holding has introduced QoderWork CN. Alibaba is the owner of the South China Morning Post.

By embedding such tools into daily operations, businesses are eroding some of the traditional boundaries between various corporate functions, allowing a single employee to fulfil tasks that previously required a team of specialised workers.

Rather than being an optional résumé highlight, a headhunter who specialises in tech talent said that “AI fluency has become a requirement for many employers”, creating a hostile environment for fresh university graduates and inexperienced workers.

“It is hard to persuade an employer to hire a newcomer and spend more time and money training them when AI agents can already do a good job – and won’t quit,” she said.

It is a development that has prompted expressions of deep concern from some of the country’s leading economic minds. Cai Fang, a prominent labour economist and member of the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, has warned that the initial shock wave of the AI boom will disproportionately hit “the young and the old”.

Cai’s research shows that traditional employment rates by age form an inverted U-shaped curve, where middle-aged workers experience the highest job stability, while younger and older cohorts face greater vulnerability.

He said AI was reinforcing that divide by eroding the economic value of entry-level skills for younger workers and also widening the digital capability gap for older employees who struggled to adapt to rapid technological advancement.

The economic data underlines the stakes.

China’s youth unemployment rate for 16- to 24-year-olds, excluding students, fell to 15.6 per cent in May but remained much higher than that of the whole urban population, according to data released by the National Bureau of Statistics on Monday. The Ministry of Education has estimated a record 12.7 million university graduates are preparing to enter the labour market.

A convenient scapegoat?

But some insiders argued that blaming AI for the wave of lay-offs misdiagnosed a conventional corporate malaise.

“To say that companies are laying people off just because AI has improved workflows is basically a lie,” said Tuxi, the veteran developer, who argued that many of China’s largest tech conglomerates spent years inflating their headcount. AI had simply provided executives with a convenient justification to trim the fat, he said.

Global tech leaders have also pushed back against the narrative that AI is the primary engine of global job cuts. In an interview last month with Singaporean broadcaster CNA, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang dismissed a direct causal link between AI deployment and tech lay-offs as an explanation that was “too lazy”.

He argued that the implementation timeline of enterprise AI had not matched spikes in unemployment, suggesting that the volatility was driven by a mix of over-hiring and the staggering costs required to develop AI.

That financial strain can be seen among China’s tech titans, where resources are being aggressively diverted to fund the AI arms race.

TikTok owner ByteDance has ramped up investment in AI infrastructure this year, boosting its planned capital expenditure to over 200 billion yuan (US$29.5 billion) – an increase of at least 25 per cent compared with preliminary internal budgets discussed late last year, the SCMP reported last month.

Several current and former employees said ByteDance had been consolidating business lines to free up headcount and computing power for its flagship AI products, including its AI assistant Doubao, its AI coding environment Trae, and the Coze agent platform.

The internal realignment coincided with the rapid expansion of Seed, ByteDance’s foundation-model team. Launched in 2023, the Seed team has swollen to about 2,000 people.

Meituan is playing defence and offence. After weathering a brutal local-services price war with Alibaba that squeezed its profit margins last year, the company has trimmed secondary teams, according to employees.

However, it continues to recruit at the high end. Meituan’s “Beidou” talent programme for top-tier campus technology recruits features openings focused on next-generation frontiers: AI infrastructure, large language models, embodied intelligence, autonomous agents and drones.

Alibaba has also been trimming its headcount and pivoting its sprawling business towards AI. The e-commerce giant shed around 34 per cent of its staff last year, mainly by offloading offline retail operations, according to corporate disclosures.

The macroeconomic impact of AI on employment was expected to be neutral in the long run, GF Securities’ chief economist Guo Lei wrote in a recent report. Still, in the short term, the market might need to navigate a painful “displacement effect” before it could unlock the “reinstatement effect”, he said.

Guo said AI would fundamentally redistribute jobs, reshape the nature of jobs and alter income structures. Workers stuck in less differentiated roles would lose their labour market bargaining power, while those who mastered the use of AI stood to see their productivity soar.

The regulatory balancing act

As the transition accelerates, Chinese tech firms are steering clear of the blunt rhetoric used by some of their Silicon Valley counterparts.

In February, Block founder Jack Dorsey said the US payments firm would reduce its workforce from more than 10,000 to under 6,000, pointing explicitly to the deployment of “intelligence tools” and flatter, leaner organisational structures.

We must do everything possible to protect the livelihoods of hundreds of thousands of employees

Richard Liu Qiangdong, JD.com chairman

In contrast, Chinese tech companies rarely, if ever, admit publicly that human workers are being replaced by algorithms.

Richard Liu Qiangdong, the billionaire founder and chairman of e-commerce powerhouse JD.com – which employed 951,420 people across the company and its affiliates at the end of last year – stressed the company’s responsibility to protect jobs during a speech to staff last month.

“Facing the impact of new technologies … we must do everything possible to protect the livelihoods of hundreds of thousands of employees, including the jobs of our blue-collar brothers,” Liu said, making a reference to the company’s extensive logistics network.

At a recent Apec panel discussion, he said JD.com would retrain its roughly 700,000 delivery and other blue-collar workers in robot maintenance as AI and automation reshape last-mile logistics.

Beijing has stepped up regulatory oversight to ensure the AI transition does not trigger widespread social friction.

The State Council’s “AI Plus” action plan, released in August, called for stronger assessment of employment risks from AI applications, more AI skills training and guiding innovation resources towards areas with greater job creation potential, with the aim of reducing the technology’s impact on employment.

More recently, the State Council unveiled its employment blueprint for the five years ending in 2030. It placed a new mandate at the top of its agenda, saying the country must “strengthen the employment-first orientation of macroeconomic regulation … and adapt to the development of AI to promote employment and entrepreneurship”.

Chinese courts and labour arbitration bodies have also begun drawing limits around AI-related dismissals.

In April, a Hangzhou court ruled that a financial technology firm had unlawfully terminated a quality-control supervisor who had been auditing large language model outputs.

The company fired him after citing the cost advantages of replacing his labour with AI. The worker had rejected an internal transfer that would have slashed his monthly salary from 25,000 yuan to 15,000 yuan.

The court ruled that while adopting AI constituted a voluntary business choice and a standard business risk, it did not automatically qualify as a “major change in objective circumstances” – the legal threshold required under Chinese labour law to declare an employment contract impossible to perform.

The court declared that the fundamental purpose of AI should be to “liberate labour, promote employment and improve people’s livelihoods”.

In an essay prepared for this month’s Lujiazui Forum, an annual financial gathering in Shanghai, Cai, the economist, said that while AI offered a chance to lift China’s labour productivity, its disruptive side effects could not be ignored.

He warned of the risk of a widening of the digital divide between tech-forward and legacy industries, urban and rural areas, and individual workers, with short-term job displacement highly probable, creating a broader macroeconomic risk that would see household consumption lag rapid productivity gains.

The solution, Cai said, was to “invest in people and ensure that intelligence is put towards good”.

But worries persist for those toiling away in the cubicles of China’s tech hubs. Amid corporate denials of sweeping lay-offs, the quiet reality of “optimisation” continues to play out one desk at a time.

Confronted by what he viewed as an uncertain employment future, the Meituan employee contacted by a friend said “some outsourced workers have already been cleared out”, though he did not know the exact proportion.

While lay-offs happened every year, “this round feels quite big,” he said.
