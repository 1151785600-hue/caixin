Malaysia’s new warship can hunt submarines but cannot sink a foe

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 721 words | 2026-07-15

Malaysia’s navy is preparing to take delivery of a warship fully equipped for anti-submarine, anti-air and electronic warfare – everything, that is, except a way to sink an enemy ship.

After nearly a decade of delays, financial scandal and shipyard mismanagement the Royal Malaysian Navy’s first littoral combat ship is finally due in December. But it will arrive missing any anti-ship missiles.

Norway’s decision to revoke the export licence for the planned Naval Strike Missile system has left Kuala Lumpur scrambling for an alternative, while the ship itself sails ahead regardless.

Defence officials insist the timeline is unchanged. “The decision does not affect the delivery schedule,” the ministry told parliament in a written reply on July 7, noting the vessel would still carry anti-submarine, anti-air and electronic warfare systems.

What the ship will not carry, for now, is a way to strike enemy ships – the capability that was meant to define the class.

Military analyst Collin Koh, a senior fellow at Singapore’s Institute of Defence and Strategic Studies, said on social media that the missing missile system left “a gaping hole in conventional naval deterrence”.

Yet Koh and other regional defence specialists argue that delay would be the costlier mistake.

With Turkey and South Korea now in the frame as potential missile suppliers, taking the ship now – rather than waiting for a resolved weapons package – lets Malaysia start training crews, testing systems and catching faults early.

More than 60 per cent of Malaysia’s naval fleet is now over 40 years old, navy chief Admiral Zulhelmy Ithnain revealed earlier this month, driving up maintenance costs and straining operations.

Getting new platforms into Malaysian waters, especially the contested South China Sea, matters more right now than firepower, according to Abdul Rahman Yaacob of the Rabdan Security and Defence Institute in the United Arab Emirates.

“At this stage, while an offensive strike capability would certainly be desirable, it is not critical,” he said, adding that early deployment meant technical and logistical problems “can be addressed alongside the integration of a new missile system once a suitable solution has been selected and procured”.

The importance of that search was highlighted by Muhammad Syameer Luthfy Hamzah, a research fellow at the International Islamic University of Malaysia, who cautioned that missile selection should not be treated as a footnote.

The search for a replacement surface-to-surface missile system will be crucial

Muhammad Syameer Luthfy Hamzah, defence analyst

“The missile is not a minor component,” he said. “It forms part of the ship’s offensive combat capability and, by extension, its contribution to conventional naval deterrence.”

Stripped of such a system, the vessel can still handle maritime surveillance, disaster relief and search-and-rescue work, but Malaysia already has an agency for that.

The navy was never meant to double as a coastguard, Luthfy said – and the littoral combat ship “was not procured merely as a patrol vessel”.

“For this reason, the search for a replacement surface-to-surface missile system will be crucial,” he added.

A troubled history

The five Maharaja Lela-class littoral combat ships, scaled down from an original order of six, are costing Malaysia roughly US$2.55 billion, or US$510 million each.

Built to the French Naval Group’s Gowind-class design, they are due for delivery in stages through 2029.

Originally due in 2019, mismanagement at the debt-laden Boustead Naval Shipyard caused a series of delays, forcing the finance ministry to step in and rebrand the yard as Lumut.

Public sentiment soured against the programme in the scandal’s wake, but has since shifted again as the need for new ships becomes harder to ignore.

“Malaysia ultimately requires the LCS to possess a credible anti-surface warfare capability,” Luthfy said, using the abbreviation for littoral combat ship.

“Otherwise, questions will naturally arise over whether the RMN [Malaysia’s navy] is receiving the full operational capability for which the programme was designed.”

Rahman also sees a long battle ahead, over budgets and political will as much as missiles.

“Maintaining consistent funding and strategic direction will be essential to ensure the navy’s long-term effectiveness,” he said.

For now, Malaysia’s newest warships look set to slip into service as capable escorts with no sting, hallmarks of a navy modernising in every respect but the one that matters most.
