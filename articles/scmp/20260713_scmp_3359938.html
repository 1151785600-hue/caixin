Hong Kong stocks retreat as regional tech volatility offsets short covering

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 548 words | 2026-07-13

Hong Kong stocks closed mixed on Thursday as a rally in major technology firms and solid mainland inflows offset a broader market sell-off driven by escalating Middle East tensions.

The Hang Seng Index retreated 0.7 per cent to close at 24,030, reversing some of the previous session’s strong gains, while the Hang Seng Tech Index edged up 0.01 per cent.

Among high-profile tech movers, artificial intelligence model developer Knowledge Atlas Technology, also known as Zhipu AI, bucked the downward trend to surge over 11 per cent as volatility surrounding its recent IPO share lock-up expiration eased. In contrast, rival MiniMax tumbled nearly 18 per cent during its first major lock-up expiry.

Semiconductor players also bucked the broader market decline. Smart-Core Holdings and GigaDevice jumped more than 27 per cent and 21 per cent, respectively. Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation surged around 10 per cent.

On the mainland, equities reflected a more resilient underlying sentiment. The SSE Star 50 index, which tracks Shanghai’s tech board, surged more than 8 per cent by the close, while the ChiNext 50 gauge of Shenzhen-listed start-ups gained around 5 per cent. The broader CSI 300 Index advanced 2.5 per cent.

“Both [mainland and Hong Kong] markets were driven by a technical rebound, as the recent sell-off was overdone,” said Hong Hao, chief investment officer at Hong Kong-based Lotus Asset Management.

Supportive news, including a 300 billion yuan (US$44.1 billion) expansion of the southbound trading scheme and the coming red-hot IPO of ChangXin Memory Technologies, was well received by the market, Hong added.

The People’s Bank of China governor Pan Gongsheng announced at the Hong Kong FIC & Bond Connect Summit earlier this week that mainland China’s foreign reserves would continue to increase asset allocations in the city. The annual net investment quota under the Bond Connect scheme would also be raised to 800 billion yuan, up from the current 500 billion yuan.

Sentiment across the broader region took a hit after the US and Iran launched fresh military strikes following the collapse of a temporary ceasefire. The geopolitical flare-up pushed oil prices higher, fuelling concerns about higher global inflation.

Separately, China’s producer price index rose 4.1 per cent, marking its fastest pace since July 2022, stoking fears that rising raw material costs will squeeze factory margins and export inflation globally.

Despite the slide in Hong Kong, market observers noted that foreign institutions were aggressively unwinding their “funding shorts”. Regional corrections have prompted global investors to cover Chinese equity shorts previously used to finance other Asian trades.

“Funding short unwinding and southbound flows have supported China’s latest outperformance,” Laura Wang, chief China equity strategist at Morgan Stanley, wrote in a note on Thursday.

Global investors have used Hong Kong and China as funding shorts for increased allocations to South Korea, Taiwan, and Japan, but the recent correction in those markets has significantly reduced this pressure, she added.

The decline in Hong Kong came as regional bourses stabilised after severe weekly corrections. South Korea’s Kospi index edged up 0.6 per cent on Thursday, a day after the benchmark officially entered a technical bear market by plunging more than 20 per cent from its June peak due to heavy selling in the tech sector. Japan’s Nikkei 225 also staged a modest rebound, rising around 1.4 per cent after days of heavy losses.
