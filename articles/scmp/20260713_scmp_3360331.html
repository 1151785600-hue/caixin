North Korea’s Kim fires warning shot at own military with corruption purge

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 914 words | 2026-07-13

North Korea rarely airs its dirty laundry in public, but Kim Jong-un’s latest purge of a senior military official was staged for maximum visibility – a warning, analysts say, to generals growing too comfortable with their expanding economic power that they answer to him alone.

“Kim is effectively telling military and party leaders: ‘Don’t even think about it. You are under round-the-clock surveillance,’” said Yang Moo-jin, a professor at the University of North Korean Studies.

On Friday, Kim convened a rare joint meeting of the party, state and military – the first of its kind devoted specifically to corruption among senior officials, according to observers – to denounce former major general Pak Hui-chol in unusually public terms.

Pak was, until recently, deputy director for organisational affairs in the Korean People’s Army General Political Bureau, a powerful institution tasked with enforcing ideological conformity, loyalty and ensuring the military stays subordinate to the Workers’ Party through an extensive network of political commissars and instructors.

He was accused of running a four-year scheme that sold promotions, extracted bribes and filled key military posts with loyalists, all while embezzling state funds.

Kim himself described Pak on Friday as “the ringleader of corruption” who had weaponised his authority for personal gain.

The state-run Korean Central News Agency called it an “extra-large crime” of a magnitude that “goes beyond imagination”, while Rodong Sinmun, the party’s official newspaper, denounced Pak and his “confidants and toadies” as greed-obsessed “corrupt elements”.

Echoes of Kim’s uncle

Analysts say it is highly unusual for North Korea to disclose corruption allegations involving such a senior official, let alone publicise the punishment so prominently.

The unprecedented publicity suggests Kim is seeking not only to tighten military discipline but also to reaffirm the party’s absolute control over the armed forces at a time when their influence is expanding beyond national defence.

Yang said Pak’s prosecution harked back to the 2013 purge of Kim’s uncle, Jang Song-thaek, although the charges differ. Jang, once regarded as North Korea’s second most powerful figure, was executed by firing squad two years after Kim took power.

“Jang was executed as an antirevolutionary ringleader, the most serious political charge, while Pak has been punished for corruption,” Yang said. “But like the removal of Jang … this appears to be politics of fear designed to strengthen regime cohesion.”

State media did not disclose Pak’s ultimate fate, but observers suggest that the language used to describe him and his associates – as well as Kim’s labelling of the affair as a “political crime” – suggests that a prison camp or possible execution awaits.

Additional officials implicated in the case have yet to be identified as investigations continue.

In addition to bribery and embezzlement, Pak was accused of cultivating a personal patronage network by placing loyal followers in influential military posts, effectively weakening North Korea’s system of one-man rule.

“By framing the case this way, the regime characterises corruption as a direct political challenge to Kim’s authority,” said Professor Lim Eul-chul of Kyungnam University’s Institute for Far Eastern Studies.

It also provided “indirect evidence of deeply rooted corruption within North Korea’s military leadership”, Lim said, noting the speed of the purge, with the investigation, prosecution and sentencing of Pak reportedly taking little more than a fortnight.

It suggests that practices such as selling promotions … have become institutionalised

Lim Eul-chul, Kyungnam University

“It suggests that practices such as selling promotions, abusing personnel authority and diverting state resources have become institutionalised among senior officers who are officially responsible for national defence while also playing an expanding role in economic development,” he added.

Once confined to guarding North Korea’s borders and advancing its nuclear ambitions, the Korean People’s Army has increasingly become an economic actor in its own right, running mining operations, building housing and driving infrastructure projects, among other responsibilities.

Every new responsibility has brought fresh access to resources, patronage – and opportunities for corruption, analysts say.

“The leadership is emphasising that despite the military’s expanding role in national defence, economic construction and strategic development, it remains fully subordinate to party discipline, organisational oversight and the state’s judicial system,” Lim said.

Ordinary North Koreans enduring chronic shortages of food, construction materials and consumer goods are a secondary audience for this particular purge.

By training public anger on individual “corrupt elites” rather than systemic failures, the state can redirect frustration that might otherwise curdle into something more threatening to the regime, according to Lim.

“By identifying internal enemies, the government can channel public frustration away from systemic policy failures and towards individual officials accused of corruption,” he said.

The campaign also appears designed to reassure the public that even senior military figures are not beyond punishment, reinforcing Kim’s image as the ultimate guardian of party discipline and state authority.

Lim expects Jo Yong-won, one of Kim’s closest confidants, to play a central role in expanding the investigation. Jo was recently reinstated as secretary for organisation and head of the party’s powerful Organisation and Guidance Department after a brief stint in a different role.

Long regarded as Kim’s chief disciplinarian, Jo has spent years overseeing personnel appointments and enforcing party discipline without facing any known disciplinary action himself.

“Kim has brought back the aide he trusts most because eliminating corruption is essential to ensuring the smooth implementation of party policies,” Lim said.
