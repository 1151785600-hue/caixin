Asean at 60: peace, prosperity and polite paralysis

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 2105 words | 2026-07-08

Dan Rae Hugo has never heard of the Asean Petroleum Security Agreement. All he knows is that his diesel costs have doubled, his profit margins have evaporated and the rice on his neighbours’ tables now costs 20 per cent more than it did before the Iran war.

The 43-year-old has been farming the fields of Iloilo, the Philippines, for 18 years. He has never worked harder for less.

“It’s all the inputs: the diesel, the labour for operating the machines, the transportation when we harvest … It’s the highest I’ve ever seen,” Hugo said.

Some 150km (93 miles) from his farm, in the convention halls of Cebu, leaders of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations were meeting to address the very crisis threatening Hugo’s livelihood: a global energy shock affecting all 11 member states.

They agreed – as they have agreed before – to accelerate an energy security framework and push forward a regional power grid that could, in theory, stabilise electricity prices for the nearly 700 million people who call Southeast Asia home.

Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jnr gave voice to the urgency required. “We needed it last month. Forget about next month, six months, a year from now,” he said. “We needed it yesterday, if not sooner.”

His remarks struck at the heart of a long-standing critique of the bloc, that its consensus-driven approach hampers its ability to respond swiftly to crises and renders it little more than a talking shop.

As Asean approaches its 60th anniversary next year, analysts and former diplomats told This Week in Asia that the bloc had, by almost any historical measure, been a remarkable success.

It has kept the peace in a region that was tearing itself apart when it was founded. It has presided over one of the most spectacular economic ascents in human history. It has woven together 11 of the world’s most diverse nations into a functioning diplomatic community that punches well above its collective weight.

And yet, as it prepares to mark six decades of existence, a cluster of compounding crises – from the Iran war energy shock and an unresolved military coup to the disintegrating world order – is putting Asean’s limitations on full display.

Born of distrust

Asean was born in August 1967 in Bangkok, the product of five governments – Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, Singapore and Thailand – who had every reason to distrust one another and chose, improbably, to cooperate.

The Cold War was at its height. Communism was spreading through the region. Indonesia and Malaysia were barely past a military confrontation that had spilled into Singapore, which had itself only just been expelled from the Federation of Malaya.

Save for Thailand, every founding member had recently emerged from colonial rule – under different powers, with different grievances, different languages and different faiths.

That they held together at all was, as former Philippine foreign affairs secretary Delia D. Albert put it, “something to be proud of”.

That they have now held together for 60 years, expanding from five members to 11 and becoming the world’s fifth-largest economic bloc – projected to rank fourth by 2030 – is an even greater achievement.

“Judging it [Asean] according to what it was meant to do and what it has achieved is a healthier way to do it,” Albert told This Week in Asia. “Of course everybody wants it to do more, but one has to understand why it was set up, what it was meant to do and to see what it has done these past 60 years.”

Since 1991, there has been no major armed conflict between Asean members, with the notable exception of the recent Thailand-Cambodia border clashes. The bloc has sealed landmark trade agreements with China, India, Japan and South Korea. And its foundational compact of mutual respect and non-interference, the Treaty of Amity and Cooperation, has been signed by 58 countries.

“It has fostered regional norms of peace and dialogue which have allowed the region to dramatically raise living standards,” said Hunter Marston, director of the Lowy Institute’s Southeast Asia programme, of Asean. “It has created a diplomatic community … which has deepened regional integration.”

But the same principles that made Asean possible – consensus, non-interference, sovereign equality – are now the source of its most persistent failures, analysts say. Decisions that require unanimity tend to arrive late, diluted or not at all.

Agreements signed at summits gather dust as implementation founders on the shoals of competing national interests. The energy security framework Hugo has never heard of has existed, in various forms, for years. The power grid has been in the planning stages for decades.

Asean needs to experiment with some innovative ways to improve decision-making processes

Sharon Seah, foreign policy analyst

“Asean needs to experiment with some innovative ways to improve decision-making processes,” said foreign policy analyst Sharon Seah, a former diplomat who now serves as a principal fellow at the ISEAS–Yusof Ishak Institute in Singapore.

She described the way the bloc currently makes decisions as slow and cumbersome, bogged down by years of expanding red tape.

Albert, who served as the Philippines’ first female foreign secretary under former president Gloria Macapagal Arroyo, is clear-eyed about the trade-off Asean embodies.

“You have to remember that we’re not like the EU, with a supranational body that tells us what to do,” she said. “We are still independent on our own in many ways and that’s the only way it would work.”

Asean, at its heart, was designed to include, not to compel – and inclusion, at scale, across this degree of diversity, demands patience that crises rarely permit.

The Myanmar impasse

Few crises have tested the bloc’s patience more severely than Myanmar.

When the country’s armed forces, known as the Tatmadaw, seized power in February 2021, arrested Aung San Suu Kyi and turned their guns on protesters, Asean moved with uncharacteristic speed.

Within weeks, it had brokered a “five-point consensus” plan with junta chief Min Aung Hlaing as a framework for ceasefire, dialogue and humanitarian access.

For a bloc defined by non-interference in each other’s affairs, it was an unprecedented step. It was also, in the years since, almost entirely ineffective. Min Aung Hlaing prevaricated. Asean barred him from summits. The killing continued.

Thinzar Shulei Yi was barely 20 years old when she first started organising protests against the military dictatorship that had ruled Myanmar for nearly five decades until 2011. She was motivated to act after teaching orphans whose parents had been killed in the long years of civil conflict.

Now 34, she has watched soldiers fire on her friends at protests and seen others disappear into interrogation rooms. Her parents have been harassed by the Tatmadaw.

“Asean has covered its crimes internationally,” she said of the military, “which will make Asean lose trust and legitimacy, not just among Myanmar people but among Asean citizens.”

Tun Khin carries a different wound. The president of the Burmese Rohingya Organisation UK was stripped of his citizenship under Myanmar’s 1982 Citizenship Law, which classified nationality by ethnicity, effectively rendering the Rohingya stateless.

Since the 1970s, his people have been expelled from Rakhine state, denied education and forbidden to marry freely, with hundreds jailed for doing so without permits. He now lives in Britain.

He described the junta’s use of Asean membership as a “protective shield against international pressure”.

“When Asean allowed Myanmar to join, it argued membership would encourage change and reform,” he said, referring to the country’s accession to the bloc in 1997. “Instead, we have seen another coup, genocide and conflict worse than ever.”

Both activists rejected the framing of Myanmar’s crisis as a domestic matter: the refugee flows, the narco-networks and the spillover criminality are already regional problems.

Thinzar Shulei Yi called on Asean to abandon the five-point consensus, end any platforming of the Tatmadaw and channel humanitarian aid directly through civil society. Tun Khin added that measurable, time-bound targets for human rights and democratic progress were required, backed by the credible threat of Myanmar’s expulsion if the benchmarks were not met.

Albert argued against expulsion but conceded that the current approach was failing.

Her solution is to dust off a provision the bloc has long overlooked: the “High Council” – a formal dispute-settlement mechanism embedded in the Treaty of Amity but never activated, which she envisions as a body of respected Asean elder statesmen and women empowered to act without the paralysing constraint of their political positions.

“If Asean wants to be able to solve its own problems, then create that High Council,” she said. “Everybody tells me ‘you are too ambitious’ and ‘you are too naive’, but I say we can try.”

US-China squeeze

Myanmar may be the bloc’s thorniest issue, but it is far from the only one. The international rules-based order that Asean grew up in and shaped to fit its own needs is coming under serious strain.

US-China rivalry has increasingly pushed member states towards bilateral deals on tariffs, investment and security that risk “eventually hollowing out the enduring regional body”, according to foreign affairs analyst Lucio Blanco Pitlo III of the Manila-based Asia-Pacific Pathways to Progress Foundation think tank.

The bloc’s edge lay in its collective clout, he said. If that was fragmented into 11 separate transactional relationships, the institution would lose any power it once had.

“Asean creates a platform to make its voice heard on the international stage and be an active shaper of norms to govern its neighbourhood,” Pitlo said.

Peace is being overtaken by a tendency towards economic and military coercion

Muhammad Faizal bin Abdul Rahman, security analyst

Meanwhile, the global security outlook is more volatile today than it has been in decades.

Muhammad Faizal bin Abdul Rahman, a security analyst at the S. Rajaratnam School of International Studies in Singapore, noted that the regional norms of cooperation Asean had spent decades building were coming under pressure from a new logic.

“Peace is being overtaken by a tendency towards economic and military coercion and cooperation is being undermined by growing strategic distrust,” he said. “It remains to be seen how Asean can improve its platforms while maintaining its neutrality and cohesion.”

Invisible to millions

Back in Cebu, running parallel to the leaders’ summit, the Foreign Policy Community of Indonesia (FPCI) think tank organised “Asean for the Peoples Week” in an attempt to bridge a gap that even the bloc’s former diplomats acknowledge has never been bridged.

Barry Desker, who previously served as Singapore’s representative to the Asean Intergovernmental Commission on Human Rights, identified it as far back as 2017.

Genuine commitment to Asean’s ideals existed largely among policymakers and professionals, he wrote in a commentary.

“For most of the diverse peoples living in Southeast Asia, the idea of an Asean community with shared values and a common identity looking towards a common destiny is a wish still to be fulfilled,” Desker said.

Asean has an official anthem, though the overwhelming majority of the region’s 700 million citizens are unaware of it.

Few take pride or even show interest in which country currently holds the Asean chair, according to Faizal. The institution that has lurked in the backgrounds of their lives for years remains, to most of them, invisible.

“These are symptomatic of the lack of awareness and appreciation of Asean among ordinary citizens in Southeast Asia,” Faizal said.

That invisibility may be the bloc’s deepest long-term vulnerability. Marston suggests that tangible results could change the dynamic, however.

“While its primary dealings are more esoteric and less germane to the everyday lived experience of the people of Asean, if it can successfully mediate disputes like that between Thailand and Cambodia or mitigate rising fuel prices via a strategic petroleum reserve, it may come to be seen as having a more material impact at the grass-roots level,” he said.

Dino Patti Djalal, a former Indonesian envoy and minister who runs the FPCI, said that civil society needed to be woven into the Asean conversation more deliberately, especially in an increasingly multipolar world.

“The people-centred Asean community,” he told a Cebu town hall during the Peoples Week event, “is driven and shaped not just by policies of government … but by the peoples of the region”.

Hugo is one of those people. He does not know what decisions were taken 150km away from his farm last month. But he does know that his diesel costs have never been higher, and he is waiting – as many Southeast Asians have waited before – to see whether the agreements reached in his name will find their way to his fields.
