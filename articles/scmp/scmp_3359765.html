World working together ‘as one ecosystem’ on RISC-V chip research: executive

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 527 words | 2026-07-08

Global cooperation on the RISC-V chip architecture remains resilient despite intense US-China technological rivalry, according to a chip veteran at Chinese e-commerce and AI giant Alibaba Group Holding.

Speaking at the South China Morning Post’s China Conference 2026 on Tuesday, Alibaba vice-president Qi Xiaoning said major companies from around the world including the United States, China, India, Brazil and Europe were working together “as one unified ecosystem” on the RISC-V open-standard chip architecture for edge and cloud applications.

“When we use a single architecture, people from different continents, different cultural backgrounds, we get together, we work together and make friends,” Qi, a semiconductor veteran, told the audience. “And also like trade, [we] promote friendship between different countries.”

“Collaboration [in RISC-V] is already the reality; it’s happening.”

RISC-V, which stands for fifth-generation reduced instruction set computer, is an open-standard instruction set architecture and a highly accessible blueprint that enables users to design central processing units (CPUs).

The architecture has surged in popularity in China because it is free to use and modify, as Beijing and local businesses race to reduce their reliance on foreign proprietary technology amid Washington’s tightening tech curbs.

First developed in 2010 by researchers at the University of California, Berkeley, the standard is now managed by RISC-V International, a Zurich-based non-profit organisation. Qi, who spearheads Alibaba’s RISC-V initiatives and previously held senior management technical roles at Intel, sits on the organisation’s board of directors. Alibaba is the owner of the SCMP.

While RISC-V has gained traction globally, it is not nearly as widely used as competing proprietary standards such as Intel’s x86 and Arm’s namesake architecture, which respectively dominate the personal computer and smartphone markets. To bridge that gap, technical committee members from around the world were working together to rectify the universal specifications the industry followed, Qi said.

The Chinese e-commerce and AI giant was an early mover in China’s RISC-V space, having launched the XuanTie RISC-V chip series in 2018. The company’s research arm, Damo Academy, unveiled its latest XuanTie flagship, the C950, in March this year. Designed for high-performance tasks in cloud and AI computing, the CPU core, Alibaba said, was the most powerful of its kind globally, based on internal performance benchmarks.

Qi’s remarks came as US-China technological competition intensifies across both semiconductors and artificial intelligence. However, Qi noted that a major gap remained in physical chip manufacturing due to limited access to critical chipmaking tools, including extreme ultraviolet lithography machines.

Hong Kong, meanwhile, has declared its ambitions to secure a foothold in the RISC-V space amid the city’s drive to become an innovation and technology hub.

The city’s strengths in chip design included deep capital markets, the ability to attract international talent and leading universities, according to Qi.

In his capacity as a member of the board of RISC-V International, Qi once floated the idea of Hong Kong potentially hosting a future RISC-V summit, and quite some members from the US and Europe showed strong interest, he said.
