China’s satellite engine smashes record, leaves US rival far behind

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 549 words | 2026-07-08

China has tested a satellite engine with a record-breaking operating life, a key technology that could help propel the world’s largest communications, military and deep-space spacecraft into their intended orbits faster and more reliably.

The upgraded engine, developed by the China Academy of Aerospace Propulsion Technology in Xian, can produce 750 newtons of thrust, according to Chinese media reports.

The Chinese engine fired for 11,617 seconds – 3.2 hours – across five orbit-raising manoeuvres during its maiden flight in late June, placing Communications Technology Experiment Satellite 26A into orbit about 35,800km (22,000 miles) above Earth.

The engine, which was designed to run for nearly 10 hours, operated continuously for more than 14 hours during testing, thanks to a novel heat- and oxidation-resistant coating, the reports said.

In contrast, the leading engines currently used to raise spacecraft into their final orbit after launch, including the US-made R-42DM and Europe’s Leros-1B, are typically designed to operate for about seven hours.

The extra operating time did not come at the expense of performance, according to the reports.

The engine achieved a specific impulse of 320 seconds during the mission – a measure of how efficiently it converts propellant into thrust – putting it on a par with the world’s best chemical satellite engines from the United States, Europe and Japan, they said.

“The engine not only provides a reliable propulsion system for testing new space technologies, but also marks China’s transition from a follower to a leader in high-thrust satellite propulsion,” the Workers’ Daily report said.

China’s push to develop these engines dates back to the 1980s, when it was building its first geostationary communications satellites and faced foreign embargoes on the critical propulsion technology.

A communications satellite is not placed directly into its final orbit by the rocket. The launch vehicle leaves it in an elliptical transfer orbit, and the engine on board the satellite must fire repeatedly to circularise the orbit at geostationary altitude.

At the time, only countries like the US and West Germany had this capability, the Workers’ Daily report noted.

Starting from scratch, the Chinese team spent more than a decade overcoming a series of engineering challenges before the first domestically-developed, orbit-raising 490 N engine flew aboard the Dong Fang Hong-3 communications satellite in 1994.

By 2021, the first-generation engine had achieved a 100 per cent success rate, supporting missions that included China’s first lunar mission and the dramatic rescue of ChinaSat 9A, which needed 10 orbit-raising manoeuvres following a launch anomaly in 2017.

As satellites have grown larger, 490 N engines have had to fire repeatedly to reach their final orbits.

According to China Space News, the 750 N engine can cut the orbit-raising time of heavy spacecraft by about 30 per cent, leaving more fuel for years of operation while allowing military and communications satellites to be deployed more quickly when needed.

The Workers’ Daily said development was already under way on much more powerful engines, including a 5,000 N model, which could potentially be used for space tugs and other large spacecraft – an area being actively pursued in the US and Europe.
