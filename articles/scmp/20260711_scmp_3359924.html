China, Hawaii and the bridge of memory I helped build between them

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 874 words | 2026-07-11

During Ching Ming Festival this year, I travelled to China for the first time and my family gathered on Sanzao Island in the Pearl River Delta to honour our ancestors. On a spring morning, as relatives offered incense and arranged paper offerings before the ancestral graves and thin streams of smoke drifted into the warm coastal air, I was at long last standing on the island my family had spoken about for generations.

My great-grandfather had left Sanzao as a sojourner in the late 19th century and eventually built a life across the Pacific, in Maui, with my great-grandmother, a native Hawaiian Chinese. Like many migrants of his generation, my great-grandfather carried his future abroad while leaving parts of his life behind – including a frail, handwritten manuscript dating back to the twilight of the Qing dynasty. Unexpectedly, this was still waiting for me during my trip, having been preserved over generations and donated to the Zhuhai Museum.

His journey was part of a broader history linking China to Hawaii, where Chinese migrants formed families across cultures, shaping communities that bridged ocean and origin.

For me, Sanzao existed first through memory: stories passed down, photographs and other fragments carried across distance, names of places I had never set foot in. For two decades, my father, Robert Cupchoy, travelled to Sanzao to listen to elders and record their memories of the Japanese occupation. As I worked with him on what would eventually become our book, Echoes of Memory: War, Testimony and Survival on Sanzao Island, I came to know the island before I ever saw it myself.

Returning to our ancestral homeland during Ching Ming, not only as a descendant but also as a co-author, gave my journey particular weight. The festival is a time of remembrance and for many in the Chinese diaspora, a chance for a homecoming of sorts.

My cousins, Yingxiu Chen and Jianming Chen, welcomed me and accompanied me as I retraced my family’s history across the island. Through them, I was able to see places such as the home built by my great-grandfather. And in the Zhuhai Museum, I held in my hands the yellowing slim volume he had filled with his thoughts as a young man in 1877. Reading his observations of daily life and poetic reflections on the natural scenery around Sanzao created a sense of connection across time.

Conversations with my relatives became an exchange of family history. Over the years, my own research had uncovered immigration records, photographs and documents connected to relatives who had left China for Hawaii.

Yingxiu had never seen childhood photographs of his grandfather – one of my great-grandparents’ 13 children, who had been sent back to China as a boy to continue his education and would go on to help oversee the family business. Sharing those records with my cousin was a reminder that family history had travelled in multiple directions across the Pacific, leaving gaps that descendants on both sides were still working to fill in.

I also presented a copy of the book I co-wrote to the museum, where it now sits alongside my great-grandfather’s manuscript. In that moment, to my mind, the direction of memory shifted.

As we went around Sanzao, my cousin Yingxiu guided me through places connected to the wartime past – former military sites, memorial grounds and locations where violence had unfolded amid ordinary village life. Some places were clearly marked, but others required explanation.

At one site, he told me quietly, “If you just look, you won’t see it. You have to know what happened here.” He pointed to what looked like an ordinary concrete bunker surrounded by trees and explained that it had once been a Japanese gun post overlooking a civilian community.

In each place, the past was not distant. It was embedded in the landscape and transmitted by those who continue to remember and interpret it. Watching the crane dance performed on Sanzao was especially meaningful for me because it was one of the traditions my great-grandfather had taught on the island before migrating to Maui. Along with kung fu, these traditions have continued to be passed down on Sanzao.

For those of us in the diaspora, return is often imagined as a way of recovering what was lost. Working on the book with my father taught me that heritage is not simply inherited; it is sustained through the stories, relationships and responsibilities we choose to carry forward.

But what I experienced on Sanzao was something more complex. The past was present, but not intact. It had been shaped by war, by development and by time. What remains are traces – held in landscapes, archives and living memory.

Standing in the museum with the manuscript my great-grandfather had written nearly a century and a half ago, I understood that return is not only about going back. It is about carrying something forward.

The work my father and I did together, rooted in the voices of those who lived this history, is now part of that movement. Bringing our book back to Sanzao felt like I was completing a journey we had begun together. We were not simply remembering the past. We were extending it.
