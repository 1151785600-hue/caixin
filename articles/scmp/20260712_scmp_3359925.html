Investors have more to worry about than yen bears on the hunt

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 887 words | 2026-07-12

Why are global investors so bearish on the yen? There are good reasons Japan’s currency should be gaining in value. Last month, the Bank of Japan (BOJ) raised interest rates to a 31-year high of 1 per cent and signalled it would continue increasing borrowing costs. Bond markets are currently pricing in a nearly 90 per cent probability of another increase by December.

Nominal wages in Japan have risen by more than 3 per cent for four straight months, the longest streak since 1992. Moreover, the findings of the BOJ’s quarterly Tankan business survey on July 1 showed confidence among large manufacturing firms rose to its highest level since 2018, allaying fears that the global energy shock would undermine sentiment.

Another reason the yen should be strengthening is the dramatic rise in Japanese government bond yields. The 10-year yield, which was barely above 1 per cent as recently as the end of 2024, has shot up to 2.8 per cent, its highest level since 1996. This has narrowed the yield gap between benchmark Japanese and US bonds to 1.7 percentage points, down from about four in October 2023.

Even so, on June 30 the yen fell to its weakest level against the US dollar since December 1986. Hedge funds have ramped up their bets against Japan’s currency to the highest level since 2007, according to Commodity Futures Trading Commission data.

Yen bearishness is pervasive. In a report on July 7, Bank of America said that “for the first time in years, we didn’t meet a single yen bull during our investor meetings last week”. Some analysts believe there is a risk that Japan’s currency, which is trading at around 162 yen per dollar, could fall to 180 in a year’s time.

The yen’s persistent decline is attributable to several factors. One of the most important ones is the breakdown in the correlation between movements in bond yields and the currency. Worryingly, as yields rose steeply in the last 18 months, the yen continued to fall, a phenomenon typically associated with vulnerable emerging markets.

Robin Brooks of the Brookings Institution believes Japan’s long-term bond yields should be much higher because of mounting fiscal risks that could lead to a full-blown debt crisis. “To avoid a debt crisis, the BOJ caps bond yields. But this means risk premia aren’t reflected in bond yields, i.e. interest rates are too low versus the risk of crisis in the eyes of markets,” he wrote in a social media post, adding that this puts depreciation pressure on the yen.

Another factor is the surge in Japanese stocks as investors pile into semiconductor firms benefiting from the artificial intelligence boom. Many foreign funds have hedged their exposure to the yen. The fierce rally in Japanese equities has increased hedging activity, weighing on the currency. “Should Japanese equities continue to make strong gains, this downward pressure on the [yen] will continue,” Citigroup said in a report.

However, the main reason for the yen’s prolonged slide, as Goldman Sachs pointed out in a report on July 3, is the “combination of expansionary fiscal policy and seemingly constrained monetary policy”.

While the BOJ is tightening policy, Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s aggressive fiscal stimulus programme in which the government plans to invest 370 trillion yen (US$2.3 trillion) over the next 14 years is exacerbating tensions in policymaking. Pressure from the government to prevent sharper increases in interest rates is reinforcing the perception that the BOJ is tightening policy too slowly, especially at a time when the inflationary impact of the weak yen is amplified by the rise in the US dollar.

Although investors are resoundingly downbeat about the yen, many of them benefit greatly from its weakness. The yen is the funding currency of choice for the “carry trade”, whereby investors borrow in low-yielding currencies to fund their purchases of higher-yielding assets, such as technology stocks and emerging market bonds and currencies.

If the yen suddenly strengthened sharply – possibly as a result of more forceful and effective intervention by the government to shore up the currency – investors, especially hedge funds, would be forced to rapidly unwind their leveraged bets, throwing global markets into turmoil.

This is what happened in 1998 and, in a less disorderly fashion, in 2024. The dramatic decline in the yen is a bigger problem for Japanese households, whose purchasing power has been crushed by the collapse of the currency.

A sharper sell-off in Japanese bonds, on the other hand, could prove much more damaging to global markets. If yields on long-term debt continue to rise, domestic investors could start to repatriate overseas assets, lured by more attractive returns at home.

If the BOJ ends up raising interest rates more quickly than markets anticipate to dispel fears it has fallen behind the curve, foreign investors could increase their exposure to Japanese sovereign debt. Any sign that capital flows are moving in Japan’s favour could push up bond yields in the rest of the world.

In a report on May 22, HSBC said that “as bond yields rise in Japan, they end up pushing up funding costs not only locally, but across the world, too”, making the rise in Japanese yields “everybody’s problem”.

Global investors might be bearish on the yen, but it is what happens in the bond market that will determine whether the bearishness extends far beyond Japan.
