Formal China-US G2 framework key to ‘guarantee’ world peace: top Beijing adviser

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 777 words | 2026-07-13

An “institutionalised” China-US G2 framework could offer a “structural guarantee” for global peace in a world beset by crises, a high-profile adviser to Beijing has said.

Zheng Yongnian, a noted Chinese political scientist, said the world was faced with four intertwined challenges – geopolitical conflicts, increasingly “religionised” societies, imbalances in the global economy and the fading of the UN-centred governance system. He characterised this collective breakdown as a “crisis of modernity”.

A pressing question was how to move beyond the US’ or European Union’s bilateral trade disputes with Beijing, to rebalance the global economy by having all three sides work together, Zheng told the Baichuan Forum in Shenzhen, southern China, on Saturday.

He warned that while it might be economically “possible” for the US and Europe to set up cooperative global development mechanisms with China, deep geopolitical, ideological, and structural differences would make this “very difficult” to achieve.

Zheng, dean of the school of public policy at Chinese University of Hong Kong, Shenzhen, argued that China and the United States might each adopt an “open-source” approach to international development, applying their strategies to regions where they held a distinct comparative advantage.

In this scenario, the US would pair its advanced technologies with the manufacturing power of developed economies like Japan and Europe, while China would focus its efforts primarily on the Global South, he said.

“It must be pointed out that this process is open-source and will not lead to what many fear – a world with two centres or two markets,” Zheng said at the forum hosted by the university’s Institute of International Affairs, Qianhai, a think tank he leads.

He argued that China and the US would remain open to each other’s spheres, with talent and capital flowing in both directions based on market and cost considerations. As evidence, he cited 2025 Nobel laureate in chemistry Omar Yaghi’s recent move from the United States to a full-time position at Beijing’s Tsinghua University.

The world’s two largest economies remained “highly complementary”, Zheng said, noting that while competition was fierce in the hi-tech sector, the two sides were still deeply interdependent.

“In terms of economy and trade, China and the US are 80 per cent cooperative and 20 per cent confrontational,” he said. “The main source of trade and economic friction [between them] is low political trust, having politicised and even securitised too many areas that should not have been treated either way.”

“If political trust improves, the two economies will not decouple but become even more interdependent,” he added, pointing to the significance of the multiple meetings between Chinese and US leaders this year.

Zheng reiterated that a de facto “Group of Two (G2)” structure had emerged between China and the US, and that if the bilateral relationship was managed effectively, the world could largely avoid major turmoil.

“Once this G2 framework is institutionalised, it will not only promote global development and help rebalance the world economy but also deepen cooperation between the two countries, thereby providing a structural guarantee for world peace,” he said.

Shenzhen is due to host the Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation leaders’ gathering in November, with the Group of 20 summit in Miami, Florida, to follow in December.

The two multilateral events are expected to offer additional opportunities for face-to-face talks between Chinese President Xi Jinping and Donald Trump, beyond the US leader’s visit to Beijing in May and Xi’s anticipated reciprocal US trip in September.

Trump floated the idea of a US-China G2 landscape ahead of his meeting with Xi on the sidelines of the Apec summit in Busan, South Korea, last October, pointing to a duopoly to manage global affairs as the world’s leading superpowers.

China expressed reluctance to accept the concept at the time, stressing that it would not subscribe to “the logic that the world can be run by [a few] major countries”.

China should have sufficient wisdom and capability to choose a path of achieving cooperation through struggle in its relations with the US

Zheng Yongnian

However, following the Beijing summit, China announced that both sides had agreed to build a “constructive relationship of strategic stability”. The White House later echoed this framing, adding that the relationship must operate “on the basis of fairness and reciprocity”.

Zheng noted that while certain factors could push China and the US towards conflict, one should “not underestimate” the role of politics in shaping ties.

As the inheritor of an uninterrupted, centuries-old civilisation, he said, “China should have sufficient wisdom and capability to choose a path of achieving cooperation through struggle in its relations with the US.”
