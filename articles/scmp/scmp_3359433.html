Energy transition scientist Chen Peipei leaves Cambridge to build her own lab in Hong Kong

SCMP | FREE DEEP | 938 words | 2026-07-11

For some early-career scientists, the prestige of British academia is being marred by shrinking research funding and a complex geopolitical climate, prompting top-tier talent to look elsewhere for stability and resources.

Chen Peipei, who moved from a research associate role at the University of Cambridge to a presidential assistant professorship at City University of Hong Kong in May, said she was drawn by a landscape supportive of young faculty.

The energy transition scientist joins the school of energy and environment at CityU, where she has access to the research budgets and PhD student recruitment quotas necessary to build a team – an opportunity she said was becoming increasingly rare in Britain.

“For scientists, having funds and a team to carry out research is hugely appealing,” she said in an interview. “In Britain, a teaching position rarely comes with research start-up funds. New faculty members are mostly expected to teach, leaving little time to develop their own research.”

As British universities grapple with a financial crisis, a recent survey by Universities UK found that nearly one third of the 48 responding member institutions had cut academic research activity in the past three years, more than double the 14 per cent reported in 2024.

It also found staffing was a primary cost-saving target, with 79 per cent of universities pursuing voluntary redundancies over the past three years or putting hiring freezes in place.

Many institutions across Britain have announced job-cut plans over the past two months, including the universities of Dundee, Sussex, Nottingham, Glasgow and Aberdeen.

Another survey of about 460 junior researchers in physics and astrophysics – conducted after a UK Research and Innovation council announced grant cuts of around 30 per cent in January – found that only 8 per cent would now recommend a career in the field in Britain, down from 74 per cent before the cuts.

Nearly three-quarters of postdoctoral researchers were considering leaving Britain, with a quarter already actively applying for international roles. The survey pointed to “a loss of confidence, loss of reassurance and a measurable risk” driving trained researchers to leave.

Chen said moving to Hong Kong offered her a distinct research environment. Her work, which focuses on decarbonisation pathways and low-carbon technology deployment, addresses a paradox common to global financial hubs: how to cut emissions where there are no heavy industries to target.

“Hong Kong is a service and finance-oriented economy,” Chen said. “Unlike the mainland, where major emitters like steel and cement are the primary targets, we look to sectors like transport and construction.

“The challenge is: how do you further reduce emissions in an economy that is already relatively low-carbon?”

One way to tackle this is integrating technologies such as carbon capture, use and storage into dense urban environments, a struggle shared by global cities as they push towards carbon neutrality targets.

Having experienced Hong Kong’s intense rainfall and with the typhoon season approaching, Chen said: “Achieving carbon neutrality with climate-resilient energy systems amid environmental shocks and the broader climate crisis presents an even bigger challenge.”

While maintaining an international outlook, the city offers what she describes as “a more consistent and neutral environment” for research collaborations, paired with close connections to the Greater Bay Area, mainland China and Southeast Asia.

Chen joined the Energy Policy Research Group at Cambridge as a research associate in 2024 after earning a PhD in ecological economics from University College London. Her academic journey in Britain began in 2020 after graduating from the University of Chinese Academy of Sciences and Anhui University.

Beyond funding gaps, the geopolitical climate is reshaping the daily reality of research in Britain.

Chen said Britain’s approach to China-related research collaborations was partly influenced by its alignment with the United States, potentially affecting long-term career planning and international research partnerships, especially in high-stakes fields such as biotechnology.

“Geopolitics changes in phases,” she said. “This is difficult [to navigate]. It is hard to be certain about the timing of your work – when you are establishing project collaborations or when you are looking for a job – whether that specific point in time might be problematic.”

She said heightened scrutiny stemming from geopolitical tensions might discourage British scientists from maintaining ties with Chinese peers, with some choosing to pause plans in the hope of a less volatile future.

As for Chinese students ready to advance their research overseas, the fallout of geopolitical friction – such as frozen recruitment or visa denials – could set back professional development by a year or two, she said.

As early-career researchers weigh their career paths, university rankings can serve as a key reference point. While comprehensive scales such as the QS university rankings are mostly topped by Western universities, research output-focused benchmarks like the Nature institution rankings are led by Chinese universities.

Chen said rankings designed differently could not be directly compared, yet they might point to different approaches to education.

“Education in the East is more pragmatic and places emphasis on tangible output, while the West tends to focus on comprehensive reputation and internationalisation,” she said. “They are different paths chosen based on the actual needs of each country’s development stage.”

“In Britain, I found that few people pay a lot of attention to QS rankings,” she said. “In the academic and industrial circles, genuine professional strength is valued. We often collaborate with institutions that are outstanding in industrial technologies, although they might rank low in a comprehensive ranking.

“Academic circles would not question [the status of universities highly specialised in certain fields], but Chinese employers who use a blanket ‘QS top 50’ rule during recruitment would put their graduates at a disadvantage.”
